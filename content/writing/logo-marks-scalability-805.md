## The Favicon Test

I use the favicon test as a quick filter for any logo mark I'm designing. If I reduce the mark to 32x32 pixels and it's still recognizable and legible, I'm in good shape. If it collapses into noise, I have a problem to solve.

Most logo marks fail this test because they were designed at presentation size — 400px or larger in a nice document — and never tested at the extremes. The complexity that looks sophisticated at large size becomes visual mud at small size.

## Designing for Extremes

My process is to design at both extremes simultaneously. I'll have the mark at large scale in one artboard and at favicon scale in another, and I'll work on both as I develop the concept. Changes that help one often hurt the other, and seeing both together forces better decisions.

The most common culprit is stroke weight. Thin strokes that look elegant at 300px become invisible or merge with adjacent elements at 24px. I design with strokes that have real mass at small sizes, even if they feel slightly heavy at display scale.

## When to Use Alternate Lockups

Some marks need simplified alternate versions for very small applications. This isn't a failure — it's professional execution. I'll design a primary mark with full detail and a simplified version (sometimes just an initial or a reduced form) for use below a certain size threshold.

The brand guidelines I produce always specify these thresholds clearly: "Use the full mark at 60px and above. Use the simplified mark at 32–59px." No ambiguity, no guessing.

## The Single-Color Test

After scale, the next test is single-color reproduction. The mark needs to work in solid black, solid white, and as a knockout on both light and dark backgrounds. This immediately reveals if the mark depends too heavily on color to carry its meaning.

A mark that only works in its full-color version is fragile. It'll fail on embroidered merchandise, engraved signage, and anywhere the printer is working with a single ink. Designing for single-color from the start produces stronger marks.

## Geometric Precision

I build all my marks on geometric foundations — not because geometry looks better, but because it reproduces better. Precisely constructed curves and angles don't drift when scaled, reproduced, or vectorized for cutting.

I work in Illustrator with rulers, guides, and the transform panel. No eyeballing. The difference between a curve that's precisely tangent to a line and one that's close-but-not-quite is invisible at design scale and very visible on a large-format vinyl cut.

## The Real Mark of a Good Mark

A mark is finished when I can't remove anything else without losing meaning. That's the standard. Not when it looks good in the presentation deck, not when the client says they love it — when every element is doing specific necessary work and nothing is decorative. That's the mark worth delivering.
