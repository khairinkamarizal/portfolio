## Contrast is what makes type come alive

A poster set entirely in one weight at one size, regardless of how beautiful the typeface is, reads as a flat plane. The eye has nothing to move toward or away from. Typographic contrast — variation in size, weight, style, color, or spacing — is what creates visual movement and hierarchy on a poster.

The challenge is that most designers understand contrast as a concept but apply it timidly in practice. They'll make the headline a bit bigger and the subhead a bit lighter, when they should be making the headline dramatically bigger and the subhead so light it nearly disappears.

## The scale jump needs to be bold

The single most common mistake in poster typography is insufficient scale contrast between hierarchy levels. If the headline is 60pt and the secondary text is 40pt, the hierarchy is barely perceptible. If the headline is 120pt and the secondary text is 18pt, the hierarchy is unmistakable.

I think of it as a ratio problem. For posters that will be read at a distance, I want at least a 4:1 size ratio between the primary and secondary hierarchy levels. That means if the secondary text is 16pt, the primary should be at least 64pt. This feels extreme until you see it working — and then you can't unsee how timid smaller ratios look.

## Weight contrast and scale contrast amplify each other

Size and weight work together. An extra-bold display face at 100pt has enormous visual weight. Set next to a thin or light face at 14pt, the contrast is visually violent in the best possible way — the eye jumps between them and the information structure becomes immediately clear.

When I'm pairing weights for maximum contrast, I avoid the middle. I don't use medium and bold; I use thin and black. I don't use regular and semibold; I use light and extrabold. The middle weights are useful for fine-tuning hierarchy within a level, not for creating contrast between levels.

## Color as a contrast tool

Color adds a third dimension to typographic contrast. A headline set in the brand's primary color, a subhead in black, and a date in a lighter gray creates contrast through color value even when size and weight differences are moderate.

I use color contrast sparingly in poster typography — usually one highlighted element per composition, not a rainbow of colors across every level. The highlight should be the most emotionally important information: the artist's name, the event date, the provocative question that makes someone stop.

## Spacing as contrast

Spacing is the most underused contrast tool. A headline with generous letter-spacing signals a different register than a tightly-tracked one. A section of text isolated with significant white space above and below it feels elevated above text that runs continuously.

I use spacing to create breathing room around the most important information. The visual silence around an isolated element makes it feel significant without requiring more size or weight.
