## The Redesign Reflex

There's a bias in our industry toward redesign. It's more visible work, it generates more discussion, and it signals change in ways that incremental refinement doesn't. But the redesign reflex — the instinct to solve brand problems by replacing the identity — is often wrong.

Many brands with genuine brand problems have those problems because of inconsistent execution, not weak design. Replacing a perfectly good identity with a new one doesn't fix inconsistent execution — it just gives you new assets to apply inconsistently.

## What Qualifies as a Refresh

A refresh updates and modernizes an existing identity without replacing its core elements. It might mean: redrawing an existing mark with cleaner geometry and updated proportions, extending the color palette with contemporary additions while keeping the core brand color, refreshing the typeface (perhaps moving from a generic system font to a custom or premium equivalent), and updating the visual language (illustration style, photographic approach) to feel more current.

A refresh preserves the equity in the existing identity while addressing specific problems. It's lower risk, less expensive, and often delivers better business results than a full redesign.

## What Qualifies as a Redesign

A full redesign is warranted when: the brand's positioning has fundamentally changed (different audience, different competitive context, different promise), the existing mark has become so visually outdated that no amount of refinement will address the problem, the brand has acquired such negative associations that distance from the current identity is strategically valuable, or the existing identity is so poorly constructed that it can't serve as a foundation.

These are real cases. But they're less common than the number of redesigns that actually happen would suggest.

## The Business Case for Continuity

Brand recognition has economic value. Customers who've learned to recognize a brand — its colors, its mark, its visual character — carry that recognition into future encounters. Every redesign resets some of that recognition. The cost of that reset needs to be justified by the gains the redesign delivers.

I push clients to quantify this cost before recommending a redesign. If we're throwing away years of recognition, what do we get in return?

## How I Make the Call

My process: start with the audit (what does the existing identity actually look like, in practice?), identify the specific problems (what is the identity failing to do?), evaluate whether those problems are solvable within the existing identity's framework, and then recommend the lightest intervention that addresses the real problems.

Sometimes that's a redesign. Often it's a refresh. Occasionally it's better execution of what already exists.

The least intervention with the most impact is always the right goal.
