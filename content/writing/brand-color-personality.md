Most branding projects I've seen go wrong in the same sequence. The client sees some color palettes in the early presentation, picks the one that feels nice, and suddenly the entire brand is built around a color choice that was made before the brand's character was defined.

Color is a communication tool. You can't choose the right tool until you know what you're trying to communicate.

## Character First, Always

Before I show a client a single color swatch, I need to know what the brand's personality is. Not in vague, aspirational terms — in specific, differentiated ones. Not "innovative and trustworthy" (every company says this). Instead: "technically rigorous but humanistically motivated" or "playful but never frivolous" or "authoritative without being exclusive."

These character definitions come from the brand strategy work that should precede identity design. If that work hasn't been done, the first phase of identity work is doing it.

## Color Carries Meaning, Not Just Mood

Color associations aren't arbitrary, but they're also not universal. The meanings color carries depend on cultural context, category conventions, and the visual landscape the brand occupies.

In the financial services category, dark blues and greens carry associations of stability, trustworthiness, and growth that are deeply conventional. Using these colors in a financial brand can feel safe and credible — or generic and undifferentiated, depending on how everything else in the identity is handled.

Knowing the category conventions gives me a starting point for a conversation about whether to respect, subvert, or ignore them.

## The Competitive Audit

Before finalizing a brand's color direction, I map the color territory occupied by direct competitors. This isn't about avoiding colors for the sake of originality — it's about understanding what the color landscape means in this specific context.

If the category is saturated with a particular hue, choosing that hue requires a strong reason. Differentiation isn't the only valid reason (alignment with category expectations can be a deliberate choice), but differentiation has to be consciously weighed against conformity.

## Testing Color Decisions

I test color decisions by creating simple, text-only mock-ups with the proposed palette and asking: does this feel like the brand character we defined? Not "do you like this?" — that's the wrong question. The right question is whether the color is communicating the right thing.

When clients answer "do you like this?" instead of "does this communicate correctly?", I redirect them. Personal preference is irrelevant. Communication effectiveness is the standard.

## The Palette as a System

A brand color palette is a system with defined roles, not a collection of swatches. Primary color: used most, carries the most brand weight. Secondary colors: support and complement the primary. Accent colors: used sparingly for specific purposes.

Each color in the palette should have a reason for existing. Colors that are there because "we needed another option" are colors that dilute the system.
