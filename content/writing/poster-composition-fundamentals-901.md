## Starting With the Wall, Not the Screen

Before I open any software, I think about where the poster is going to live. A concert poster taped to a utility pole at eye level is a completely different problem than a gallery announcement in a white-walled space. Context drives every composition decision I make, and skipping this step is how you end up with technically competent work that somehow feels wrong in the real world.

I've been designing posters professionally for about eight years. In that time I've produced work for music festivals, cultural institutions, political campaigns, and independent restaurants. The medium never gets old because the constraint is so clean: you have one rectangular surface, usually viewed from a distance first, and you need to communicate something fast.

## The Three-Zone Rule

I divide every poster into three reading zones. Zone one is the instant read — what registers in under a second from ten feet away. This is usually a single dominant image or a massive typographic element. Zone two is the two-to-five second engagement — the supporting information that rewards the viewer who steps closer. Zone three is the fine print: dates, addresses, URLs, legal copy.

Most failed posters fail because the designer treated zones one and two as equals. You cannot have two dominant elements. One thing has to win.

## How I Build Tension Without Chaos

Static, perfectly centered compositions feel safe. They're also forgettable. I deliberately introduce asymmetry, but asymmetry with a purpose — a heavy typographic block offset by negative space, or a diagonal image element that pulls the eye toward the most important text.

The key is that tension needs resolution. The eye should feel pulled, then satisfied. If I'm placing a large image on the right two-thirds of the poster, the left third needs to do real work — either a strong vertical type treatment or enough negative space that the breathing room itself becomes a compositional element.

## Grids Are Not Cages

I always start with a grid. Usually a twelve-column system at whatever document size I'm working with. But grids exist to be broken deliberately, not followed blindly. The most effective thing a grid does is give you a baseline so that when you violate it, the violation reads as intentional.

I'll lock supporting body copy to the grid columns. I'll let the headline run edge-to-edge, bleeding past the margins. The contrast between structured and free creates the visual interest.

## Type as Image

On posters, type isn't just information delivery — it's a graphic element competing with everything else on the surface. I treat letterforms the way I treat shapes. The counter of a capital O, the diagonal stress of an italic, the texture of a block of small-point text — all of these create visual weight and movement.

When the poster brief allows it, I prefer to work with type alone. No photography, no illustration. Just letterforms, scale contrast, and a tight color palette. It's the most demanding format and the most satisfying when it works.

## The Test I Always Run

Before I send anything to print, I take a screenshot of the file at 25% zoom and squint at it. If I can still tell what the most important element is, the hierarchy is working. If everything blurs into an undifferentiated gray mass, I have a composition problem, not a detail problem.

Posters are a distance medium first. Get that right, and the close-up details will have an audience.
