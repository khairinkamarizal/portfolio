## A Different Kind of Client

Cultural institutions — museums, galleries, arts organizations, nonprofits — are some of the most interesting branding clients I work with, and some of the most challenging. The challenges aren't technical. They're organizational and political in ways that commercial clients often aren't.

A museum board is a committee. A gallery has a director with strong aesthetic opinions. A nonprofit has multiple stakeholders with competing visions of what the organization is and who it serves. Getting any visual identity approved in these environments requires as much facilitation as design.

## The Mission, Not the Brand Manager

Commercial brands are managed by people whose job is the brand. Cultural institutions often don't have this — the executive director manages programs, the development director manages donors, the curator manages the collection. Nobody owns the brand as their primary responsibility.

This means brand decisions get made by whoever shows up to the meeting, which changes based on the week. Early in a project I try to identify who the actual decision-maker is — the person whose yes gets us to approval — and focus the presentation on their priorities.

## Timelessness Over Trend

Cultural institutions need visual identities that can survive leadership changes, programming shifts, and the inevitable design fashions of the decades to come. The Met's identity doesn't need to be updated every five years. A community arts center's mark shouldn't look like it was designed for this year's grant cycle.

I push hard toward timeless solutions in this sector. That means avoiding fashionable display typefaces, pushing back on overly complex marks, and arguing for simplicity even when the client wants more.

## The Program Identity Problem

Many cultural institutions have a core brand and multiple program identities — the museum brand, but also the education program brand, the gala brand, the youth initiative brand. These sub-brands often developed independently before a coherent system existed.

Bringing these into alignment is a significant design challenge. The goal is a system where the program identities feel related to the parent brand without being identical to it. This usually means a shared typographic system and color logic, with the mark adapting at the program level.

## Working with Existing History

Most established cultural institutions have significant equity in their existing identity, even if it's visually dated. Throwing everything out typically meets fierce resistance — and often rightly so. A refresh that preserves recognizable elements while modernizing execution usually gets more traction and produces better outcomes than a full rebrand.

I start every cultural institution project by mapping what's working, what has equity, and what's genuinely broken. The answer to "what needs to change?" is usually smaller than the client initially believes, and the answer to "what has earned the right to stay?" is usually larger.
