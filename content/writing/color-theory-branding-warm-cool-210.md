## Temperature is the first thing we feel

Before a viewer processes what a brand's logo looks like or what the tagline says, they've already received an impression from the color temperature of the visual. Warm colors — reds, oranges, yellows — signal energy, warmth, appetite, urgency. Cool colors — blues, greens, purples — signal calm, trust, precision, restraint.

These associations aren't arbitrary. They're deeply embedded in human perception through millennia of experience with fire (warm, urgent) and sky and water (cool, calm). Brands that understand and use this deliberately have an edge over brands that choose colors purely for aesthetic reasons.

## Reading the category

The first thing I do when building a color strategy for a brand is map the color temperature of its competitive category. Not to follow the category norms — but to understand them clearly enough to either align with them or consciously deviate.

Financial services: predominantly cool blues and greens. Associations — trust, stability, precision. A financial brand that breaks into warm territory is making a statement: "we're different from those stiff, cold banks." That statement might be exactly right for a challenger brand. It might be completely wrong for a wealth management firm targeting conservative investors.

Fast food: predominantly warm reds and yellows. The associations here are appetite-stimulating and urgent. If I'm branding a fast-casual restaurant that wants to position against fast food as a healthier, more considered choice, moving the palette toward cooler, earthier tones makes that positioning visible before a customer sees the menu.

## Mixed temperature palettes

The most sophisticated brand color systems use both warm and cool tones in deliberate combination. A primary brand color in a warm amber, balanced by a cool dark navy as the secondary — this creates visual tension that feels dynamic and considered.

The key is establishing a clear temperature dominant. The palette should read primarily warm or primarily cool from a distance. When the palette is genuinely temperature-neutral, it often reads as muddy or unresolved rather than sophisticated.

## Saturation is part of temperature

Temperature doesn't operate alone — it interacts with saturation. A highly saturated warm red reads very differently than a desaturated dusty terracotta, even though both are warm. The desaturated version feels more premium, more restrained, more contemporary. The saturated version feels more energetic, more populist, more accessible.

When I'm calibrating the warmth of a brand palette, I'm always calibrating saturation alongside temperature. A warm but low-saturation palette can work beautifully for premium and artisan brands where the warmth signals craftsmanship rather than urgency.

## Consistency across media

Color temperature is notoriously difficult to maintain consistently across different media. The warm amber that looks perfect in a print brand system can read as orange-yellow on a backlit screen, and as brown on certain fabric printing processes.

I always specify brand colors in multiple color systems — Pantone for spot printing, CMYK for process print, RGB/Hex for digital — and I test them in all three before finalizing. The temperature character of the color needs to survive the translation from one system to another, even if the exact values shift.
