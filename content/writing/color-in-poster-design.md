Color is the first thing people perceive and the last thing they can articulate. Before they've read a word on your poster, they've already formed an emotional response based on your palette. That's either an advantage or a liability, depending on whether you made intentional choices.

I didn't truly understand color as a communication tool until I started designing under severe constraints. One-color print jobs, risograph limitations, two-spot-color budgets. Constraint teaches you that color is doing real work, not just filling space.

## Limiting the Palette Forces Intention

I start almost every poster project by setting a maximum of three colors, including black. This isn't a stylistic affectation — it's a discipline that forces me to make each color earn its place.

When you have ten colors available, you can always add another hue to solve a problem. When you have two, you have to solve it with composition, contrast, and typography. The problems you solve with constraint are usually better solutions than the ones you solve with abundance.

## Color Temperature and Hierarchy

Warm colors advance; cool colors recede. This is basic perceptual psychology, but it has direct implications for poster hierarchy. If your headline is in a warm red and your background is cool blue, the headline comes forward. If you reverse it, the hierarchy flattens.

I map color temperature to information hierarchy early in the process. What needs to come forward? Give it warmth. What's supporting context? Cool it down or neutralize it.

## The One-Color Discipline

Some of my best poster work has been one-color. Not because of budget constraints, but because the constraint produces focus. One color means every decision about emphasis has to come from value, scale, and negative space. There's nowhere to hide.

If you're struggling with a poster palette, try stripping it to one color and building the hierarchy without color as a crutch. When you add color back, you'll know exactly what job it's doing.

## Color in Print vs. Screen

This is where designers get burned. RGB on screen and CMYK in print are different universes. Colors that look vivid and saturated on screen often print flat and dull. Neons are almost impossible to reproduce faithfully in CMYK without a spot color.

I always check my palette against CMYK gamut before finalizing. If a color falls significantly outside the CMYK gamut, I adjust it in the design phase — not after receiving proofs. And I always order press proofs for anything where color accuracy matters.

## Pantone and Spot Color

For brand-aligned poster work, spot colors are often non-negotiable. A client's Pantone color is a legal and contractual matter as much as a design one. I've seen printers match a spot color within a Delta E of 1. I've seen offset presses nail a vivid orange that CMYK couldn't touch.

When I'm designing a poster that's part of a brand system, I start with the brand's spot colors and build the palette outward from there. The brand color is the anchor. Everything else serves it.
