Early in my career I used to present five or six logo concepts. I thought volume showed effort and gave clients options. What it actually did was create decision paralysis and dilute my own conviction. I've settled on three concepts, and that number has held up for years.

## The Problem With Too Many Options

When a client sees six concepts, they start mixing and matching. "I like the font from number two, the icon from number four, and the color from number six." This is Frankenstein design — assembling parts from concepts that were each internally coherent but incompatible with each other.

More options also signal uncertainty on the designer's part. If you've done your strategic homework, you should be able to narrow the viable directions to a small set. Presenting everything you explored suggests you haven't filtered the work.

## Three Directions, One Recommendation

My three concepts represent three genuinely different strategic directions — not three variations on the same idea. Each embodies a different interpretation of the brief. And for each presentation, I have a clear recommendation.

The recommendation matters. It's not just "here are three options, you choose." It's "here are three viable directions, and here's why I think this one is right for you and your organization." The recommendation is based on the strategic work that preceded the design.

Clients can disagree with my recommendation — and sometimes they do, and they're right. But a designer with a clear recommendation is easier to work with than a designer who presents options without conviction.

## How I Structure the Presentation

For each concept, I present: the concept in its primary application, the strategic rationale (why this direction for this brand), the form rationale (why these specific formal choices), and a few additional applications showing how the concept extends.

I present one concept at a time rather than showing all three simultaneously. Showing all three at once invites immediate comparison and ranking. Presenting sequentially allows each concept to be evaluated on its own terms first.

After presenting all three, I show them together and give my recommendation.

## Handling the "Can We Combine Them?" Request

This request is common and I've developed a clear response: "We can, but I'd want to understand what specifically you feel is missing from the recommended direction before we look at combining elements. Usually the instinct to combine comes from a specific gap, and I'd rather address that directly."

This reframes the conversation from "mix and match" to "what isn't working." The answer to that question is usually more useful than Frankenstein design.

## The Revision Process

After the presentation, clients choose one direction to develop. From that point, revisions are refinements within a direction, not pivots to a new concept. Keeping this boundary clear prevents revision rounds from turning into additional concepting rounds.
