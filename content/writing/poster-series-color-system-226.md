## Series coherence is a color problem

Designing a single poster is one kind of challenge. Designing a series of twelve posters that need to feel unmistakably related while each standing independently — that's a different and harder problem, and most of it is a color problem.

I've designed enough poster series to know that the decisions that make a series hold together are almost always made at the beginning, in the color system, before a single layout is produced. Getting those decisions right early saves an enormous amount of backtracking.

## The series palette vs. the individual palette

For any poster series, I establish two levels of color: the series palette — the shared colors that unify all posters — and the individual palette — the variation that distinguishes each poster within the series.

The series palette might be as minimal as a shared background color and a shared typographic color. Every poster in the series uses black type on cream stock. Within that constraint, each poster has its own accent color — one per poster, drawn from a pre-selected set of six that work together without clashing.

The ratio of shared to variable color determines how tight or loose the series feels. More shared color makes the series feel very unified but can start to feel repetitive. More variation gives each poster more individuality but risks the series looking like a random collection.

## Building the variation set

The individual palette variations need to be pre-selected as a set, not chosen individually for each poster. If I'm designing a twelve-poster series and I choose each poster's accent color when I'm designing that poster, I'll end up with a collection that drifts — the tenth poster will feel disconnected from the first because the decision context was different.

I choose the full set of variation colors before I start designing any individual posters. I lay them all out together and evaluate them as a palette — do they work as a group? Is there a logical relationship between them, or do they feel arbitrary?

## Progression and rhythm

For series that will be displayed together — a gallery installation, a wall display — the color sequence matters as much as the individual colors. A sequence that moves gradually from cool to warm across the series creates a visual arc. Alternating warm and cool creates rhythm. Random sequence creates disorder.

I almost always design the full series in miniature — all posters at thumbnail scale on one artboard — so I can evaluate the color sequence before committing to any individual poster at full scale.

## When the series needs to breathe

The biggest creative mistake in poster series work is over-tightening the system. A series whose design rules are too rigid produces posters that look like templates rather than designs. The system should be tight enough to create clear unity and loose enough to allow genuine creative decisions within each individual piece.

I build in deliberate flexibility — not as a lack of rigor, but as a planned feature of the system.
