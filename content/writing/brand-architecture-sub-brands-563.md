## The Architecture Problem

Most brand identity projects are about a single brand. But as organizations grow, brand architecture becomes a design problem in its own right: how do multiple brands, product lines, and sub-brands relate to each other visually?

Get this wrong and you end up with a portfolio of brands that either look indistinguishable from each other or have no visual relationship at all. Both are failures. The goal is a system where each brand has its own identity while the family relationship is clear.

## The Three Architecture Models

Brand architecture theorists have different models, but I work with three in practice:

**Monolithic (Branded House)** — everything operates under one master brand. Sub-brands are descriptive suffixes, not separate identities. The design system is unified. Apple operates this way. Every product is "Apple [product name]" and the visual language is consistent across everything.

**Endorsed** — sub-brands have their own identities but carry an endorsement from the parent brand. The parent's mark appears alongside the sub-brand's mark, in a subordinate role. The sub-brand has autonomy within the parent's implicit quality umbrella.

**Pluralistic (House of Brands)** — each brand is fully independent. The parent's identity may not appear at all in consumer-facing materials. The relationship exists in ownership and organization, not in visual identity.

Most real-world situations fall somewhere between these models, which creates the actual design work: defining how connected vs. independent each brand should be.

## Designing the Connection

When sub-brands need to feel related to a parent brand without being subservient to it, I use a set of design tools:

**Shared structural elements** — the same grid, the same type scale, the same compositional logic. Sub-brands that share the same underlying structure feel related even when their specific elements differ.

**Color family** — a parent brand with a defined color palette can spawn sub-brands in related colors. Different hues within the same temperature, or the same hue at different saturations. Variation within a family reads as related.

**Typography relationship** — using typefaces from the same foundry, the same historical period, or the same classification creates subliminal family feeling.

**Common graphic elements** — a pattern, a shape, a treatment that appears across all brands in the family, adapted for each brand's specific character.

## The Separation Problem

When sub-brands need to be clearly distinct — different audiences, different price points, different positioning — the design challenge is separating them enough that they don't bleed into each other.

This is where I resist the temptation to over-connect. Forced family resemblance can damage a sub-brand that needs to stand on its own. A luxury sub-brand connected too strongly to a mass-market parent inherits the mass-market associations. The separation has to be sufficient to allow independent perception.

## Documenting Architecture Decisions

Brand architecture decisions need explicit documentation because they get re-litigated constantly as organizations grow and change.

I document the architecture model, the rationale, and the specific rules for how brands relate to each other. When a new product line or sub-brand is proposed, the architecture document provides the framework for deciding how it fits rather than solving the problem from scratch every time.

The architecture is the strategy. The visual systems are the execution.
