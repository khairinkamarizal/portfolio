## The Document Nobody Reads

Most brand guidelines are designed to impress the client at handoff, not to be used daily by the people who apply the brand. They're beautifully laid out, thoroughly designed, and functionally useless. The marketing coordinator who needs to know which blue to use for a banner ad isn't going to page through forty slides to find the answer.

I've produced brand guidelines for organizations ranging from ten-person startups to multi-division enterprises. The ones that work in the field all share the same qualities: they're direct, they answer real questions, and they're easy to navigate.

## What Guidelines Need to Do

A brand guideline document has one job: enable someone without design training to make brand-consistent decisions. If the document requires design expertise to interpret, it's not a guideline — it's a portfolio piece.

Every section should answer a practical question. Not "here is our color palette" but "here is which color to use for text on a white background, which to use for a button on a dark background, and which to use as an accent only sparingly." The distinction seems minor. In practice it determines whether the guidelines actually influence behavior.

## The Sections That Matter

I organize every brand guideline around a core set of sections in order of how often they'll be consulted.

Logo usage comes first, because it's referenced constantly. I show the logo on white, on black, on the primary brand color, and on photography. I show minimum sizes with actual pixel and millimeter values. I show the clear space requirement with a visual example. I show what not to do — stretched, recolored, shadowed — because people will do all of those things if you don't explicitly forbid them.

Color comes second. Each color gets its hex, RGB, CMYK, and Pantone values on one page. I add a primary/secondary/accent designation and a one-sentence use case. No ambiguity.

Typography comes third. I show the typeface name, weight, and size for each typographic role — headline, subheading, body, caption, label. Real examples using actual brand copy, not placeholder text.

Everything else — photography style, iconography, tone of voice — is organized the same way: here is what it looks like, here is when to use it.

## Length and Format

Brand guidelines should be as long as they need to be and no longer. For a small business with a focused identity, twenty pages is often enough. For a multi-brand organization with complex guidelines for different business units, a hundred pages might be right.

What doesn't scale is density. I keep each concept to its own spread. Crowded pages save paper and lose readers. White space in guidelines isn't decorative — it's clarity.

## Digital First

I deliver guidelines as a navigable PDF with a clickable table of contents and bookmarks. Not a printed book. Not a slide deck. A PDF that someone can open on a second monitor, find the answer they need in thirty seconds, and close.

For larger organizations, I also create a one-page quick reference card that covers the most commonly needed elements: logo files, colors with hex values, typefaces with download links. This card lives as a pinned message in the company Slack. It gets consulted far more than the full document.

## The Maintenance Problem

Guidelines that aren't maintained become fiction. The brand evolves, new applications arise, the guidelines don't keep up, and people start making their own decisions. Within two years the brand is fragmented again.

I build update schedules into every guidelines delivery and offer annual review retainers. The document should be a living reference, not a timestamped artifact.
