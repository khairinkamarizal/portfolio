## Photography Is Identity

The photographs a brand chooses — their style, their subjects, their color treatment, their compositional approach — define a significant portion of how that brand is experienced visually. This is especially true for brands that use photography extensively: e-commerce, hospitality, consumer goods, lifestyle brands.

Despite this, photography style is often treated as a campaign decision rather than a brand decision. The result: a brand whose logo and color system are consistent while the photography looks different every time.

## The Photography Brief

Every brand identity should include a photography brief — a document that defines the visual character of brand photography. This includes: subject and talent direction (who appears in the photography and how they're styled), location and environment guidance (what spaces and settings are appropriate), lighting approach (natural vs. artificial, high key vs. moody), color direction (the palette and treatment that photography should contribute), compositional principles (scale, density, framing conventions), and post-processing style (color grading, retouching standards).

Without this brief, every photographer who shoots for the brand makes their own decisions, and the result is an incoherent archive.

## Lifestyle vs. Product Photography

Different types of photography have different briefs and serve different functions. Lifestyle photography establishes the brand's world — who uses the products, in what settings, with what emotional qualities. Product photography presents the products themselves — their form, detail, and material quality.

Both types need to feel like they come from the same visual world. A brand with a warm, human lifestyle style and a cold, clinical product photography style creates a jarring disconnect.

## Stock Photography and Its Limits

Stock photography is a practical resource. It's also one of the most significant threats to brand visual consistency. Generic stock photos — inauthentically styled, visually inconsistent with each other — undermine any brand identity.

If stock photography is used, it should be selected according to the photography brief with the same rigor as commissioned photography. Consistent direction applied to stock selection produces more coherent results than leaving selection to whoever needs an image.

## Building a Photo Library

For brands that commission photography, building a consistently directed photo library over time is one of the highest-leverage visual identity investments available. A library of hundreds of on-brand images produces consistent visual quality across all materials without requiring a new shoot for every application.

The brief is the tool that makes the library coherent. Shoots without a brief produce archives that look like collections of random photographs.
