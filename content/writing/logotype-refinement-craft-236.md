A logotype — a wordmark where the letterforms have been custom-designed or substantially modified — is one of the most labor-intensive deliverables in brand identity work. The client often sees a final mark that looks like it was simply typed in a font. What they don't see is the dozens of individual decisions made letter by letter, space by space, terminal by terminal, that separate a logotype from a word set in a typeface.

This work is invisible when it's done right. It's very visible when it isn't.

## Why Typefaces Need Modification for Logotypes

Typefaces are designed for use across thousands of combinations, at many sizes, for diverse applications. They're optimized for versatility, not for any single word. When you set a specific brand name in a typeface — at a fixed size, as a single logo unit — the typeface's general optimizations often produce visible imperfections.

Specific letter-pair combinations create optical spacing imbalances. At logo scale, the stroke widths that look perfect in a text paragraph look different than they do at a large display size. Terminal forms and curve endings that are appropriate for text use may feel too generic for a distinctive brand mark.

Logotype development addresses all of these specifically, for this word, at this size, for this brand.

## Spacing: The Foundation

Optical spacing — adjusting the space between each pair of letters so the rhythm feels even — is the first and most fundamental logotype refinement. I set every logotype with metric kerning turned off, then adjust each letter pair by eye.

The standard is visual rhythm: if you look at the spaces between each pair, they should feel consistent in optical area, not geometric distance. Certain combinations (T-a, W-o, V-A, A-V) naturally create large gaps that need tightening. Others (H-I, L-L, M-N) may need slight opening.

This process takes as long as it takes. Rushing optical spacing produces logotypes that look slightly off to trained eyes even when they can't articulate why.

## Stroke Weight and Curve Refinement

At large sizes, the relationship between thick and thin strokes in a typeface can read differently than at text sizes. Very thin strokes may need slight reinforcement. Very thick strokes may need reduction. Optical compensation that the typeface builds in for text use may need to be adjusted for the fixed display size of a logo.

Curves in letterforms — the bowl of a 'b', the arc of an 'n', the shoulders of an 'h' — may need point-by-point refinement to eliminate any mechanical flatness or irregularity visible at logo scale. Good curves should feel natural and continuous, not like vectors manipulated by a person who doesn't understand letter anatomy.

## Custom Character Development

After spacing and weight refinements, I evaluate whether any specific characters in the word warrant custom development — new terminal forms, modified proportions, altered stroke relationships — that would add distinctiveness while maintaining coherence with the other characters.

This is the highest-craft level of logotype work. Changes here should be subtle enough that a non-designer couldn't point to them, but significant enough that they contribute to the mark's feeling of being made rather than chosen.

## The Final Specimen Test

When the logotype is refined, I create a test specimen at multiple sizes — very large, standard, small — and evaluate whether the refinements hold across the range. Adjustments made at one size sometimes create problems at another.

I also test in context: the logotype in actual applications at actual sizes. A logotype that looks perfect in isolation sometimes reveals spacing or proportion issues when surrounded by other elements.

The work is complete when nothing needs to be explained. The logotype just looks right.
