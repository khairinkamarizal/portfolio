Poster design is unusually public. The work goes on walls, on streets, in windows. The client sees it in the wild. Their colleagues see it. Their audience sees it. This publicity changes the client relationship in ways that affect every conversation from brief to final delivery.

## Setting Expectations Early

Poster clients often don't know what they want until they see what they don't want. This is true of all design work, but it's particularly true of posters because the format is so visible and the stakes of a bad decision feel so high. A poster that doesn't land is very visible evidence of a bad decision.

I manage this by being very specific in the brief phase about the conceptual territory I'm going to explore. Not "I'll show you some options" but "I'm planning to explore a type-led approach with strong color, drawing from constructivist influences — does that direction fit the event?" Getting conceptual alignment before I sketch saves enormous revision time.

## The Concept Rationale

When I present poster concepts, I present them with rationale. Every formal decision — typeface, color, composition — gets a brief explanation of why it was made. Not a defensive justification, but a clear connection between the decision and the brief.

This serves two purposes: it demonstrates that the work is reasoned rather than arbitrary, and it gives the client a framework for evaluating what they're seeing. Instead of reacting to "does this look good?" they can react to "does this express what we want to say?"

## Managing the "Can We Make the Logo Bigger?" Response

This is universal. The client has a logo and wants it larger. In poster design, a logo that's too large destroys the composition — it creates a hierarchy clash between the organizational identity and the event communication.

My response: show the logo at the requested size on a comp so the client can see the effect. Let the composition demonstrate the problem rather than arguing abstractly. Most clients, when they see the actual impact, choose the composition over the larger logo.

## Revisions and Scope

Poster revisions can spiral. A change to the headline affects the hierarchy, which affects the spacing, which affects the layout. I specify in every poster contract: how many rounds of revisions are included, what constitutes a round, and what triggers a change order.

This isn't about being inflexible — it's about making sure both parties understand what they're buying. A client who expects unlimited revisions on a fixed-fee poster project will be unhappy when I start charging for the fourth round. Clear expectations prevent that conversation.

## Photographing the Work

A poster isn't finished until it's documented. I photograph every poster in situ — on the venue wall, on a poster column, in the event space. These in-context photographs are more valuable portfolio documentation than studio shots, and they give the client better marketing material than a flat scan.

Building the photography into my delivery process means I always have the in-context images. It takes thirty minutes and pays back in portfolio value for years.
