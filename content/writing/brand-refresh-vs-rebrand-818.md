## Two Different Projects

A brand refresh and a rebrand are fundamentally different projects with different scopes, budgets, timelines, and risks. I've watched clients confuse the two and end up with the wrong one, to their cost.

Understanding which one a brand actually needs requires honest analysis of what's working, what isn't, and what the brand's equity is worth preserving.

## What a Refresh Looks Like

A brand refresh keeps the strategic DNA and core recognizable elements intact while updating the visual execution. The brand is still recognizable before and after — the change is evolutionary, not revolutionary.

A refresh makes sense when:

- The mark is distinctive and has real equity but looks visually dated
- The color palette needs modernization but the strategic positioning is sound
- The typography is aging but the overall brand personality is right
- The system needs to extend into digital applications it wasn't originally designed for

A refresh is lower risk, faster, and less expensive than a rebrand. It respects the investment the organization has made in brand recognition.

## What a Rebrand Requires

A rebrand is the right answer when the existing brand actively works against the organization's positioning. When the mark is associated with something the brand is moving away from. When the audience the brand needs to reach doesn't connect with the existing visual identity. When a merger or acquisition requires visual unification.

A rebrand also makes sense when the existing identity has no real equity to preserve — startup brands, businesses that have been operating without a coherent identity, or organizations that built their visual presence incrementally without strategy.

The risk of a rebrand is losing existing recognition. For established organizations with real brand equity, this is a genuine cost that needs to be weighed against the benefit of the new direction.

## The Equity Question

The central question in every brand decision is: how much equity does the existing brand have? Equity means recognition, association, and trust built up over time. You don't discard it lightly.

I use a simple test: if I show the brand to its core audience without the brand name, do they recognize it? Do they have positive associations? If the answer to both is yes, the equity is real and worth protecting. If the answer is no to either, the rebrand risk is much lower because there's less to lose.

## My Recommendation Framework

When a client comes to me with "we need a new brand," I ask three questions before I recommend a path:

First: what's not working about the current brand, specifically? Vague dissatisfaction is not a brief.

Second: is the strategic positioning changing, or just the visual execution?

Third: who knows this brand and what do they associate with it?

The answers usually point clearly toward refresh or rebrand. When they don't, I recommend the refresh — it's the lower-risk path, and if it doesn't fully solve the problem, the remaining issues become much clearer after the work is done.
