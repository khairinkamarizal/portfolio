Concert posters, film festival posters, art exhibition announcements, theater season posters — this category of work sits at the intersection of commercial communication and cultural expression in a way that most design work doesn't. The poster has to do its job (communicate what, when, where) but it also has to feel like it belongs to the cultural world it's representing.

This is the work I enjoy most, and it has its own discipline.

## The Poster as Cultural Object

The best event posters become objects in their own right. They get kept, framed, collected. This happens when the poster transcends its informational function and becomes an expressive artifact that captures something true about the event or the cultural moment.

That's an ambitious goal for a piece of print that also has to list venue addresses and ticket prices. The tension between these two functions — expressive cultural object and functional information carrier — is what makes the work interesting.

I resolve this tension by being clear about hierarchy from the start. What information is essential and has to be clear? What can be secondary? What's the expressive latitude once the essential information is placed?

## Understanding the Cultural Context

Before I design anything, I research the cultural context. For a jazz festival, I might spend time with the history of jazz poster design — the Blue Note album covers, the Newport Jazz Festival posters, the work of Reid Miles. Not to copy them, but to understand the visual language the audience might associate with this music, and to find a way to respond to or subvert that language deliberately.

For a contemporary art exhibition, I look at the gallery's existing visual language and the work of the exhibiting artists. The poster should feel related to the work it's announcing without illustrating it too literally.

This research changes the formal decisions. A poster for a classical music series in a 150-year-old concert hall has different requirements than a poster for an experimental electronic music event in an industrial space, even if both posters are communicating roughly the same information.

## Hierarchy in Event Posters

The essential information hierarchy for event posters is almost always the same: event name or artist name first, then the date and venue. Everything else — supporting acts, ticket info, sponsors — is tertiary.

I fight for this hierarchy with clients who want to elevate sponsor logos, give supporting acts equal billing, or insist on verbose event descriptions. The poster doesn't have space for everything at equal importance. The decisions about what's prominent are editorial decisions, and they shape whether the poster is effective.

## The Typography-First Approach

For most cultural event posters, I work typographically first. Strong typographic composition can carry the poster entirely without illustration or photography. This approach tends to produce work that feels more distinctive than photo-based posters because it forces a more deliberate relationship between the type and the subject.

I'll often spend 80% of a poster design project on the typographic composition before introducing any other visual elements. If the type isn't working, adding images doesn't save it — it just adds more complexity to an already problematic layout.

## Printing as Part of the Design

For posters I believe in, I push clients toward more interesting print specifications. Screen printing over digital offset for smaller runs. Fluorescent inks for contemporary work. Kraft stock for a raw, immediate feel. The print process is part of the expression.

A digitally designed poster that's printed beautifully on the right stock feels categorically different from the same design output on generic bright white coated paper. The material matters.
