## The Smallest Canvas

The standard business card is 85mm x 55mm — one of the smallest print canvases in professional design. It's also one of the most scrutinized. People hold business cards, look at them closely, flip them over. The card gets more intimate attention than almost any other brand touchpoint.

This means every decision on a business card is examined. Cheap stock, small fonts, misaligned elements, clumsy hierarchy — all visible. Good stock, considered typography, elegant information hierarchy — all felt.

## Information Architecture

The business card has to carry specific information: name, title, contact details, website, possibly social handles or QR code. The design challenge is organizing this information with clear hierarchy while keeping the card feeling spacious rather than crammed.

The hierarchy I establish: name first (largest, most prominent), then title or tagline if meaningful, then contact information at supporting scale. The logo or wordmark either anchors one corner or anchors the reverse.

I almost always recommend using the reverse of the card. Two-sided cards provide more space, which makes the front feel more spacious and elegant. The back can carry the full contact information or the full logo presentation, depending on what's been prioritized on the front.

## The White Space Commitment

Business card design is where I have to most firmly defend white space to clients. The instinct is to fill every millimeter. The result is usually a card that looks like a classified ad and communicates nothing clearly.

A business card with generous margins and deliberate white space communicates quality and confidence. It says: we have so much going on that we don't need to cram everything onto this card. That impression is worth the space it requires.

## Material Decisions

For clients where the business card is a primary first-impression touchpoint — consultants, creative agencies, professional services — I almost always recommend upgrading the stock. The minimum I specify is 400gsm for a standard card. Often I go higher, and often I specify uncoated or cotton stock depending on the brand personality.

Specialty treatments — soft-touch laminate, spot gloss, foil on the logo, debossed name — can dramatically elevate a card's physical impact. These treatments add cost. They're worth it when the card is doing real work in business development.

## The Card You Keep

The goal of a business card is to be the card people keep rather than discard. In a world where contact information lives in phones, a business card that survives as a physical object in someone's desk or wallet has to earn its place through design quality.

I design every business card with that question in mind: is this worth keeping? If the answer isn't clearly yes, I haven't done enough.
