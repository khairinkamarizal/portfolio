Print on demand — services like Printful, Printify, or Moo for stationery — occupies a specific niche in print design. The quality ceiling is lower than offset printing, but the flexibility and low minimum quantities make it the right solution for many use cases.

Designing well for print on demand means understanding its specific quality characteristics and designing to its strengths.

## What POD Does Well

Print on demand services have improved dramatically. For standard formats at small quantities — business cards, postcards, simple brochures, apparel graphics, poster prints — the quality is now genuinely good rather than merely acceptable.

The strengths of POD:
- No minimum quantity (order one, order a hundred)
- No setup costs
- Fast turnaround
- Direct-to-consumer fulfillment for e-commerce
- Wide format options without the capital investment of dedicated printing equipment

For designers managing small brands, independent projects, or e-commerce merchandise, POD is often the practical and economical choice.

## Understanding the Quality Ceiling

POD quality has a ceiling that's lower than offset. Color accuracy is less precise — the printer matches your file reasonably well but doesn't offer the color management and proofing of a commercial offset press. Paper options are limited compared to offset, and the available stocks are sometimes lower quality than what you'd specify for commercial printing.

Designing for POD means not building your design around quality characteristics that POD can't deliver. Fine hairlines, extremely high-contrast small type, subtle tonal gradients — these are risky in POD contexts. Bold, clear design with generous detail and robust contrast performs better.

## File Preparation for POD

Each POD service has specific file requirements that differ from standard print specifications. Resolution, color mode, bleed specifications, and file format all vary by service.

I always download the service's technical guidelines before designing for a specific POD platform, not after. Some platforms require RGB files (they convert internally); others require CMYK. Bleed requirements vary. The services that have clear, detailed technical documentation are generally the ones that produce more consistent results.

## Color Calibration Reality

POD color is less consistent than offset. The same file can print with noticeably different color on different runs or different dates. For designs where exact color matching is critical, POD is not the right solution.

I design POD work with color robustness in mind — using colors that communicate correctly even if the printed result is somewhat lighter, darker, or shifted in hue. Designs that depend on very specific color values are fragile in POD contexts.

## When POD Scales

POD's unit economics generally favor offset printing above a certain quantity (typically 250–500 units depending on format). For high-volume production, offset is more cost-effective and offers more control.

The design files for a POD project, prepared correctly, can be adapted for offset production when the volume justifies it. Designing with future offset conversion in mind — maintaining CMYK-compatible colors, avoiding RGB-only effects — keeps options open.
