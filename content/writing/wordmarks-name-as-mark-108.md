## The Wordmark as Identity

Not every brand needs a symbol. Some of the most recognized identities in the world are pure wordmarks — names set with such precision and intentionality that the letters themselves become the mark. FedEx. Google. Coca-Cola. The name is the logo.

This is a legitimate and often underappreciated approach to logo design. When it's done well, a wordmark can be more distinctive than a symbol, because it carries the brand name and the visual identity in a single element.

## Starting with Type — Then Departing from It

Most wordmarks start with an existing typeface as a foundation. The work is in what you do from there. Custom letterform modifications — adjusted spacing, redrawn curves, modified joints, unique ligatures — transform a type choice into a proprietary mark.

The degree of customization varies. Some wordmarks are barely modified from their source typeface. Others are so extensively redrawn that they're effectively custom type. Both can work, but the more customized versions tend to be more defensible as marks and more distinctive in the marketplace.

## Spacing is Everything

Letter-spacing in a wordmark is not set arbitrarily. I work through it methodically, adjusting the spacing between each pair of letters (kerning) until the visual rhythm is even throughout. No two letters feel too close or too distant. The name reads as a single cohesive unit.

This takes time. Automated kerning from even the best type engines is a starting point, not a finish. The human eye is the only reliable tool for the final adjustments.

## Geometric vs. Organic Wordmarks

Wordmarks have a personality axis that runs from geometric to organic. Geometric wordmarks — precise, constructed, often using modified sans-serifs — feel modern, systematic, structured. Organic wordmarks — drawn, flowing, with calligraphic influence — feel human, crafted, personal.

Most brands want to land somewhere on this spectrum in a way that reflects their values. I make that positioning explicit in the brief before I start drawing.

## Testing Across Contexts

A wordmark has to work across a huge range of sizes and contexts. I test them small (favicon, business card), large (signage, billboard), in color, in black on white, in white on dark, and on complex backgrounds. If it fails any of these, the form needs more work.

The strength of a wordmark is its simplicity of concept. The challenge is achieving that simplicity with impeccable execution.
