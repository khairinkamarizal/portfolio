## Type Is Character

Before a viewer reads a single word, the typeface has already communicated something about the brand. The letterforms carry associations built from decades of cultural use. A condensed grotesque says something different from a humanist serif, which says something different from a hand-drawn script.

These are not arbitrary associations. They're built from the history of how these typefaces have been used, who made them, and in what contexts they've appeared. Understanding this history is what allows type choices to be strategic rather than intuitive.

## The Personality Axes I Work With

I evaluate typefaces on four axes that I've found useful for brand work:

**Formal vs. Informal** — how much does the typeface signal ceremony, authority, or establishment? Classic serifs sit at the formal end. Hand-lettered faces and casual scripts sit at the informal end. Most brands want to be somewhere in the middle, but the exact position matters.

**Cold vs. Warm** — geometric typefaces tend to read as cool and rational. Humanist faces, with their connections to hand-drawn letterforms, tend to read as warmer. Temperature affects how approachable the brand feels.

**Contemporary vs. Historical** — every typeface was designed in a specific cultural moment and carries traces of that moment. A mid-century grotesque reads differently from a contemporary optical size design even if they're structurally similar.

**Quiet vs. Loud** — some typefaces are invisible-by-design, stepping back to let the content speak. Others have strong personality that asserts itself regardless of content. Which one a brand needs depends on whether the brand's personality should be carried by the type or by the message.

## Matching Type to Brand Adjectives

My practical method: I take the brand's three to five personality adjectives and evaluate candidate typefaces against each one.

If the brand is "precise, contemporary, and confident," I'm looking for a typeface that communicates precision (clean geometry, consistent stroke weight), reads as contemporary (designed recently or with modern proportions), and projects confidence (medium to bold weight, generous x-height, clear authority).

If a typeface satisfies fewer than three of the five adjectives, I look elsewhere.

## The Difference Between Typeface and Setting

A typeface's default personality can be significantly modified by how it's set. Generous tracking makes a typeface feel more spacious and premium. Tight tracking makes it feel urgent or compressed. Uppercase setting adds formality. Mixed-case setting feels more approachable.

Before I dismiss a typeface for personality reasons, I test different settings. Sometimes a typeface that feels slightly too casual in its default state becomes right when set in spaced uppercase. Sometimes a formal typeface loosens up when set in lowercase at generous leading.

## The Category Convention Check

I evaluate every typeface choice against the category conventions. What typeface families are overused in this industry? Avoiding them reduces the risk of the brand visually merging with its competitors.

This doesn't mean I always avoid category conventions — sometimes meeting the convention signals trustworthiness in categories where trust is the primary purchase driver. But I always know what I'm doing and why.

Type is voice. Set it deliberately.
