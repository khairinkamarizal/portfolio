Color is the most immediately perceived element of any brand. Before a viewer reads a word or parses a logo, they've registered the color. That's enormous power — and enormous responsibility.

## Start With One Color

I never start by designing a palette. I start by choosing the primary brand color. That one color has to carry the brand's personality on its own. It has to work on white, on dark backgrounds, in print, and on screen. It needs to be distinctive enough to own territory in its category.

Category differentiation matters more than people admit. If every competitor in a space uses blue, going blue is a safe choice that makes you invisible. Sometimes invisible is strategic. Usually it isn't.

## Building the System

Once the primary color is locked, I build the supporting palette around it. I use a few consistent approaches depending on the brand's needs:

A **monochromatic system** — tints, shades, and tones of the primary — works for brands that want singular authority. It's disciplined and forceful.

A **complementary system** uses a color directly opposite on the color wheel. The tension between complementary colors creates energy and contrast. Good for brands that need to feel dynamic.

An **analogous system** — colors adjacent on the wheel — creates harmony and cohesion. Works well for brands that need to feel warm, approachable, or natural.

I don't combine approaches. Mixing a complementary accent into an analogous palette creates incoherence unless you know exactly what you're doing.

## CMYK, RGB, and the Gap Between Them

Every brand color has to be specified in multiple color models: HEX/RGB for screen, CMYK for offset print, and usually a Pantone spot color for applications where accuracy is non-negotiable (packaging, signage, merchandise).

The gap between screen and print color is real, and ignoring it creates problems. A vivid screen blue is often impossible to reproduce accurately in CMYK. I build palettes with one eye on the print gamut, choosing colors that translate well rather than chasing vibrancy in RGB and being disappointed on press.

## Accessibility

Every color pair I use in a system — text on background, icon on background — gets checked against WCAG contrast ratios. AA compliance (4.5:1 for normal text) is the floor. I aim for AAA (7:1) wherever the design allows.

Accessible color isn't a constraint on creativity. It's a constraint on exclusion. A brand that can't be read by someone with low vision has failed part of its audience.

## The Neutral System

Neutrals are where most brand systems live day-to-day. The primary color might appear on 10% of surfaces. Neutrals handle the other 90%. I build a neutral ramp — usually 6–8 values from near-white to near-black — that has a subtle hue shift informed by the primary color. A warm primary gets warm neutrals. A cool primary gets cool neutrals. This keeps the system feeling unified even when the primary color isn't visible.
