Brand identity increasingly appears in three-dimensional contexts: dimensional signage, product industrial design, architectural applications, exhibition structures, merchandise with physical form. Logos designed only with flat reproduction in mind often fail in these contexts or require expensive redesign.

Understanding how marks translate into three dimensions — and designing for that translation from the start — produces more versatile identity systems.

## The Flatness Assumption

Most logo design is done in vector software, evaluated on screen, and presented in flat print formats. This workflow creates an implicit flatness assumption: the mark is a flat, two-dimensional graphic. When that mark needs to become a dimensional object — a raised letterform on a wall, an extruded symbol as a free-standing sculpture, a cast metal emblem — the flatness assumption creates problems.

Marks with very thin strokes may be structurally fragile when extruded into dimensional form. Marks with tight enclosed spaces may be impossible to produce in certain fabrication methods. Marks with high complexity may look beautiful in print but read poorly in shadow-creating dimensional form.

## Designing with Dimensional Awareness

I think about dimensional applications during the design process rather than after. For marks that will have dimensional use, I evaluate the design in simulated 3D: extruded view, lit from various angles, viewed from below and above.

A mark that looks clear in plan view but becomes unreadable when lit from the side — because shadow fills the spaces and eliminates the legibility of negative space elements — needs to be reconsidered.

## Signage and Architectural Applications

Dimensional signage — letters or marks built from metal, foam, acrylic, or other materials and mounted to surfaces or standing freestanding — is one of the most common three-dimensional brand applications.

For signage applications, I specify not just the graphic but the dimensionality: letter depth, finish, mounting method, lighting specification. A brushed aluminum letter lit from behind reads completely differently from the same letter painted and surface-mounted.

These specifications are part of the brand application guidance, not afterthoughts left to signage vendors.

## Physical Material and Brand Meaning

The material a three-dimensional brand application is made from carries meaning. Cast bronze communicates permanence and heritage. Polished stainless communicates modernity and precision. Powder-coated steel communicates durability and utility. Formed acrylic communicates accessibility and contemporary production.

I specify materials as brand decisions, not just technical choices. The material should reinforce the brand's positioning.

## Industrial Design Collaboration

When brand elements appear on products — embedded logos on physical goods, brand marks on industrial equipment, identity integration with product design — I collaborate closely with industrial designers rather than providing a flat asset and expecting it to work.

The integration of brand identity and product form requires conversation about how the mark will be produced (molded in, printed on, applied as a label), at what scale, in what orientation, and how it will relate to the product's other visual elements.

Flat graphics handed off to industrial designers without this collaboration produce awkward integrations where the brand graphic and the product feel like they don't belong together. The collaboration is how they become one coherent object.

## Prototyping Before Committing

For significant dimensional applications, I prototype before committing to production. A small-scale model of dimensional signage, a 3D-printed prototype of a product integration — these reveal how the mark behaves in physical form in ways that screen and print previews can't.

The prototype investment is always smaller than the cost of getting large-scale dimensional production wrong.
