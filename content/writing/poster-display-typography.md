Display type — type set large enough to be the visual centerpiece of a poster — has different design requirements from body text. The scale exposes details that are invisible in smaller settings and creates optical phenomena that don't appear at reading sizes. Mastering display type means understanding what changes at scale and designing accordingly.

## The Scale Exposure Effect

At small sizes, the eye integrates type holistically — individual letter details and slight irregularities blend into an overall texture. At display scale, every curve, every junction, every spacing decision is visible as an individual element.

This exposure works in both directions. Beautiful details in a well-crafted typeface that are invisible at small size become visible and enriching at large scale. Subtle optical corrections built into the type design — the way a skilled type designer slightly adjusts the geometry of a round letter to look optically correct — become apparent. The craft shows.

But so do the flaws. Poor stroke quality, uneven spacing, and weak letterform construction that survive at text size fail at display scale.

## Type at Posters vs. Type on Screens

Poster display type lives in physical space at physical scale. A 10cm headline on an A1 poster is seen from distances ranging from 2 meters to 30cm. The same headline on a screen is seen from 50–60cm at 72–96 DPI.

These are very different perceptual conditions. Type that works beautifully on screen at large display size may behave differently printed at actual size. The resolution of print — at 300 DPI or higher — renders curves and fine details more precisely than screen display, which can make some details appear sharper or more prominent than they do digitally.

## Headline as Architecture

A large headline on a poster is architectural — it's a structure occupying space, creating weight, defining the visual landscape. The weight distribution of the headline determines the visual weight of the top, center, or bottom of the poster.

I think about headline placement as an architectural decision as much as a compositional one. A heavy, large headline at the top of a poster creates a top-weighted composition that the rest of the layout needs to balance. A centered headline divides the poster horizontally. A headline flush at the bottom grounds the composition differently.

## Contrast at Display Scale

High-contrast serif typefaces — those with dramatic differences between thick and thin strokes, like Bodoni or Didot — have a specific quality at display scale. The thin strokes that feel fragile at text size become elegant at large scale; the thick strokes become bold structural elements. The contrast reads clearly and creates visual drama.

The same high-contrast typefaces can fail at smaller display sizes — around 36–60pt in print — where the thin strokes become uncomfortably fine. Display-size high-contrast serifs live at large scale or not at all.

## The Typographic Poster

Some posters are purely typographic — the type is the entire design, with no image. These pieces require the most considered application of display type principles. The letterforms are the only visual elements. Every spacing decision, every weight choice, every line break is completely visible and carries full visual weight.

The discipline of typographic poster design is one of the most rigorous in graphic design. With nowhere to hide, the quality of the typographic thinking is the entire quality of the work.
