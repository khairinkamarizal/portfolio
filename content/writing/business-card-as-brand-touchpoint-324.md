The business card has been declared dead repeatedly for the last fifteen years. LinkedIn will replace it. NFC will replace it. QR codes will replace it. And yet I still exchange business cards regularly, and the physical card still makes an impression that no digital transfer replicates.

## What the Card Is Actually Doing

A business card exchange is a physical transaction that signals respect and intention. The quality of the card communicates something about the organization before anyone reads the content. A flimsy, digitally-printed card on thin stock says one thing. A heavy letterpress card on cotton paper says something completely different.

The card's first job is tactile impression. Weight, texture, thickness — these are perceived immediately and associated with the organization's quality standards before a word is read.

## Information Hierarchy on a Small Format

A standard business card gives you 3.5 × 2 inches. That's not much space. The hierarchy question is: what must this card accomplish, and in what order?

For most professionals: primary identity (name and organization), role clarification (title or specialty), and contact information. The logo reinforces the identity but rarely needs to dominate the card.

I resist the temptation to put everything on a card. Email, phone, website, LinkedIn, Instagram, office address, tagline — these compete with each other and dilute the hierarchy. What does this person actually want you to do? Start there.

## The Case for Restraint

The most impressive business cards I've seen use restraint as their statement. A single side, generous white space, one strong typographic treatment, high-quality stock. Everything else is stripped away. The card says: we're confident enough not to need to explain ourselves.

This approach only works if the brand has genuine confidence behind it. A sparse card from an unknown organization looks empty. The same card from an established, well-regarded organization looks authoritative.

## Special Finishes and Unusual Formats

A distinctive format or finish — letterpress, foil, spot UV, an unusual dimension, rounded corners, thick board — makes a card memorable. But the finish has to serve the brand, not just the card.

A letterpress card for a heritage craft brand is coherent. The same letterpress card for a tech startup is cosplay. The print treatment should feel inevitable given the brand.

## The Back of the Card

Most cards waste the back. White or plain stock color. I use the back as a brand canvas: a full-bleed brand color, a graphic element, a pattern from the identity system. The back is the first surface the recipient touches when they set the card on a desk — it's valuable real estate.
