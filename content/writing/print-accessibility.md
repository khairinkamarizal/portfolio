Accessibility in print is discussed less than accessibility in digital design, partly because the standards are less codified and partly because print can't adapt dynamically to user needs the way digital can. But print designs reach audiences with visual impairments, and the decisions I make affect whether they can access the content.

## Contrast Is the Foundation

Sufficient contrast between type and background is the most impactful accessibility decision in print design. Low-contrast type — light gray on white, dark gray on black, colored type on a similarly-valued background — is harder to read for everyone and effectively illegible for people with low vision or color vision deficiencies.

I evaluate type/background contrast in every print project. The WCAG 4.5:1 minimum for body text is a useful benchmark even in print contexts, though it was designed for screen. I aim to exceed it wherever the design allows.

## Type Size for Extended Reading

For extended body text in print, 10–12pt is the typical minimum for comfortable reading in standard conditions. For audiences that include older adults or people with low vision, 12–14pt is more appropriate.

Display type and headings don't have the same size requirements, but any body text intended for extended reading should be set at a size that doesn't require effort to read.

## Color and Information Encoding

When color is the only way information is encoded — red means warning, green means safe, different colors distinguish different categories — people with color vision deficiency lose access to that information. I pair color encoding with a secondary signal: shape, pattern, label, or position.

This isn't just good practice; it often produces better design. A chart where data series are distinguished by both color and line pattern is clearer to everyone, not just to people with color vision differences.

## Typeface Selection for Legibility

Some typefaces are more legible than others at body text sizes, particularly for readers with dyslexia or low vision. High contrast, very thin strokes, and highly stylized letterforms reduce legibility. More neutral, moderately weighted typefaces with clear letterform differentiation tend to read more easily across audiences.

The typefaces I reach for in accessibility-critical contexts are humanist sans-serifs with moderate weight and clear letterform differentiation — good counters, distinct letter shapes, adequate x-height.

## Layout Considerations

Dense layouts with minimal leading, tight margins, and small type sizes create reading barriers. Generous leading (at least 120% of type size), adequate margins, and clear typographic hierarchy reduce cognitive load and make extended reading more sustainable.

The good news is that accessible layout is usually also more aesthetically refined layout. The same qualities that make a page easier to read — good proportion, generous white space, clear hierarchy — are the qualities of well-designed print work.

## The Honest Limitation

Print can't adapt. A digital document can be zoomed, text can be resized, color themes can be changed. Print is fixed. For readers who need significant accessibility accommodations, the most important design decision might be ensuring that a digital version is available alongside the print version — one that can adapt to individual needs in ways the print version never can.
