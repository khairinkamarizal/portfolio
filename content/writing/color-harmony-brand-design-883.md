## Harmony Systems as Starting Points

Color harmony systems — the formal relationships between colors on the color wheel — are useful starting points for palette construction, not final answers. Understanding them gives you a vocabulary for discussing color decisions and a set of structural options to explore. Treating them as rules produces palettes that feel theoretical rather than designed.

## Complementary Color

Complementary colors sit opposite each other on the color wheel. Orange and blue. Red and green. Purple and yellow. Complementary pairs create maximum contrast and visual tension — they make each other appear more vivid when placed adjacent.

In brand design, complementary palettes create energy and presence. A brand that uses orange and blue as its primary palette is inherently high-contrast and visually active. The challenge is controlling the tension — complementary pairs at full saturation can feel aggressive. Most strong complementary brand palettes use one color at full saturation and the other at reduced saturation or value.

## Analogous Color

Analogous colors sit adjacent on the color wheel. Yellow, yellow-green, green. Blue, blue-purple, purple. Analogous palettes feel harmonious, natural, and cohesive — the colors transition smoothly into each other.

In brand design, analogous palettes communicate a calm, unified personality. They're lower in visual tension than complementary palettes, which makes them appropriate for brands that want to feel settled and assured. The challenge is creating enough contrast within the palette to support hierarchy.

## Triadic Color

Triadic palettes use three colors equally spaced on the color wheel. Red, yellow, blue. Orange, green, purple. Triadic palettes are visually vibrant and balanced, but complex to manage in a brand context.

I use triadic structures less frequently in brand work because the three strong colors require significant discipline to deploy without visual chaos. They work better in contexts with controlled, designed applications than in open-ended systems that many people will use.

## Split Complementary

A split complementary palette uses one primary color and the two colors adjacent to its complement. Yellow, blue-purple, red-purple. This provides the visual tension of complementary without the direct opposition, making it slightly easier to manage.

## Beyond the Wheel

It's worth noting that the color wheel represents hue relationships but doesn't account for value and saturation, which are equally important dimensions of color harmony. Two colors that are theoretically complementary can feel harmonious or discordant depending on their relative value and saturation.

I use the wheel as a starting point for hue selection and then work extensively with value and saturation to arrive at a palette that works in actual application. The harmony system sets the structure; the refinement work makes it useful.
