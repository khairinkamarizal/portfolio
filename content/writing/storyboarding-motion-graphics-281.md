Motion designers who skip storyboarding always pay for it later. They open After Effects, start animating, discover the sequence doesn't work, tear it down, rebuild it differently, and ultimately spend three times longer on the project than they would have if they'd planned it first.

I storyboard every motion project before opening the animation software. The time investment is always returned many times over.

## What a Motion Storyboard Contains

A storyboard for motion graphics isn't the same as a storyboard for film. Film storyboards capture camera angles and character positions. Motion graphics storyboards capture: the content of each screen state, the transitions between states, the hierarchy of each scene, approximate timing, and the conceptual through-line of the piece.

I work with simple boxes — roughly the delivery format's proportions — and sketch the essential visual information in each frame. I don't need to show beautiful renderings. I need to show what's on screen, in what order, and how it transitions.

## The Animatic Step

After the storyboard is approved (either by a client or my own judgment for self-directed work), I create a basic animatic — the storyboard frames played back in sequence at approximate timing with placeholder audio or music.

An animatic reveals timing problems that aren't visible in static frames. A sequence that reads perfectly in a twelve-frame storyboard might feel rushed or static in motion. Adjusting timing at the animatic stage is a five-minute task. Adjusting it after the animation is built is often an hour.

## Timing as a Design Variable

Storyboarding forces decisions about timing before animation begins. How long does the title card hold? How much time does the logo reveal take? How quickly do we cut between scenes?

These timing decisions are design decisions with real implications for how the final piece feels. Fast, rhythmic cuts create energy. Longer holds create consideration and weight. The timing should match the brand's motion personality and the content's purpose.

## The Revision Filter

A storyboard is also a revision management tool. Clients who see a storyboard before production begins can provide feedback on the sequence and structure at a stage when changes are cheap. Clients who see changes for the first time in a finished animation often request structural changes that require tearing down built work.

I present storyboards to clients as a required approval checkpoint, not as an optional courtesy. No storyboard approval, no production. This policy protects both parties — the client from paying for built work that doesn't match their intent, and me from rebuilding animations because the sequence wasn't confirmed.

## Simple Boards Win

The most effective storyboards are often the simplest. Clear boxes, simple sketches indicating hierarchy and content, arrows showing transitions, timing notes in the margins. The purpose is communication and planning, not aesthetic presentation.

I've seen designers spend hours making beautiful storyboards that look like finished illustrations. This is the wrong investment. Spend the time on the animation.

## Storyboarding as Thinking

The real value of storyboarding isn't the artifact — the document you can show the client. It's the thinking that producing the storyboard forces. You can't storyboard a sequence without deciding what happens in it, in what order, and for how long. Making those decisions on paper, where they're easily changed, is always better than making them for the first time inside the animation software.

Plan first. Animate after.
