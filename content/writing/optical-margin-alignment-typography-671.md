InDesign has had an optical margin alignment feature for years. I encounter professionally designed print work with it disabled constantly. This is a small, high-impact setting that takes two seconds to enable and immediately improves the quality of typographic columns.

## What Optical Margin Alignment Does

When text is set with optical margin alignment disabled (the default), punctuation at the beginning and end of lines sits inside the text column boundary. A quote mark, a hyphen, or a period at the right margin creates a visible dent in the column edge — a place where the column visually steps back because the punctuation takes up space.

Optical margin alignment moves punctuation slightly outside the column boundary so it hangs in the margin. The column edge becomes visually flush, and the block of text reads as a uniform rectangle rather than an irregular form with indentations at every punctuation mark.

## Why It Matters

The effect of optical margin alignment is subtle but cumulative. In a page with multiple columns, or in a typographically refined magazine spread or book page, the columns with optical margin alignment look significantly cleaner. The text block has gravity and presence that it lacks without alignment.

It's one of those refinements that the reader doesn't consciously notice when it's present — they just feel that the page looks right. When it's absent, typographers and careful readers notice the irregularity, even if they can't name it.

## How to Enable It in InDesign

In InDesign: select the text frame, open the Story panel (Type > Story), and enable Optical Margin Alignment. The size setting should match the type size — this tells InDesign how far to push the punctuation into the margin.

For body text at 10pt, set the optical margin size to 10pt. For display text, set it to match the display size. If a frame contains mixed type sizes, set it to the predominant size.

## When to Use It

I enable optical margin alignment for: any body text that's set in multiple lines of a column (books, long-form print, magazine layouts); any high-quality print work where typographic refinement is a design goal; any presentation or document where the typographic quality will be scrutinized.

I don't apply it to: short single-line or two-line settings where the effect doesn't accumulate; type that's set very large (display type at 60pt or above, where the hanging punctuation might be too visually prominent); text in tables or structured layouts where the strict column alignment is a visual element.

## The Broader Point

Optical margin alignment is an example of the category of typographic refinements that separate competent setting from exceptional setting. These refinements are invisible individually and transformative cumulatively. They're the difference between typographic work that's correct and typographic work that's beautiful.

Learning what these refinements are, and developing the habit of applying them consistently, is a long-term investment in typographic quality that shows in every piece you produce.
