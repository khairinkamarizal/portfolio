The tension between text and image is the central compositional problem in poster design. Too much text and the poster reads like a flyer — functional but not compelling. Too much image and the necessary information gets lost. Finding the right balance isn't a formula, but it's also not purely intuitive. There's a logic to it.

## What the Poster Is Actually Doing

Before I decide anything about text-image ratio, I clarify the poster's primary function. Is it announcing an event? Building awareness of a brand or exhibition? Conveying a concept or message?

Event announcement posters need to communicate specific information efficiently. The text is load-bearing. Images support.

Conceptual or artistic posters — for galleries, cultural events, film — often operate more like images themselves. The text might be secondary, almost incidental. The image and composition carry the communication.

Knowing which mode you're in is the first decision.

## The Hierarchy Determines the Ratio

Once I know what the poster is doing, I let hierarchy determine the text-image balance rather than trying to set the ratio independently.

If the primary element is typographic — a band name, an event title, a headline — then type gets the dominant real estate. Images support it. The ratio emerges from that decision: text leads, image fills the remaining space or integrates with the type.

If the primary element is an image — a portrait, an illustration, a photograph — then image leads. Text becomes supporting information at a scale that doesn't compete.

The mistake is trying to give both text and image equal compositional weight. That fight usually produces neither a great typographic poster nor a great image-based one.

## Integration vs. Separation

One compositional choice that affects the balance significantly: do the text and image occupy separate zones on the poster, or do they integrate?

Separation is cleaner and more legible. The image lives in one area, the text in another. This works well for event posters with a lot of practical information to communicate.

Integration — type overlaying image, image elements breaking into text space, color from the image bleeding into typographic areas — creates more visual complexity and energy. It requires more careful hierarchy management because the two elements compete for attention.

I default to integration when the content warrants visual energy and the client can tolerate some legibility tradeoffs. For anything with multiple layers of practical information, separation is usually the better call.

## The Practical Test

My test for text-image balance: can a viewer read the most critical information (headline, date, location for event posters) in under three seconds from a normal viewing distance?

If yes, the balance is probably right. If the text is fighting with the image for prominence, or if the image is so dominant that the text disappears, the balance needs adjustment.

This sounds simple but it catches problems that aren't obvious at 100% zoom on a screen. Print a reduced-scale version and walk away from it, then approach it fresh. What do you see first?

## When in Doubt, Simplify

The versions of posters I'm most proud of are almost always the ones where I removed something — an image that was competing with type, a typographic element that was cluttering an otherwise strong image composition.

Restraint is a skill that takes time to develop. The first instinct when something isn't working is usually to add. The right instinct is almost always to subtract.

Less text. Stronger image. Or less image. More dominant text. Pick one lead and commit to it.
