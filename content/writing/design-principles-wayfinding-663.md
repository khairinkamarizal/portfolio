Wayfinding design is a context in which good design is invisible and bad design is actively harmful. When wayfinding works, people move through a space without thinking about it. When it fails, people get lost, miss flights, arrive late, or don't find the service they need. The stakes are higher than most graphic design work.

I've worked on wayfinding systems for cultural institutions and healthcare facilities. The lessons those projects taught me apply to any design work where absolute clarity is the goal.

## The Hierarchy Is Functional, Not Aesthetic

In wayfinding, visual hierarchy exists to serve a specific functional purpose: it tells the user what level of decision they're at and what information is relevant to their current need. Someone just entering a building needs orientation information — where are the main zones, where is the entrance hall, where is the elevator core. Someone who's already navigating to a specific room needs confirmation and correction signals.

The hierarchy in a wayfinding system maps onto the decision tree a user moves through. At each decision point — an intersection, an entrance, a fork in a path — the right information needs to be prominent and the wrong information (useful elsewhere, not useful here) needs to recede.

This is a more demanding hierarchy design task than editorial hierarchy. An editorial hierarchy serves the designer's argument. A wayfinding hierarchy serves the user's specific context in real time.

## Typography Requirements

Wayfinding typography has requirements that override aesthetic preference:

**Legibility from distance and in motion:** People are often moving when they read wayfinding signs. The typeface needs to be highly legible at a range of distances, under variable lighting conditions, for viewers moving at walking or running speed. Humanist sans-serifs with generous apertures perform best.

**Legibility for everyone:** Wayfinding serves the full range of human visual ability. High contrast, appropriate minimum sizes, and typefaces with clearly distinguishable letterforms are not optional. The letter 'l', numeral '1', and letter 'I' need to be clearly distinct. Paired 'rn' should not read as 'm'.

**Consistent application:** The same information category should always look the same throughout the system. Room numbers, floor indicators, facility names, directional indicators — each has a consistent type treatment applied without exception.

## The Arrow Is a Design Element

Directional arrows in wayfinding communicate movement and direction in a way that language doesn't. The style of the arrow — its weight, its angle, its precision — communicates confidence and clarity. A thin, delicate arrow feels uncertain. A bold, geometric arrow feels decisive.

I design arrows as part of the typeface system rather than as separate graphic elements, ensuring they share weight and geometric character with the type. An arrow that looks unrelated to the type it accompanies undermines the coherence of the system.

## Testing by Walking Through

The only way to properly evaluate wayfinding design is to move through the space. I test every wayfinding project by walking the routes users will take, at the pace users will walk them, and checking at each decision point: is the relevant information visible and clear before I need to act on it?

Problems that are invisible in a design review become obvious in the physical environment. Testing on site, with physical mockups if necessary, is not optional.
