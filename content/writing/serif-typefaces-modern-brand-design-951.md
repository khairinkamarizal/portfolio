## Serifs Are Back, for Real Reasons

For about a decade, the default brand typeface was some variation of a geometric or humanist sans-serif. Clean, neutral, modern. The visual landscape of brand typography became a sea of Futura variants and Helvetica derivatives.

The reaction against this homogeneity is now well established. Contemporary brand work increasingly uses serifs — not stuffy, conservative serifs in the way they were used in the 1980s, but contemporary high-contrast serifs, editorial-style text serifs, and expressive display serifs that feel genuinely current.

I've been using serifs deliberately in brand work for several years now. Here's what I've learned.

## What Serifs Communicate That Sans-Serifs Don't

Serifs carry different semantic freight than sans-serifs. The strokes terminating in serifs visually echo handwriting and the physical act of making marks. This gives serif typefaces an inherent connection to craft, tradition, and human authorship that most sans-serifs lack.

For brands that want to communicate heritage, care, editorial authority, or artisanal quality, this is exactly the right signal. A luxury goods brand, a heritage food producer, a legal or financial firm building on decades of expertise, a literary or cultural institution — these brands often find serifs more authentic to their identity than the cleaner sans alternatives.

## The Spectrum of Serif Personalities

Serifs aren't one thing. The category spans an enormous range from the rational, uniform stroke weight of neo-classical serifs to the high-contrast drama of Didone faces, from the sturdy, workhorse quality of slabs to the warm, handwritten character of transitional serifs.

My evaluation process for a serif recommendation considers: the historical period the brand's values most align with, the contrast ratio appropriate for the brand's personality (low contrast for approachable, high contrast for sophisticated), the x-height proportions (generous for accessible, smaller for formal), and the specific application contexts the brand needs to prioritize.

## High-Contrast Serifs for Luxury and Editorial

High-contrast serifs — Didones like Canela, Sang Bleu, or Ogg — have extremely thick downstrokes and extremely thin crossstrokes. This dramatic contrast reads as luxury, precision, and editorial sophistication. It's the visual language of high fashion and premium publishing.

These faces are demanding. The hairline strokes disappear at small sizes and in poor printing conditions. They require generous size, excellent paper, and careful color choices. But in the right conditions, they produce brand typography that nothing else can match for elegance.

## Slab Serifs for Robustness

Slab serifs — with rectangular, uniform-weight serifs — communicate something quite different from high-contrast serifs. They're sturdy, confident, and direct. The visual language of early American commercial printing, of bold editorial display, of brands that have something to say and say it plainly.

For brands with a strong voice and a willingness to occupy space — food and beverage brands, media companies, direct-to-consumer brands with personality — slab serifs often feel more honest than the neutral sans options.

## Mixing Serifs With Sans-Serifs

The classic serif/sans pairing remains reliable for a reason. Serif headlines with sans body text creates clear hierarchy while maintaining the visual sophistication of the serif without compromising the readability of the text.

I'm also increasingly comfortable with all-serif systems where the serif family has enough weight variation to handle all typographic roles. A family like Freight or Lyon has sufficient range that a single-serif system is viable and creates a distinct, unified typographic character.
