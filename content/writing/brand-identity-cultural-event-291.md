Event brand identity work has a built-in obsolescence that other brand identity doesn't. A company brand is designed to last years or decades. A festival brand might run for three months before it's shelved until next year. A one-time symposium brand has a single lifecycle.

This temporal dimension changes the creative calculus significantly — and in ways that free rather than constrain.

## Exploiting the Time Limit

Because an event brand isn't permanent, it can take creative risks that permanent brands can't. It can be more visually daring, more referential to current cultural moments, more experimental in its formal approach. If it feels slightly wrong in two years, that doesn't matter — it was designed for now.

I exploit this freedom in event brand work by pursuing stronger, more specific formal choices than I would for equivalent permanent brand work. The event brand can afford to be definitive about its aesthetic moment.

## The System Expansion Problem

An event brand typically needs to expand rapidly once created. A music festival brand that starts with just a logo and a color palette will need, in quick succession: a website, print posters in multiple formats, social media templates, stage graphics, wristbands, merchandise, wayfinding signage, and press materials.

This rapid expansion means the system needs to be designed for application breadth from the start. I think about the full range of anticipated applications when designing the core identity, not just the initial deliverables.

## Designing for the Timeline

Event brands operate on strict timelines tied to the event calendar. An announcement needs to look like the brand before the full system is built. The first poster is often being designed while the logo is still being refined.

I sequence development to prioritize: the core mark and primary color first, then the critical-path deliverables (the announcement poster, the primary digital presence), then the full system elements. This sequence keeps the brand visible and coherent even as the system is still being completed.

## Poster as Brand Flagship

For cultural events — festivals, exhibitions, conferences — the poster often is the brand's primary expression. It's the most visible, most shared, most collected piece of communication.

I treat the event poster as a brand flagship, not just a piece of promotional communication. The poster should express the full creative ambition of the event brand, setting the visual standard that all other materials aspire to.

## The Recurring Event Question

Some events recur annually. Do you keep the same brand and refresh it, or do you completely redesign each year?

There's no universal answer, but my default for recurring cultural events is: maintain the primary brand identity (the mark, the name treatment) while refreshing the visual campaign each year. This preserves recognition and brand equity across editions while allowing the campaign to feel fresh and current each year.

The festival that looks identical year after year eventually feels stale. The festival that looks completely different each year sacrifices the recognition it has built. Threading this needle is the specific creative challenge of recurring event design.

## Archival Value

Great event brands become artifacts. Festival posters from important cultural moments are collected and exhibited. Design for this possibility. The work has the potential to outlive its immediate function and become part of cultural history.

That's a serious responsibility and a genuine opportunity. Take it as seriously as it deserves.
