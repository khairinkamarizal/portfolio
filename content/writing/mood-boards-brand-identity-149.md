## The Purpose of a Mood Board

A mood board is the first visual artifact in a brand identity project. Its job is to establish alignment on visual direction between me and the client before any design work has been produced. It answers the question: what world does this brand live in?

Used correctly, a mood board prevents the most common and costly design project problem: getting through multiple rounds of concepts before discovering that the client and designer had different visual expectations.

## What Goes on a Mood Board

I include a mix of references across categories: typography samples (showing the personality of different type approaches), color environments (photos, graphics, and real-world applications that evoke the palette direction), texture and material references (surfaces, finishes, physical qualities), compositional examples (layout approaches, density and whitespace choices), and imagery that captures mood and emotional quality.

The images aren't necessarily from the brand's category. I'm showing emotional and aesthetic direction, not competitive references.

## Constructed, Not Curated

There's a meaningful difference between a mood board constructed with intention and one assembled by dumping everything that looks related into a grid. The intentional mood board has a coherent visual logic — the references reinforce and extend each other, collectively pointing to a specific visual world.

I edit ruthlessly. If a reference doesn't strengthen the overall direction, it comes out. A mood board should feel like a world, not a search results page.

## Using Multiple Directions

I typically present two or three mood boards to clients at the start of a project, each representing a distinct visual direction. The directions are genuinely different — not subtle variations on the same theme — so the client has a real choice to make.

The selection process is often revealing. When clients choose a direction, they typically explain why. Those explanations contain information about their values and preferences that feeds directly into the design brief.

## The Mood Board Isn't the Design

The most important thing to communicate to clients about mood boards: these are not concepts. We are not designing the logo or the identity yet. We're establishing a visual territory.

Clients who understand this engage with mood boards more productively. They're making a directional choice, not approving a design. That distinction keeps the project on track.

## After the Mood Board

Once a direction is selected, I write a design brief that translates the mood board's visual logic into design principles. What qualities does the mark need to have? What type personality is right? What color approach? The brief converts the mood board from images into actionable guidance.

That brief is what I design against.
