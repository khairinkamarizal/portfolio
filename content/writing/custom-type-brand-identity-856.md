## The Most Ownable Asset

A custom typeface is the most completely ownable typographic asset a brand can have. Not licensed — owned. No competitor can use it, no trend can devalue it through ubiquity, and its visual character is entirely determined by the brand's personality rather than by what was available in a type library.

For the right clients at the right scale, custom type is worth the significant investment it requires.

## What Custom Type Actually Costs

Let's be honest about the numbers: a quality custom typeface commission from a skilled type designer starts at around $10,000–20,000 for a single weight and goes up significantly from there for a full family. Typeface development typically takes months.

This is appropriate for large brands with extensive typographic needs, brands where the typeface will be used in enormous volume across many applications, and brands where distinctiveness and ownership are strategic priorities. It is not appropriate for most small and midsize businesses, and I tell clients this honestly.

## The Middle Path: Type Customization

Between using an off-the-shelf typeface and commissioning a full custom typeface is type customization — working with a type designer to modify an existing typeface. This might mean:

- Adjusting specific letterforms to be more distinctive
- Adding ligatures or alternates for brand-specific uses
- Creating a custom variable font from an existing foundation
- Extending an existing typeface with custom characters

Customization at this level costs significantly less than a full commission and produces a typeface that's more distinctive than off-the-shelf licensing. For many clients, this is the right answer.

## Evaluating the Need

When a client asks about custom type, I ask several questions before recommending it:

Is the brand at a scale where type distinctiveness is a meaningful competitive advantage? A brand with 50 employees and regional distribution doesn't need custom type; a national brand with major advertising spend might.

Is there an existing typeface that is sufficiently distinctive and appropriate for the brand? Sometimes thorough type library research finds the right face. Custom is not always better — it's just more ownable.

What is the full scope of typographic applications? A brand used primarily in digital contexts might be better served by variable font customization than a full type commission.

## When to Proceed

Custom type investment makes clear sense when: the brand has scale to amortize the cost, no existing typeface serves the brand's needs, and long-term brand distinctiveness is a real strategic priority.

When these three conditions are met, custom type is not an extravagance — it's the right tool.
