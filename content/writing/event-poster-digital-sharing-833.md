## The Dual Life of the Event Poster

Event posters used to live in one place: on a wall. Now they live on walls and on Instagram and in WhatsApp group chats and on Facebook events pages. The same design serves multiple contexts, and those contexts have different requirements.

A poster designed purely for print often looks wrong when reduced to a square thumbnail. A poster designed purely for Instagram looks generic when printed and stapled to a telephone pole. Getting both right requires thinking about both from the start.

## The Square Format Problem

Most print posters are portrait format — A3, A2, A1. Instagram is square or 4:5 portrait. When you reduce an A2 poster to a square crop, you typically get either a muddy small-type composition or an awkward crop that loses key information.

My solution: design the poster in portrait for print, then design a native square version for digital. They share visual language — same type, same color, same image if applicable — but the composition is specific to the format. The square version isn't a crop of the print version. It's a separate composition using the same assets.

This doubles the production work slightly, but the results in both contexts are substantially better than trying to make one composition serve both.

## Legibility at Thumbnail Scale

Event details that are clearly readable at A2 print scale often become illegible at the 300px social media thumbnail. I design knowing that many people will first encounter the poster as a thumbnail and decide whether to tap for more detail based on that tiny version.

The thumbnail needs to communicate: what is this? Is this for me? At 300px wide, that means the primary visual element and the event name need to be clear. Supporting details can be revealed at full size.

## Contrast and Vibrancy for Screen

Print and screen have different contrast and color rendering. A poster with subtle color relationships that looks nuanced on matte uncoated stock can look flat and muted on a phone screen. For posters that will circulate digitally, I push contrast and vibrancy slightly higher than I would for print-only work.

This isn't about making the print version wrong — it's about calibrating for the viewing conditions where most people will encounter the work.

## The Text Information Hierarchy for Sharing

When someone shares an event poster digitally, the expectation is that the image contains the key event information — date, venue, time. If that information requires zooming to read, the share becomes less useful and people stop sharing.

I now treat the shareable information hierarchy as a design constraint equivalent to the print hierarchy: date, event name, venue, time — all readable without zooming on a standard phone screen.

## Making It Worth Sharing

Beyond legibility, a poster gets shared because it looks good. This sounds obvious but it's worth stating: the design quality of an event poster directly affects how many people share it organically. A visually compelling poster gets shared as art, not just as information. That's free distribution.

Treating the event poster as a piece of visual work worth sharing — not just a functional information delivery mechanism — produces better results for the event and better work for the portfolio.
