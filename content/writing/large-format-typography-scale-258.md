## Scale changes everything

Typography at text sizes and typography at large-format sizes are governed by different perceptual rules. The conventions and best practices that produce excellent results at 10-14pt text sizes don't transfer mechanically to 10-14 inch display sizes. Scale reveals things that small sizes conceal, and conceals things that small sizes reveal.

Large-format typographic work — building signage, wall murals, trade show graphics, architectural lettering — requires rethinking assumptions that desktop design makes invisible.

## What scale reveals

At large scale, every imperfection in a typeface becomes visible. The slight unevenness in a curve that's imperceptible at 24pt becomes a noticeable wobble at 24 inches. The ink trap — a tiny notch cut into the interior corner of a letterform junction, designed to compensate for ink spread in small-size printing — opens into a visible void at large scale.

I examine typefaces for large-format use at very high zoom in vector software before committing to them. I'm looking for curve quality, corner resolution, and junction handling that will survive the translation to large scale.

## Optical corrections at large scale

Many optical corrections built into typefaces for small-size use become problems at large scale. Type designers often make thin strokes very slightly thicker than their mathematical proportion in order to maintain visual consistency at text sizes — at display sizes, this produces strokes that look optically unequal even though they're dimensionally closer to equal.

For large-format typographic work, I sometimes make manual corrections to outline letterforms — adjusting specific stroke weights or curve tensions to correct for optical problems that only appear at display scale. This is painstaking work, but it's what separates large-format typography that looks right from large-format typography that looks like it was produced by scaling up a text font.

## Material and output considerations

Large-format typography is usually output to vinyl, printed on fabric, applied as individual dimensional letters, or painted directly. Each output method has constraints.

Vinyl cutting can produce very fine detail but has minimum stroke width requirements — strokes too thin for the cutting blade will tear or not hold adhesion. Dimensional letters have minimum depth requirements for stability and visibility from an angle. Paint application on rough surfaces requires stroke widths generous enough to be read through surface texture.

I consult with the fabricator before finalizing any large-format typographic design. The fabrication constraints are design constraints.

## The experience of reading at scale

Reading at large scale is a different physical experience than reading at text size. The reader is often moving — walking past a sign, approaching a building, passing a mural. The typography needs to work at the distance and pace of that movement.

I always test large-format work by mocking it up at a reduced scale that preserves the proportional viewing distance. A 4-foot sign viewed at 20 feet is the same angular experience as a 4-inch mockup viewed at 20 inches. This test reveals spacing, weight, and size relationship issues that flat screen evaluation never shows.
