## Why Embroidery Is Different

Embroidery isn't printing. Instead of ink on a surface, it's thread stitched into fabric. The implications for logo design are significant: fine detail that's achievable in print becomes a tangled mess in thread. Thin lines that read clearly at small print sizes disappear or merge in embroidery. Color gradients are impossible to stitch accurately.

A logo designed without embroidery in mind often requires significant simplification for embroidered applications — or produces poor results when forced into the format.

## Minimum Line Weight

Thread has physical mass. A stroke that's too thin to hold as thread will either be dropped entirely by the embroidery machine or will appear as a broken, uneven line. The minimum viable stroke weight for embroidery is significantly larger than the minimum for print.

The specific minimum depends on the embroidery machine and the thread size, but as a general rule: if a stroke is thinner than approximately 1mm at the embroidered size, it's at risk.

## Minimum Text Size

Text in embroidery is particularly challenging. Very small letterforms lose their definition in thread. The x-height needs to be large enough for the letterforms to stitch cleanly. Typically, text below about 6mm in cap height becomes difficult to read in embroidery.

This minimum size is much larger than print minimums. A logo designed with small text elements that read fine in print will need a separate embroidery version with larger or simplified text.

## Complex Details and Fill Areas

Detailed inner elements — fine lines within larger shapes, complex textured fills, subtle gradations — don't translate to embroidery. The stitch types used to fill areas (satin stitches, fill stitches) have their own texture that overrides fine detail.

For embroidery applications, I design either a simplified version of the mark or I choose the primary mark specifically for its embroidery suitability.

## Working with the Digitizer

Embroidery files are created by a digitizer — a specialist who translates the logo into machine instructions for the embroidery equipment. Working closely with the digitizer produces better outcomes than simply handing over a vector file.

The digitizer's expertise about stitch types, direction, and sequence should inform the design. Their knowledge of what stitches well is design-relevant information.

## Delivering the Right Files

For embroidery, I deliver clean vector files with clearly separated colors and no gradients or complex effects. I also provide color specifications in thread color systems (Madeira, Isacord, or the digitizer's preferred system) rather than Pantone or CMYK, since thread colors are a different color model entirely.
