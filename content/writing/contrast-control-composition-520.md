## Contrast Is the Steering Wheel

Every composition I make is an attempt to control where the viewer's eye goes and in what order. Contrast is the main tool for that control. High contrast pulls attention. Low contrast releases it.

If I understand this clearly, every design decision becomes a question of contrast: do I want this element to attract attention or recede? If attract, increase contrast. If recede, decrease it.

The mistake I see most often is unintentional contrast — moments in a composition where something is high-contrast not because the designer wanted it to be the focal point, but because they didn't think about it. Every contrast moment in a design should be deliberate.

## The Five Types of Contrast I Work With

**Value contrast** — light against dark. The most fundamental type. A white form on a black background has maximum value contrast. This is the one the brain is most sensitive to.

**Color contrast** — complementary colors, or saturated against desaturated. A bright orange on a field of muted gray uses color contrast to pull attention even though the value difference might be moderate.

**Scale contrast** — large against small. A 200pt word next to 8pt supporting text. The scale gap creates hierarchy.

**Texture contrast** — complex against simple. A detailed photographic element against a flat color field. The eye rests on the flat area and moves toward the complex one.

**Shape contrast** — organic against geometric, or angular against curved. A free-form illustration element against a rigid grid creates tension that draws the eye.

## Building a Contrast Hierarchy

I think about contrast in levels. The highest-contrast element in the composition is Level 1 — the first thing the eye goes to. The next-highest is Level 2. Then Level 3.

If I have more than three distinct contrast levels, the composition usually feels cluttered. If I have only one level — everything is equally high-contrast or equally low-contrast — the composition feels flat and undirected.

The sweet spot is three levels, clearly differentiated. The viewer enters at Level 1, moves to Level 2, then settles at Level 3.

## Value Contrast as the Foundation

I build value contrast first before any other type. If the value structure of a composition is clear — strong lights, strong darks, defined midtones — I can add color, texture, and scale without losing control of the hierarchy.

When a composition feels confused, the first thing I do is strip it back to grayscale and evaluate the value structure. Almost always, the problem is that the values are too similar across too many elements. Everything is in the midtone range. Nothing is very light or very dark. The composition has no structure.

The fix is almost always: push the lights lighter, push the darks darker, and be decisive about which areas belong in each zone.

## Restraint in Contrast

The temptation is to make everything high-contrast because high contrast feels exciting. But maximum contrast everywhere means maximum contrast nowhere — it cancels itself out.

I allow myself one or two high-contrast moments in a composition. The rest needs to be quieter so those moments land.

The quiet areas aren't failures. They're what make the loud areas possible.
