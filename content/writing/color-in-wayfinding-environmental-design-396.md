Wayfinding design uses color as a coding system — different colors signal different zones, building types, or service categories. This is a different use of color than brand identity or aesthetic design. Here, color's job is pure communication: this color means this place or this direction.

## Color as Zone Identification

The most common wayfinding color application is zone coding. A large complex — a hospital, an airport, a university campus — divides into zones, and each zone gets a distinct color. The color appears on signage, flooring, wall graphics, and architectural elements throughout the zone, creating a consistent visual identity for each area.

Effective zone color systems have enough colors to differentiate all zones while keeping each color clearly distinct from its neighbors. Four to six colors can typically serve even complex buildings. More than six starts to require viewers to memorize an extended color vocabulary.

## The Memorability Requirement

Wayfinding colors must be easily remembered after a brief exposure. A viewer who is told "take the blue corridor to reach the clinic" needs to be able to identify blue reliably at a distance and distinguish it from the green corridor alongside it.

This requirement favors bold, saturated, easily-named colors. Not any blue — a specific, recognizable blue that reads as unambiguously blue. Not a blue-green that could be read as either. The naming test: if a non-designer can quickly and consistently name the color with a single common color term, it passes. If they say "kind of teal, maybe?" it doesn't.

## Contrast for Safety

In safety-critical wayfinding — exit routes, emergency paths, hazard marking — contrast is a safety issue, not just a design quality. International safety color standards specify: red for danger and fire equipment, yellow for caution and hazards, green for safety and exits, blue for mandatory information.

These standards exist because consistent color use across buildings and contexts means people bring an existing color vocabulary to any new environment. Breaking these conventions in the name of aesthetic consistency is a mistake.

## Brand Color vs. Wayfinding Color

Many buildings and institutions want their brand color system to serve as the wayfinding system. This creates tensions. Brand color systems are built for recognition and expression; wayfinding color systems are built for clarity and differentiation.

I separate these concerns in every environmental graphic design project. The brand identity is expressed in the signage design, typography, and graphic elements. The wayfinding color system is built independently, optimized for navigation. They coexist but serve different functions.

## Accessibility in Color Coding

Any wayfinding system that relies solely on color fails users with color vision deficiency (affecting approximately 8% of males). Every color code needs a secondary identifier: a shape, a letter, a number, or a pattern. The color accelerates navigation for the majority; the secondary identifier ensures it works for everyone.
