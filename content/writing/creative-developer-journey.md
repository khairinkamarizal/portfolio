I didn't set out to become a developer. I set out to make things look good.

For the first couple of years of my career, that was enough. I was a graphic designer — working on brand identities, print layouts, marketing collateral. I knew Illustrator like the back of my hand and I could kern a headline in my sleep. Design felt complete.

Then I started handing things off to developers, and something shifted.

## The Handoff Problem

The frustration started with websites. I'd spend weeks crafting a design — every spacing value considered, every interaction mapped out in Figma — and then watch it get interpreted in ways I hadn't intended. Not because the developers were bad; most were genuinely skilled. It was the translation problem. What's obvious in a design file isn't always obvious in code, and what's technically simple isn't always visually apparent.

I started writing more detailed specs. Then annotating every component. Then sitting next to developers during implementation sessions to answer questions in real time. And somewhere in those sessions, I started watching what they typed.

CSS wasn't magic. It was just rules. Rules I already understood intuitively from design — the box model is just margin, padding, border. Flexbox is just alignment and distribution. I'd been thinking in these terms for years without knowing the names.

Within a month I was writing my own CSS. Within three months I was building full pages.

## Learning in Public (Sort Of)

Malaysia has a small but genuinely tight-knit tech and design community. A lot of my early learning happened through osmosis — Telegram groups, Twitter/X threads from local devs, the occasional Kuala Lumpur tech meetup. There's something specific about learning in a local context that I think gets underestimated.

When you're watching international tutorials, there's always a layer of translation happening — not just technical, but cultural and contextual. What does a portfolio look like for the Malaysian market? What do local clients actually want? What tech stack makes sense when your hosting budget is tight and most of your users are on mobile?

Those questions pushed me toward practical solutions fast. Nuxt for static sites because Cloudflare Pages is essentially free. Tailwind because design tokens in CSS felt familiar from my systems work in Figma. Vue because the template syntax maps closely to how I think about components in design.

## The Hybrid Identity Problem

Here's the thing nobody tells you about being a creative developer: you'll spend years explaining yourself.

To designers, you're "the one who can code" — which sometimes means you're expected to handle everything technical. To developers, you're "the design person" — which sometimes means your code opinions are taken less seriously. The hybrid role is genuinely valuable, but it takes time for the people around you to figure out how to work with you.

At Horuz Technology, my first proper job that used both sides, I was brought in as a Digital Strategist and Front-End Developer. In practice, that meant I was doing design work, writing frontend code, writing strategy decks, and occasionally explaining to clients why their request would take three weeks, not three days. It was chaotic and I loved it.

What I learned there: the most valuable thing about being a hybrid isn't that you can do two jobs. It's that you understand both sides of a conversation that usually has people talking past each other.

## What Cyberjaya Taught Me

I'm based in Cyberjaya — Malaysia's tech hub, a purpose-built city that's equal parts startup energy and sleepy suburban quiet. It's an interesting place to work from. Close enough to KL to tap into the city's creative scene, far enough to think clearly.

The local tech ecosystem has grown a lot in the years I've been working. More startups, more design-aware founders, more clients who understand that "make it look like Apple" isn't a brief. The appetite for quality digital work is real, even if the budgets don't always match the ambition.

Working independently here has been a lesson in positioning. Malaysia is a competitive market for freelance creative work. What's differentiated me — at least I think — is the ability to go from brand conversation to live site without the work passing through five different people. Some clients want that tightly integrated process. The ones who do tend to be the best to work with.

## Where I'm At Now

I'm three-plus years into treating design and development as one practice rather than two adjacent ones. At Trustori, I'm leading visual design while staying close to the product side. In my independent work, I'm building sites and brand systems for clients who want someone that can hold the whole thing.

The journey from "I just want to make things look good" to "I want to understand every layer of how digital things are made" has been longer and more winding than I expected. But I haven't wanted to go back to pure design since the first time I pushed something live and saw exactly what I'd imagined appear in a browser.

That moment still gets me. It probably always will.
