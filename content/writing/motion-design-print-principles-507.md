## Print Taught Me to Read a Frame

Before I made anything move, I made things still. Posters, mostly. A poster has one frame to communicate everything — the hierarchy, the mood, the message. You can't rely on time. You can't use movement to clarify. If the composition doesn't work as a frozen moment, nothing saves it.

That constraint turned out to be the best preparation for motion design I could have had.

When I started doing motion work, I noticed that most of the problems I saw in other people's animations were really composition problems. The hierarchy was unclear. The eye didn't know where to enter the frame. Elements competed for attention in ways that weren't intentional. These weren't timing problems — they were poster problems that happened to be moving.

## Hierarchy Is the Same in Both Disciplines

In print, hierarchy means deciding what the viewer reads first, second, third. Size, weight, contrast, position — these are the tools. A headline dominates. A subhead supports. Body text fills. The eye moves through in a sequence the designer controls.

In motion, you have the same problem plus time. But the logic is identical: what does the viewer see first, second, third? The difference is that in motion, you can control the sequence more explicitly by bringing elements in at different moments.

What I learned from print: if your hierarchy isn't clear in a frozen frame, it won't be clear when it moves. I still design key frames of every motion piece as static compositions first. If the hierarchy reads in the freeze frame, the animation amplifies it. If it doesn't, no amount of easing will fix it.

## Negative Space Moves

One of the things I love most about motion design is what you can do with negative space over time. In print, a well-placed empty area creates tension and breathing room. In motion, that empty area can contract, expand, fill, reveal.

Some of my favorite motion moments come from treating negative space as an active element — having it close around a piece of text, or open outward to reveal a second layer of content. This is pure poster thinking applied to time.

## Color as a Temporal Tool

In poster design, color creates hierarchy instantly. The highest-contrast element reads first. I use this principle in motion: when I want to direct attention to something new appearing in the frame, I bring it in at a higher contrast than the existing elements. The eye follows contrast. This is the same rule that governs a good poster, just applied across a timeline.

## What Motion Adds That Print Can't

The honest answer is that motion adds emphasis and sequence control. It lets you deliver information in a specific order rather than trusting the viewer to follow a visual hierarchy.

But here's what I've found: the best motion design doesn't depend on movement to communicate. The best motion design communicates in any single frame, and the movement is expressive — it adds personality and energy rather than carrying the message.

That's exactly what good poster design does. The composition communicates. The craft makes it memorable.

The two disciplines are closer than most motion designers realize. If you want to get better at motion, make more posters.
