Color harmony is not about colors that look pretty together. It's about color relationships that serve the composition — that create hierarchy, generate tension or calm, and give the poster a unified visual character. Pretty combinations that fight the composition aren't harmony; they're decoration.

## Starting From a Dominant Color

Every strong poster palette has a dominant color — the one that occupies the most visual real estate and establishes the poster's fundamental character. I choose this first, before any other color decision, because every other color will be evaluated against it.

The dominant color establishes the emotional register: warm or cool, vivid or muted, light or dark. The rest of the palette supports and develops that register.

## The Harmony Strategies

**Analogous:** Colors adjacent on the color wheel — blue, blue-green, green. Creates a unified, cohesive atmosphere. Quiet and harmonious, sometimes too quiet. Needs contrast from value variation to create hierarchy.

**Complementary:** Colors opposite on the color wheel — orange and blue, red and green, yellow and violet. Creates maximum visual tension. Vivid and energetic when both colors are saturated; calmer when one is desaturated. The most attention-grabbing strategy.

**Split-complementary:** A base color plus the two colors flanking its complement. More variety than complementary, less tension. Flexible and visually interesting.

**Triadic:** Three colors evenly spaced on the color wheel. Creates rich, varied, dynamic palettes. Difficult to balance — one color usually needs to dominate significantly.

**Monochromatic:** Variations of a single hue at different values and saturations. Elegant and cohesive. Creates sophisticated depth with restraint.

## Value Harmony vs. Hue Harmony

Most discussions of color harmony focus on hue relationships, but value harmony is often more important for poster work. Colors of dramatically different values create strong contrast; colors of similar values create flat compositions.

I design poster palettes with deliberate value contrast: a light value for backgrounds or large areas, a dark value for primary elements, a mid-value for secondary elements. The hue relationships can follow any strategy; the value structure is what creates legibility.

## The Accent Color

A well-designed poster palette usually has one accent color — a color used sparingly and specifically to draw the eye to the most critical element or to create a point of visual intensity in the composition.

The accent should contrast with everything else in the palette — either by hue (a warm accent in a cool palette, a cool accent in a warm palette) or by value (a very dark or very light accent against a mid-value palette). Its power comes from its rarity and contrast.

## Testing the Palette in Context

I never finalize a poster palette by looking at swatches. I apply the palette to the actual composition — the rough layout with all the elements placed — and evaluate how the color relationships work spatially.

Colors interact differently at different scales. A color that reads as a quiet secondary element as a small swatch can become visually dominating when applied to a large area of the layout. The composition is the test of the palette, not the swatch sheet.
