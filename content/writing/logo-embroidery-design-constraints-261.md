## Embroidery is a completely different design problem

When a client mentions that their logo will appear on embroidered uniforms, hats, or merchandise, that single piece of information changes the design requirements substantially. Embroidery is not printing — it's stitching, and stitching has physical constraints that printing doesn't.

A logo designed without embroidery in mind will almost always need modification before it can be embroidered well. A logo designed with embroidery in mind from the start needs no modification and produces better results.

## The minimum detail threshold

Embroidery machines work with thread, which has physical width. The smallest element that embroiders cleanly is determined by the thread count and the density of the stitch. As a practical rule, any element thinner than 1.5mm at the final embroidery size will either disappear or blur into adjacent elements.

This minimum threshold eliminates hairline type, fine serif details, thin geometric elements, and small text below approximately 5-6mm cap height. For a typical chest logo at 90mm wide, this means significant restraint in detail.

## Simplifying for stitch count

Complex logos with gradients, many colors, or intricate details require high stitch counts — more time on the machine, more thread, and higher cost per piece. For mass-produced merchandise, stitch count is a real economic consideration.

I design embroidery-primary logos with deliberate simplicity: bold outlines, limited colors, no gradients, generous spacing between elements. These constraints often produce marks that are stronger than their more complex counterparts — forced simplicity is a useful discipline.

## Color limitations and separation

Most embroidery jobs use a limited number of thread colors — typically 3-6 for a standard commercial job. Colors need to be solid, clearly separated, and chosen from available thread color ranges. The Pantone-to-thread-color translation is approximate — thread colors don't precisely match Pantone swatches.

I specify embroidery colors with reference to the thread manufacturer's color catalog (Madeira, Isacord, or similar) rather than to Pantone. This gives the embroiderer a precise reference rather than asking them to match a color system their materials don't support.

## Creating embroidery-specific versions

For brands where embroidery is a significant touchpoint, I create an embroidery-specific version of the mark as a formal brand asset. This version has the minimum detail level for the embroidery context, uses bold weights, eliminates fine details, and specifies thread colors rather than ink colors.

Having this version as an officially sanctioned brand asset prevents well-meaning vendors from improvising their own simplifications — which almost always produce worse results than a professionally designed simplified version.
