There's a version of brand identity work that starts with a logo and calls it done. I've moved away from that. A logo in isolation isn't a brand identity — it's a symbol waiting for a system.

## Discovery Before Design

I spend the first chunk of every identity project asking questions, not drawing. What does this organization actually do, and who are the people it serves? What does it want to be known for five years from now? Who are its competitors, and what visual territory have they claimed?

The answers to those questions are more useful than any mood board. They tell me what the identity needs to do functionally, and they tell me what territory is already occupied — which I usually want to avoid.

## From Positioning to Visual Language

Positioning work gives me a set of attributes the brand needs to embody. From those attributes, I derive a visual language: the emotional register, the formality level, the energy. Is this brand authoritative or approachable? Traditional or progressive? Playful or earnest?

These decisions drive everything downstream. Typeface selection, color palette, mark style, photographic direction — all of it should express the same set of attributes. When the components express different things, the identity feels incoherent. When they're aligned, the brand feels certain of itself.

## The System Components

A complete identity system for most organizations includes: a primary mark and its variations, a color palette (primary, secondary, and neutral), a typography system (primary and secondary typefaces with usage guidance), and a set of graphic elements that extend the visual language beyond the logo.

Those graphic elements are often where the identity really lives. A distinctive pattern, a specific way of cropping photography, a recurring geometric motif — these are the things that make a brand recognizable even when the logo isn't present.

## Guidelines That Actually Get Used

I write brand guidelines that are specific enough to be actionable but flexible enough to be useful. Guidelines that are too rigid get ignored the first time someone hits an edge case. Guidelines that are too loose become suggestions.

The most important sections are the ones that explain the *why* behind the rules. If a designer understands why the secondary typeface is used only for captions, they can make a smart judgment call about an unusual use case. If they only know the rule without the reasoning, they'll either break it arbitrarily or follow it in situations where it doesn't apply.

## Handing Off

A brand identity isn't finished when I deliver the files. I do a working session with the client team to walk through the guidelines, answer questions, and make sure the people who'll use the system daily actually understand it. The best identity system in the world fails if the people using it don't know how.
