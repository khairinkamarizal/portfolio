## The paper is part of the design

I've seen beautiful poster designs get mediocre execution because the designer treated paper selection as a production detail rather than a design decision. Paper stock — its weight, texture, finish, and color — fundamentally changes how a printed design reads and feels. Making paper choices after the design is complete is backwards.

I spec paper before I finalize color and typography. The paper is the canvas, and the canvas changes the painting.

## Coated vs. uncoated: the most fundamental choice

Coated stocks (gloss or matte laminated surfaces) produce sharp, color-rich printing. Ink sits on top of the coating rather than absorbing into the substrate. The result is high contrast, vibrant color, and fine detail reproduction. Coated stocks are right for design work where color accuracy and photographic reproduction are important.

Uncoated stocks absorb ink, which creates a softer, more organic print quality. Colors are less vibrant but feel more natural and tactile. Letterforms look slightly more textured. This quality — sometimes called "ink character" — is something I actively design for when the project calls for a handcrafted, artisanal, or vintage feeling.

For music posters, art exhibitions, and craft-focused brands, I almost always choose uncoated. For commercial retail and product-focused print work, coated.

## Weight and its physical presence

Paper weight affects the physical presence of a printed piece in a way that's hard to communicate in a brief or presentation. A 100lb text weight poster feels noticeably different in the hand than a 100lb cover weight one, even at the same size.

I always specify paper weight with the production context in mind. A poster that will be handed out at an event and immediately rolled up can be lighter than one that's meant to be kept and displayed. A poster mailed in a tube needs enough weight to recover from curling. These are physical design requirements, not just aesthetic ones.

## Off-white and colored stocks

The "white" in white stocks varies enormously. A warm off-white stock changes every color on it — blues become slightly purple, cool grays warm up, pure black feels less harsh. A bright white stock is the most neutral, but sometimes too clean-feeling for work that wants warmth.

I swatch papers before every significant print project. I don't make paper decisions from screen previews or PDF proofs. Ink on actual paper is the only accurate preview.

## The tactile argument for print

In an era where most design is digital, the tactile quality of print is one of its strongest differentiators. A well-chosen paper stock — textured, weighty, with a finish that catches light beautifully — creates a physical experience that digital can't replicate. Treating paper as a purely utilitarian production variable throws away one of print design's most powerful qualities.
