There's a category of poster design that never gets discussed in design education: the poster that has to survive terrible reproduction. Not the gallery print, the museum piece, the letterpress run on premium stock. The gig poster that gets photocopied fifty times. The community flyer that gets photographed on a phone and shared on Instagram at 40% quality. The political broadsheet that gets reproduced on a machine from 1987.

I've worked in all of these contexts. They have their own set of constraints and their own aesthetic logic. Ignoring those constraints produces posters that look fine on a monitor and fall apart in the real world.

## Design for the Worst Reproduction

The rule I follow for any poster that will be reproduced outside of a controlled print environment: design for the worst likely reproduction, not the best.

If the poster will be photocopied on a standard black-and-white copier, design it in black and white from the start. Don't design in full color and assume a B&W copy will work — it won't. Midtones collapse. Subtle gradients become flat gray fields. Fine lines disappear.

If the poster will be photographed and shared digitally, design for legibility at 1080px wide, compressed to 70% JPEG quality. That's roughly what Instagram does to uploaded images. At that resolution and compression, fine serif type at small sizes becomes unreadable. Subtle texture becomes compression artifact noise.

## High Contrast as the Foundation

Low-fidelity reproduction favors high contrast. Black on white. White on black. Strong color fields with no gradients. Bold type weights. Clear silhouettes.

This isn't a limitation — it's an aesthetic. The Xerox aesthetic of punk flyers and protest posters is visually powerful precisely because of the high contrast. The reproduction constraint became the style. Working within it deliberately produces work that feels intentional.

When I design for photocopied reproduction, I'll often start in a strict two-tone mode — black and white only — and only add color if I know a full-color version will be available alongside the B&W reproduction.

## Type That Survives Compression

Small, light type is the first thing to die in a bad reproduction. For any poster that might be shared as a compressed image or photocopied, I follow a minimum weight rule: nothing lighter than Regular weight at body text sizes, nothing lighter than Medium weight at 12pt or below.

Serif faces with hairline strokes — elegant in a gallery print — become a mess in a photocopy. For low-fidelity contexts, I prefer sans serifs with consistent stroke widths, or slab serifs where the serifs are thick enough to survive.

## The Instagram Story Problem

A huge amount of poster distribution now happens through social media stories — vertical, full-bleed, ephemeral. This is effectively a second poster format with its own requirements: 9:16 aspect ratio, viewed for seconds, often with the sound off, on a phone screen in a bright environment.

Designs that were conceived as A2 landscape posters rarely translate well to this format. The hierarchy gets compressed, the type becomes tiny, the image loses its context. If social story distribution is part of the plan, design for it explicitly — don't treat it as an afterthought crop of the main design.

## Knowing Your Distribution Context

The bottom line: know how your poster is going to reach people before you design it. A poster that will only ever exist as a high-res print file on Behance can do things that a poster being distributed as photocopied flyers cannot.

Neither context is less legitimate. But they require fundamentally different design decisions. A poster optimized for Instagram compression looks different from a poster optimized for risograph printing looks different from a poster optimized for screen printing on kraft paper. Honor the constraints of the medium you're actually working in.
