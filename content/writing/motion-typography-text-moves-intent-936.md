## Text in Motion Is a Different Problem

Static typography solves one problem: how do these letters look on a surface? Motion typography solves two problems simultaneously: how do these letters look, and what does the movement say?

The movement of text is a layer of meaning on top of the textual meaning. A word that slams into frame communicates urgency. A word that drifts in slowly communicates contemplation. A word that assembles letter by letter communicates process or discovery. Getting these two layers aligned is the core challenge of kinetic typography.

Getting them misaligned — a soft, lyrical message delivered with aggressive, staccato motion — creates a dissonance the audience feels even if they can't identify it.

## Motion as Punctuation

I think about motion the way I think about typographic punctuation. The movement inflects the text — it adds emphasis, pause, or rhythm that the letters themselves can't provide.

A sudden stop at the end of a word's movement is a period. A trailing settle is an ellipsis. A word that bounces slightly on landing is almost an exclamation point. A word that fades out is a question.

Building a motion vocabulary for a piece and applying it consistently creates the same sense of authorship that consistent typographic treatment creates in print.

## The Three Motion Properties I Control

Every text element in a motion piece has three motion properties: timing (when does it move), speed (how fast does it move), and trajectory (what path does it travel).

Timing creates rhythm. If all text elements enter at regular intervals, the piece feels metronomic and predictable. Varying timing — grouping some elements closely together, leaving space before others — creates a more musical, breathable rhythm.

Speed communicates urgency or calm. Fast-moving text demands attention. Slow-moving text invites contemplation. Within a single piece, I use speed variation to signal to the audience which moments are most important.

Trajectory creates personality. Horizontal movement feels stable and direct. Vertical movement feels aspirational or falling. Diagonal movement feels dynamic. Rotational movement feels playful or disorienting depending on context. I match trajectory to the word's meaning wherever possible.

## Scale Reveal

One of my favorite kinetic typography techniques is the scale reveal — text that starts at a very different scale from its reading size and animates to its final position.

A word that starts huge and scales down to reading size while entering from outside the frame creates a sense of the text arriving from a larger world. A word that starts small at the center of the frame and scales up creates a sense of emergence or growth.

Scale reveals work because the size contrast between starting and ending states creates visual drama. The audience perceives the movement as significant — something is happening — before they can fully read the word.

## When Motion Should Be Still

Not all text in a motion piece should move. Static text — text that holds position while everything else moves — commands immediate attention by its stillness. It becomes a visual anchor that the moving elements orbit around.

In a piece with a lot of motion, I often design the most important text element as the still one. The stillness is the hierarchy signal: everything is moving except for the thing that matters most.

## Accessibility in Kinetic Typography

Rapidly moving text can be genuinely difficult to read for some audiences. I set a minimum duration for any text element that needs to be read: at least one second for a short phrase, two to three seconds for a longer sentence.

For complex motion typography, I also ensure the final held frame shows all text in a readable position. Audiences who miss elements in motion can catch them at the hold.

## The Script Is the Design Document

Before I touch animation software for a kinetic typography piece, I annotate the script with motion intentions for each element. This annotation — timing marks, speed descriptors, trajectory notes — is my design document. It keeps the motion intentional throughout production and gives me a reference to check against when the animation is getting complex.
