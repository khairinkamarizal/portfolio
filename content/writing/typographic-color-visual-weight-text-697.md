In typography, "color" refers to the overall visual density of a block of text when viewed at a scale where individual letters aren't readable — when you see the block as a texture rather than as words. Some blocks look dark and heavy; others look light and airy. This visual weight is the typographic color.

Understanding typographic color is useful because it's one of the mechanisms through which text blocks participate in the visual composition of a page, not just its information architecture.

## What Affects Typographic Color

The typographic color of a text block is determined by several variables working together:

**Typeface weight:** Heavier weights produce darker color. A bold weight text block has dramatically more visual density than the same text in a light weight.

**Type size:** Larger type at a fixed leading produces denser color because the ratio of ink to white space increases.

**Leading:** More leading produces lighter color. The additional white space between lines reduces the overall density.

**Tracking:** Tighter tracking produces darker color (letters closer together, less white space). Open tracking produces lighter color.

**Typeface design:** The inherent darkness of a typeface varies with its stroke weight, x-height, and design style. A humanist typeface and a geometric typeface at the same nominal weight will have different actual colors because of design decisions in the letterforms.

## Using Typographic Color in Layout

The color of text blocks is a compositional element. A dark, dense text block is visual mass that anchors a layout. A light, airy text block recedes and creates openness.

In editorial layouts — magazine spreads, book pages, annual reports — the play between denser text areas and lighter text areas, combined with image placement and white space, creates the visual rhythm of the page. Designers who think only about the position of elements, and not about their visual weight, miss half the compositional vocabulary available to them.

## The Even Color Standard

For body text, the standard I aim for is even typographic color: a text block where the density is consistent throughout, with no visible bands of lightness or darkness caused by inconsistent word spacing or hyphenation.

Fully justified text in particular requires attention to word spacing to maintain even color. Badly justified text produces "rivers" — visible lines of white space running vertically through the block where word spaces happen to align across lines. These rivers are the most visible form of uneven typographic color and are a mark of poor typographic control.

I address uneven color in justified text by: adjusting the H&J (hyphenation and justification) settings in InDesign to allow more hyphenation (which reduces the need for large word spaces), adjusting the paragraph-specific tracking if necessary, and occasionally rewriting a line or two if the text is critically important.

## The Contrast Between Text Tiers

The different tiers of a typographic hierarchy should have different colors — this is one of the mechanisms that distinguishes them. A heavy headline has dark color. Body text has medium color. Captions at small size have lighter color. The color differences reinforce the hierarchy established by size and weight.

When a layout's hierarchy feels unclear even though the sizes are correctly differentiated, the color relationships are often the problem: the tiers are too close in color to create clear visual distinction.
