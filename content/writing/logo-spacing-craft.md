Designers who haven't worked at logo scale often underestimate the importance of spacing. They set letter spacing mathematically, align elements to the pixel grid, and deliver work that is technically precise and visually wrong.

Optical adjustment is the craft that closes the gap between mathematical precision and visual truth.

## Why Optical Spacing Differs from Mathematical Spacing

The eye doesn't measure distances; it measures perceived density. Two letters set 10pt apart don't look equally spaced if one pair is O and C (open forms facing each other) and another is I and I (parallel vertical strokes). The O-C pair looks wider than the I-I pair at the same numerical spacing.

This is why mechanical letter spacing in wordmarks always looks slightly wrong. The spacing has to be adjusted optically — set by eye, not by the numbers.

## The Process I Use

I start with mathematically even tracking, then zoom in on each adjacent pair of characters and ask: does this gap feel consistent with the gaps on either side? I adjust pairs that feel too tight or too loose until the rhythm feels even.

Then I zoom out to full composition scale and look at the whole wordmark as a unit. Optical illusions can appear at full scale that weren't visible when I was close — a cluster of visually heavy characters near the beginning or end, an area of openness that creates an unintended visual break.

## Vertical Alignment Surprises

Optical vertical centering is different from mathematical centering. A shape with a visual center above its geometric center — like a triangle — needs to be positioned above the mathematical center line to appear optically centered. A wordmark with ascenders needs different treatment than one with only cap-height letters.

I test vertical alignment in every logo application by placing the mark in a container and asking: does it look centered? If the answer is "almost but not quite," I move it until it looks right, then check the math to understand the offset for specification purposes.

## Spacing in the System

Spacing consistency is part of the logo system, not just the primary mark. When the logo appears with a tagline, with a strapline, in a badge, or in a compact application, the internal spacing relationships need to be documented and specified.

Designers who receive these files and "adjust" the spacing without understanding the optical relationships built into the original mark are one of the main sources of logo degradation over time. Good documentation prevents this.

## The Margin of Safety

One spacing specification I always include: the clear space requirement. A minimum area around the logo — usually defined as a multiple of a reference dimension within the mark itself — that should be free of other visual elements.

This isn't just a stylistic preference. It's a functional requirement for the mark to be legible and for the brand to be clearly perceived as distinct from surrounding content.
