## Screen Lies

I'll say it plainly: designing type on screen and printing it at large scale are not the same activity, and treating them as equivalent is a reliable way to produce work that disappoints in real life.

The main lie the screen tells you is about tracking. Type that looks correctly spaced on a monitor often reads as too tight when printed large. The eye processes spatial relationships differently when the type is physically bigger, and the tracking needs to account for that.

## The Optical Reality of Scale

When type gets larger, optical spacing issues that are invisible at small sizes become prominent. Certain letter pairs create visual gaps or collisions that don't register at 12pt but are clearly awkward at 200pt on a printed banner.

This means large-format typesetting requires more individual attention to spacing — sometimes kerning pairs manually that would be fine to leave to the typeface's built-in values at smaller sizes.

## Contrast and Legibility

The contrast equation also changes at scale. On screen, a light type on a medium background might look legible because you're close to it. On a poster read from ten meters away, that same combination might fail entirely.

For outdoor print work I treat WCAG contrast standards as a floor, not a target. I aim significantly higher. Environmental conditions — sun glare, shadows, viewing angle — degrade apparent contrast in ways that don't show up in a PDF proof on a backlit screen.

## Physical Proofing Is Not Optional

I proof everything going to large-format print. If I can print a portion at actual scale on a large-format inkjet, I do. If I can't, I at least print a section at full scale on tiled sheets from a desktop printer and tape them together.

This sounds like extra work. It is, and it's worth it every time. I've caught spacing problems, ink behavior issues, and tonal compression artifacts in proofs that would have been expensive corrections in production.

## The Distance Test

When proofing large-format type, I look at it from the actual intended viewing distance. Not the desk. The distance a viewer would be standing. What's legible from a foot away is completely different from what's legible from five meters.

The distance test has saved several pieces of my work from going to print with type that was too small or too similar in value to the background. It's a simple habit that requires no special tools — just the willingness to actually walk across the room.

## Baseline Grids Don't Scale Linearly

One more thing: the relationship between point size and leading that works at body copy sizes doesn't simply scale up. At 200pt, type that's set solid (type size equals leading) looks crushingly tight. At display sizes I typically open up leading to 115–130% of type size, sometimes more.

The right answer is always determined by looking at the actual typeset text rather than applying a formula — but the formula breaks down faster than most designers expect.
