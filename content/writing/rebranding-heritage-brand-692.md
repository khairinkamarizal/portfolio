Heritage brands present a specific design challenge. They have equity — recognition, association, loyalty — built over decades of consistent communication. That equity is genuinely valuable, not just sentimental. And yet the same brands often face real challenges: they read as dated, they fail to attract new audiences, or they don't work in the digital contexts where modern brands need to function.

The identity work for a heritage brand is a delicate balance between honoring what's built and enabling what's next.

## Auditing the Equity First

Before I change anything about a heritage brand, I spend significant time understanding what the existing brand has built. What associations does it carry? What do loyal customers say about it? What elements of the visual identity — the mark, the color, the typeface — carry the most recognition?

This isn't always the element the brand is most proud of. Sometimes the equity lives in a color more than a mark. Sometimes it's in a specific graphic element that's been used consistently in all communications. Sometimes it's in a pattern or a spatial treatment rather than the logo itself.

Understanding where the equity actually lives, as opposed to where the brand thinks it lives, shapes every subsequent decision.

## The Minimum Viable Change Principle

My starting position for heritage brand work is: change only what needs to change. This is the minimum viable change principle. The goal is to address the specific problems that necessitated the rebrand without disturbing the equity that makes the brand valuable.

If the mark needs to be modernized but the color is working, update the mark and keep the color. If the typeface has dated badly but the mark is still strong, update the typeface and keep the mark. Comprehensive rebrands that update everything simultaneously are appropriate when everything is broken — not when specific elements are the problem.

## Evolutionary Techniques

Several techniques help modernize heritage marks without destroying their equity:

**Refinement of existing forms:** Cleaning up irregular paths, improving geometric precision, adjusting proportions — all without changing the fundamental character of the mark. The result looks like the same mark, but better.

**Simplification:** Removing detail that no longer serves the mark at current production scales, particularly detail that was added in eras when print production was more forgiving of complexity.

**Weight adjustment:** Slightly increasing or decreasing the visual weight of the mark to work better in contemporary contexts.

**Typographic update:** Replacing a dated secondary typeface while keeping the primary mark unchanged.

These interventions can collectively modernize a heritage brand substantially while maintaining the recognizable character that carries the equity.

## Communicating the Evolution

When a heritage brand updates its identity, the communication matters. Long-term customers may have emotional attachment to the visual identity — especially for consumer brands. Abrupt change without communication reads as rejection of the heritage.

I recommend communications that honor the heritage while explaining the evolution: "We've grown. Our mark has evolved to reflect that." This acknowledges continuity rather than break, which is almost always the accurate story when a heritage brand undertakes evolutionary rather than revolutionary change.

## When Full Rebrand Is Right

Sometimes the honest answer is that the heritage equity is a liability rather than an asset — the brand carries associations the company has moved past, or is entering a new market where the heritage is irrelevant or confusing. In those cases, a full rebrand is correct.

But this conclusion should be reached after genuine analysis of the equity, not assumed from the start.
