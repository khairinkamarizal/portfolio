## The Hierarchy Challenge

An event poster typically needs to communicate: who's performing or presenting, what the event is, where it's happening, when it's happening, how to get tickets, and often a supporting context (a venue's brand, a series identity, a sponsor). That's a lot of information for a single surface.

The challenge is that viewers don't read posters — they scan them. The hierarchy has to be so clear that someone can extract the essential information in a glance, even if they never stop to read the full details.

## The Three-Second Rule

I design event posters with a self-imposed three-second rule: a viewer passing the poster at walking speed should be able to get the essential information — who, what, and when — in three seconds or less. If the hierarchy doesn't deliver that, the poster is failing at its primary job.

This means the headline hierarchy (typically artist or event name, then date, then venue) needs to use scale aggressively. The primary element should be dramatically larger than the secondary elements.

## What Gets Visual Priority

The decision about what gets visual priority is strategic, not just aesthetic. For a headlining artist with strong name recognition, their name should dominate. For an event concept that's more compelling than any individual performer, the concept should lead.

I always ask: what is the thing that will make someone stop and want to know more? That thing should be the visual anchor.

## How I Handle Supporting Information

Ticket prices, door times, age restrictions, sponsors — this is supporting information that needs to be present but shouldn't compete with the primary hierarchy. I use a smaller, neutral type treatment for this tier: often a clean sans-serif in a smaller size, at reduced color intensity, kept together in a consistent location on the poster.

This creates a natural reading flow: primary headline → secondary details → supporting information. Viewers can absorb as much or as little as they need.

## When Design Serves Information

Some poster design tilts heavily toward expressive image-making — the design itself is the content, and the information is almost secondary. That approach is appropriate for high-profile events where the artist's name alone can be the visual.

For most working event posters, the design serves the information. The visual decisions should amplify and clarify the hierarchy, not obscure it. I keep that priority clear.

## Consistent Templates vs. Fresh Design

For ongoing event series — a weekly venue listing, a recurring festival — there's value in establishing a consistent template. The template maintains brand recognition and reduces design time. Individual events get distinguished through variable content within a consistent structure.

For one-off or special events, fresh design is appropriate. The poster itself signals that this event is different.
