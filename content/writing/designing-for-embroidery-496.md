Embroidery is a production technique most graphic designers encounter when a client wants their logo on a polo shirt, a cap, or a bag. The logo that works fine in every other application suddenly becomes a problem. The embroidery shop says it can't be done as designed. The client is frustrated. The designer is confused.

Understanding why embroidery works the way it does prevents these situations and allows you to design logos that work in this medium from the start.

## How Embroidery Works

Embroidery is literally sewing thread onto fabric. The design is digitized — converted into a set of stitch instructions — and then a machine executes those stitches. The digitization process is a translation from vector artwork to stitch patterns, and it's where most problems originate.

Stitches have physical dimensions. The minimum readable stitch area is larger than the minimum printable detail in any other medium. Fine lines, small text, and complex gradients that reproduce perfectly in offset printing become impossible in embroidery.

## The Constraints

**Minimum element size.** Elements smaller than approximately 4mm in physical dimension generally can't be embroidered. Fine lines thinner than 1.5mm physical width are unreliable. Text below approximately 6mm cap height is often illegible.

**No gradients.** Embroidery uses thread colors, not ink. You can't blend thread colors the way you blend ink in printing. A gradient from blue to purple in a logo becomes either a hard boundary between two thread colors or a coarse pattern of alternating thread colors. Neither looks like the original.

**Color count limitations.** Each color requires a thread change and separate digitization. Simpler is better. Most embroidery works best with 4-8 colors. Some applications have maximum color counts due to machine limitations.

**No thin outlines at small scale.** A logo with a thin outline stroke that works at large scale loses the outline at embroidery scale because the stroke width becomes too small to stitch.

**Registration between colors.** Adjacent areas of different thread colors have registration tolerance. A logo where two colors need to meet at a precise boundary may have slight gaps or overlaps in embroidery.

## Designing Embroidery-Ready Logos

The requirements for embroidery-ready logos:

- Bold, simple forms with no fine detail
- Minimum stroke widths that account for the 1.5mm thread width minimum
- No gradients, replaced by solid color fields
- Text at 6mm cap height minimum (larger is better)
- Generous spacing between small elements
- Maximum 6-8 colors

A logo designed with these constraints works in embroidery. A logo designed without them needs a simplified version for embroidery applications.

## The Sub-Mark for Embroidery

For complex logos that can't be fully embroidered, I design a sub-mark: a simplified version specifically for embroidery contexts. This is usually the most essential element of the mark — the symbol alone, or the first initial, or the most geometrically simple element of the composition.

The sub-mark shouldn't look like a degraded version of the full mark. It should look like a purposeful, complete design in its own right that happens to be appropriate for the embroidery application.

## Working With Embroidery Digitizers

The digitization step — converting your vector file to stitch instructions — is done by a digitizer, either at the embroidery shop or as a separate service. A skilled digitizer can work around some design limitations. An unskilled one will produce a poor result regardless of how well the design is prepared.

I always ask to see a physical sample before approving a full embroidery run. The digitizer's interpretation of my artwork may differ from my intent, and the only way to know is to see the sewn output. Color matching is particularly variable — thread colors don't map precisely to Pantone or CMYK — so I evaluate the physical sample in the intended lighting condition.

Embroidery is a beautiful application when the design is built for it.
