A poster series is a different design problem from a single poster. Each piece needs to work independently — communicating its specific content clearly and completely — while also functioning as part of a visual family. The series creates meaning through accumulation that no single poster could achieve alone.

## The System Before the Posters

Before I design any individual poster in a series, I design the system: the rules that will govern all the posters. The grid, the typeface family, the color approach, the compositional principles. These rules create the family resemblance.

Once the system is defined, each poster becomes an application of the system to specific content. The variation comes from the content; the consistency comes from the system.

## Consistency Through Constraint

The constraints of the system are what make series design interesting. Each poster uses the same grid, the same typefaces, the same limited color palette. The challenge is making each poster visually distinct and appropriate to its specific content within those constraints.

This is a more demanding creative problem than designing each poster from scratch. When anything is possible, obvious solutions are always available. When the tools are limited, you have to find more inventive solutions.

## Variation Strategies

Within a constrained system, variation can come from: the image or graphic element (each poster has a different central image, but they all use the same compositional approach to that image), the color accent (each poster uses a different color from the defined palette as its primary accent), or the primary content type (some posters are image-led, some are type-led, within the same grid system).

The best series designs use variation that is content-driven — the formal differences between posters emerge from the differences in what they're communicating. Form follows content.

## Scale and Format

Series design often involves a single format repeated across all pieces. This creates the strongest family resemblance. But series can also use consistent proportions at different sizes — a series of prints at A4, A3, and A2 that share the same compositional system but work at their respective scales.

Mixed-format series are harder to make feel cohesive, but they can work if the compositional system is strong enough to carry across the format differences.

## The Series as Installation

When poster series are displayed together — as a gallery installation or an event display — the arrangement of the posters becomes a design decision. Series designed for installation should be considered as a group composition as well as individual pieces. How do adjacent posters relate? Does the series read as a sequence or as a field?

I design series with installation in mind, thinking about which pieces should be adjacent, how the colors interact across the group, and what reading order (if any) the arrangement implies.
