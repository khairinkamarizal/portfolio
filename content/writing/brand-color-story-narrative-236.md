## Colors need a reason

A brand palette of five colors selected because they look good together is not a color strategy — it's a mood board. A color strategy connects each choice to a reason: what this color communicates, why it belongs to this brand specifically, and how it relates to the other colors in the system.

When I present color to a client, I tell the story of the palette before I show it. The story makes the colors legible as decisions rather than aesthetics.

## Anchoring in meaning

Every palette I build has at least one color anchored in genuine brand meaning. Not "blue feels trustworthy" (generic), but "this specific blue references the color of the ocean at the latitude of this brand's founding city, and the brand's entire identity is built around the idea of depth and navigation" (specific and ownable).

The level of specificity in the meaning determines whether the color is ownable or generic. Any brand can claim "blue = trust." Almost no brand can claim a particular warm cobalt that references a specific geographic or cultural association tied to their founding story.

## The palette as a complete argument

When I lay out a brand palette, I'm making an argument: these colors belong together because of a shared quality — a shared temperature, a shared level of saturation, a shared cultural reference point. The argument should be articulable in a sentence.

"Warm amber, cream, and deep forest green — the palette of a working farm at harvest time, communicating craft, abundance, and rootedness." That's a coherent argument. "Navy, orange, and lime green" is not an argument — it's a list.

## Proportion and usage hierarchy

A color palette without usage proportion is incomplete. Having six brand colors and treating them all as equally usable produces chaos in application. I specify the approximate proportion of each color in a typical layout: the primary color covering 60-70% of surfaces, the secondary covering 20-30%, accents covering 5-10%.

These proportions don't need to be followed mathematically — they establish the visual weight hierarchy of the palette. Primary colors are backgrounds and dominant surfaces. Secondary colors are major UI elements, supporting surfaces. Accents are callouts, highlights, interactive elements.

## Seasonal and contextual flexibility

Some brands need their color system to flex across different communication contexts — a brand that runs bold seasonal campaigns can shift the emphasis within the palette to signal a new season without changing the palette itself.

I build this flexibility in deliberately. If the core palette is warm amber, cream, and forest green, a summer campaign might lean amber and cream. A winter campaign might lean into forest green and a deeper navy that lives at the edge of the palette. The brand is still recognizable; the mood shifts.

## Protecting the primary color

Whatever the primary brand color is, it needs protection in the guidelines. "Never use [primary color] as a background for red type" is a real guideline. "This color should never appear smaller than 20% of a given surface" is a real guideline. These specific prohibitions are what keep a color valuable as a brand asset rather than letting it become generic through overuse or misuse.
