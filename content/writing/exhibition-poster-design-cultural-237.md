## Cultural institutions are not commercial clients

Designing posters for museums, galleries, and cultural institutions is different work from commercial poster design, and the differences go deeper than aesthetic sensibility. The communication goals, the audience relationship, the institutional constraints, and the design vocabulary are all distinct.

When I started doing cultural institution work, I made the mistake of bringing a commercial poster mindset to it. The client wanted something different — not more conservative, not less creative, but differently directed.

## The content is the argument

In commercial poster design, the content (event name, date, location) is in service of a persuasive goal: get people to attend, buy, act. In cultural institution work, the content itself often has intrinsic value. An exhibition poster isn't just advertising the show — it's a piece of communication that represents the institution's intellectual and aesthetic position.

The best exhibition posters I've seen function as mini-statements of critical intent. The design choices — typeface, color, image selection, compositional approach — embody something about the exhibition's argument. An exhibition about brutalist architecture might have a poster that uses brutal, uncompromising typography and spatial organization. An exhibition about textile arts might have a poster that uses pattern and layering as compositional strategies.

## Working with institutional constraints

Cultural institutions often have significant existing design systems that new work needs to respect. A museum with a decades-long typographic tradition, a gallery with a strict masthead placement policy, a cultural program with a consistent visual identity across multiple years of programming.

These constraints are design material, not obstacles. The challenge is making genuinely creative work within a framework that was established before this project. I treat the institution's visual conventions the way I'd treat a typeface's proportional system — as rules that give the work structure while leaving room for expression.

## The image question

Many exhibition posters face a specific challenge: the images available are either artworks from the collection (which may have licensing and reproduction constraints) or generic photography that could belong to any exhibition. Finding a third option — a design approach that communicates the exhibition without being either reproductive or generic — is often where the most creative work happens.

I've produced strong exhibition posters that use no photography at all — purely typographic posters where the composition and scale of the type communicates the exhibition's character more directly than any image could.

## Longevity as a design quality

Exhibition posters often need to work across the full run of the exhibition — weeks or months of display. A design that feels fresh on opening day should still feel considered three months later.

This longevity requirement pushes me away from design choices that feel very of-the-moment — trendy type treatments, fashionable color palettes — toward more considered, structurally sound design decisions. The best exhibition posters I've made aged well precisely because they were grounded in fundamental design thinking rather than stylistic trend.
