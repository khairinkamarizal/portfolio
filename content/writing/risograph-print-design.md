Risograph printing sits in an interesting space between screen printing and photocopying. It uses soy-based inks, produces prints with a distinctive texture and slight misregistration, and is limited to spot colors that interact with each other in unexpected ways when overprinted.

It's also one of the most creatively interesting print processes I've worked with, precisely because its constraints are so specific and so unforgiving.

## Spot Colors With Personality

Risograph inks have a character that CMYK offset printing doesn't. Fluorescent pink, deep blue, yellow — these colors are vivid and specific in ways that feel different from their CMYK equivalents. And they overprint in ways that can't be perfectly predicted: blue over yellow becomes a particular kind of green that varies from printer to printer and from paper to paper.

Designing for riso means designing with the overprinted colors, not just the spot colors. The intersections are part of the palette. The unexpected color that emerges from two overlapping inks is a feature, not a bug.

## Registration as Character

Risograph prints with natural slight misregistration. The two colors won't align perfectly — they'll be offset by a fraction of a millimeter, and the amount varies. This used to frustrate designers trained on offset precision. I've learned to design for it.

That means avoiding designs where exact registration is critical. It means building in trapping — extending color areas slightly beyond their logical boundary so misregistration produces a visible overlap rather than a visible gap. And it means choosing compositions where a slight color offset between layers is acceptable or even desirable.

## Paper Selection

Paper affects riso printing significantly. Absorbent uncoated papers — newsprint, colored stock, textured papers — produce distinct results from smoother papers. The ink soaks in more on absorbent stock, which reduces the sheen but increases bleed. Smooth papers keep the ink on the surface, which looks brighter but can crack or smear.

I always specify paper alongside the ink colors for riso projects. The paper is as much a design decision as the ink palette.

## File Preparation

Riso files are prepared as separate black-only files for each color layer. The printer's operator loads a different ink drum for each layer and runs the sheets through once per color.

This means the design file needs to be built in layers — each ink color as a completely separate document. No multi-channel files, no Pantone swatches in an otherwise RGB document. Clean, unambiguous, layer-per-color preparation.

## The Aesthetic Is the Argument

Risograph printing has a specific aesthetic — textured, slightly imperfect, with vivid spot colors and characteristic overprints. That aesthetic carries cultural associations: independent publishing, zine culture, small-batch creative production, handmade quality.

Using riso printing is choosing that aesthetic and those associations. It's not appropriate for a corporate annual report, but it's ideal for a music festival program, an independent booklet, or a limited-edition poster. The process and the communication should be aligned.
