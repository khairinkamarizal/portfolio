## Design That Exists in Space

Environmental graphic design is fundamentally different from print or screen design because it exists in physical space alongside real people moving through real environments. The viewer is not stationary, not necessarily facing the work, not controlling their viewing distance or angle.

This changes almost everything about how you design — scale, contrast, information hierarchy, material choice.

## The Moving Viewer

In an exhibition or wayfinding context, the viewer is typically in motion. They're walking past a panel, approaching a sign, scanning an environment for orientation cues. The design has to work at multiple viewing distances, often simultaneously.

I think about environmental graphics in terms of three reading distances:

**Approach distance** (10+ meters): only the most dominant visual element should communicate. Shape, color, possibly a single word.

**Mid distance** (2-5 meters): primary information reads clearly. Headline, section title, primary navigation.

**Close reading distance** (under 1 meter): full detail available for viewers who choose to engage more deeply.

Designing without these distinctions produces panels that try to communicate everything at all distances and succeed at none.

## Type Size for Environmental Work

Type sizes for environmental applications follow completely different logic from print. For signage read at 5 meters, the standard recommendation is approximately 25mm of cap height per meter of viewing distance — so signage read at 5 meters needs cap heights of at least 125mm.

These are minimums, not targets. High-traffic environments, dim lighting, and viewers with visual impairments all argue for larger type than the minimum.

## Material and Production Integration

Environmental graphics exist on surfaces — walls, floors, windows, dimensional objects. The material isn't separate from the design; it's part of it. Vinyl applied to a painted wall behaves differently from the same design printed on aluminum composite. Dimensional letters in illuminated acrylic read differently than the same forms in painted wood.

I design environmental graphics in conversation with the fabricator rather than handing over a file and hoping for the best. The fabricator's knowledge of what materials can achieve — and what they can't — shapes my design decisions.

## Wayfinding as Systems Design

When I'm working on wayfinding systems rather than individual panels, the logic shifts from composition to system design. Every sign needs to follow consistent rules: type hierarchy, arrow style and position, color coding for different zones. The individual signs are less important than the system they belong to.

The test of a good wayfinding system: a new visitor can navigate the environment without asking for help. If they can, the system works. If they can't, something is wrong — usually either missing signs at decision points, or inconsistent information logic that confuses rather than guides.
