## The Handoff Problem

I've seen good identity work destroyed by bad handoff. A thoughtful, well-constructed brand identity — careful type pairings, a considered color palette, a mark with real geometric logic — handed off as a PDF of logos and a hex code. Six months later the brand looks nothing like what was designed.

The problem isn't the designers who inherited the system. They didn't have the information they needed to make good decisions. That's on the designer who built it.

## What a Real Identity System Contains

When I deliver a brand identity, the guidelines document includes:

**Logo usage**: the full mark, alternate lockups, minimum sizes, clear space rules, approved color versions, and explicit examples of incorrect usage. Not just "don't stretch the logo" — actual examples of the distortions I've seen happen.

**Color system**: primary palette with Pantone, CMYK, RGB, and hex. Secondary palette with the same. Neutrals. Specific guidance on which colors go on which backgrounds. Contrast ratios for digital accessibility.

**Typography**: the primary typeface with licensing information, fallback fonts for when the primary isn't available, size hierarchy recommendations, and line spacing guidance. Examples of correct and incorrect type treatments.

**Graphic elements**: any patterns, textures, icons, or supporting graphics that are part of the system, with usage guidance.

**Photography or illustration style**: if the brand has a defined visual style for imagery, document it. Reference images help enormously.

**Tone and examples**: one or two pages showing the identity in realistic applications — a business card, a social post, a letterhead. Something that shows the system in use, not just as abstract components.

## Making Guidelines That Get Used

Long brand guidelines don't get read. I've seen 120-page brand books that sit on a server and no one opens them. I aim for something thorough enough to be useful and short enough to be consulted.

The sweet spot for most small-to-midsize brands is 20–30 pages. Focused, specific, full of examples. I also increasingly deliver a one-page "quick reference" that covers the most critical rules — logo versions, core colors, primary typeface — for people who just need the essentials.

## Editable vs. Locked Assets

I deliver both locked files (PDFs, locked Illustrator files) and editable source files for in-house designers who'll need to work with the system. I annotate the editable files with notes. The logo Illustrator file includes a layer with construction lines showing how the mark was built, so anyone who needs to redraw or scale it has the underlying logic.

## The Living Document

Brand identities evolve. New applications come up that weren't anticipated at launch. Good guidelines have a version number and a "last updated" date. I make it easy for the client to know they're looking at the current version. And when I do brand work that requires updating the guidelines, I always update them — never let the document drift from the reality of how the brand is actually being applied.
