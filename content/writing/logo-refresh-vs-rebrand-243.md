## Starting over is almost never necessary

When a client comes to me saying their brand needs work, the first question I ask is not "what should the new brand look like?" It's "what problem are we actually solving?" The answer to that question determines whether the right solution is a refresh, a refinement, or a full rebrand — and starting over is less often the right answer than clients (and designers) usually assume.

A full rebrand is the nuclear option. It abandons existing brand equity, requires updating every touchpoint, and creates a period of brand confusion while the market learns the new identity. It's sometimes the right answer. But it's the right answer less often than its expense and difficulty would suggest it should be.

## What a refresh accomplishes

A logo refresh is an evolution, not a replacement. It modernizes the mark while preserving its essential character. The shapes, proportions, and color relationships that make the existing mark recognizable are preserved; the execution is updated for contemporary standards of type quality, digital reproduction, and visual refinement.

A well-executed refresh produces a mark that longtime customers recognize immediately — it feels familiar and improved simultaneously. The best refreshes are ones where, when you put old and new side by side, the improvement is obvious, but the relationship between them is undeniable.

## When to refresh vs. rebrand

I recommend a refresh when the brand has meaningful equity — when customers recognize and have positive associations with the existing visual identity — and the problem is execution quality or dated aesthetics rather than a fundamental positioning problem.

Signs that a refresh is appropriate: the brand has operated continuously for more than five years and has customer recognition. The existing mark has the right conceptual approach but poor execution quality. The brand is moving to new digital applications (app icons, animated logos) that the original mark doesn't support well.

I recommend a full rebrand when the problem is strategic, not aesthetic. When the brand has the wrong positioning for the market it's in. When a business has fundamentally changed its offering and the existing identity actively misrepresents it. When a brand has significant negative associations that the visual identity is now inseparable from. Cosmetically improving a mark that stands for the wrong thing doesn't fix the underlying problem.

## The middle path: brand evolution

Between a subtle refresh and a full rebrand is the brand evolution — a significant redesign that preserves strategic continuity while making substantial visual changes. This is appropriate when the brand is sound strategically but the visual identity has become so dated or inconsistent that a minor refresh can't fix it.

A brand evolution replaces the mark and updates the visual system while maintaining the brand name, positioning, and core brand narrative. The visual discontinuity is acknowledged and embraced: "we've grown, our identity reflects that growth."

## Making the recommendation honestly

My job is to recommend the right level of intervention for the actual problem, not to recommend the option that generates the largest project fee. A refresh that solves the problem is better for the client than a rebrand that doesn't. I've turned down rebrand projects and recommended refreshes. The trust that comes from that honesty generates more work over time than the short-term project revenue would have.
