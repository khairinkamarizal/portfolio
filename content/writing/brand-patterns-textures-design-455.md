Brand patterns are one of the most versatile elements in a visual identity system, and one of the most frequently done badly. A pattern that feels disconnected from the rest of the identity creates visual noise rather than visual coherence. A pattern that's derived directly from the brand's core visual logic becomes an extension of the identity that works everywhere from packaging to apparel to digital backgrounds.

## Where Patterns Come From

The patterns that work best in brand systems are those with a direct genealogical connection to the mark or the brand's design language. The Louis Vuitton monogram canvas. Burberry's tartan. The Hermès saddle stitch pattern. Each is derived from something intrinsic to the brand's history or craft.

I derive brand patterns from three sources:

**Logo geometry.** The shapes and proportions that make up the mark can be extracted, repeated, and recombined into pattern systems. A mark built from intersecting circles can generate a pattern from those circles. A mark built from diagonal strokes can generate a chevron or diamond lattice.

**Brand values expressed visually.** A brand about craft might have a pattern that references material textures — woven fabric, cross-hatching, hammered metal. A brand about precision might have a pattern built from fine grid lines or technical drawing conventions.

**Cultural or category references.** Brands with strong cultural or geographic identities can reference those in pattern development. An Irish whiskey brand might reference Celtic knotwork. A Japanese cosmetics brand might reference traditional textile patterns. This requires cultural research and sensitivity, but the connection to heritage can be powerful.

## Geometric Pattern Construction

For most brand pattern work, I construct patterns using the same geometric logic as the mark. This creates a pattern that feels like it belongs to the same family as the logo without literally repeating it.

The process: take the primary geometric element from the mark construction. Repeat it at a consistent spacing. Explore different scales — tight repeats for subtle texture effects, open repeats for more graphic surface design. Explore rotation and alternation.

Test the pattern at three scales: tight (as texture), medium (as decoration), large (as expressive surface). A well-constructed brand pattern works at all three scales.

## Color Application

Patterns can be deployed in multiple color versions to serve different contexts. A monochrome version in the primary brand color on white for clean applications. An inverted version (white on primary color) for dark backgrounds. A multicolor version using the full palette for expressive applications. A knockout version in a single neutral for watermarked or embossed use.

Specifying these variations in the brand guidelines prevents ad-hoc color decisions that fragment the pattern's coherence.

## Where Patterns Work

The strongest use cases for brand patterns:

**Packaging interiors.** The inside of a box or envelope that reveals the pattern when opened. The pattern isn't the primary communication surface, so it doesn't compete with the logo.

**Liners and substrates.** Shopping bag interiors, gift tissue paper, folder inserts. Again, secondary surfaces where the pattern adds delight without competing with primary brand communication.

**Apparel and merchandise.** A pattern that works on fabric or as a repeat print opens up licensing and merchandise opportunities.

**Digital backgrounds and textures.** Social media background textures, presentation slide patterns, website section backgrounds. Used at low opacity or reduced scale, they add visual richness without overwhelming content.

## What to Avoid

Patterns that look like clipart collections: a series of brand-related icons arranged in a grid. These usually look cheap and feel arbitrary. A real pattern has a structural logic, not just a thematic one.

Patterns that don't relate to the mark. Adding a geometric pattern to a brand because patterns are popular this year, without any connection to the identity, creates visual confusion. Every element in the system should earn its place by strengthening the whole.
