## Six Seconds to Communicate Something

The average social media video gets about three seconds of attention before the viewer scrolls. If you haven't communicated something meaningful in those three seconds, you've lost them.

This is a radically different creative brief than a broadcast commercial or a brand film. The entire structure of the piece has to be rethought from the ground up for the medium.

## Design for Muted First

A significant percentage of social video is watched without sound. Autoplay is muted on most platforms by default. This means any motion piece that depends on audio to communicate — a voiceover, a music bed that sets the tone — is delivering a degraded experience to a large portion of its audience.

I design every social motion piece to communicate completely without audio. Sound enhances; it doesn't carry.

This means visual hierarchy has to do all the work: text that's legible, motion that's meaningful, a sequence that tells a complete story in images and type alone.

## The First Frame as a Poster

The first frame of a social video functions like a thumbnail — it's the static image that represents the piece before it plays. In many cases, it's the only frame some viewers see.

I design the first frame as a poster. It should communicate independently. The hierarchy should be clear. The visual impression should be compelling enough to make someone want to watch.

If the first frame works as a poster, the video has a much better chance of getting watched.

## Short-Form Structure

For 6–15 second pieces, I use a simple three-act structure: hook, message, brand.

The hook is the first two seconds: something visually arresting that stops the scroll. This might be unexpected motion, high contrast, or a provocative visual question.

The message is the middle section: the content itself, delivered as efficiently as possible.

The brand is the final moment: the logo lockup, the brand color, the identity that claims the piece.

Three acts, tight execution.

## Text Legibility at Mobile Size

Most social video is consumed on phones held vertically. The effective viewing area for important content is relatively small. Text needs to be set at sizes that are legible at that scale — which means larger than seems necessary when designing on a desktop monitor.

I test at actual phone size regularly during development. What looks fine at 100% on a 27-inch monitor can be completely unreadable on a phone at arm's length.

## Motion That Earns Attention

In a muted, short-form context, motion does specific work: it creates visual interest, it communicates energy, and it directs the eye.

The motion should feel motivated — it should be doing something, not just moving for the sake of moving. Purposeless motion reads as noise. Motivated motion reads as communication.

I ask of every animated element: why is this moving? What does the motion add? If the answer is "nothing," the element should either be static or removed.

## Looping as a Feature

Many social platforms loop short videos. If a piece is designed to loop seamlessly, the loop becomes an advantage: the viewer watches more than once without noticing, which deepens the impression.

Designing for loop means ending in approximately the same visual state as the beginning, or creating a loop point where the end transitions naturally into the start. The extra design effort is small and the benefit is real.

Design for the scroll. Design for mute. Design for three seconds. The constraints are real — work with them.
