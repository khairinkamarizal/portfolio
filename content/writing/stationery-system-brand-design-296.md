## Stationery is the brand in formal mode

A brand's stationery system — letterhead, envelope, and business card — is the brand identity in its most formal, considered expression. These objects are produced in relatively small quantities, handled personally, and associated with the most significant business communications: proposals, contracts, formal correspondence.

The quality signals in stationery matter enormously. A business card printed on flimsy stock with digital printing communicates something very different from a card printed on 600gsm cotton stock with letterpress. Both carry the same logo; only one carries the brand's quality intention.

## The system logic

A stationery system works through consistent application of a small set of design decisions: the logo position and size, the color palette, the typeface, the spacing system. These decisions should be identical across all three pieces — letterhead, envelope, business card — creating an unmistakable family.

The variation within the system comes from format, not from design. A business card is a different shape from a letterhead; the design adapts to the format while maintaining the visual logic.

## Letterhead: the balance between function and presence

Letterhead has a specific functional challenge: the design must accommodate the body of a letter without competing with it. The brand elements — logo, address, contact information — are typically confined to the header and footer, leaving the center of the page as a neutral field for content.

The header treatment is the primary design moment on letterhead. I give it real attention: the logo at the right scale for the format, the contact information in a typographic treatment that's refined without being elaborate, the spatial relationship between the logo and the page edge carefully considered.

## Envelope: design that works twice

An envelope is seen twice: when received, and when opened. Both moments are design opportunities. The front of the envelope — with the return address and the recipient area — is the primary face. The back closure and any interior liner are secondary but worth attention on premium stationery.

Return address placement is a key decision. Positioned at the top-left is the conventional approach — expected, functional. Positioned on the back flap is a more distinctive choice that gives the front face more visual freedom.

## Business card: the portable brand touchpoint

The business card operates under the most severe space constraint of the three pieces: 3.5 by 2 inches to carry brand identity, personal identity (name, title), and contact information. Every millimeter matters.

I design business cards by establishing the hierarchy first — mark, name, essential contact — then designing the layout to express that hierarchy as clearly and elegantly as possible within the format constraints. Production specifications (stock, printing method, finish) are decided alongside the design, not after.

## Cohesion across the system

The stationery system should feel like a matched set — placed together on a desk, they should clearly belong to each other. Achieving this cohesion requires consistent application of design decisions and vigilance against the small inconsistencies that accumulate when pieces are designed at different times or by different people. The system logic documented in the brand guidelines is what maintains cohesion over time.
