## The Startup Reality

Startup clients are different from established business clients in one important way: the timeline. They're launching in six weeks, not six months. They need a mark, a palette, and enough of an identity to build a website and pitch deck — now.

This is a real constraint and I take it seriously. But fast doesn't have to mean poor quality. It means being selective about what you invest time in and what you defer.

## What Doesn't Change Under Time Pressure

The brief. Regardless of timeline, I always do a discovery conversation and write up the key strategic information: who is this for, what does it need to communicate, what are the key competitors. This takes two hours and saves many more.

Single-color testing. Every mark I produce gets tested in black, white, and grayscale before delivery. This takes twenty minutes and catches problems that are expensive to fix after launch.

Scalability testing. The mark gets checked at favicon scale before it's called done. Also twenty minutes.

These aren't optional quality steps I can defer. They're the minimum that makes the work professional rather than amateur.

## What Can Move Faster

For startup work, I typically:

- Present two concepts rather than three, having identified the stronger strategic direction in discovery
- Provide a streamlined identity system rather than a comprehensive brand guidelines document — key colors, the primary typeface, logo usage rules on two pages
- Use typefaces from the standard Google Fonts or Adobe Fonts library rather than custom type, deferring custom lettering to a later stage when the brand has more runway
- Deliver fewer logo lockup variations — primary, horizontal, and stacked are sufficient to launch

## The "Good Enough to Launch" Standard

There's a real difference between "good enough to launch" and "permanent." Startup brands often evolve significantly in the first two to three years as the business finds its footing. The identity I deliver for a pre-launch startup is designed to be a solid foundation that can grow, not a final answer.

I tell clients this honestly: what we're building today is high-quality and appropriate for launch, and in two years when you know more about who you are, we should revisit it. This expectation-setting prevents both over-engineering at launch and disappointment when they outgrow the initial work.

## Protecting the Essentials

The one thing I won't compromise regardless of timeline: the mark needs to be original. Not a modified free icon, not something that could be confused with an existing brand in the category, not generated from a template.

A startup that launches with a generic-looking or derivative mark has a harder time building recognition. The time investment in an original mark is small relative to the cost of rebranding after launch when the company is bigger.
