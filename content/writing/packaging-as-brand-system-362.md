Packaging is often the most high-stakes application of a brand identity. It's the physical object the customer interacts with directly. It lives in their home. It's seen repeatedly. It sits on shelves next to competitors. When packaging works as brand design — not just as container design — it does more to build brand equity than almost any other touchpoint.

## The Shelf Impact Test

Before I design a single element of packaging, I ask: what does this need to communicate in 0.5 seconds from three feet away? That's the shelf impact question, and it should drive the primary design decisions.

On a retail shelf, packaging competes for attention in a dense visual environment. The design that wins that attention usually does so through one or two clear signals: a distinctive color, a strong typographic statement, a singular graphic element. Packaging that tries to communicate everything at once communicates nothing at distance.

## The Information Hierarchy

After the shelf impact, there's the pickup moment — when the customer picks up the package and examines it. This is when the full information hierarchy matters. What's the brand? What's the product? What are the key benefits or features? What are the ingredients or specifications?

I organize this hierarchy not just typographically but architecturally — front panel, back panel, side panels each serve a role in the information flow. The front panel owns brand and product identity. The back panel owns detailed information. Side panels own secondary features and supporting claims.

## Consistency Across a Product Line

For brands with multiple SKUs, the packaging system has to maintain consistency across the range while differentiating individual products. Color is usually the differentiator — each product or flavor or variant owns a color within the brand's palette. The structural elements (logo placement, typography system, grid) remain consistent across the line.

This approach creates what I call "range recognition" — the ability to see any product in the line and immediately identify both the brand and the variant. Neither piece of information should require decoding.

## Print Constraints on Packaging

Packaging is often printed on unusual substrates and with unusual processes. Flexible packaging, cartons, labels, aluminum, glass — each substrate has different print characteristics, different lighting behavior, and different handling challenges.

Foil, embossing, varnishes, and spot UV are common packaging embellishments that can elevate a design significantly. I work with packaging printers early in the process to understand what's achievable within the budget, rather than designing effects that have to be removed during production.

## The Unboxing Experience

For premium products, the packaging isn't just the outside. The interior surfaces, the tissue or wrap, the instruction card — all of these are brand touchpoints. The unboxing experience is increasingly a brand moment that gets shared, photographed, and discussed.

I design unboxing experiences by mapping every surface the customer will see during the opening sequence, then applying the brand system consistently across all of them.
