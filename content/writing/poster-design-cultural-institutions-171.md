## The Cultural Institution Context

Designing posters for museums, galleries, theaters, and arts organizations is a specific practice with its own constraints and conventions. These institutions carry institutional identities that have accumulated meaning over time. The design work needs to honor that accumulated meaning while communicating something specific about the current program.

That balance is harder than it sounds.

## The Identity vs. Expression Problem

A strong institutional identity — a well-established logo, a recognized color palette, a defined typographic system — can feel like a cage in poster work. The identity applies consistency; the poster needs to communicate the individual character of a specific exhibition, performance, or program.

The resolution is usually in understanding what's fixed and what's flexible. The institutional mark and its placement may be fixed. The color, the image, the typographic treatment may be flexible within certain parameters. Working within those parameters creatively is the skill.

## Art Direction for Exhibition Posters

Exhibition posters have a specific challenge: representing visual art through a designed artifact. The work being exhibited is the hero, but the poster isn't a catalogue page — it's a communication tool that needs to attract attention, communicate the exhibition's character, and meet the institution's identity requirements simultaneously.

The relationship between the artwork, the typographic information, and the institutional identity needs to be worked out deliberately for each exhibition. There's no one-size-fits-all approach.

## Approval Processes and Multiple Stakeholders

Institutional poster design typically involves multiple approval stakeholders: the institution's communications team, curatorial staff with views about how the art should be represented, the artist in some cases, and the marketing team focused on audience conversion.

Managing these stakeholders' often-conflicting needs is a real part of the project. Clarity about who has final approval on what — design decisions versus content decisions — prevents circular revision processes.

## The Series Problem

Many cultural institutions produce programs in series — seasons, recurring festivals, ongoing exhibition programming. The series needs visual coherence while individual pieces need individual character.

A flexible system template — consistent structure with variable content — is often the solution. The system holds the series together; the variable elements give each piece its own character.

## The Archival Dimension

Cultural institution posters often end up in institutional archives, gift shops, and collector collections. They're expected to function as objects of value beyond their immediate purpose.

I bring extra craft attention to cultural institution work because of this archival dimension. The work should hold up as an artifact, not just as communication.
