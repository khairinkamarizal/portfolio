## The Education Hidden in Old Posters

I have a folder on my hard drive with over 400 scanned vintage travel posters. Cassandre, Carlu, the Swiss Federal Railways series, the British Transport Commission work, the WPA national park posters. I return to this folder regularly, not out of nostalgia but for the same reason a sculptor studies anatomy — to understand structure.

These posters solved hard problems with limited tools. No digital type manipulation. No stock photography. No opacity controls. Color was expensive — every additional color meant additional cost. The constraints forced a discipline that produced some of the most effective poster work ever made.

## What Flat Color Teaches You

Most vintage travel posters use flat, limited color — four to six colors, sometimes fewer. Each color fills a region solidly, with no gradients, no blending, no texture effects.

This limitation created a discipline I try to recreate deliberately: every color has to carry its own weight. You can't use gradients to smooth over a composition that isn't working. You can't add a subtle texture to make a flat area more interesting. If the flat color composition works, it works on its own terms.

When I design posters today, I sometimes constrain myself to five flat colors before I'm allowed to introduce any other treatments. The constraint is artificial, but the results are often cleaner and more decisive than work I do with no constraints.

## Shape-Based Composition

Without photographic complexity, vintage travel posters composed with shapes. Mountains were simple triangles with color variation to suggest layers. Figures were flat silhouettes. Architecture was reduced to geometric solids.

This shape-based approach created compositions that read immediately and from a distance — both essential properties for work that was designed to be seen from a moving train or a busy street.

I practice this by sketching poster compositions as flat shape studies before adding any typographic detail. If the shapes don't tell the story, the type won't save it.

## The Proportion of Sky

Something I noticed studying these posters: they use sky — open, light, unobscured sky — as a compositional element. The sky isn't background, it's space. It breathes. It creates contrast against the landscape or architectural forms. It gives the eye somewhere to rest before returning to the main subject.

Modern design often feels airless because there's no sky — no open space performing that function. I've started thinking about every poster's "sky equivalent": the breathing room that gives the composition room to expand.

## Color as Place

The travel poster designers understood that color could evoke a place before any image did. The warm ochres and terracottas of Mediterranean work. The cool blues and greens of Scandinavian railway posters. The desert neutrals of American national park posters.

These weren't arbitrary palettes. They were carefully distilled impressions of what a place felt like at a sensory level. I try to do the same thing when designing work that needs to evoke a specific place, time, or atmosphere. What is the color distillation of this brand's world?

## The Letter That Anchors the Poster

Almost always in great travel poster work, there's a single typographic element — usually the destination name — that anchors the composition. It's not just labeled; it's placed with compositional intent. It creates a horizontal anchor at the bottom, or a counterweight at the top, or a vertical axis along one side.

Typography in these posters is never just labeling. It's structural. The text is part of the composition as a shape, not just as language. That's the lesson I carry into every poster I design.

Study the work that came before. It has more to teach than most contemporary design writing does.
