## Color That Moves

In static design, color is fixed. A brand color is a specific hex value on a specific element, and it stays there. In motion design, color can change, transition, shift — and each of those changes communicates something.

The relationship between color and time is one of the most expressive tools in motion design, and one of the least systematically applied. Most motion designers use color transitions because they look good, not because the transition is communicating something specific.

I've been working on using color change in motion as deliberately as I use color in static work — as a communication tool with intention behind every shift.

## Color Transitions as Narrative

A color transition can mark a change in emotional state, a movement from one phase to another, a shift in the brand's communication register.

I've used warm-to-cool transitions to represent a shift from problem to solution. I've used a palette that starts desaturated and becomes progressively more saturated to represent a brand coming to life. I've used a single accent color appearing for the first time at a moment of emphasis — its arrival is a compositional event because it breaks the established palette.

These uses are only possible if the color system is planned as a temporal system, not just a static palette.

## The Brand Palette in Motion

The challenge with brand color in motion is maintaining brand recognition while allowing the temporal variation that makes motion interesting.

My approach: define which colors can change and which are fixed. The primary brand color is usually fixed — it's the anchor of recognition. Secondary and accent colors have more flexibility to appear, intensify, fade, or shift within the brand's color family.

The motion color system is documented as an extension of the static color system: here are the allowed transitions, here are the temporal behaviors, here is how the brand color enters and exits.

## Color and the Cut

In edited video content, color consistency across cuts creates visual continuity. When adjacent shots have significantly different color casts — one warm, one cool — the cut feels jarring because the color shift is unintentional.

I ensure that brand video content has consistent color grading across all footage, tying everything back to the brand's color palette. This doesn't mean everything looks the same — it means the color differences are intentional, serving the narrative, rather than accidental variations in production conditions.

## The Transition Palette

I build what I call a transition palette for motion projects: the set of intermediate colors that appear during transitions between states. These are the colors the brand passes through, not where it lives — but they need to be consistent with the brand's world.

A brand whose palette is warm and earthy should pass through warm and earthy transitions, not cold blues. The transitions are part of the brand's color identity even though they're transient.

## When to Break the Palette

Sometimes the most effective motion moment is a deliberate departure from the brand palette — a moment of contrast that creates emphasis before returning to the established colors.

I use this technique sparingly: a brief flash of a complementary color at a moment of impact, a desaturation followed by re-saturation to create drama. The departure is only effective if the established palette is consistent enough that the departure reads as significant.

Color in motion rewards the same discipline as color in print. Plan it. Own it. Make it mean something.
