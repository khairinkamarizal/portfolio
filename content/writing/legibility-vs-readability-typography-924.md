## Two Different Problems

Legibility and readability are often used interchangeably, but they describe different things. Legibility is the ability to distinguish one letter from another — it's a property of individual letterforms. Readability is the ease of reading extended text — it's a property of how type is set.

A typeface can be highly legible but poorly readable. Large, clear letterforms set in a very long line measure with tight leading and insufficient contrast against the background will be legible but tiring to read. Conversely, a typeface set in an ideal text environment can support good readability even if its individual letterforms are somewhat unusual.

Understanding this distinction changes how I approach every typography problem.

## When Legibility Is the Priority

Signage, wayfinding, labels, and very small-size applications prioritize legibility above all else. The viewer has a fraction of a second to identify each character, and the reading environment may be far from ideal — movement, glare, distance.

For these applications, I choose typefaces designed specifically for high legibility: open apertures, clear differentiation between similar letterforms (I, l, 1), generous x-heights, and consistent stroke widths. Many humanist sans-serif typefaces excel here. I also increase letter-spacing beyond normal to give each character more visual isolation.

Decorative or display typefaces that have personality but sacrifice legibility have no place in these applications. The consequences of a misread sign can be serious.

## When Readability Is the Priority

Long-form print — books, reports, articles, brochures with substantial text content — prioritizes readability. The reader is engaged for minutes or hours, not seconds. Fatigue resistance matters. The typeface needs to sustain reading without the reader becoming aware of the effort.

Readability is primarily determined by how type is set, not just which typeface is chosen. Line length (measure), line spacing (leading), paragraph spacing, and contrast between type and background all have as much impact as the typeface itself.

My default settings for comfortable long-form reading: 55–75 characters per line, leading at 120–145% of type size, a serif or humanist sans at 10–12pt, and a contrast ratio between type and background of at least 7:1.

## Designing With Deliberate Illegibility

Not all graphic design needs to be read comfortably. Sometimes the goal is to create visual texture, to imply text without requiring it to be read, or to create a sense of density and information even when the specific content is secondary.

Deliberately illegible type — set extremely small, overlapping, heavily tracked, or in low contrast — creates visual texture. I use this in poster design as a background element, in brand materials as a pattern, and in motion graphics as a secondary layer that adds visual complexity without competing with the readable foreground content.

The key is intent. Deliberately difficult-to-read type that serves a compositional purpose is a design decision. Accidentally difficult-to-read type that was supposed to communicate is a failure.

## Testing in Context

I test legibility by viewing type at the actual conditions of use — actual size, actual viewing distance, actual environmental contrast. Testing poster type on a calibrated monitor at full zoom tells me almost nothing about how it reads on a wall.

I test readability by actually reading. Not skimming, not checking that characters are distinguishable — sitting down and reading several paragraphs at normal speed. If I notice myself working, if my eyes feel strained, if I lose my place frequently, the readability needs improvement.

These are experiential tests, not metric tests. The numbers — tracking value, leading ratio — are starting points. The reading experience is the truth.
