"I don't know, I just don't love it" is not a design problem. It's a communication problem.

Client feedback during logo presentations ranges from specific and useful to vague and emotional. My job in the room is not to defend the work — it's to translate the emotional reaction into actionable information.

## The Common Feedback Categories

Over enough logo presentations, client reactions tend to fall into recognizable categories:

**Aesthetic preference:** "It feels too cold" or "I don't like the font." These are often personal reactions to style, not strategic objections. They need probing. Is this a real brand concern, or a personal preference that conflicts with the strategic direction?

**Strategic misalignment:** "This doesn't feel like us." This is valuable feedback. It means the brief wasn't complete, or I misread it, or the client didn't know what they wanted until they saw what they didn't want. All of these require a deeper conversation.

**Competitive concern:** "It looks like someone else." If a client recognizes a competitor in the work, that's a legitimate concern, even if the resemblance isn't obvious to me. Their perception of the competitive landscape is real, regardless of whether the visual similarity is intentional.

**Fear of departure:** "It feels too different from what we have now." Change is uncomfortable. This feedback often means "I'm not sure we're ready to be this bold." It needs a conversation about what the brand is trying to accomplish, not a more conservative design.

## Probing the Reaction

When I receive vague negative feedback, I ask specific questions:

"Which element specifically bothers you — the symbol, the type, the color?"
"If you set aside whether you like it personally, does it communicate the right things for the brand?"
"What does this remind you of? What category does it feel like it belongs to?"

These questions separate personal taste from strategic evaluation. They also help clients articulate things they felt but couldn't name.

## When to Push Back

Some client feedback needs to be challenged, respectfully and directly. If a client wants to add color because "it feels boring" but the brief specified that the mark needed to work in single-color applications, that's a strategic conflict, not just a preference. I explain the constraint and the consequence.

If a client wants to make the mark more complex because "it doesn't look expensive enough," I explain that simplicity is the most durable quality in a logo and that complexity usually reads as insecurity, not sophistication.

## When to Listen

When multiple people in the room express similar discomfort that they can't articulate, I take that seriously. Collective unease often indicates something the design is communicating that wasn't intended. I listen to those reactions as signal, even without a clear diagnosis.

The client is always right that they're uncomfortable. They're not always right about why, or about the solution.
