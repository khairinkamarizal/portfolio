Every wordmark or type-based logo involves a choice: custom lettering drawn from scratch, or an existing typeface modified to varying degrees. This is partly a budget decision, but it's also a strategic and creative decision that shapes what the final mark can be.

## What Custom Lettering Gives You

Custom lettering gives you absolute formal control. Every curve, every weight distribution, every proportional relationship is yours to determine. The result can be completely unique — impossible to replicate by simply installing a font.

Custom lettering also allows forms that no existing typeface has. If you need a letterform that has a specific formal relationship with a logo mark, or that creates a specific character interaction between two letters, custom lettering can deliver exactly that. No off-the-shelf typeface will do it.

The cost is real: a single custom wordmark can run from several thousand dollars to well over ten thousand for a large, complex treatment. And it requires a letterer with not just drawing skill but typographic understanding — the ability to create forms that feel like a coherent typographic system, not a collection of individual letter drawings.

## What Modified Type Gives You

Starting from an existing typeface gives you a foundation with proven letter-spacing logic, established formal relationships between characters, and typographic history. A Futura wordmark with customized terminals and a bespoke ligature is legible, refined, and ownable — while benefiting from all the type design work that went into Futura.

Modified type is also faster and more affordable. The base work is done; you're making targeted modifications. For many organizations, this is the right balance.

The risk is that insufficiently modified type looks like a typeface, not a logo. The threshold for "enough modification" is lower than most designers think. Changing the weight and adding a custom ligature often isn't enough. The modifications need to be comprehensive enough that the mark reads as a designed object, not a font selection.

## The Hybrid Approach

The approach I use most often: start from a typeface that has the right structural qualities, then modify aggressively. Not a few small tweaks — substantial modifications to terminal angles, stroke contrast, spacing, and one or two letterforms that become signature characters.

At this level of modification, the typeface is unrecognizable as the source material. The result is something that has typographic coherence (because it's built on a sound foundation) and genuine uniqueness (because it's been transformed, not just selected).

## Making the Call

My decision framework: if the brief requires a mark that is uniquely, protectably distinctive and the budget supports it, custom lettering. If the budget doesn't support full custom work, start from a strong typeface and modify comprehensively. If the brand will live primarily in digital environments at small sizes, a clearly modified commercial face is usually sufficient. If the brand is on packaging, signage, and physical objects at large scale, the investment in custom lettering is more justified.
