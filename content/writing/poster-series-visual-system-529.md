## The Series Problem

A single poster is a composition problem. A poster series is a system problem. The individual pieces need to stand alone — each one complete and effective in its own right — while also being unmistakably part of a family.

Getting both right simultaneously is harder than it sounds, and most series fail in one direction or the other. Either the pieces feel too similar and repetitive, or they feel so varied that the series concept breaks down.

## Establishing the System First

Before I design a single poster in a series, I define the system: the set of rules that will hold across all pieces.

These rules always include: a consistent color approach (same palette, consistent application logic), a typographic system (same fonts in the same hierarchy), and a layout framework (consistent proportions, consistent placement of series elements like dates, titles, and identifiers).

The rules should be specific enough to create family resemblance, but loose enough that each piece can have its own character. If the rules are too tight, the series feels stamped rather than designed. If they're too loose, the system breaks down.

## What Varies, What Stays Fixed

I think about series design in terms of fixed elements and variable elements.

**Fixed elements** — the things that are the same across every piece. This might be: the placement and size of the series logo, the typeface for all titling, the color of the primary background, the typographic treatment of issue number or date, the margin system.

**Variable elements** — the things that change from piece to piece. This might be: the primary image or illustration, the secondary color accent, the specific layout configuration within the grid.

The more variable elements you allow, the more dynamic the series feels. The more fixed elements you require, the more coherent the family feels. Finding the right balance is the core design decision.

## Photography vs. Illustration Series

Series based on photography have a different challenge than illustration-based series. Photography introduces compositional and tonal variation that illustration can more easily control.

For photography series, I establish strong typographic and color consistency to compensate for the visual variation in the images. The type and color system does the family-building work that the images can't do.

For illustration-based series, I might allow more typographic variation because the illustrations are being produced with consistent style and intent.

## The Test: Strip Away the Variable Element

If I remove the primary image or the dominant variable element from each poster in the series, do the remaining elements look like a coherent system? If yes, the system is working. If not, the fixed elements aren't doing enough.

This test also reveals whether any piece is relying too heavily on its variable element for its identity — which means that piece doesn't belong to the system as strongly as it should.

## Cohesion Across Time

Series often unfold over time — a concert series, a lecture series, a publication series. Each new piece is produced at a different moment, potentially by a different designer.

This is where documentation matters. I write a brief series guide for any multi-piece project: what's fixed, what's variable, what tolerances are allowed, and what constitutes a system violation. This guide makes it possible for the series to continue producing work that belongs to the same family even without the original designer present.

A well-documented series system is a gift to everyone who works on it after you.
