## The Transition Problem

Motion designers are obsessed with transitions in a way that I find hard to justify from a communication standpoint. An enormous amount of production time goes into building complex transition effects that draw attention to themselves rather than serving the movement of content from one state to another.

A transition exists to move the viewer from one scene or state to the next. The best transition is the one the viewer doesn't notice — they simply find themselves in the next scene, absorbed in the new content. A transition that calls attention to itself is, by that measure, a failure.

## The Whip Pan Problem

The whip pan transition — where the camera appears to move rapidly left or right, blurring the current scene into the next — became ubiquitous in social media and YouTube content around 2018. It's remained ubiquitous since, despite the fact that it has become invisible from overuse and contributes nothing to most of the content it appears in.

I avoid it not because it's inherently wrong — there are contexts where a rapid camera-like motion is genuinely expressive — but because its automatic, unthinking application produces transitions that are indistinguishable from thousands of other pieces.

## The Cut Is Often the Answer

The simple cut — one scene ending, the next beginning without any transition effect — is massively underused in motion graphics. It's crisp, it's confident, and it doesn't waste any of the viewer's time.

When I'm building a motion piece and I find myself reaching for a transition effect, I first try a cut. If the cut works, I use the cut. Most of the time, the cut works.

## When Transitions Are Worth Building

Transitions earn their production time when they are:

**Conceptually motivated** — the transition expresses something about the relationship between the two scenes it connects. A morph transition that transforms one form into another is saying something about the relationship between those forms.

**Rhythmically timed** — the transition hits on a musical beat or accent that makes the movement feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.

**Brand-specific** — the transition uses a brand's visual language in a way that makes it proprietary and recognizable. A brand that always reveals new scenes from behind the primary brand color is building a visual signature.

Outside of these cases, I'd rather spend the production time on the content within the scenes than on the moments between them.
