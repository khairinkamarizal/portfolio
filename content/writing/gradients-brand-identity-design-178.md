## The Gradient Problem

Gradients are one of the more contentious elements in contemporary brand design. Used everywhere in the early 2000s, largely abandoned in the flat design era, and now back in various forms — their relationship to brand identity is complicated.

My view: gradients aren't inherently wrong. But most gradient use in branding is wrong.

## Why Gradients Date Quickly

The specific character of a gradient — the hue range it covers, the saturation and brightness of its component colors, the direction and speed of the transition — is heavily influenced by contemporary design tools and aesthetic fashions. What looks current in 2021 will look precisely 2021 in 2031.

This is the core problem with gradient use in marks and primary identity elements: they age in a way that flat color doesn't. The gradient carries a timestamp.

## Where Gradients Actually Work

Gradients work better in secondary identity elements — backgrounds, gradient washes on photography, graphic overlays — where they can be refreshed as part of aesthetic updates without touching the core mark. The mark stays constant; the surrounding system can evolve.

Gradients in the mark itself are a higher-risk choice because the mark is supposed to be permanent. Secondary system elements are expected to evolve.

## Brand Gradients as Color Systems

When gradients are used in branding, the most defensible approach is to define them as systems derived from the brand's color palette — structured rules for how gradient transitions work — rather than as specific visual effects. A color system that specifies gradient behavior creates coherence across applications without locking the brand into one specific visual moment.

## The Practical Constraints

Gradients create practical problems that flat color doesn't: they can't be used in single-color applications (embroidery, laser engraving, stamp production, single-color print), they behave differently on different screens and printing processes, and they require more complex management across the full application range.

Before committing to a gradient as a primary brand element, I walk through every application context and verify the gradient can either be reproduced accurately or replaced with a workable flat-color alternative.

## Making the Call

I use gradients in brand identity when they serve a specific expressive purpose that flat color can't achieve — when the gradient is genuinely right for the brand's character. I don't use them as decoration or because they look contemporary. The aesthetic payoff has to be worth the practical cost.
