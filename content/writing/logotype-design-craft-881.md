## The Logotype Defined

A logotype is a wordmark treated as a complete visual identity — the brand name, typographically designed, serving as the full logo without an accompanying graphic mark. Some of the most recognizable brands in the world use logotypes: Coca-Cola, Google, Sony, Disney, FedEx.

The logotype places everything on typography. There's no mark to carry recognition when the name isn't legible, no symbol to use when space is too small for the full name. The type has to do all of it.

## When a Logotype Is Right

A logotype works best when the brand name has strong recognition and is short enough to function as a visual object. One to three words are the practical range. Beyond three words, the logotype becomes a sentence rather than a visual signature.

It also works best when the brand has — or is building — strong name recognition. A logotype depends on the name carrying the brand, which means the name needs to be worth carrying.

For new brands with no recognition, a combination mark (symbol plus wordmark) is usually more practical because the symbol provides a secondary recognition point that can be used in space-constrained applications.

## The Design Craft

Designing a logotype is entirely a typographic design problem. The work involves:

**Typeface selection or creation**: the type must be distinctive enough to be ownable, appropriate to the brand personality, and capable of being a visual signature rather than just set text.

**Letter modification**: individual letterforms are adjusted to serve the logotype as a whole object. A letterform that's beautiful in a text setting might be improved by modification for logotype use.

**Spacing tuning**: tracking and individual pair spacing are adjusted until the logotype reads as a single cohesive object rather than a sequence of letters.

**Weight and proportion decisions**: sometimes the typeface's default weight is wrong for the logotype; sometimes individual characters benefit from proportion adjustment.

## The Tight Tracking Tendency

Many logotypes benefit from tighter tracking than the typeface's default. Bringing the letters closer together reduces the word's visual footprint and makes it read more as a unified object. But tight tracking can create collision problems with certain letter pairs and can reduce legibility at small sizes.

I set tracking to the tightest value that maintains legibility at the smallest application size, then evaluate whether the logotype reads as a unified object.

## The Scale Range

A logotype must work at favicon scale (where only the overall form and color are perceptible), at business card scale (where letterforms read clearly), and at display scale (where every design decision is visible).

At favicon scale, the logotype reduces to a colored rectangle with some internal texture. If the overall color and proportion are distinctive, this works. At display scale, every modification to the letterforms is visible and must be intentional.

The design decisions need to be right across the full range.
