## The Rollout Gap

Many rebrand projects invest heavily in the design and almost nothing in the implementation plan. The new identity is designed, guidelines are written, files are delivered — and then the organization is left to figure out how to actually transition from old to new.

The result is typically a long, inconsistent transition period where old and new brand elements coexist chaotically, undermining the clarity the rebrand was meant to create.

## Prioritizing the Transition

A rebrand rollout needs a clear priority order based on visibility and cost of change. High-visibility, low-cost-to-change items should be updated immediately: digital properties (website, social profiles, email signatures), presentation templates, document headers. These can typically be changed in days or weeks.

High-visibility, high-cost-to-change items need scheduling: printed collateral, signage, packaging, merchandise. These require lead time and budget, but they're often the most visible brand touchpoints.

## The Hard Stop vs. Gradual Transition

There are two basic rollout models. A hard stop transitions everything simultaneously — all old materials are retired and replaced at once. A gradual transition phases out old materials as stock is depleted and new materials are produced.

The hard stop is cleaner and creates a more definitive brand moment. It also requires pre-producing replacement versions of everything before the launch date. The gradual transition is more practical for organizations with large existing material inventories, but it requires a clear sunset policy for old materials.

## Managing External Stakeholders

A rebrand affects more than internal materials — it affects vendors, partners, and distributors who use the brand's assets. These stakeholders need advance notice, updated asset packages, and clear guidance on the transition timeline.

I always build a stakeholder communication package into rebrand projects: updated brand guidelines formatted for external use, an asset package ready for immediate download, and a clear FAQ about what changes and when.

## The Day Zero Campaign

The most effective rebrand rollouts treat the launch as a communication event, not just an operational change. The announcement of the new identity — with explanation of why it changed and what it represents — generates engagement and media coverage that amplifies the rebrand's impact.

That narrative should be designed as carefully as the visual identity itself.

## Post-Launch Monitoring

After a rebrand launches, I monitor how it's being applied in practice for the first 90 days. Inconsistencies in application, questions about specific use cases, and edge cases not covered by the guidelines all surface during this period.

A brief post-launch review and guidelines update addresses these issues before they become entrenched bad habits.
