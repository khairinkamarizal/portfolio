## Identities Don't Break All at Once

Brand identity degradation is slow and cumulative. It happens one small deviation at a time. Someone uses the wrong blue for a brochure because the correct blue wasn't in the template. A social media post uses a typeface that "looks similar enough." A contractor produces a banner without access to the guidelines.

None of these individual deviations is catastrophic. Together, over two or three years, they produce a brand that looks like several different organizations depending on where you encounter it.

## The Core Cause: Inaccessible Standards

Most brand identity degradation isn't intentional. The people applying the brand aren't trying to break it. They're working with the tools they have, under deadline pressure, without easy access to the standards they're supposed to follow.

The most common cause I find when doing brand audits is that the guidelines are technically available but practically inaccessible. They're a PDF on a shared drive that nobody can navigate. The logo files are in a folder with fifteen different versions and no indication of which is current. The color values aren't pre-loaded in any of the tools people actually use.

Fixing the access problem fixes more brand consistency issues than any amount of policing behavior.

## The Asset Library

The foundation of brand maintenance is a well-organized, easy-to-use asset library. This is a single, authoritative location — typically a shared cloud drive — that contains:

Current logo files in all required formats, clearly labeled with their use case (print, digital, dark background, etc.). No legacy versions. No "logo_final_v3_FINAL.ai" files. Just the current versions.

Color swatches pre-loaded in the formats people actually use. An Adobe Color file, a Figma library, a PowerPoint color theme file.

Approved typefaces with instructions for installation and download links or direct files.

Brand photography organized by subject and tagged for easy search.

Approved templates for the most common brand applications: presentation, email signature, letterhead, social post.

The asset library is a designed system, not a dumping ground.

## Regular Audits

I recommend quarterly brand audits for clients with active communications programs. This isn't a major production — it's a one-hour review of a representative sample of brand touchpoints, checking against the standards.

The audit catches drift before it compounds. A small deviation corrected promptly takes minutes. The same deviation propagated through eighteen months of materials takes weeks to correct.

## Who Owns Brand Consistency

Every brand needs a named owner of brand consistency. Not a committee. One person with the authority to approve brand applications and the responsibility to flag deviations.

In small organizations, this is often the founder or the most senior design-aware person. In larger organizations, it's a brand manager role. In either case, the role needs to be explicitly assigned, not assumed.

Without a named owner, brand consistency exists as everyone's responsibility — which means it's nobody's priority.

## The Training Investment

New team members, contractors, and agency partners need brand onboarding. A thirty-minute orientation to the brand standards, the asset library, and the approval process saves weeks of corrections later.

I build brand onboarding materials into every identity project: a quick-start guide that covers the twenty percent of the guidelines that apply to eighty percent of the use cases. Not the full guidelines document — the quick-start guide that gets someone up to speed in one sitting.

Brand maintenance isn't glamorous. It's also not optional. The return on a well-maintained identity is compound — every consistent touchpoint reinforces the brand value that the original design work built.
