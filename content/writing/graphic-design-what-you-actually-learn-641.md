I mentored a design intern last year. Watching her navigate the gap between what she'd learned in school and what professional practice actually required reminded me of my own early years — the things I had to unlearn as much as the things I had to learn.

## The Biggest Gap: Feedback Culture

Design school critique prepares you for feedback in a controlled, educational context. Professional feedback is different in kind. A client who doesn't like something won't always be able to tell you why. A creative director's feedback may be vague or seemingly contradictory. A stakeholder who approved a direction last week will reverse course this week.

Learning to receive this kind of feedback without defensiveness, to extract useful information from imprecise criticism, and to respond with solutions rather than arguments — this is the most important professional skill in design, and school prepares you for it least.

The reframe that helped me most: feedback is not about the design. It's about the client's relationship to the design problem. When I stopped hearing criticism as an assessment of my work and started hearing it as information about what the client actually needs, the whole dynamic shifted.

## The Technical Gap

Most design school graduates can produce good design in the applications they were taught. What they often can't do is produce production-ready files — print-ready PDFs, properly structured source files, files that another designer can pick up and work in without confusion.

This matters enormously in professional practice. A beautiful design delivered in a file that can't be used downstream isn't complete work. I was embarrassed more than once early in my career by files that failed at prepress or couldn't be handed off correctly.

Learn print production. Learn file organization. Learn naming conventions. These aren't glamorous skills but they mark the difference between junior and senior practice.

## Client Communication

Presenting work is different from presenting work well. Most design school presentations are peer-facing: you're explaining your decisions to people who share your visual language and are evaluating your design reasoning.

Presenting to clients requires translating design decisions into business and communication terms. Not "I chose this typeface because of its humanist warmth" but "this typeface communicates approachability, which supports your goal of making first-time clients feel welcome." The design reasoning is the same; the language is different.

## Scope and Time

School projects have a fixed scope defined by the brief and unlimited iteration time within that scope. Professional projects have a negotiated scope and a fixed budget of time. Learning to work within that constraint — to make good decisions efficiently rather than perfect decisions eventually — is a skill that only comes with practice.

The intern I worked with was producing excellent work but spending three times the appropriate time on it. The next step in her development was learning how to allocate time to the decisions that matter most and let the others be good enough.

## The Long Game

Everything you do early in your career is practice for the next decade. The clients are real and the work is real, but the main product of early-career work is learning. Approach it that way — not as a performance to be evaluated, but as a process of building a practice — and the trajectory takes care of itself.
