## The Package Is the Product

Before a customer uses a product, they experience the packaging. They see it on a shelf, they pick it up, they read it, they open it. Each of those moments is a brand experience, and the design either makes those moments feel like the brand intended or like an afterthought.

Packaging design is where brand identity is most physically tested. The identity has to work on a small label on a dark bottle, on a folding carton under fluorescent retail lighting, on a shipping box that arrives at someone's door.

## Starting from the Hierarchy

Every package has a hierarchy of information: what is this, who made it, what does it do, what do I need to know before I use it. That hierarchy should determine the visual structure, not be retrofitted to a visual structure decided for aesthetic reasons.

I map the information hierarchy before I design anything. Primary — the product name and brand. Secondary — the variant, the category descriptor, the key benefit. Tertiary — the details, the claims, the regulatory information.

Each tier gets a specific typographic treatment. The hierarchy should be readable in the same sequence on the physical package as it is in the map.

## The Front Panel Challenge

The front panel of any package is prime real estate. It has to work as a thumbnail on an e-commerce product page, from shelf distance in a retail environment, and in the hand of someone deciding whether to buy.

Three different viewing distances, three different contexts, all from the same design. The solution is the same as always: strong hierarchy, significant scale contrast, simple and distinctive.

I eliminate anything from the front panel that doesn't help make the decision. Legal text, contact information, detailed instructions — these belong on the back panel. The front panel has one job: make the customer want to pick it up.

## Material and Finish as Brand Communication

The substrate and finish of packaging communicates before the design does. A heavy, rigid box with a matte soft-touch laminate says something different from a thin, flexible pouch with high-gloss printing.

For premium brands, the physical quality of the packaging should be consistent with the premium positioning. Lightweight, cheap-feeling materials undermine even the strongest design.

I specify materials and finishes as part of the design brief, not as a downstream production decision. The design should be created for the specific substrate — not designed in a vacuum and then adapted.

## The Unboxing Moment

E-commerce has made the unboxing experience a significant brand touchpoint. The inside of a box, the wrapping, the first thing a customer sees when they open the package — these are designed brand moments that generate social sharing and memorable impressions.

I design unboxing as a sequence: what do they see first when the package arrives, second when they open the outer packaging, third when they reach the product? Each step in that sequence is an opportunity to reinforce the brand experience.

## Consistency With the Broader System

Packaging that looks completely different from the brand's other materials is a system failure. The packaging should use the same color palette, the same typographic system, the same visual language as every other brand touchpoint.

This consistency is what makes the brand recognizable across contexts — when someone sees the package on a friend's shelf, they should recognize it as the same brand whose social posts they follow and whose website they've visited.

Packaging is not a separate design problem. It's the brand identity in three dimensions.
