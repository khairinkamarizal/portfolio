## Photography is the most misunderstood brand element

Ask most designers to explain a brand's visual identity and they'll talk about the logo, the colors, the typography. Few will lead with photography direction. But in practice, photography is often the most prominent visual expression of a brand — it covers more real estate on any given page, website, or advertisement than any other element.

Getting photography direction right is as important as getting the logo right. Getting it wrong undermines everything else.

## Defining a distinctive photographic worldview

Every brand that uses photography should have a defined photographic worldview — a set of principles that govern how subjects are lit, composed, and processed. Not "use good photography," which means nothing. Principles like: natural light only, not studio-lit. Real environments, not sets. Subjects looking away from camera, not posed. Processed for warmth and grain, not clinical sharpness.

These principles should be distinctive enough that you could identify the brand's photography without the logo in frame. Nike's sports photography has a visual character distinct from Under Armour's. Apple's product photography has a visual character distinct from Samsung's. That distinctiveness is designed, not accidental.

## Lighting as personality

The lighting approach in brand photography communicates personality more directly than almost any other visual variable. Hard, directional light creates drama and edge. Soft, diffused light creates approachability and warmth. Natural light creates authenticity. Studio light creates precision and control.

These lighting choices need to be matched to the brand's personality. A brand that positions itself on craft and authenticity should not have photographs that look like they were shot in a commercial studio with three-point lighting setups. A brand that positions on luxury and precision should not have casual natural-light iPhone snaps.

## Composition and cropping conventions

Consistent composition conventions are what tie photography together across brand touchpoints. Always tight crop on faces. Always environmental context, never isolated subjects. Always room to breathe at the top. Always subjects at rest, never in staged action.

I document composition conventions with actual annotated examples — not just descriptions but marked-up reference images that show exactly what "room to breathe at the top" means in practice.

## The processing pipeline

Color grading is the final stage of photography direction and the one most likely to drift without explicit documentation. A warm, slightly desaturated, filmic grade that looks beautiful in the first season of photography can drift to a colder, more saturated look by the third season when different photographers and retouchers are in the pipeline.

I specify color grading with before-and-after examples and, when the budget allows, a custom LUT (lookup table) that can be applied during post-production to ensure consistency. The LUT doesn't replace skilled retouching — it establishes a baseline that keeps the processing character consistent.
