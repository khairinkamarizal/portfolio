## The Double Mandate

An exhibition poster has two jobs that exist in tension with each other. It needs to be visually compelling enough to stop someone on the street and make them want to attend. And it needs to communicate essential information: what is it, where is it, when is it.

These jobs don't always point in the same direction. Pure artistic expression resists the clarity that information design requires. Pure information design resists the boldness and atmosphere that compels attention.

The best exhibition posters do both. The information is completely clear and the poster is completely arresting. Finding that balance is the specific creative challenge of this format.

## Leading With Atmosphere

I almost always lead with atmosphere rather than information. The first impression of an exhibition poster should communicate what it feels like to be in the presence of the work — the mood, the period, the aesthetic territory.

This means the primary visual element — the dominant image, the typographic treatment, the color palette — is chosen to evoke the exhibition's world. For a show of Brutalist architecture photography, the poster should feel architectural, weighty, structural. For a show of delicate watercolors, the poster should feel light, atmospheric, dissolving at the edges.

If the atmosphere is right, the viewer is already partially in the show before they've read a single word.

## Image Selection and Cropping

When the exhibition provides images to work with — photography of the work, detail shots, documentation — selection and cropping are among the most important design decisions I make.

I look for images that have strong compositional potential at the scale the poster will be used. An image that looks interesting as a thumbnail may not have the internal structure to hold up at A1 or larger. I test candidate images at full scale before committing.

I crop aggressively. A detail of a larger work, cropped to emphasize a particular quality, is often more evocative than a straightforward documentary image. The poster is not trying to document the work — it's trying to evoke the experience of the work.

## Typography That Belongs to the Show

The typographic choices in an exhibition poster should feel connected to the show's content. A show of modernist design deserves modernist typography. A show of 19th-century illustration deserves a typographic palette that resonates with that era.

This doesn't mean pastiche or literal quotation. It means choosing typefaces and treatments whose visual DNA belongs to the same world as the work being exhibited.

The most important typographic decision: what is the hierarchy? Exhibition name, venue, dates. In that order in most cases. The hierarchy should be resolvable in three seconds from a viewing distance.

## Working With Institutional Constraints

Exhibition posters almost always involve institutional constraints: a logo that must appear in a specific position, a required credit block, an approved color palette from a sponsor. These constraints are real and I work within them rather than against them.

The skill is integrating constraints gracefully. The required sponsor logo at the bottom of the poster doesn't have to feel like an afterthought — it can be part of the compositional structure of the lower third. The credit block can be set with the same typographic care as the headline.

Constraints met with craft stop reading as limitations.

## The Test at Scale

I always proof exhibition posters at or close to actual scale before finalizing. A design that looks strong at screen size can feel underwhelming or, conversely, overwhelming at full poster scale.

The hierarchy that seemed clear on a monitor needs to hold up when the poster is on a wall at eye level and the viewer is standing two meters away. The atmospheric quality of the image needs to survive large-format printing.

Test at scale. Commit to the format you're designing for.
