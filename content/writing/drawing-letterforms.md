Drawing letterforms is not calligraphy practice and it's not lettering practice. It's a specific exercise in understanding how type is made — what the relationships between strokes are, what optical corrections experienced type designers make, how the shapes that form letters are constructed.

Designers who have drawn letterforms understand type differently from those who haven't. The difference shows in their work.

## What Drawing Teaches About Optical Correction

Type design is full of counterintuitive optical corrections that experienced type designers make automatically. Drawing letterforms exposes these corrections and makes them tangible.

The most instructive examples:

**Round forms appear smaller than flat-topped forms at the same height.** The bottom of an 'O' and the flat base of an 'H' can't be at the same pixel position — the 'O' needs to extend slightly below the baseline to appear optically aligned with the 'H'. Drawing this correction teaches it in a way that seeing it on a specification never does.

**Optical center is higher than geometric center.** A horizontal bar through the middle of a letterform looks low if it's placed at the exact geometric center. Drawing letters with horizontal strokes — 'H', 'E', 'B' — teaches this correction viscerally.

**Symmetrical letters need asymmetric construction.** The 'S' looks symmetrical but isn't. Its top loop is smaller than its bottom loop to create optical balance. Drawing the 'S' carefully reveals this asymmetry and explains why mechanically symmetrical S constructions look wrong.

## The Anatomy Vocabulary

Drawing letterforms teaches the vocabulary of type anatomy: baseline, cap height, x-height, ascender, descender, bowl, stem, counter, serif, link, ear, spur, terminal, leg. These aren't just words — they're handles for understanding and discussing type.

When I describe what I want to modify in a typeface or what's wrong with a specific letterform, I use this vocabulary precisely. Drawing letters is how that vocabulary becomes precise.

## What Drawing Reveals About Spacing

When you draw individual letterforms and then set them next to each other, the spacing problems become immediately visible in ways they don't on screen. The visual weight of each letter's positive and negative form, the way adjacent shapes create optical gaps or density — these are felt in the hand when drawing in a way that looking at type on a screen approximates but doesn't replicate.

## The Practice Recommendation

I recommend drawing complete lowercase and uppercase alphabets in a consistent typeface style — typically beginning with a basic sans-serif to understand proportions, then a simple serif to understand the relationship between stem and serif. Once a week for six months changes how you see type permanently.

The goal is not to become a type designer. The goal is to develop the observational literacy that makes you a better user of other people's type — more sensitive to quality, more precise in your modifications, and more confident in your typographic decisions.
