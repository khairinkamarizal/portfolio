Lettering and typography are not the same discipline. Lettering is drawing letters; typography is arranging them. A letterer crafts individual letterforms designed to work together in a specific phrase or composition. A typographer sets existing typefaces into layouts. Both are valid, both are skilled, and choosing the wrong one for a project costs time and money.

## When Typography Is the Right Answer

Typography is almost always faster, more flexible, and more consistent than lettering. A typeface can be reset in any language, at any size, for any content without involving the original designer. The decision to use typography is the right one when:

- The brand text will change frequently (different taglines, variable product names)
- The identity needs to scale to many different pieces with variable content
- Consistency of execution across many practitioners is critical
- Budget and timeline are constrained

Custom typography — modifying existing typefaces — sits between pure typography and lettering. It gives character and distinctiveness without the complete hand-craft of bespoke lettering.

## When Lettering Earns Its Cost

Lettering is justified when the specific quality of hand-drawn or custom letterforms adds meaning or distinction that typography can't provide. This is most often true for:

- Brand names where the letterforms are the primary visual identity (a single word or phrase that will never change)
- Applications where a human, craft quality is part of the brand story
- Contexts where an illustrative approach is appropriate (product labels, packaging, editorial)
- Marks where the letterforms need to integrate with illustrative elements in ways that require custom construction

The cost of lettering — in time and money — needs to be justified by what it adds. Lettering for its own sake, without a reason, is cost without benefit.

## Evaluating Lettering Quality

A critical skill for working with lettering is evaluating its quality. Good lettering has consistent stroke weight appropriate to the style, smooth and intentional curves, balanced proportions across all letterforms, and an overall rhythm that feels designed rather than accidental.

Lettering that has obvious digital construction — too-perfect circles from a circle tool, mechanical symmetry that looks like software rather than craft — often looks worse than a well-chosen typeface. The characteristic of lettering that makes it valuable is its humanness; lettering that looks like a bad font loses the quality that justifies its existence.

## Working With Letterers

If I'm commissioning lettering for a brand project, I provide reference that clearly shows the register and character I'm after, the specific phrase or content to be lettered, and the applications where it will live. Letterforms need to survive their intended applications — delicate flourishes that look beautiful in a high-resolution flat image may not survive embossing, small-scale printing, or digital display.

Collaborating early and providing clear technical requirements is more efficient than requesting revisions after the lettering is complete.
