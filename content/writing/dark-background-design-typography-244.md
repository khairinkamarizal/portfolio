## Dark fields are not inverted light fields

The most common mistake when designing with dark backgrounds is treating them as inverted versions of light-field designs — taking a light-background layout, inverting the colors, and calling it done. Dark-field design has its own rules, and violating them produces work that looks like an inversion rather than a design.

The way the eye perceives form, contrast, and space changes substantially on dark backgrounds. Type that's well-spaced on white needs looser tracking on black. Colors that are vivid on white become overwhelming on dark. Thin lines and delicate details that work on light backgrounds disappear or feel fragile on dark ones.

## Tracking adjustments for dark fields

On a light background, dark type is defined by its ink. On a dark background, light type is defined by the space around it. This reversal means that the same tracking value produces different visual results — light type on dark feels tighter than dark type on light at identical tracking settings.

I add 5-15% more tracking to any type reversed out of a dark background compared to what I'd use at the same size on a light background. This compensates for the visual compression effect of light type on dark and maintains the reading rhythm I'm trying to achieve.

## Color behavior on dark backgrounds

Colors become more vivid and saturated on dark backgrounds — a saturated red that looks bold on white looks almost incandescent on black. This can be an asset (for dramatic accent uses) or a liability (when the vivid color fights with the content).

I often reduce the saturation of colors slightly when moving them from a light-field to a dark-field context. A color that's calibrated for light-field use will almost always need adjustment before it works correctly on dark.

## Weight choices for dark fields

Heavy typeface weights that work well at text sizes on light backgrounds can become too dense and heavy on dark backgrounds — the reversed type starts to bleed visually, with the dark counters inside the letterforms appearing to grow.

For body copy and smaller text on dark backgrounds, I typically use one weight lighter than I'd use on light. If I'd use regular weight text at 12pt on light, I'll use light or thin weight at 12pt on dark. The visual weight perception is different, and the weight choice needs to compensate.

## The deep vs. flat dark background

There's a meaningful difference between a flat black (#000000) background and a deep, rich dark background — a very dark navy, charcoal, or brown. Flat black is the most stark and high-contrast option. Deep darks feel more sophisticated and less severe.

I rarely use flat black for large background areas in premium design work. A very dark navy (e.g., #0A0E1A) or rich charcoal (#1C1C1E) gives almost the same contrast advantage as true black while reading as considerably more refined. The choice between them is a personality decision: stark and bold versus deep and premium.
