Editorial design — magazine layouts, book design, annual reports — is a different discipline from poster design. The timescales are different, the relationship to text is different, and the way viewers move through the content is different. But I've learned as much about poster design from editorial work as I have from studying posters.

The two disciplines inform each other in ways that aren't obvious until you've worked seriously in both.

## What Grids Mean at Document Scale

Poster work uses grids, but a single-page format is forgiving — you can establish a grid, set a few elements on it, and be done. Magazine design at scale means building a grid that's robust enough to handle every type of content the publication uses: single images, multiple images, long text, short text, captions, pullquotes, infographics.

A grid that can handle this variety without breaking — that keeps the publication feeling unified across dozens of formally varied pages — has to be extremely well-considered. Building and using grids at editorial scale teaches you what grids are really for.

## Typographic Systems and Rhythm

Editorial design lives or dies on typographic rhythm — the visual beat created by consistent text treatment across a publication. Body copy at a consistent size and leading, headlines following a defined type scale, captions in a consistent voice. When the typographic system is working, the reader moves through the publication with a sense of comfortable familiarity. When it breaks down, the publication feels chaotic.

The discipline of typographic systems in editorial work has made me much more rigorous about typographic systems in poster work. Even a single poster benefits from having a defined type system — hierarchy that's consistent and considered — rather than type set ad hoc.

## The Art of the Spread

Magazine design thinks in spreads — two facing pages viewed simultaneously. The relationship between the left and right pages, the way the eye moves across the gutter, the visual dialogue between the two sides — these are design problems specific to the spread format.

This spatial thinking across multiple adjacent surfaces has influenced how I think about poster series. The relationship between two adjacent posters on a wall is analogous to the relationship between two facing pages. Designing with that relationship in mind produces series that are more coherent and more interesting than designing each piece in isolation.

## Managing Visual Complexity

Editorial design handles more visual complexity than poster design — more text, more images, more varied content. The discipline of making complex information feel clear and accessible without oversimplifying is a specific skill that editorial work develops.

That skill transfers to information-dense poster work. Exhibition posters, informational posters, promotional materials with multiple events or offerings — these pieces benefit from editorial thinking about how to manage complexity with structure.

## The Long Read vs. The Glance

Editorial design assumes time. Readers sit with magazines, return to them, read at different paces. Poster design assumes almost no time — a glance is the most you can count on. Designing in both modes keeps the differences sharp and prevents the habits of one discipline from contaminating the other inappropriately.
