Music festival posters are one of the most demanding poster formats. They need to communicate the emotional promise of the event (the feeling, the energy, the identity), convey a substantial amount of information (multiple headliners, dates, venue, ticket information), and do both with enough visual power to stand out in competitive environments.

These demands often conflict. The more information is present, the harder it is to communicate energy and feeling. The more the design chases emotional expression, the harder it is to fit the content clearly.

Managing this tension is the core design challenge of festival poster work.

## The Hierarchy for Festival Posters

Festival posters have a specific information hierarchy that I've refined through working on multiple festival identities:

**Level one — Event identity:** The festival name or festival wordmark. This is what needs to be memorable and recognizable. It should be the most visually dominant element in the composition.

**Level two — Headline acts:** The names of the two or three most significant artists on the bill. These need to be large enough to be a draw in themselves for people who recognize those artists.

**Level three — Date, venue, and ticket information:** Essential practical information. Needs to be clearly legible but shouldn't compete with the event identity or headline acts.

**Level four — Full lineup:** The complete roster. Often set at a small size that rewards close inspection without dominating the visual field.

**Level five — Supporting information:** Sponsors, social handles, website, partner logos. Necessary but clearly tertiary.

This hierarchy needs to be established before the layout begins. Every spatial and scale decision should reinforce it.

## Managing Lineup Size

Festival lineups can include dozens of names. Getting all of them on a poster in readable type while maintaining any sense of visual hierarchy requires systematic approach.

I typically divide the lineup into tiers (matching their billing status) and use size to signal those tiers. The three or four headline acts might be set at 24pt. The second-tier acts at 16pt. The third tier at 10pt. A simple, clean typeface at these sizes produces a clear, scannable lineup block that doesn't dominate the composition.

The lineup block often becomes a textural element — too dense to read in a quick glance, but clearly signaling "there are lots of artists here" at a distance.

## Visual Energy Through Typography

For festival poster work, I lean heavily on typographic expression for the visual energy that communicates the festival's feeling. A large, bold, expressive typographic composition in the headline treatment can carry the emotional register of the festival better than any illustration or photography in many cases.

The type choice for the festival name is critical. It should feel specific to this festival's personality — not generic "festival type" — and it should hold up across the full range of festival communications (banners, merchandise, digital).

## Working With Photography and Illustration

When a festival has strong visual assets — artist photography, a signature illustration style — integrating them with the information-dense lineup content requires careful spatial planning.

I establish clear zones: an image zone where the visual expression happens, and an information zone where the structured content lives. The zones need to feel related (sharing color, spatial logic, or compositional relationship) without competing.

The failure mode is treating the photography as a background for information. The image should be a first-class element in the composition with its own space, not a surface to print type over.
