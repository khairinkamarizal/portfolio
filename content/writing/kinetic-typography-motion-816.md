## Type Is Already Visual

Before I animate a single character, I remind myself that type is already doing visual work. A well-chosen typeface at the right size, weight, and color communicates before it moves. Motion should add to that communication, not replace it.

This sounds obvious, but it's easy to forget when you're deep in After Effects with a full toolkit of animation options. The temptation to animate everything is real. The discipline to animate only what motion genuinely serves is the skill.

## The First Question: Why Is This Moving?

Every time I animate type I ask: why is this moving? What does motion add that static type doesn't provide?

Good answers: motion reveals information progressively to control pacing. Motion draws attention to a specific element at a specific moment. Motion reinforces the meaning of the words (active words that move feel more active). Motion creates continuity between scenes.

Bad answers: because it would look cool. Because other motion designers do it. Because I have time to fill.

If I can't answer the question well, I don't animate the type.

## Readability Is the Constraint

Kinetic type exists to be read. This sounds obvious until you're watching a showreel full of gorgeous type animations where I have no idea what the text says because it moved too fast, too chaotically, or with too much visual complexity competing for attention.

My rule: the viewer should be able to read every line of text before it leaves the frame, without effort. If I have to pause the video to read something, the timing is wrong. Full stop.

## Animation Styles I Return To

A few approaches I use regularly because they serve readability while adding real expressiveness:

**Reveal animations** — text builds in one word or phrase at a time, timed to narration or music beats. Gives the viewer exactly as much text as they need at each moment.

**Scale and settle** — text arrives slightly oversized and settles to its final scale. Creates arrival energy without obscuring the letterforms.

**Mask reveals** — text wipes in behind a geometric mask. Clean, professional, works with almost any typeface and brand tone.

**Staggered character entry** — individual characters or words enter with a short stagger. Works best with display sizes; gets muddy at small sizes.

## What I Avoid

Aggressive blur, spin, and distortion effects that make letterforms temporarily unreadable. Excessive easing that drags out every movement. Type that moves continuously without settling — never letting the viewer's eye rest on the text and read it.

These approaches look active but they work against communication. A viewer who's watching effects isn't reading words.

## The Test

I play back any kinetic type piece at half speed to evaluate the readability at each moment. If it reads well at half speed, it reads well at full speed. If text is illegible even at half speed, I adjust the timing or simplify the animation.

Motion exists in time. Use that time to serve the communication.
