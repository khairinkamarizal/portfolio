## Color Means Different Things Everywhere

I keep a reference document for every global brand project I work on. It maps each proposed color against its primary cultural associations in the key markets the brand serves. The document is never finished — cultural associations shift over time and vary by subculture, generation, and context. But having it forces me to ask the question before assuming a color is universally safe.

The instinct to treat color associations as universal is understandable. Western design education often presents color psychology as if it describes human responses rather than culturally conditioned responses. Most of what's written about color psychology in design texts is describing Western European and North American associations. This is a partial picture at best.

## The Colors With the Most Cross-Cultural Variance

White is perhaps the most culturally variable color in brand work. In Western contexts it communicates purity, cleanliness, and simplicity. In many East Asian and South Asian cultural contexts, white is associated with mourning and funerals. This doesn't mean white can never be used in brands serving these markets — design conventions are increasingly global and sophisticated audiences understand Western design language. But it's information that affects how prominently white features in the identity.

Green has strong positive associations in most Western contexts (health, nature, sustainability) but complex political and religious associations in some Middle Eastern contexts that could affect how a brand using prominent green is perceived in those markets.

Red is perhaps the most dramatically variable. Associated with danger, emergency, and warning in Western industrial contexts. Associated with luck, prosperity, and celebration in Chinese and many East Asian contexts. Both associations are simultaneously present in contemporary global culture, which means a brand using red needs to understand which association is dominant for its specific audience.

## The Luxury Exception

Luxury brands operate somewhat outside cultural color conventions because their visual language is itself globally understood as a cultural code. The deep navy and gold of a European luxury brand reads as "luxury" in Tokyo and São Paulo as clearly as in London, because the luxury visual language has itself become a global standard.

This creates an interesting exception: brands positioning themselves clearly as premium European luxury can rely on the conventions of that category more than on the specific cultural color associations of each market they serve. The category does the cultural translation.

## Black Is Almost Universal

Black is the most cross-culturally stable color in brand work. Its associations with authority, sophistication, and quality are remarkably consistent across cultures, though the specific character varies — black communicates formal authority in some contexts, elegant sophistication in others, contemporary coolness in others.

For brands that genuinely need to operate globally without cultural color friction, anchoring the palette in black (with white and a carefully chosen accent) is a reliable approach. It sacrifices some distinctiveness for cross-cultural reliability, which is sometimes the right trade.

## How I Actually Do the Research

I don't rely on single sources for cultural color research. I use academic anthropological research where available, consult with cultural advisors in specific markets, and whenever possible talk directly to people from the relevant cultural contexts.

I'm also careful about overgeneralizing. Saying "in China, red means luck" is true but incomplete. Urban professional audiences in China navigate Western design conventions fluently. A younger demographic has different associations from an older one. Regional variation within large markets matters.

The goal isn't to find one definitive answer. It's to identify the significant associations that could affect how a brand is perceived, and to make informed decisions about which associations to lean into and which to account for in the design.
