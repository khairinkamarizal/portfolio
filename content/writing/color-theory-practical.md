Color theory as taught in design school is mostly useful for passing exams. Complementary colors, triadic relationships, the color wheel — these are frameworks for understanding, not recipes for execution. The color principles I actually use daily are narrower, more specific, and harder-won.

## Simultaneous Contrast

Put two colors next to each other and they change each other. A neutral gray on an orange background looks cooler than the same neutral gray on a blue background. This is simultaneous contrast, and it's the most practically important color phenomenon in design.

Implications for brand color work: you can't evaluate a color in isolation. A brand's primary color needs to be evaluated in the context of the colors it will actually appear alongside — backgrounds, photography, secondary palette colors. A color that looks vibrant in isolation can look washed out or muddy in context.

## Color Constancy

The human visual system adjusts for different lighting conditions, which means colors can look different under different light sources and still be recognized as the same color. This is useful for understanding why brand colors can drift in different production contexts and still feel consistent to viewers.

It's also why I specify Pantone references rather than relying on RGB or CMYK specifications alone for critical brand colors. Pantone references define the intended color in a way that's independent of how it's reproduced.

## Value First, Hue Second

Value — how light or dark a color is — is more important than hue for legibility and hierarchy. A dark color on a light background will always have more contrast than two colors of similar value regardless of their hue difference.

I evaluate all color combinations against their value contrast first. If the value contrast is insufficient for the communication need, I fix the value relationship before worrying about the hue relationship.

## The Neutral Is Never Neutral

Every "neutral" in a palette — grays, off-whites, taupes — has a color cast. A warm gray is yellow-orange cast. A cool gray is blue cast. These casts interact with the other colors in the system in ways that can either support or undermine the palette's overall character.

I select neutrals deliberately, choosing the color temperature of neutrals to complement the system. A warm primary palette usually benefits from warm neutrals that support the palette's warmth. A cool palette benefits from cool neutrals.

## The 60-30-10 Distribution

A guideline I find reliably useful: 60% dominant color (usually the background or primary surface), 30% secondary color, 10% accent. This distribution creates visual interest and hierarchy without overwhelming the viewer with competing elements.

It's not a formula — it's a starting proportion to test from and adjust. But it prevents the most common palette mistake: competing colors applied in roughly equal proportions, creating visual noise with no clear hierarchy.

## Warm Colors Activate, Cool Colors Settle

In compositions with multiple colors, the warm colors will always demand attention first. This is perceptual, not cultural. I use this to control where the eye goes first in a brand composition — warm accents draw the eye, cool backgrounds recede.
