## The Scale Principle

If everything is the same size, nothing is important. This sounds obvious. The application is harder than it sounds.

I see posters where the headline is 60pt, the subhead is 48pt, and the body is 36pt. The designer thought they were creating hierarchy. What they actually created was monotony. Those sizes are too close together — the eye doesn't register them as a hierarchy, it registers them as noise at slightly different volumes.

Real scale contrast means making the size jumps dramatic enough that the viewer doesn't have to work to understand what to read first.

## What Dramatic Scale Actually Looks Like

On a well-designed event poster, the most important element — usually the headline or the event name — might be eight to ten times the size of the smallest element. That's not a typo. Eight to ten times.

If your body copy is 10pt, your headline can be 100pt. If your smallest detail text is 7pt, your headline can be 80pt. The gap between these sizes is what creates clear, immediate hierarchy.

When I look at vintage Swiss poster design, what strikes me most is how aggressively they used scale. A single word in enormous type commanding most of the poster's vertical space, with everything else arranged modestly below it. The hierarchy is resolved in the first fraction of a second.

## Three Levels Is Usually Enough

I organize poster hierarchy into three levels, and I rarely need more:

**Primary** — the single most important thing. The event name, the headline, the key message. This is the biggest element on the page by a significant margin.

**Secondary** — supporting information that earns its own visual tier. A subheading, a date and venue, a supporting artist.

**Tertiary** — the fine print. Contact information, sponsor logos, ticket details. Small, consistent, receding.

When I'm designing a poster with hierarchy problems, the first thing I do is identify which elements belong to each tier. Then I adjust sizes until the tiers are visually distinct. The fix is almost always to make the primary bigger, the tertiary smaller, and to leave the secondary roughly where it is.

## The Role of Weight

Scale does most of the work, but weight amplifies it. A bold typeface at 80pt reads with more authority than a light weight at 80pt. But I'm careful not to use weight as a substitute for scale — the mistake is keeping everything the same size and differentiating by weight alone. That creates tension but not clarity.

My rule: establish hierarchy with scale first, then use weight to reinforce it.

## Restraint in the Primary

Here's a counterintuitive point: the primary element almost always needs more white space around it, not less. Cramping a huge headline makes it feel accidental. Giving it breathing room makes it feel intentional.

The empty space around the dominant element is part of the hierarchy. It signals: look here, this is what matters, everything else is supporting cast.

## A Test I Run on Every Poster

I hold the poster at arm's length and ask: what did I read first? If the answer is the most important thing, the hierarchy is working. If I read something secondary first, I find out why — usually it's because something nearby is competing — and I fix it.

Scale contrast isn't subtle. It should be obvious. That's the point.
