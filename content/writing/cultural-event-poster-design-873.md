## The Cultural Event Poster Is Different

Cultural event posters — for art exhibitions, film festivals, music events, theatre productions, literary events — operate in a different register from commercial advertising. The audience for a gallery opening or an independent film festival has higher visual literacy than a general consumer audience, and the poster is often evaluated as a piece of design in its own right, not just as an information delivery mechanism.

This raises the standard. A poster for a corporate conference needs to be clear and professional. A poster for a contemporary art exhibition needs to be all of that and visually interesting enough to reward the attention of people who look at art for a living.

## The Artistic Brief

Cultural event briefs are often more open-ended than commercial ones. "Design a poster for our autumn exhibition season" with a list of artists and a theme is a much wider brief than a concert poster with specific artist, date, and venue.

Wide briefs require the designer to develop a conceptual position — a point of view about what the poster should do visually, derived from research into the exhibition content, the artists, the institution's visual history, and the audience.

I treat cultural event briefs as creative research projects. I spend time with the work being presented or the content being communicated before committing to a visual approach.

## Typography as Curatorial Voice

In much cultural institution poster design, typography does the primary visual work. The choice of typeface, the scale relationships, the compositional logic — these communicate the curatorial voice and aesthetic position of the institution as much as any image does.

A gallery with a rigorous, research-oriented program uses a different typographic register than one with a populist, accessible focus. The poster's typography should reflect and reinforce that position.

## Image Selection and Art Direction

For exhibition posters that include artwork, the image selection is a critical curatorial decision. Which work represents the exhibition? How is it cropped, scaled, and positioned relative to the typographic elements?

I treat image selection for cultural event posters as a collaborative conversation with the curator rather than a design decision I make independently. The visual choices I make in the poster communicate something about how I interpret the exhibition's content, and that interpretation should align with the curator's intent.

## The Poster as Part of the Cultural Record

Cultural event posters — especially for significant exhibitions or performances — become part of the cultural record of those events. They end up in archives, collections, and the memories of people who attended. This gives them a permanence that most graphic design work doesn't have, and it's worth designing with that permanence in mind.
