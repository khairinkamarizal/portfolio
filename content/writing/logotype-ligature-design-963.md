## What a Ligature Is and Isn't

In traditional typography, a ligature is a designed connection between two adjacent letters that would otherwise create awkward spacing or visual conflict — the classic fi and fl ligatures that join the dot of the i or the crossbar of the f to the following letter.

In logo design, a ligature is something both more deliberate and more expressive. It's a custom-designed connection between letters in a wordmark that creates a unified form, reveals a visual relationship, or simply produces a more elegant result than the letters standing independently.

A ligature in a logo is always a design decision, never a technical accommodation. That distinction is important.

## When Ligatures Serve a Logo

The best logo ligatures emerge from letters that have a natural formal relationship — shared strokes, compatible terminals, or complementary angles that suggest a connection.

The letters A and R, for instance, can share their right diagonal if a wordmark includes that combination and the typeface has compatible stroke angles. T and H can share the T's crossbar, extending it to serve as the cap-height baseline for the H. E and R often have compatible arm geometries.

These opportunities aren't in every wordmark. I look for them during the refinement phase, not as a starting design approach. The ligature should feel discovered, not manufactured.

## How I Develop a Ligature

When I identify a potential ligature opportunity, I test it at a very large size — 400pt or larger. At this scale, every decision about how the connection is drawn is visible and evaluable.

I build the ligature from the letterforms' existing geometry. If the letters share a stroke weight, the connection uses that same weight. If one letter's terminal has a specific angle or treatment, the connection respects that treatment.

The test is whether the ligature reads naturally. Does it look like the two letters have always been connected in this way? Or does it look like a joint was welded on? The former is right. The latter needs more work.

## Ligatures as Brand Differentiators

A well-designed ligature makes a wordmark unmistakably distinctive. It's a detail that immediately signals that the typography was designed specifically for this brand rather than selected from a type menu.

Clients often don't notice ligatures consciously but respond to them positively — they register as quality and attention to detail. Design-aware clients and observers notice them and appreciate the craft.

This is the kind of invisible quality that separates excellent logo design from good logo design.

## When Ligatures Undermine Legibility

Not all ligature experiments work. Some connections create visual confusion — the letters become hard to read individually, or the combined form looks like a different letter entirely.

The legibility test: show the ligature to someone unfamiliar with the brand and ask them to read the wordmark aloud. If they hesitate, misread, or are visibly uncertain, the ligature has compromised legibility. Back to the drawing board.

A ligature that sacrifices legibility for visual interest is the wrong tradeoff. The wordmark's primary job is to communicate the brand name.

## The Delivery Consideration

When I deliver a wordmark with custom ligatures, I include the ligature logic in the design documentation. This helps anyone modifying the wordmark in the future understand why the letterforms are shaped the way they are and how to maintain the design integrity if changes are ever necessary.

Undocumented custom typography decisions are a liability for clients who need to extend or modify their brand over time.
