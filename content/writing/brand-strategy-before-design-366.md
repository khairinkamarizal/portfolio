I've turned down projects where clients want to jump straight to logo design. Not because I couldn't use the work, but because I know what happens when design precedes strategy: the work gets revised endlessly, the client gets frustrated, and the final identity often fails to actually solve the problem it was supposed to solve.

## Strategy Is a Design Tool

Brand strategy isn't a management consulting exercise that happens before design starts. It's a design tool — it gives the designer the criteria against which every visual decision can be evaluated.

Without strategy, design choices are arbitrary. With strategy, design choices are arguments. "I chose this typeface because the brand needs to communicate precision and authority, and this face's geometric construction and weight contrast expresses those qualities" is a designable sentence. "I chose this typeface because it felt right" is not.

## What Strategy Actually Involves

The brand strategy work I do before any visual design includes: understanding the business context and competitive landscape, defining the target audience and their relationship with the category, identifying the brand's positioning (what it offers that's meaningfully different), and defining the brand personality (how the brand would present itself if it were a person).

This work produces a strategic brief that defines the territory the identity needs to occupy. It's not a visual brief — it doesn't prescribe colors or typefaces. It defines the emotional and rational space the brand wants to own.

## The Discovery Phase

Discovery is the structured process of gathering the information that feeds brand strategy. It includes stakeholder interviews, competitive audits, audience research, and often a visual audit of the existing identity (if there is one).

Stakeholder interviews are the most important and least comfortable part of discovery. They surface disagreements about the brand's direction that need to be resolved before design begins. An organization where the CEO and the head of marketing have fundamentally different ideas about what the brand should be cannot be designed for until that disagreement is resolved.

## Moving From Strategy to Design

The strategy brief is the transition document from strategy to design. It articulates the positioning, personality, and visual direction without prescribing specific design decisions. It answers the question: what does a design have to accomplish to succeed?

The best strategic briefs are short and specific. A brief that contains every possible interpretation of the brand's personality isn't guidance — it's noise. The brief should take clear positions, even on questions where reasonable people might disagree.

## The Client's Role in Strategy

Brand strategy is a collaborative process — the client knows their organization, their audience, and their market better than I do. My role is to structure the process and synthesize what I learn. The strategic brief is co-authored, not delivered.

This collaboration is what gives clients confidence in the eventual design. They've participated in building the criteria; they can evaluate the design against those criteria rather than reacting based on personal preference.
