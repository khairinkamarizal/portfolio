Taste is the most valuable and least teachable quality in graphic design. Technical skills can be learned relatively quickly. Conceptual thinking can be developed with practice and feedback. Taste — the ability to consistently make the right formal decision among many possible ones — develops slowly, over years, through specific habits of looking and reflecting.

I think about this a lot because it's central to why some designers with strong technical skills produce mediocre work while others with modest technical skills produce excellent work. The difference is almost always taste.

## Taste Is Learnable, Not Innate

The first thing to understand: taste isn't something you're born with. It's accumulated through sustained, intentional exposure to excellent work and through developing the capacity to analyze why it's excellent.

People who claim to have "natural" taste are usually describing the early formation of taste through childhood environments — they were surrounded by good design, or their parents had developed aesthetic sensibility that shaped their early visual experience. That's environmental, not genetic. It's a head start, not a fixed advantage.

This means taste can be developed by anyone willing to do the work.

## Looking Widely and Specifically

The foundation of developed taste is looking at a lot of excellent work across many periods, geographies, and design traditions. Not casually — analytically. Asking: why does this work? What decision created this effect? What would this look like if this one choice were different?

I maintain a discipline of looking at design history deliberately rather than just consuming contemporary work. International Typographic Style. Polish poster tradition. Japanese graphic design. American corporate modernism. Art Deco commercial art. Each tradition offers formal solutions and aesthetic values that contemporary work doesn't always provide.

Looking only at contemporary design is like only reading books published this year. The range of influence shapes the range of solutions available.

## The Vocabulary Problem

Developing taste requires developing vocabulary — the ability to describe and analyze what you're seeing specifically. "This design is good" is not useful analysis. "The proportion between the headline weight and the subhead weight creates just enough hierarchy without overwhelming the secondary information" is useful analysis.

Building this vocabulary happens through reading — design criticism, typographic theory, design history — and through conversation with other designers who take formal analysis seriously. The vocabulary for discussing design is itself a tool for perceiving design more precisely.

## Feedback as Calibration

The fastest way to develop taste is to get honest, rigorous feedback on your own work from people whose taste you respect. Not "I like this" or "this doesn't work for me" — specific formal analysis of what is and isn't working and why.

This kind of feedback is rare and valuable. When you find people who can give it, maintain those relationships. When you can give it yourself — to yourself and others — your taste is developing.

## Uncertainty and Decisiveness

Undeveloped taste often manifests as decisiveness without justification: "I like this, I don't know why." Developing taste passes through a phase of uncertainty — you can see when something isn't quite right but you don't yet know exactly what to change. Mature taste produces both decisiveness and justification: "This is right because these specific formal decisions work together to produce this specific effect."

The uncertainty phase is where real learning is happening. Don't rush through it by defaulting to either "this feels right" or "I don't know."

Stay curious about why things work. That question, pursued rigorously over years, is how taste develops.
