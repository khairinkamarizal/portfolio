Font licensing is the least glamorous topic in typography and also one of the most practically important. I've seen designers get into serious trouble — with foundries, with clients, and occasionally with lawyers — because they didn't understand what their license actually permitted. Here's what you need to know.

## Licenses Are Not Universal

There is no standard font license. Every foundry sets its own terms, and those terms vary enormously. A license that permits everything you need from one foundry may prohibit the same use from another. Reading the license is not optional — it's professional due diligence.

The most common license types are desktop licenses (for use in design software to create static output), web licenses (for self-hosting fonts on websites), application licenses (for embedding in apps), ePub licenses (for e-books), and broadcast licenses (for motion and video).

Using a desktop license to embed fonts in a PDF for digital distribution, or to embed them in a video, may violate the license terms even though it feels like a natural extension of desktop use.

## What "Commercial Use" Actually Means

Most premium font licenses permit commercial use — meaning you can use them for client work and the output can be sold or used commercially. But some licenses, particularly free fonts from certain distribution platforms, restrict commercial use to personal projects only.

Always check explicitly whether a font can be used for paid client work. "Free" doesn't mean "free for everything."

## Client Delivery and Embedding

When you deliver a print-ready PDF to a client, fonts are typically embedded in the file. This is usually permitted under desktop licenses, but the client cannot extract those fonts from the PDF and use them independently. They're embedded for rendering, not transferred as assets.

If a client needs to use fonts in their own internal documents or templates going forward, they need their own license. You cannot legally transfer your license to them. This is a conversation I have with clients early in identity and brand system projects: "The fonts I've specified require their own license purchase. Here's the cost and where to buy."

## App and Broadcast Work

If you're creating assets that will be used in video, motion, or app contexts from a desktop license, you almost certainly need additional licenses. Desktop licenses explicitly exclude these uses in most foundry terms.

For a brand identity that includes motion graphics, I specify separately which fonts require broadcast licenses and include that cost in the project recommendation.

## Free Font Traps

Free fonts on aggregator sites are often uploaded without authorization. A font that's freely available doesn't mean it's freely licensed — someone may have stripped the license from a commercial font and shared it illegally.

I use free fonts only from sources I trust: Google Fonts (with their specific license), The League of Moveable Type, or directly from foundries offering free licenses under documented terms like SIL Open Font License.

## Managing Licenses Professionally

I maintain a record of every font license I own, what it permits, and how many seats it covers. When I bring a new team member on a project, I verify they're covered by existing licenses or purchase additional seats.

This isn't just legal protection — it's professional behavior. The foundries producing the type we use are often small operations run by talented type designers. Respecting their licensing terms is how that ecosystem sustains itself.

License before you spec. It's part of the job.
