Kinetic typography — text that moves — has been part of motion design since the title sequences of the 1950s. It's one of the most expressive tools available to a motion designer, and it's also one of the most abused. Random bouncing, spinning letters, unmotivated movement — bad kinetic typography is everywhere, and it usually makes content harder to read and understand, not easier.

Good kinetic typography treats movement as an extension of typographic meaning. The way the type moves says something about what the type means.

## The First Principle: Movement Serves Reading

Before any other consideration, kinetic type must serve readability. If the animation makes the text harder to read, the animation is wrong, regardless of how visually interesting it is.

Legibility has a temporal dimension in motion that it doesn't have in print. Still text gives the reader infinite time to parse what they're seeing. Moving text has a duration, and if that duration is too short for the text to be read, the viewer falls behind and disengages.

I set the minimum duration for any text element at the point where it can be read comfortably — at normal reading pace, not rushed. Then I work within that constraint to create animation that enhances rather than competes with reading.

## Movement as Meaning

The most effective kinetic typography uses the movement itself to add a layer of meaning to the text. A word that expands as it arrives communicates growth or expansion. A word that fragments as it exits communicates dissolution or uncertainty. Words that flow together communicate unity.

This isn't decoration — it's the text's meaning being expressed through form and motion simultaneously.

I think about this before I animate: what does this text say, and what could the movement add to or emphasize about that meaning? The answer shapes the animation.

## Character-Level vs. Word-Level vs. Line-Level

One of the foundational decisions in kinetic typography: do individual characters animate separately, do whole words move together, or do complete lines move as units?

Character-level animation creates energy and complexity. It can feel playful, chaotic, or sophisticated depending on execution. It's also harder to read during animation and usually requires more time per word.

Word-level animation is a middle ground — more energy than line-level, more legible than character-level. It's the mode I default to most often.

Line-level animation is the most legible and the calmest. It works best for content that needs to be read carefully or for brand contexts where controlled energy is appropriate.

## Choreography in Multi-Line Sets

When multiple lines of text appear in sequence, the choreography between them matters. Lines that all animate identically feel mechanical. Lines with slight variations in timing, offset, or style feel deliberate and considered.

I use staggered timing — each element beginning its animation slightly after the previous — almost universally. The stagger creates a ripple effect that implies sequence without feeling robotic.

## Type Style and Motion Compatibility

Not all typefaces work well in motion. Very thin or hairline weights often flicker or vibrate at certain sizes in video, particularly with horizontal motion. Highly decorative typefaces can become unreadable during animation.

I test typeface choices in motion, at the sizes and speeds I'll use them, before committing to them for a kinetic project. Screen renderings of type in motion are different from static type.

The rule that holds across everything: the text is primary, the motion is the verb. Type carries the meaning. Motion communicates how.
