Not every brand needs a mark. A strong wordmark can carry a brand identity on its own, and plenty of the most recognized companies in the world operate without a standalone symbol. But when a mark is right, it's irreplaceable — it can communicate in contexts where the full wordmark can't, at scales where type becomes illegible, in places where only an icon will fit.

The question isn't whether to have a mark. The question is whether the mark is doing real work.

## What a Mark Is For

A logo mark needs to be recognizable without the wordmark accompanying it. If it only makes sense next to the company name, it's not doing its job as an independent symbol — it's just decoration.

I test marks early by stripping the wordmark. Does the mark communicate anything on its own? Does it evoke the right feeling? Does it hold up at favicon size, embossed on packaging, reversed on a dark background, debossed on a business card? A mark that only looks good in one application isn't a mark — it's a design exercise.

## The Problem With Illustrative Marks

Highly illustrative marks — complex, detailed, literal depictions of something — rarely hold up across scales and applications. They look impressive in a presentation but fall apart at small sizes. They're expensive to reproduce in embroidery, engraving, or single-color print. They date quickly because the illustration style carries its era.

I prefer marks that are reduced to their essential geometry. The concept can be rich and layered, but the execution should be lean. When I'm working on a mark, I'm constantly asking: what can I remove? The mark I end up with should feel inevitable — like there was no other way to draw this.

## Concept First, Form Second

The worst marks I see are those built form-first — someone drew something that looked cool and then worked backward to justify it. This produces generic results because cool shapes are everywhere. A mark that comes from a specific, defensible concept is harder to arrive at but far more distinctive.

The concept doesn't have to be obscure or clever. It just has to be true to the brand. A construction company doesn't need a visual pun — it needs a mark that communicates stability, precision, and scale. Those qualities should be legible in the form, the weight, and the geometric logic of the mark.

## Geometric Rigor Matters

A mark needs to be geometrically clean. Casual curves, near-parallel lines, accidentally similar angles — these create visual noise that reads as carelessness, even when the viewer can't articulate why. I use construction grids, golden section proportions, and optical corrections to make marks feel balanced even when they're not mathematically symmetrical.

Optical correction is something a lot of designers skip. A circle that appears visually centered is not mathematically centered — the eye reads equal optical weight, not equal measurement. If you're placing a mark at the center of a composition and it feels slightly off, measure it. It's probably correct. Trust your eye and adjust.

## When to Walk Away From the Mark

Sometimes, after exploring a dozen concepts, none of them are working. The brand doesn't have a strong conceptual anchor that translates to a mark. In that case, I'll redirect to a strong wordmark and a well-designed type lockup. A mediocre mark is worse than no mark. The wordmark is the brand — the mark is earned later if the brand grows into needing it.
