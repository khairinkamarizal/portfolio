Stationery systems are among the oldest applications of brand identity and among the most neglected in contemporary practice. When a client hands someone a business card or sends a letter on company letterhead, that physical object represents the organization as directly as any advertisement or website. The quality of that representation matters.

## The Stationery Suite as a System

A complete stationery system typically includes: business cards, letterhead, continuation sheets, envelopes, and compliments slips. Each piece has different dimensional constraints, different use contexts, and different information requirements. Designing them as a system — with consistent proportional logic, consistent typographic hierarchy, and consistent use of brand elements — creates a coherent family that reinforces the brand at every touchpoint.

The mistake is designing each piece independently and hoping they look related. They need to be designed together, with explicit decisions about how the brand elements scale and adapt across different formats.

## Letterhead Design Principles

A letterhead has two jobs: to identify the sender clearly and to provide a clean, appropriate canvas for the letter content. These jobs can conflict. An overdesigned letterhead competes with the letter; a generic letterhead fails to identify the sender.

I design letterheads with the letter content in mind — setting up the typographic zone where the letter text will sit, and ensuring the brand elements don't intrude on that zone. The letterhead's design is complete only when a sample letter is laid over it.

## Business Card Information Priority

The information on a business card should be prioritized, not just listed. What does the recipient most need? Usually: the person's name, their role or specialty, and one primary contact method. Everything else is secondary.

I design business card information hierarchically — the name is the most prominent element, followed by the role, followed by contact information in a clear but subordinate treatment. A card where every piece of information is the same visual weight communicates nothing about priority.

## Paper as Brand Signal

The paper stock of a stationery system is a brand decision. Thick, textured paper communicates quality and substance. Standard office weight communicates practicality and accessibility. Recycled stock communicates environmental values. The stock should match the brand's positioning.

I specify paper as part of every stationery system design. "Whatever the printer has" is not a specification; it's an abdication of a brand decision.

## Digital Stationery Equivalents

Alongside print stationery, most organizations need digital equivalents: email signature templates, digital letterhead for PDF use, presentation templates. I design these in parallel with the print system, adapting the print design logic to digital constraints rather than designing them separately.

The email signature especially deserves careful attention — it appears on every outgoing email and is often the most-seen brand application an organization has.
