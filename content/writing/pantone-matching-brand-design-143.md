## What Pantone Actually Is

Pantone is a proprietary color matching system. When you specify a Pantone color, you're specifying a pre-mixed ink formula — a specific proportion of base pigments that produces a defined, reproducible color regardless of who's printing it, on what press, in what country.

This is different from CMYK color, where the printer mixes cyan, magenta, yellow, and black inks in varying densities to approximate a color. Pantone gives you the exact color. CMYK gives you an approximation.

## When to Use Pantone

For most everyday print work, CMYK is sufficient. Where Pantone matters is when a specific color is non-negotiable — usually a brand's primary color that needs to be identical across all print applications, consistently reproduced for years, across different printers.

The classic use case is brand stationery, packaging, and collateral where color consistency is critical. If your brand's blue must look identical whether it's printed in Chicago or Tokyo, CMYK won't guarantee that. Pantone will.

## The Cost Reality

Spot color printing costs more than CMYK. Each Pantone color requires a separate printing plate and a separate ink (which may need to be pre-mixed or special-ordered). A two-color Pantone print job has two plates. A four-color Pantone job has four.

For most brands, specifying core brand colors as Pantone for critical applications and using CMYK equivalents for everyday materials is the right balance. I always provide CMYK and RGB equivalents alongside Pantone specs in brand guidelines.

## Coated vs. Uncoated Paper

Pantone specifies separate color codes for coated (C) and uncoated (U) paper. The same Pantone number printed on coated and uncoated paper looks different — coated paper reflects more light, making colors look more vivid; uncoated absorbs more ink, making colors look softer.

This matters: always specify the right suffix for the paper stock. Pantone 286 C and Pantone 286 U are not the same color visually, even though they're the same ink formula.

## The Pantone Bridge

Pantone publishes bridge guides that show how Pantone colors translate to CMYK. These are essential references for any print designer. The translation is rarely perfect — some Pantone colors are simply outside the CMYK gamut — but the bridge shows you the closest achievable equivalents.

If a brand's core Pantone color translates poorly to CMYK (the blues and greens are frequent offenders), it's worth knowing that upfront so you can make informed decisions about where spot color is required.

## Digital Color Values

Pantone also publishes hex and RGB values for digital use. These are approximate equivalents, not exact translations. A Pantone color on screen will not match the physical ink — but having consistent digital values ensures brand digital materials are using the best available approximation.

Include all four color values — Pantone, CMYK, RGB, hex — in every brand guideline. Future designers will thank you.
