## Illustration as Identity

A distinctive illustration style is one of the most powerful brand differentiators available. Unlike typography or color, which are shared resources that competing brands can adopt, a genuinely distinctive illustration style is harder to copy without looking derivative. When a brand develops a strong illustration language and applies it consistently, the style itself becomes recognizable as that brand's property.

I've developed illustration systems for brands across hospitality, technology, consumer goods, and cultural sectors. The process is consistent: define the style, develop the rules, build the system, and document it well enough that it can be applied by illustrators who weren't part of the original development.

## What an Illustration Style Consists Of

An illustration style is defined by several interdependent decisions.

Line quality: is there a visible line at all? If so, how thick, how consistent, how organic or geometric? Some illustration styles use flat color with no line. Others use a single consistent stroke weight. Others use variable-weight lines that feel like drawing.

Color handling: does the illustration use the brand palette directly? Does it use a reduced subset? Does it use colors that are derived from but slightly different from the primary brand colors? The color handling is where illustration either integrates with or competes against the other brand elements.

Geometry vs. organicism: are the forms simplified toward geometric abstraction, or do they retain organic curves and variation? The choice determines whether the illustration style feels contemporary and precise or warm and hand-crafted.

Perspective and dimensionality: flat illustration, isometric, subtle shadow, full dimensional rendering. This choice affects how the illustration style relates to the real world — whether it's clearly graphic and designed or trying to simulate photographic space.

Subject matter and conventions: what gets illustrated? Humans? Objects? Concepts? If humans, how are they simplified? What's included and excluded from a typical scene?

## Developing the Style Definition

I typically develop an illustration style through a collaborative process with the client. I produce a style exploration sheet — ten to twenty small illustrations in different style directions, alongside reference imagery — and use this as the basis for a focused conversation about which direction fits the brand's character.

The style exploration makes abstract style concepts tangible. Clients find it much easier to respond to actual illustrations than to descriptions of illustration styles.

## The Style Guide Document

Once the style is defined and examples are produced, I create a style guide document that's specific enough to allow another illustrator to produce on-brand work without my direct involvement.

The style guide includes: technique specifications (what software, what brush or setting, what overall approach), annotated examples showing what's correct and why, annotated counter-examples showing what to avoid, and a library of approved marks, icons, and repeating elements that can be used across illustrations.

Without this documentation, the illustration style exists only in my head and in the initial examples. With it, the style is a scalable asset.

## Consistency vs. Expression

The tension in any illustration system is between style consistency and individual expression. If every illustration in the brand's library looks like it was made by the same robot, the style is consistent but lifeless. If every illustration is highly individual, the brand loses the recognition value that consistency creates.

I resolve this by defining the style constraints tightly (these rules must always be followed) while leaving room for compositional and narrative expression (these elements can vary). The style is the constant; the story each illustration tells is variable.

## When to Commission vs. License

Some brands commission all their illustration, which gives them maximum style consistency and ownership. Others license an illustrator's personal style, which gives them the quality of an established illustrator's work but means they don't own the style and may share it with other brands.

For brands where illustration is a primary identity asset, commissioning custom work with full ownership is the right investment. For brands where illustration is secondary, licensing a well-matched existing style can be more cost-effective.
