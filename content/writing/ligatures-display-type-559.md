## Beyond the Standard Ligatures

Every decent typeface includes standard ligatures — the fi, fl, ff, ffi, ffl combinations that prevent the dot of the i or the ascender of the l from colliding with the hook of the f. These are housekeeping. They're on by default and they solve a technical problem.

The ligatures I find interesting are the discretionary ones: the historically motivated combinations like ct and st, the stylistic alternates that connect letters at their terminals, the custom ligatures that a type designer built as expressive options rather than corrections.

In body text, these are subtle touches. In display work and logotypes, they're compositional elements.

## What a Ligature Does

At its core, a ligature does one of two things: it solves a collision problem, or it creates a visual connection between letters that implies they belong together.

The second purpose is the interesting one. When two letters share a stroke — when they connect physically — the eye reads them as a unit within the word. This creates rhythm within the letterform sequence, interrupting the visual flow with moments of binding.

In a logotype, a well-placed ligature is a small surprise. The viewer sees the word, then sees the connection, then sees the word again differently. That dual reading creates engagement.

## How I Approach Ligatures in Logo Work

When I'm developing a wordmark, I look for ligature opportunities in the same way I look for negative space opportunities: as a potential design event, not a default feature.

I ask: do any adjacent letters have natural connection points? Where do terminal strokes reach toward the next letter? Where do two ascending or descending elements create natural overlap?

Then I sketch the possible connections and evaluate. A ligature has to feel motivated — the connection should look like it was meant to happen, not forced. An unnatural ligature reads as a mistake.

## The Letters That Ligature Most Naturally

Some letter combinations invite ligatures more than others:

Adjacent letters sharing horizontal strokes (T-E, H-I, E-T) can connect across their crossbars. Letters with descenders and ascenders that happen to be adjacent in a word can link vertically. Round letters (O, C, G, Q) can blend into adjacent curves.

Script and calligraphic typefaces have ligature potential built into their design — the flowing strokes naturally extend toward adjacent letters. Geometric and grotesque typefaces require more deliberate design to create ligatures that feel native to the letterforms.

## Discretionary Ligatures in Print

In high-quality print work — book design, editorial work, premium marketing materials — discretionary ligatures add a layer of typographic refinement that rewards attention. Headlines set with all available ligatures have a different texture from the same text set without them.

This is micro-typography: details that most readers won't consciously notice but that collectively give the typography a crafted quality. Like optical kerning or precise leading, ligatures in print work are part of the invisible craft that distinguishes thoughtful typesetting from automatic typesetting.

## When Not to Use Them

Ligatures in body text can interrupt reading if they're too visually prominent — the connection becomes a distraction rather than an enhancement. Save the expressive ones for display sizes.

Also: never use ligatures in contexts where letters might be read individually — monospaced text, code, tracking numbers, serial numbers. The ligature collapses individual character identity, which is sometimes a feature and sometimes a very serious problem.

Know what you're connecting and why. Intentional ligature use is sophisticated. Accidental ligature use is a production error.
