## Where Graphics and Space Meet

Environmental graphic design (EGD) is the practice of applying graphic communication to the built environment. Wayfinding systems, wall graphics, experiential installations, brand environments in retail and office spaces — this is where brand identity escapes the flat surface and inhabits three-dimensional space.

It's a discipline that requires collaborating with architects and interior designers, understanding material and fabrication constraints, and thinking about how people move through and experience space — not just how they look at it.

## Scale and Proportion in Three Dimensions

The fundamental design challenge in EGD is scale. You're designing graphics that will exist alongside architecture — walls, floors, ceilings, columns — at sizes ranging from small plaques to full building wraps.

The proportion of graphic elements to architectural elements needs to be worked out in the actual space, not just on a screen. I use physical mockups and on-site visits to calibrate scale decisions. What looks overwhelming in a rendering may feel appropriately bold in the actual space.

## Material Selection as Design

In EGD, materials are design decisions. Vinyl graphics, dimensional letters, painted surfaces, printed and mounted panels, LED, back-lit graphics, etched metal, poured concrete with embedded color — each material communicates something and performs differently over time.

The durability requirements of different spaces shape material choices: a high-traffic retail environment needs materials that withstand physical contact; an exterior installation needs UV and weather resistance; a corporate lobby needs materials that maintain their appearance over years of use.

## Collaboration with the Build Team

EGD projects require close collaboration with the installation teams, architects, and contractors who will fabricate and install the work. Design decisions that seem simple may have fabrication implications that need to be worked out before they become expensive surprises.

I involve fabricators early in the process and treat their input as genuine design input, not just production logistics.

## The Brand System in Space

Good EGD takes the brand's visual identity and translates it into spatial language. The brand's colors, typography, graphic patterns, and visual personality need to be expressed in three-dimensional terms that feel coherent with the two-dimensional identity work.

This translation is often the hardest part. A flat graphic language doesn't always translate directly into spatial application. New rules and applications may need to be developed — always in service of the same brand character, but appropriate to the medium.

## The Experience Over Time

Unlike a printed piece that's experienced at one moment, an environment is experienced over time as people move through it. A good EGD solution changes as you move — revealing more, providing different information at different points, contributing to a journey through the space.

Designing for movement and time is a different mode of thinking from designing for a fixed viewing position.
