Logo design trends cycle through visual culture the same way fashion trends do, and with similar consequences: work produced at the height of a trend looks dated within a few years, and the brands that chased the trend are left with identities that signal "we were paying attention to design in 2019" rather than anything meaningful about who they are.

I've seen this play out repeatedly. The extreme minimization trend of the early 2010s produced logos stripped of all character and detail, most of which have since been quietly revised. The gradient trend of the mid-2010s produced thousands of purple-to-orange logos that have aged exactly as poorly as predicted. The neo-brutalist typography trend is currently producing work that will likely look dated before the end of the decade.

## Why Trend-Chasing Happens

Clients are exposed to trend-driven design work constantly. It shows up in their Instagram feeds, in design awards coverage, in the portfolios of agencies they've talked to. It looks current. It looks like what "good design" looks like right now.

Designers follow trends for their own reasons: to stay culturally current, to get published in design press that favors the trendy, to attract clients who want the current look.

Both motivations are understandable. Neither produces durable brand identity work.

## The Specific Failure Modes

**Trend as concept.** The trend becomes the design rationale rather than a genuine strategic response to the brand brief. "We're using a gradient because that's what premium brands look like right now" is not a concept. It's a fashion statement.

**Trend as differentiation.** Using a current trend to stand out from competitors works for approximately eighteen months, until every competitor has also adopted the trend and the differentiation has evaporated.

**Trend over function.** Trend-driven designs sometimes sacrifice functional requirements to achieve the aesthetic. Overly simplified logos that don't work at small scale. Thin, refined typefaces that fail in challenging reproduction environments. Light color palettes that fail contrast requirements.

## What to Do Instead

The alternative to trend-chasing is concept-driven design that happens to be executed in a contemporary way. The concept is derived from brand strategy: what this brand is, what it believes, who it's for. The execution uses current design craft and tools but isn't determined by current fashion.

The resulting work may incorporate elements that are aesthetically contemporary because contemporary design craft is what I know how to do. But the conceptual foundation is brand-specific rather than trend-specific. When the trend cycles away, the concept remains.

The test: can I articulate why this logo looks the way it looks in terms that have nothing to do with current trends? If yes, the work probably has lasting potential. If the only explanation is "it feels current" or "this is what premium brands are doing right now," it will age.

## The Honest Conversation With Clients

Clients who want a trendy logo need to hear, respectfully, that trend-following has a cost. I have this conversation directly. I show them examples of work from five years ago that looked exactly like what they're describing wanting, and ask them how that work looks now.

Most clients, when they understand the tradeoff, prefer work that will represent them well over a long period. Some still want the trendy look. In those cases, I execute it as well as I can while documenting the conversation. But I try not to let trend-chasing happen by default.

Logos last a long time. Design them for it.
