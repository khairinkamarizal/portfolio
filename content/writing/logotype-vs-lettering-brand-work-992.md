## Two Different Disciplines

Logotype design and lettering are often conflated. Both involve letterforms. Both result in brand name treatments. But they're built from different starting points, use different tools, and produce work with different qualities.

Understanding the distinction helps me recommend the right approach for each project and set appropriate expectations about process, timeline, and outcome.

## What a Logotype Is

A logotype is a wordmark built from an existing typeface with modifications. The starting point is a typeface — a system of letterforms designed for consistent use across many words and contexts. The logotype development process involves selecting the typeface, applying it to the brand name, and modifying it to varying degrees.

The modifications might be minimal — optical kerning corrections, slight weight adjustments. They might be more substantial — letterform customizations, modified terminals, adjusted proportions. At the extreme end, the original typeface is barely recognizable in the finished logotype.

The advantage of starting from a typeface: the internal consistency of the letterform system gives the logotype a baseline quality and coherence. The letterforms were designed to work together.

## What Custom Lettering Is

Custom lettering starts from scratch. The letterer draws each letterform from the first mark, with no typeface as the starting point. Every letterform is designed specifically for this word, in this sequence, for this brand.

This produces letterforms that are optimized for the specific combination of letters in the brand name. The curves flow from one letter to the next in ways that no typeface can achieve, because typefaces are designed for general use and lettering is designed for specific use.

Custom lettering can achieve organic connections, expressive variation, and gestural qualities that modified typefaces rarely match. It also takes significantly longer and costs significantly more.

## When Logotype Is the Right Choice

For most brand identity projects, starting from a typeface is the right approach. Good typefaces provide excellent letterform quality, the modification process is efficient, and the result is professional and distinctive.

I recommend logotype development (typeface-based) when: the brand name benefits from the systematic quality of typeface-based letterforms, the timeline and budget don't support lettering development, and there's a suitable typeface that aligns with the brand's character.

## When Custom Lettering Is Worth It

I recommend custom lettering when: no existing typeface produces the specific quality the brand needs, the brand's character demands a visual uniqueness that's only achievable through drawing, or the brand has sufficient budget and timeline to do it well.

A craft beverage brand that wants letterforms that feel genuinely hand-drawn, not like a digitized imitation of hand-lettering. A heritage institution that needs letter forms with a specific historical character that no current typeface replicates. A brand where the letterform details are central to the identity's distinctiveness.

## The Middle Path: Lettering-Informed Typeface Modification

The approach I use most often for clients who want the quality of lettering without the full lettering process is starting from the typeface and modifying it more extensively than typical logotype development.

I take the typeface as a structural foundation, then draw into the letterforms significantly — modifying curves, extending or truncating strokes, adding details that are derived from hand lettering conventions — until the result has the visual quality of lettering while maintaining the structural integrity of the typeface starting point.

This takes longer than standard logotype development and requires broader typographic skills, but it produces distinctive results without the full custom lettering timeline and cost.

## The Quality Indicator

The question that determines the approach: does the best existing typeface produce a result I believe in for this brand? If yes, logotype development. If no, either more extensive modification or custom lettering. The answer depends on the brief, the budget, and my honest assessment of what the brand deserves.
