Most designers treat the grid as infrastructure — invisible scaffolding you set up and then cover over with actual design. I've come to see it differently. The grid, when used consciously, is itself a form of expression.

## The Swiss International Style Legacy

The grid-based poster tradition runs from the Swiss International Typographic Style of the 1950s through Josef Müller-Brockmann's concert posters to today's editorial design. These works don't hide their grids — they expose them. The grid becomes the aesthetic.

Müller-Brockmann's Musica Viva posters are the canonical example: the grid as a system of rhythmic intervals, with type and geometric forms existing in deliberate relationship to that rhythm. The poster doesn't feel gridded because the grid is invisible; it feels gridded because the grid is the point.

## When to Show the Grid

I ask myself on every poster project: is the grid a support system or is it the idea? For functional posters — event information, exhibition announcements, wayfinding — the grid is support. For more conceptual or expressive work, the grid can become the idea.

When the grid is the idea, I sometimes make it visible: printing the grid lines, using the grid as a color field, or allowing the underlying structure to dictate the composition so completely that its presence is unmistakable even without visible lines.

## Breaking the Grid to Confirm It

I mentioned in my earlier writing that a grid is most powerful when broken once. I want to expand on that. The break works because the violation is *readable* as a violation — which means the viewer must first perceive the grid as a system. You can't break a grid the viewer doesn't see.

This means establishing the grid clearly and consistently before introducing the break. If the composition is grid-conforming in 80% of its elements, the 20% that breaks the grid reads as intentional. If the grid is never clearly established, nothing reads as a break — it just reads as inconsistency.

## Proportional Systems

Beyond the standard column grid, proportional systems based on classical ratios (golden ratio, root-2 rectangle, Fibonacci sequence) give posters a harmonic quality that's felt rather than analyzed. I don't use these as rigid constraints, but as starting points for dimension decisions.

A poster sized to a root-2 rectangle (the proportion of A-series paper) has a built-in harmonic relationship between the page proportion and any rectangle divided from it. These relationships create a sense of rightness that pure intuition can struggle to produce consistently.

## My Setup Process

Grid first, content second. I spend time at the beginning of every poster project building the grid in InDesign or Illustrator before I touch any content. Column count, gutter width, baseline grid, margin size — all decided in relation to each other and in relation to the content requirements. Once the grid is set, I don't change it. Constraints are generative.
