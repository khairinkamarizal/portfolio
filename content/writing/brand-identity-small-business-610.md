I've worked on brand identities for businesses of every size, from solo freelancers to mid-sized companies to enterprises. The scope creep problem is real at every level, but it hits small businesses hardest. A startup doesn't need a 200-page brand guidelines document. They need a working identity system they can actually use.

Here's how I scope identity work for small businesses in a way that serves them rather than billing them.

## Start With the Real Use Cases

Before I design anything, I ask: where will this brand live in the first 12 months? For most small businesses, the honest answer is: a website, some social media profiles, maybe business cards, possibly some printed collateral like a menu or brochure, and packaging if it's a product business.

That list shapes everything. A brand that lives primarily on screen has different requirements than one that needs to work on kraft paper packaging, or on vehicle signage, or on embroidered workwear. I design for the actual use cases, not a theoretical comprehensive system.

## The Minimum Viable Brand Kit

For a small business client, I won't deliver less than:

**Logo system:** Primary logo, a secondary configuration (stacked or icon-only), and a monochrome version. Three files, multiple formats (SVG, PNG transparent, PDF).

**Color palette:** A primary brand color, a neutral, and one accent. With exact specifications in all relevant color modes.

**Typography:** A primary typeface for headings and display use, a secondary for body text. I almost always recommend typefaces available on Google Fonts or Adobe Fonts so the client can actually use them without licensing headaches.

**Basic usage guidance:** A one-page reference, not a novel. Where the logo works, what to do with color, which fonts for which contexts.

That's it. I can deliver this in a format a non-designer can actually reference and use.

## What Can Come Later

Extensive pattern libraries, photographic direction, iconography systems, comprehensive do/don't examples — all of that can be developed as the business grows and the needs become clearer. Building it all upfront for a business that might pivot or not survive year two is wasteful for everyone.

I'd rather deliver something lean and excellent than something comprehensive and ignored.

## The Client's Internal Capacity Matters

One question I always ask: who's going to be making collateral day-to-day? If it's the owner using Canva, the brand system needs to translate to that environment. If they have an in-house designer or agency relationship, it can be more sophisticated.

A beautiful brand system that no one can execute is worse than a simpler system that gets applied consistently. Consistency is the most powerful brand-building force available to a small business, and a system that feels manageable is one that will actually be used consistently.

## Pricing and Scope

I scope small business identity at a level that gives me enough time to do the work well and gives them enough deliverables to build from. I'm not interested in selling complexity. I'm interested in building something that works. When I frame it that way to clients, the projects almost always go better.
