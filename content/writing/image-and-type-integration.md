The worst poster cliché is a beautiful photograph with type dropped awkwardly in the corner, white with a slight shadow. The image and the type are coexisting, not integrated. The viewer sees them separately, and the design reads like two ideas stapled together.

Integration is the work. Getting image and type to function as a unified thing — that's what separates a poster from a flyer.

## The Relationship Determines the Approach

Before I think about placement, I ask: what is the relationship between this image and this text? Are they saying the same thing? Contradicting each other? Is the image context for the text, or is the text clarifying the image?

A photograph of a performer and the performer's name are redundant — they're saying the same thing. That redundancy gives me freedom to overlap them, to let the type cut across the image, to make them literally merge. When image and text are the same message, the design can reflect that unity.

When they're in tension — an unexpected juxtaposition — I want visible distance and contrast. I want the viewer to feel the gap between them.

## Zones and Contrast

If you're placing type over an image, contrast is survival. I create contrast three ways: value, color, and isolation.

Value contrast: type goes over a light area on a light background, or a dark area on a dark background — never the midtones, where everything disappears.

Color contrast: a colored type field over a neutral image area, or a knockout where the image shows through a colored type block.

Isolation: a solid color band, shape, or field that lifts the type away from the image entirely. Not a shadow. A deliberate shape.

## Letting Type Break the Frame

One of my favorite poster techniques is letting display type bleed beyond the image boundary — starting on the image, continuing on a flat color field, or vice versa. This treats image and type as two layers of the same visual field, not two separate elements.

It requires precise optical alignment. The type can't look like it accidentally wandered off the image. It has to look like it owns the whole space.

## Scale as a Tool

Scale disparity between image and type can be a feature, not a bug. A tiny image in a field of enormous type creates a particular tension — the image becomes a detail within a typographic landscape. An enormous image with restrained, small type creates a cinematic quality where the image dominates and text serves as caption.

Neither approach is wrong. Both are intentional. The mistake is choosing a scale relationship for no reason, which usually means defaulting to "headline big, image medium, subhead small" — the most generic hierarchy possible.

## Working With Illustration

Illustrated posters have more flexibility because the image is already designed. A skilled illustrator creates visual weight and empty space intentionally. I collaborate with illustrators early in a project to identify where the type will live before the illustration is finished. Getting the composition right from the start is far better than trying to retrofit type into a completed image.
