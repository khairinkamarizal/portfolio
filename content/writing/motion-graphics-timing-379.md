I spent the first year of my motion work obsessing over keyframes and easing curves. The technically correct Bezier handles. The mathematically smooth transitions. And the work felt mechanical. It wasn't until I started thinking about timing as rhythm — as music — that things started to click.

## The Basics Nobody Talks About

Every motion graphic has a tempo. Fast cuts feel urgent. Slow reveals feel considered. The tempo has to match the content. An animated logo reveal for a luxury brand should breathe. The same approach applied to a sports broadcast package would read as boring.

I establish the tempo before I build anything. If there's music, I cut to it. If there's no music, I find a reference track that matches the intended energy and use it as a temp during production. Tempo is the first decision, not an afterthought.

## Easing: The Soul of Motion

Linear motion — constant velocity from point A to point B — looks robotic because nothing in the physical world moves that way. Everything accelerates and decelerates. Easing curves simulate that physics.

But I don't use preset easing without adjusting it. The default "ease in/ease out" in After Effects is a starting point, not a solution. I edit the velocity graph by hand to get the exact feel I want — a fast snap with a slow settle, or a gradual build with a decisive end. The curve is the personality of the motion.

## Hold Frames and Stillness

One of the most powerful tools in motion is stopping. A hold frame — a moment where everything freezes — resets the viewer's attention and creates emphasis. After a complex animation sequence, a clean hold frame gives the eye somewhere to rest.

I think of still moments as punctuation. Movement is the sentence. The hold is the period. Without holds, motion becomes relentless and exhausting to watch.

## Stagger for Depth

When multiple elements animate in together, staggering their start times by small intervals creates a sense of depth and dimension. Elements that all start simultaneously read as flat. Stagger them by 2–4 frames and the sequence suddenly has a sense of physical space.

This technique works for logo element reveals, type animations, data visualizations — anything with multiple components entering the frame.

## Looping With Intention

A lot of my motion work loops. Social media content, broadcast holding patterns, digital signage. A good loop is invisible — the viewer shouldn't be able to identify the cut point. This requires designing the motion so the end state and start state are close enough that the transition reads as continuous.

I always preview loops in real-time playback for at least thirty seconds before I sign off. What looks fine as a single pass often becomes obviously wrong when you watch it repeat.
