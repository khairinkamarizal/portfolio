## Bold is not automatically strong

There's a reflex in graphic design — particularly poster design — to reach for the heaviest available weight when visual impact is the goal. Bold type. Black weight. Ultra compressed. The assumption is that heavier means stronger, more impactful, more authoritative.

It doesn't. Heavy type is one tool for visual weight, not the only one. And used as a reflex rather than a deliberate choice, heavy type produces designs that shout without saying anything — loud, undifferentiated, visually fatiguing.

## What makes type feel powerful

Visual power in typography comes from the relationship between the type and its environment, not from the weight of the type itself. A single lightweight word set in 200pt on a field of white space has enormous visual authority — more, often, than a page packed with heavy bold type.

Isolation creates power. Scale creates power. Contrast creates power. Heavy weight creates one kind of visual weight, but it requires contrast to function — heavy type on a heavy background, or surrounded by other heavy elements, loses all its force.

## The fashion for heavy type

Contemporary design culture has a fashion for heavy, compressed type — especially in poster and brand work. This fashion produces a certain visual energy and confidence, and it's appropriate for many contexts. But fashion-following is not design thinking, and applying heavy type as a default style choice rather than a contextual decision produces work that dates itself to a specific era.

The designers whose work ages well are the ones who make weight choices based on the communication requirement, not the prevailing trend.

## When light or thin type is the bolder choice

Some of the most visually striking typographic work I've done uses the lightest available weights — hairline, thin, extra-light — at maximum scale. A company name set in ultra-thin at 300pt, tracked widely across a full poster, communicates a kind of luxury and confidence that heavy type can't match.

This approach works because it subverts the expectation of what "strong" looks like. It says: we're so confident in our presence that we don't need to shout it. The visual restraint itself communicates sophistication.

## Matching weight to content

The right approach is matching weight to content rather than weight to style. An urgent public health message might genuinely benefit from heavy, immediately readable type. A luxury fragrance launch poster might genuinely benefit from hairline type with maximum white space. A youth-focused music event probably benefits from heavy compressed type. A law firm probably doesn't.

The question I ask before setting weight: what does this weight communicate, and does that communication serve what the content is actually saying?
