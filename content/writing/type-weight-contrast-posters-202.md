Weight contrast is my most-used typographic tool. More than size, more than color, more than spacing — the relationship between a heavy weight and a light weight does something to a composition that nothing else quite replicates. It creates visual rhythm, implies hierarchy, and generates tension in a way that feels effortless when it's working.

I've been designing posters seriously for about eight years, and I can trace most of the posters I'm genuinely proud of back to a bold weight-to-weight decision made early in the process.

## Why Weight Works

Human eyes are drawn to density. A black slab of letterforms at 900 weight pulls attention like a magnet. But contrast requires a counterpart — the heaviness only reads as heavy when there's something light to push against. This is the fundamental mechanic: weight is a relational quality, not an absolute one.

When I set a headline in an ultra-black condensed face and then follow it with body text in a thin or light weight of the same family, the visual snap is immediate. The reader's eye lands on the heavy element, absorbs it, and then softens into the detail. It's a rhythm — loud, then quiet.

## Working Within a Single Family

The most cohesive approach I've found is using a wide-ranging type family — something with a genuine range from Thin to Black. TypeType, Grilli Type, and Klim all make families with this kind of breadth. When you're pulling weights from the same design, the letterforms share proportions and details that hold the composition together even when the weight gap is extreme.

I used to mix separate typefaces to get contrast — pairing a display serif with a sans — and I still do that for some projects. But when I want the contrast to feel like it's coming from inside the design rather than imposed on it, single-family weight pairing is the move.

## The Mistake: Using Too Many Weights

Here's where a lot of designers lose the plot. They find a typeface with eight weights and use six of them. The contrast disappears because there's no clear anchor. Every weight is in conversation with seven others and none of them wins.

I limit myself to three weights per poster, and usually two. Heavy and light, or heavy, medium, and light used very sparingly. The discipline of the constraint forces stronger decisions.

## Setting the Ratio

When I pair weights, I think about the ratio of heavy to light coverage on the page. A poster that's 80% ultra-black and 20% thin reads very differently from one that's 80% thin with a single heavy word as the focal point. Both work. But I decide this deliberately before I start placing type.

For event posters where energy and volume matter, I tend toward more heavy coverage. For editorial or cultural institution work, I'll often let the thin or light weight dominate and use the heavy element as a sharp interruption.

## Testing at Actual Size

Weight contrast decisions made on screen at 50% zoom can fall apart when you see the poster printed. What looked bold enough on screen reads as medium-weight at print scale. I now do low-fidelity print tests early — even just printing on letter paper at reduced scale — to check whether the weight relationships are holding up.

The contrast should feel a little aggressive in the file. If it looks "right" on screen, it's probably going to look flat on the wall.

Commit to the contrast. Exaggerate it. Let the weights fight.
