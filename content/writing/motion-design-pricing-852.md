## Pricing Motion Is Different

Motion design is harder to price than static design because the variables are so much more complex. A logo design has fairly predictable scope. A thirty-second motion piece can range from a simple kinetic text treatment to a fully animated scene with custom character animation — a tenfold difference in production time for the same deliverable spec.

I learned this the hard way early in my freelance practice, quoting flat rates for motion work that I had wildly underestimated.

## How I Break Down a Motion Brief

When I receive a motion brief, I decompose it into components before estimating:

**Concept and storyboard**: strategy, creative direction, storyboard frames. Usually one to two days depending on complexity.

**Design**: creating the visual assets — backgrounds, graphic elements, type treatments, any illustration. This varies enormously based on visual complexity.

**Animation**: building and animating the compositions in After Effects. Usually the largest time component.

**Sound design and music licensing**: if applicable. I include this in my scope if I'm handling it; separate it if the client is providing assets.

**Revision cycles**: I build one round of revisions into every quote. Additional rounds are quoted separately.

## The Per-Second Myth

Some clients expect motion work to be priced per second of finished video. This is not a useful framework — the relationship between duration and production time is non-linear.

A fifteen-second brand animation with complex character movement and custom illustration takes far longer than a sixty-second explainer with simple kinetic text. Duration is one factor among many, not a pricing unit.

## Day Rate vs. Project Rate

For clients I work with regularly, I quote a day rate. They know what they're getting, I know what I'm being paid, and scope changes are handled transparently.

For new clients and fixed-scope projects, I quote project rates. These require more careful scoping upfront, but they give the client price certainty, which is often what they need to get budget approval.

## Revisions, Scope Creep, and the Estimate

The estimate is a scope document, not just a price. I write estimates that describe specifically what is included: the number of scenes, the revision rounds, the deliverable formats. When clients request changes outside that scope, I refer back to the estimate and quote the additional work.

This isn't about being rigid — it's about having a shared understanding of what was agreed. Most scope creep isn't malicious; clients just don't always know what they're asking for when they request "just one more change." A clear estimate makes those conversations factual rather than contentious.
