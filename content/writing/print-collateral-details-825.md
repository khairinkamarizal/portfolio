## Bleed Is Not Optional

Every new designer makes the no-bleed mistake. They set up a document at exactly the final trimmed size, design to the edges, and send the file. The print comes back with white slivers along some edges where the cut landed slightly off.

Bleed exists because mechanical cutting is not pixel-perfect. A 3mm bleed on all sides means any background color, image, or pattern that extends to the edge of the document extends 3mm past the trim line, providing tolerance for cutting variation.

If your design has color going to the edge, your file needs bleed. No exceptions.

## Safe Zone for Critical Content

The inverse of bleed is the safe zone — an inner boundary inside which all critical content should sit. I keep all text, logos, and important graphic elements at least 4–5mm from the trim edge. More for saddle-stitched books, where page creep affects the inner margins.

Content that sits too close to the trim edge might get cut. This is especially relevant for borders — a thin border that runs close to the edge will look uneven if one side gets trimmed slightly more than another.

## Paper Grain Direction

This one catches people who only think in digital. Paper has a grain direction — the direction the fibers run, determined by the manufacturing process. For books and folded documents, you want the fold to run parallel to the grain. Folding against the grain causes cracking and a stiff, resistant feel.

For flat pieces like postcards and business cards, grain direction affects how the piece handles and feels. Shorter grain feels stiffer against the narrow dimension.

If you're specifying a print job, ask your printer about grain direction for your application. Most won't volunteer this information unless you ask.

## Color Management in Print Files

I export print PDFs with color set to CMYK and all RGB elements converted. Images should be CMYK or at least converted at export. Fonts should be embedded, not subset unless file size is a constraint.

For PDF/X compliance — the standard press-ready format — use PDF/X-1a for maximum compatibility or PDF/X-4 for transparency support. Check with your printer which they prefer.

## The Pre-Flight Check

Before sending any file to print I run a preflight check — in Acrobat or InDesign's built-in preflight tool — that verifies: bleed is present, no RGB images, fonts are embedded, document color space is CMYK, resolution is sufficient for the output size.

Running this check takes five minutes and catches the errors that would otherwise result in a reprinted job. A reprinted job costs more money and more time than the preflight check.

## The Print Relationship

The most underrated asset in print production work is a printer relationship. A printer you've worked with before knows your standards, flags potential issues before production rather than after, and will go to bat to make something right when a job doesn't meet expectations.

I send work to the same printers repeatedly when the relationship and quality are right. The savings in time, stress, and reprints more than justify the loyalty.
