## More Than Pretty Colors

I've reviewed a lot of brand guidelines over my career. One of the most common problems I see is a color section that amounts to "here are some colors we like." There's no hierarchy, no role definition, no guidance on how to use them together. The palette is decorative rather than functional.

A real brand color palette is a system. Each color has a job.

## The Core Colors

Every brand palette needs at least one core color — the color most associated with the brand, the one that appears most consistently across all touchpoints. This is not the most exciting color in the palette; it's the most reliable one. It should be distinctive enough to be recognizable but versatile enough to work across a wide range of applications.

Some brands have two core colors (black and red for Coca-Cola; blue and yellow for IKEA). That's fine, as long as the relationship between them is clear and consistent.

## Supporting and Accent Colors

Supporting colors expand the palette's range. They provide variety without undermining consistency. I think of them as secondary — they appear in specific contexts (seasonal campaigns, sub-brands, specific product lines) but not as the default brand color.

Accent colors are used most sparingly. They're high-saturation or high-contrast options that create emphasis and focus attention. Used everywhere, they lose their impact. Used selectively, they create hierarchy and energy.

## Neutral Infrastructure

Every palette also needs neutral colors — whites, off-whites, grays, and often a near-black. These are the infrastructure of the system. They provide backgrounds, breathing room, and contrast against which the brand colors read. Their importance tends to be underestimated.

A well-chosen neutral is not generic. It can carry warmth or coolness, organic quality or technical precision, through its undertone and temperature.

## Color Ratios

How much of each color appears in a typical application matters. An 80-20 ratio of core to accent creates a different feel from 50-30-20 across three colors. I document color ratios in brand guidelines so that designers working on different materials make consistent proportional decisions.

Without ratio guidance, individual designers make individual decisions, and the brand fragments.

## Testing the System Under Stress

A color palette that looks harmonious as swatches may behave badly in application. I test palettes in the actual contexts the brand uses — digital layouts, print collateral, signage mockups, merchandise templates — before finalizing. Colors that work as swatches sometimes clash in application when used in the proportions the design requires.

The test is not "do these colors look nice together?" The test is "does this system work in every context this brand needs?"
