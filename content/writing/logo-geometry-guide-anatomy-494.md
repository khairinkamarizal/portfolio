A logo geometry guide is a technical diagram that documents the mathematical and geometric relationships that underlie a logo mark's construction. It shows the circles, grids, angles, and proportional relationships that define the mark's form.

Not every logo needs one. A simple wordmark set in an existing typeface with modest modifications doesn't require a geometry guide. A mark constructed from a custom geometric system absolutely does.

## What a Geometry Guide Contains

A complete geometry guide documents:

**The construction grid.** The underlying grid or modular system from which the mark's proportions are derived. If the mark was built on a unit circle, the circle is shown. If it was built on a golden rectangle, the rectangle and its derivations are shown.

**The circles and arcs.** Every curve in the mark identified by the circle it belongs to, with that circle shown at its full extent and the arc labeled as a fraction or portion of the parent circle.

**Proportional relationships.** Key measurements expressed as ratios or multiples of the base unit. The height of the mark is 2x the base unit. The stroke weight is 0.15x the mark width. These relationships allow the mark to be correctly reconstructed at any scale.

**Angular specifications.** Any angles in the mark expressed in degrees. Not approximate angles, but the precise values derived from the construction logic.

**Clearance and spacing specifications.** The minimum clearance zone expressed in terms of the mark's own geometry (e.g., "clearance equals the height of the cap letter in the wordmark").

## Why Geometry Guides Matter

**Reproduction accuracy.** When a vendor needs to recreate the mark for a specific application (embroidery, signage, engraving) and the vector file can't be used directly, the geometry guide provides the precision information needed to reconstruct the mark correctly.

**Legal protection.** In trademark disputes, a geometry guide can demonstrate that a mark was designed with deliberate specificity rather than assembled from generic shapes. It establishes the intellectual property of the construction logic.

**Future design work.** When the identity needs to expand into new applications, new designers can use the geometry guide to derive additional brand elements that are consistent with the mark's logic. Pattern systems, additional marks, and graphic devices can all be designed from the same geometric foundation.

**Client confidence.** Presenting a geometry guide alongside a logo design demonstrates the rigor of the process. It shows that the mark wasn't produced by clicking around in software until something looked right. It was constructed from a specific geometric system that can be articulated and documented.

## How I Build Them

The geometry guide is built in a separate Illustrator document that shows the construction geometry overlaid on the mark, then the mark alone. I use a consistent visual language: thin construction lines in a neutral color, the mark itself in black, dimensioning lines and labels in a contrasting color.

The guide is typically delivered as a PDF with the logo package. I label every geometric element clearly and add annotation text explaining the key proportional relationships.

The guide should be readable by someone who wasn't involved in the design process. If it requires the designer to explain it verbally to make sense, it's not complete.

## The Honest Admission

Some marks that look geometric are actually drawn by eye and then rationalized. The geometry guide for a drawn mark is usually sparse: a circle approximating one curve, a proportional ratio for one relationship. The geometry is post-hoc rather than foundational.

I prefer to design marks from genuine geometric foundations because the resulting geometry guides are richer and the marks are more precisely constructed. But I won't fabricate elaborate geometric justifications for marks that were actually drawn by feel. The guide should document what's actually there.
