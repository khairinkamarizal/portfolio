## The Timid Poster

The most common failure mode in poster design is timidity. The designer sets the headline at maybe twice the size of the subheading, the subheading at maybe twice the body copy. Everything reads, everything is legible, and nothing commands attention. The poster politely communicates its information and is immediately forgotten.

Posters live in competitive visual environments. A concert poster is on a wall next to six other concert posters. A retail promotion poster competes with a dozen other pieces of signage in the same aisle. The only way to win that competition is to be visually louder than everything around you, and the most reliable way to do that is with extreme typographic scale contrast.

## What Extreme Scale Contrast Actually Looks Like

When I say extreme, I mean a ratio of at least 8:1 between the largest and second-largest typographic elements. A headline at 120pt against a subheading at 14pt. A single word at 200pt against body copy at 9pt. A number — a year, a time, a price — at 300pt against everything else at human-scale sizes.

This feels wrong when you first try it. The big element seems too big. The small elements seem too small. You resist it because it violates every instinct from working in document design, where comfortable proportions rule.

Put it on a wall. Step back ten feet. Now the proportions make sense. The big element is readable from across the room. The smaller elements are readable from arm's length. Each piece of information is accessible at the distance appropriate to how important it is.

## Building a Poster Around One Scale Decision

I design every poster around a single dominant scale decision made before anything else. What is the one thing that must be immediately readable from the farthest viewing distance? That's my first element, and I set it as large as the format allows, constrained only by the line breaks and the overall composition.

Everything else on the poster is organized in relation to that first element. The size of each subsequent piece of information is set by its importance, not by trying to fill space or achieve visual balance.

## Mixing Typefaces at Extreme Scales

Scale contrast works with a single typeface, but it becomes even more powerful when paired with a typeface change. A condensed display face at 200pt with a light, wide typeface at 12pt creates contrast in scale, weight, and width simultaneously. The eye reads these as clearly different categories of information without needing visual dividers or boxes.

This is why poster designers have always been drawn to condensed display faces. A condensed typeface at extreme scale takes up horizontal space proportionally, so you can make a headline enormous without it running off the page in two characters.

## When Hierarchy Inverts

One of my favorite poster moves is hierarchy inversion: making a typically secondary piece of information large and the primary headline smaller. A venue name at 180pt, an artist name at 24pt. This works when the piece of information you're making large is itself visually distinctive — the venue has strong recognition, or the date number is inherently graphic.

The inversion creates intrigue. It forces the viewer to resolve the apparent contradiction, which means they engage with the poster more deeply.

## Practical Rule for Poster Type

If every typographic element on your poster is readable at the same distance, your type scale isn't working. Something should be readable from across the room. Something else should require stepping close. That range is what makes a poster a poster.
