Screen-to-print is a translation problem. The medium has different physics, different constraints, different failure modes. I learned most of what I know about print by getting things wrong on press — which is a more expensive education than I'd recommend. Here's what I wish someone had told me earlier.

## CMYK Is Not RGB

This is the first thing, and it can't be overstated. Colors that exist in RGB do not all exist in CMYK. The RGB gamut is larger. Highly saturated blues, vivid greens, and certain oranges that look stunning on screen will shift — sometimes dramatically — when converted to CMYK.

Build in CMYK from the start for print work. Or if you design in RGB for client presentation purposes, set up a proofing profile in your design application that simulates CMYK output so you're not surprised when the conversion happens.

## Bleed, Safe Zone, and Trim

Every print file that has elements running to the edge of the page needs bleed: artwork extending 3mm (or 0.125") beyond the trim edge. This compensates for minor variation in cutting. Without bleed, you get white slivers at the edge — a telltale sign of an inexperienced designer.

Equally important: keep critical content (text, logos, faces) inside the safe zone, typically 3–5mm from the trim edge. What looks fine in a digital file can get clipped by the cutter.

## Black: Rich vs. True

For large black areas in print (headers, full-bleed backgrounds), use rich black: 100K + 40C + 30M + 30Y or similar. Straight 100K looks thin and washed out on coated stock.

For small black text, use 100K only. Rich black on small text causes registration issues — the four plates rarely align perfectly, and the slight misregistration makes text look fuzzy.

## Paper Choice Is Part of the Design

Coated paper gives sharp dots and vivid color. Uncoated paper absorbs ink, softens color, and gives a more tactile, warm quality. Matte coated is a middle ground. Uncoated gives photographs a painterly quality that coated makes clinical.

I specify paper as part of every print design conversation with clients. "It'll be printed on whatever the printer has" is how you end up with a luxury brand on glossy brochure stock that looks cheap.

## Proofs Are Non-Negotiable

A digital proof confirms layout. A hard proof (printed on the actual press) confirms color. For any job where color accuracy matters — brand collateral, product packaging, anything with photography — I insist on a hard proof before the full run.

The cost of a hard proof is a rounding error compared to the cost of a 10,000-piece reprint.
