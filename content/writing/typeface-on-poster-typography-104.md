## Poster Type is a Different Discipline

I've been designing for screens long enough to know the habits it builds. When I move back to poster work, I have to consciously unlearn some of them. Screen typography is built for readability at close range, for flowing text, for responsive behavior. Poster typography is built for impact, distance, and a single frozen moment.

The typefaces, sizes, weights, and arrangements that work on screen often fall flat at poster scale. And vice versa.

## Weight and Contrast Under Pressure

The first thing I think about when selecting a typeface for a poster is how it performs under optical pressure. Large scale magnifies every design decision in a typeface — for better or worse. Thin strokes that look elegant at 12pt can look fragile and spindly at 120pt. Heavy weights that read as bold in body copy can look oppressive as a headline.

I look for typefaces with stroke contrast that holds up. Optical sizing — the practice of adjusting type design for different size ranges — matters here. Some type families include display cuts designed for large use. I use them.

## Setting Type for Distance

A poster often gets read from across a room, not up close. That changes how I approach spacing. Tracking (letter-spacing) that looks correct at close range can look cramped from distance. I open up tracking at large sizes, even when my instincts say it's too loose.

Leading is less of an issue with large display type — you're often working with one or two lines — but when headlines run long, I pay close attention to how the eye moves between lines.

## The Hierarchy Has to Be Obvious

Poster hierarchy must communicate instantly. The viewer's eye needs to know exactly where to land first, second, and third without any effort on their part. If I have to explain the hierarchy to someone looking at a comp, it's not working.

I use scale more aggressively in poster work than in almost any other medium. The size difference between the primary headline and secondary information can be enormous — three or four times the size ratio I'd use in a document layout. That contrast is what makes the hierarchy read fast.

## Type as Image

One of the things I love about poster typography is that it can function as image. A single word set in the right typeface, at the right scale, with the right color, becomes a graphic element in its own right — not just text carrying information.

When type reaches that level, it's doing double duty: communicating the message and contributing to the visual composition. That's when poster work gets interesting.

## Testing at Actual Size

I always test poster typography at actual print size before finalizing. What looks resolved on screen, even at a scaled-down preview, can feel completely different when you're standing in front of it. The relationship between type and the physical space around it changes.

Print a proof. Tape it to the wall. Step back. Then decide.
