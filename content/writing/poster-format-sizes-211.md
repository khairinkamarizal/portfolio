Format is a design decision, not a logistical one. The size and orientation of a poster determines how it will be seen, where it will be displayed, how far away the viewer will typically be, and what compositional options are available to the designer. Getting this wrong means fighting the format for the entire project.

I've learned to treat format selection as part of the design brief, not as background information.

## Standard Formats and What They Signal

A0 and A1 are exhibition and event scale — they demand to be seen from a distance and up close. They have the physical presence to anchor a wall. The design needs to work at both scales: an arresting composition from across a room and readable detail up close.

A2 and A3 are utility posters — meeting rooms, corridors, transit advertising, retail. Viewing distance is typically two to five feet. The hierarchy decisions are different from exhibition scale; information density can be higher because the viewer will stand closer.

Letter and A4 "posters" (or flyers, honestly) are handled and read, not viewed from a distance. These are closer to print documents than traditional posters. The design conventions for this format are completely different.

## Landscape vs. Portrait

Portrait is the default poster orientation, and there are good reasons for that — most display contexts (walls, columns, notice boards) favor vertical formats. But landscape can be exactly right for certain applications: theater marquees, horizontal banner formats, some transit advertising.

I question the default orientation on every project. Sometimes landscape creates compositional possibilities that portrait doesn't — particularly for designs where the primary typographic element is a long line of text that would require significant size reduction in portrait to fit.

## The Viewing Distance Problem

Format selection and viewing distance are connected, and I think explicitly about both before I start designing. A poster viewed from six meters looks completely different from one viewed from one meter, even if the content is identical.

At distance, fine detail disappears. Color relationships dominate. Composition is read as silhouette. Type needs to be much larger than it seems necessary at desktop zoom.

I have a rule: for posters meant to be seen from more than three meters, I test my layout at a scale where it appears approximately the same size as it would at actual viewing distance. Usually this means reducing a full-size layout to thumbnail scale and checking it there.

## Square and Unusual Formats

Square formats are underused in poster work and I reach for them when the content or concept warrants it. They have a visual weight and stillness that rectangular formats don't, and they're memorable precisely because they're less common.

Custom formats — 700×1000mm, 50×70cm, unusual aspect ratios — can also be strategic differentiators for cultural and art posters. When every poster at an exhibition is the same A1, a slightly different format already distinguishes itself.

## Constraints as Starting Point

Often format is determined by the print vendor or display context, not by me. Standard billboard ratios, standard transit ad dimensions, venue-specific sizes. In these cases, I treat the constraint as a starting condition and design to it rather than fighting it.

Understanding the format constraint deeply — its proportional logic, its viewing conditions, its relationship to surrounding design in the display context — is often more valuable than having complete freedom. Constraints sharpen decisions.
