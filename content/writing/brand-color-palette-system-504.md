## Color Is a System, Not a Selection

I see a lot of brand decks where the color palette is just five swatches that look pleasant together. No logic. No hierarchy. No explanation of which color carries the brand, which is for emphasis, which is for backgrounds. Just vibes.

That's not a color system. That's a mood board.

A real brand color palette has architecture. Every color has a job. The relationships between colors are intentional. And the whole thing holds up when you actually start applying it to materials.

## The Three-Tier Structure I Use

I organize every brand palette into three tiers:

**Primary** — the brand's signature color. One, maybe two. This is what people will remember and associate. It should be distinctive enough to own — not "blue" but a specific blue that belongs to this brand.

**Secondary** — supporting colors that give the system range. These expand the palette without competing with the primary. They're for backgrounds, dividers, supporting graphics.

**Accent** — the punctuation. High-contrast, used sparingly. These create energy and direct attention. One accent color is usually enough.

Most problems I see in brand systems come from treating secondary and accent colors like they're primaries. Everything becomes equally loud. Nothing lands.

## Choosing Colors for the Right Reasons

There are bad reasons to choose a brand color and good ones.

Bad reasons: "It's my favorite color," "It looks sophisticated," "The founder likes green," "Our competitor uses blue so we should avoid it."

Good reasons: The color communicates the correct emotional temperature for the brand. It's distinctive in the competitive landscape. It works across the media where the brand actually lives. It ages well.

I spend time researching the category before proposing a palette. I want to know what colors dominate the space so I can make a deliberate choice: meet the convention (because it signals trustworthiness) or break it (because the brand needs to stand out).

## Temperature and Saturation

Two variables that matter more than hue: temperature and saturation.

Warm colors feel approachable, energetic, human. Cool colors feel precise, calm, professional. Neither is better — the brand's personality determines which direction to go.

Saturation is about energy. High saturation is loud and punchy. Low saturation is sophisticated and restrained. Again, neither is better. But most brands live in the middle, which is where mediocrity hides. I push toward one end or the other and defend it.

## The Context Test

I never judge a color palette on a white slide with a nice font below it. I put it in context: on a dark background, on a light background, in print, at small sizes, at large sizes.

Some colors that look beautiful in isolation fall apart in application. A golden yellow that looks luxurious in a swatch looks muddy on a textured paper stock. A deep navy that feels serious in print looks heavy and suffocating as a website background.

Context always wins. Design the palette for where the brand actually lives.

## What "Ownable" Means

The goal is a color that says the brand's name before any other element does. When that works, it's a powerful asset. When a brand gets there, color becomes shorthand for everything else the brand stands for.

That kind of recognition takes consistency and time. But it starts with the right choice at the beginning.
