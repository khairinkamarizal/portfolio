## When Mathematically Equal Isn't Visually Equal

One of the things that surprises designers moving into precision mark-making is that mathematically identical measurements don't always look equal. A square and a circle with the same bounding box don't look the same size. Horizontal strokes need to be thinner than vertical strokes at the same measurement to look the same weight. Type set at the exact center of a box needs to be nudged slightly up to feel centered.

These are optical effects — the way the human visual system processes form — and compensating for them is essential to professional logo work.

## The Optical Size Principle

Shapes with different profiles look different sizes at the same mathematical measurements. A circle inscribed in a square looks smaller than the square, even though they share the same bounding dimension. This is because the corners of the square have visual mass that the circle lacks.

In practice, this means circular elements in a mark — circular outer forms, rounded letters — need to extend slightly beyond the cap height baseline to look the same size as rectangular elements. Every major type designer accounts for this. Every logo designer should too.

## Stroke Weight and Direction

The visual weight of a stroke appears heavier when it runs horizontally than when it runs vertically, at the same physical width. This is why traditional serif typefaces have thinner horizontal strokes (the thin strokes) and heavier vertical ones — this variation creates apparent consistency.

When I design marks with uniform stroke weights, I often need to adjust horizontal strokes to be slightly thinner than vertical ones to achieve visual uniformity. This is especially noticeable in geometric letterforms and circular marks.

## Visual Centering vs. Mathematical Centering

A shape placed at the exact mathematical center of a bounding area often appears too low. The visual center of a space is slightly above the mathematical center. To make an element appear centered, I place it slightly above the true center.

Similarly, in a mark with different-weight elements (thick and thin strokes, large and small shapes), mathematical balance may produce visual imbalance. I adjust by eye, using the optical result as the standard.

## Making the Adjustments

These adjustments are made in vector software manually. Auto-align and distribute functions align mathematically. I use them as starting points, then adjust by eye — zooming in and out, viewing at various scales, using my perceptual judgment rather than the numbers.

Building the eye to make these corrections accurately takes practice. It requires overriding the instinct to trust the numbers.

## Why This Matters for Professional Work

Logos without optical corrections look slightly off to trained eyes — not obviously wrong, but somehow not quite right. That vague unresolved quality undermines the work's credibility. Optical corrections are invisible when done correctly, but their absence is perceptible.

These are the details that separate marks that look professional from marks that don't.
