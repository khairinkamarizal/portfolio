The purely typographic poster is one of the most demanding formats in graphic design. No photography to provide visual interest, no illustration to carry the mood, no graphic element to break the monotony of text. Just letterforms, space, and the relationships between them. Everything the poster needs to communicate — information, personality, emotion, hierarchy — has to come from typographic decisions alone.

This constraint is also a liberation. When typography is the only tool, you use it with a depth and intentionality that mixed-media compositions don't require.

## The Typeface Selection Carries Everything

In a typographic poster, the typeface isn't one element among many — it's the whole visual character of the piece. The selection needs to be made with the understanding that the poster will be evaluated primarily as a typographic statement.

This shifts the selection criteria. I'm not asking "does this typeface work well with the image?" I'm asking "what does this typeface say when it's all there is?" The answer involves: the typeface's historical associations, its formal character at large scale, the texture it creates at body text sizes, and its range of weights and styles available for creating hierarchy.

Typefaces with wide families — genuine range from thin to black, condensed to extended — give purely typographic work the most compositional latitude.

## Composition Through Type Alone

In a typographic poster, composition is made entirely through the arrangement, scale, weight, and spacing of text. The elements I have to work with: primary headline type, secondary information, tertiary detail, and the negative space that structures everything.

The compositional principles are the same as any poster — clear hierarchy, considered balance, intentional tension — but every tool for achieving these things is typographic. Scale contrast comes from type size relationships. Visual weight comes from type weight. Rhythm comes from leading and spacing.

## Color as the Single Enhancement

Many of the strongest typographic posters use a single color accent — one element or one background color that breaks the black-on-white default. This single color decision, made thoughtfully, can transform a composition from austere to expressive without introducing any non-typographic elements.

I treat the color decision in typographic posters with the same weight I'd give to the typeface decision. Which element gets color? The primary headline, which makes it shout? A secondary element, which creates unexpected emphasis? The background, which inverts the entire tonal logic?

## Historical Reference

The typographic poster has a rich history to draw from: the Constructivist posters of Rodchenko and Lissitzky, the Swiss modernist work of Müller-Brockmann, the Emigre-era experimental typography of the 1980s and 90s. Each tradition offers a different model for what pure typography can achieve compositionally.

I know this history well enough to work in conversation with it — not to reproduce historical styles, but to understand what formal solutions different approaches have developed and why they work.

## The Discipline Required

Purely typographic work reveals mistakes that imagery can hide. An awkward line break that would be unnoticed in a busy composition becomes the most prominent element when it's surrounded by nothing. A font size that's slightly wrong has no context to absorb it.

The discipline this demands is part of the value. Working without a net teaches you to trust your typographic judgment in a way that mixed-media work doesn't require.
