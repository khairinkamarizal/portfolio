## Accessibility Is Not Optional

Print materials that people can't read are failed communications, regardless of how visually sophisticated they are. Low contrast between text and background is the most common accessibility failure in print design, and it's entirely avoidable.

I apply contrast standards to all my print work, not just to materials explicitly designated as accessible. The audience for any public-facing communication includes people with low vision, color vision deficiencies, and presbyopia. Designing for them benefits everyone.

## Understanding Contrast Ratios

WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) defines contrast ratios as the luminance relationship between foreground and background colors. A ratio of 4.5:1 is the minimum for standard-size text. 3:1 is the minimum for large text (18pt or 14pt bold). 7:1 is the enhanced standard that serves people with significant vision impairments.

These standards were developed for screen contexts, but they're a useful framework for print as well. Print contrast depends on paper stock, ink coverage, and viewing conditions, which means equivalent screen and print contrast ratios don't always produce the same perceived result. Matte uncoated paper absorbs ink and reduces perceived contrast compared to coated stock.

My rule of thumb: meet WCAG AA contrast standards (4.5:1 minimum) in my digital files, then proof on actual paper stock to verify that the print result meets the same practical standard.

## The Color Vision Deficiency Check

About 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of color vision deficiency. The most common is red-green deficiency, which affects the ability to distinguish between reds and greens that have similar luminance values.

I check every design using a color blindness simulation tool before finalizing. Coblis, Color Oracle, and the accessibility tools built into modern design software all provide simulation views. If information that depends on color distinction — a chart that uses red for one data series and green for another, for example — disappears or becomes ambiguous in simulation, I fix it before delivery.

The fix is usually simple: add shape or pattern differentiation alongside color differentiation, or choose colors that differ in luminance as well as hue.

## Type Size and Weight for Older Readers

Print materials for general public audiences — signage, brochures, annual reports — should assume that some readers have age-related vision changes. Presbyopia affects the majority of adults over 45.

My practical minimums for public-facing print: 9pt minimum for any body text, 7pt minimum for captions and footnotes. For materials specifically targeting older audiences — senior services organizations, healthcare providers, government communications — I increase these to 11pt body minimum.

I also avoid long runs of all-caps text, tight tracking, and very light type weights at text sizes. All of these reduce readability for readers with any vision challenges.

## Contrast Beyond Text

Contrast failures in print aren't limited to text. Infographic elements, icons, form fields, and decorative elements can all fail contrast standards in ways that confuse or exclude users.

A light-gray icon on a white background. A pale bar in a chart that's hard to see against the paper. A form field border that disappears on cream paper. These are all contrast failures with practical consequences.

I check contrast for every element that serves a functional purpose, not just text.

## The Proof Catches What Screen Misses

Screen calibration, display brightness, and ambient room lighting all affect how contrast appears on a monitor. A proof on the actual paper stock is the only reliable way to verify that print contrast is meeting standards under real conditions.

I require accessibility-critical print materials — government forms, healthcare instructions, educational materials — to be proofed and accessibility-checked before final approval.
