When clients ask about typography in a brand identity, they usually mean: what font are we using? That's the wrong question — or at least it's only the first question in a much longer conversation.

A brand's typographic system includes typeface selection, hierarchy rules, usage contexts, weight and style applications, size relationships, spacing norms, and the principles governing how all these elements work together across every application. The typeface choice is the starting point, not the whole answer.

## The System Thinking Shift

In my early career, I designed brand typography the way most designers do: pick a strong typeface, maybe pair it with a secondary, deliver the specimen. The problem emerged in execution — clients using the typeface in ways I hadn't specified, creating typographic treatments that used the right typeface but felt completely wrong.

The issue was that I'd delivered a component without a system. I'd specified what, but not how. The typographic system is the how.

## Hierarchy as System Logic

The most important part of a typographic system is a defined hierarchy: what role does each typographic level play, what typeface and weight applies to each level, and how do the levels relate to each other?

I typically define five to seven hierarchy levels for a brand: display (large editorial or marketing type), headline, subheadline, body, caption/small, and any specialty applications like pull quotes or legal text.

Each level has defined typeface, weight, size range (not a fixed size — a range appropriate to the application), and leading. These aren't arbitrary specs — they're derived from the brand's visual language and tested against real application scenarios.

## The Two-Typeface Discipline

Most brand typographic systems benefit from exactly two typefaces: a primary (expressive, personality-forward) and a secondary (functional, utility-oriented). Three becomes complex. Four is a system only a type nerd can implement consistently.

The two-typeface structure gives clear guidance: is this element expressing brand personality (primary) or communicating information efficiently (secondary)?

## System Fonts and the Real World

Here's something that brand identity guidelines often ignore: most people inside the client's organization will set type in Microsoft Office or Google Workspace. They don't have the brand typefaces installed. If the guidelines don't address this, those people will default to whatever system font is available.

I always include system font recommendations — Calibri, Georgia, Arial — that approximate the brand's typographic character when brand fonts aren't available. This keeps internal documents from looking completely disconnected from the brand identity.

## Usage Rules as Creative Constraints

Some typographic rules in brand systems are prescriptive (use this typeface for this application). Others are prohibitive (never set body copy in the display typeface; never use more than two weights simultaneously in a layout). Both are necessary.

Prohibitive rules might feel limiting, but they're often the most value-generating part of the system. They prevent the typographic drift that makes brands look inconsistent over time.

## Evolution Without Abandonment

Typographic systems need room to evolve. A brand's typographic needs in year one (a website and some stationery) look different in year five (a publishing program, a global campaign, a retail environment, a product range). The system needs principles flexible enough to accommodate growth while maintaining coherence.

I document the underlying principles — the why behind each typographic decision — alongside the specific rules. When a new application requires a new typographic decision, the principles provide a framework for making it consistently.
