## No Margin for Ambiguity

Wayfinding typography operates under a condition that most other typography doesn't: complete failure has real-world consequences. A person who misreads a sign in a hospital or airport ends up in the wrong place. This stakes legibility as a moral requirement, not just a design preference.

Every typographic decision in wayfinding design needs to be evaluated against: could any viewer misread this? If the answer is possibly, the decision is wrong.

## Typeface Selection for Wayfinding

The typefaces used in major wayfinding systems have been developed and refined specifically for signage applications. Frutiger, originally designed for Charles de Gaulle Airport, remains a standard in wayfinding work for good reason — its open counters, clear differentiation between similar characters (I, l, 1; O, 0), and legibility at range are the product of direct design intention.

More recent typefaces like Clearview and Transport were designed with empirical research on highway legibility. The design decisions are backed by data on reading distance and accuracy.

I don't use arbitrary brand typefaces for wayfinding applications if those typefaces weren't designed for the purpose. The brand's personality can be carried through color and layout; the type needs to be fit for the specific communication task.

## Character Disambiguation

The most critical typographic requirement in wayfinding is character disambiguation — ensuring that similar characters are visually distinct at range and under non-ideal viewing conditions. The I-l-1 group and the O-0 group are the most problematic.

In typeface selection for wayfinding, I examine these character pairs specifically at the intended viewing distance. If ambiguity exists, I select a different typeface or adjust the application to minimize risk.

## Mixed Case vs. All Caps

Research on signage legibility consistently favors mixed case over all caps for running text and long phrases. Mixed case creates the distinctive word shapes (the "bouma") that allow experienced readers to recognize words without reading letter-by-letter.

All caps is appropriate for very short labels — EXIT, OPEN, STOP — where the word shape benefit is minimal and the strong visual presence of capitals serves the urgency of the message.

## Arrow Logic

Where arrows accompany type in wayfinding, the relationship between the arrow direction and the physical direction it indicates must be unambiguous. This seems obvious but is frequently a source of confusion in poorly designed wayfinding systems.

I establish a clear, consistent arrow logic at the beginning of every wayfinding project and apply it without exception. Inconsistent arrow behavior — sometimes pointing at the door, sometimes pointing past it — is the primary cause of navigational failure in wayfinding systems.
