## Color Is the Fastest Communicator

Before a viewer reads a single word on a poster, they've already received a color impression. Color communicates faster than shape, faster than type, faster than image content. The emotional register established by a poster's color palette is felt before it's consciously processed.

This makes color the most powerful tool in the poster designer's kit — and the one most often handled carelessly.

## Building from the Subject's Emotional Core

My color process for any poster starts with the emotional core of the subject: what feeling should this poster create in the viewer? Not what the poster is about — what should the viewer feel?

From that emotional target, I work toward color associations. Warm hues (reds, oranges, ambers) create heat, urgency, passion, warmth. Cool hues (blues, greens, purples) create calm, distance, mystery, melancholy. Saturated colors feel active and immediate. Desaturated colors feel withdrawn, nostalgic, or considered.

These are tendencies, not rules, and they interact with each other and with the subject matter. The same blue reads differently on a poster for a midnight jazz concert than on one for a corporate conference.

## Dominant Color as Mood-Setter

In most of my poster work, one color dominates — typically 60-70% of the visual field. This dominant color sets the emotional tone. Secondary colors modify and nuance it; accent colors create punctuation.

A poster without a dominant color tends to feel tonally ambiguous — the viewer can't settle into a single emotional register because multiple colors are competing for it. Committing to a dominant color commits to a mood.

## High Saturation vs. Restrained Palette

High-saturation, fully chromatic posters feel energetic, contemporary, and loud. They work for concerts, festivals, high-energy events, and brands that want to project vitality.

Restrained palettes — closer to neutrals, with limited saturation — feel considered, mature, and quieter. They work for serious subjects, premium events, and contexts where refinement matters.

The choice between these approaches should come from the subject, not from personal preference. I've used both depending on what the poster needed.

## Black as a Color

Black is one of the most useful colors in poster design and one of the most underused as a deliberate choice. A black-dominated poster with one color accent has a drama and authority that multicolor palettes rarely match. The contrast between deep black and a single vivid color creates visual impact without complexity.

I reach for a black-dominant palette when the subject calls for weight, authority, or nocturnal atmosphere. It's not a default choice or a safe choice — it's a specific emotional decision.
