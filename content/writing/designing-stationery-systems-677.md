Stationery — letterhead, envelope, business card, compliment slip — is the oldest form of brand identity system and still one of the most important for professional service firms, agencies, and businesses where physical correspondence and first impressions carry weight.

A stationery system done well communicates the brand on every piece of paper that leaves the office. Done poorly, it's either generic (wasted opportunity) or inconsistent (actively harmful to brand perception).

## The Elements of a Complete Stationery System

A full stationery system for a professional brand includes:

**Letterhead:** A4 (or letter-size for US markets) with the brand mark, address, contact details, and any registration or legal information required. The design needs to leave adequate writing space while communicating the brand clearly at the top (and sometimes bottom) of the page.

**Continuation sheet:** For multi-page letters, a simpler second-page version that carries brand identification without the full letterhead treatment. Often just a subtle header with the brand mark at reduced scale.

**Envelope:** DL (for folded A4) and C4 (for flat A4) at minimum. The brand needs to be visible on the front face. Some brands also print a return address on the back flap.

**Business card:** As discussed elsewhere — the most handled piece of the system, deserving the most care.

**Compliment slip:** A DL or A5 slip for enclosing with deliverables. Similar visual treatment to the letterhead but at a different proportion.

**Email signature:** Not physical print, but part of the stationery system for practical purposes. Needs to be specified in terms that non-designers can implement in email clients.

## The Grid Logic of Stationery

Each stationery piece is a different format, but the grid logic should be consistent across all of them. The margin proportions, the position of the brand mark relative to the edges, the typographic alignment — these relationships should feel related across pieces even at different sizes.

I build a proportional grid — margins as a percentage of the format — rather than fixed measurements, so the spatial logic scales correctly from DL envelope to A4 letterhead.

## Printing Stationery Correctly

Stationery has specific production considerations:

**Letterhead printing method:** Offset litho produces the highest quality for large runs. Digital printing for short runs. For premium brands, I specify two-color or spot-color printing rather than full CMYK to ensure brand color accuracy.

**Paper stock for letterhead:** 100-120gsm uncoated laid or wove paper is traditional for professional stationery. It needs to accept ink from laser and inkjet printers without tracking or smearing since most letterhead will be overprinted with correspondence.

**Business card stock:** As already discussed — the weight, coating, and finish communicate brand quality directly.

## Digital Letterhead

Most correspondence is now sent as PDF rather than printed. I produce a digital letterhead version optimized for screen display and PDF embedding — the same design as the print letterhead but with RGB colors and potentially higher-contrast elements that work better on screen than in print.

The digital version gets specified as a Word or Google Docs template with the header and footer pre-placed, so the office team doesn't need design software to produce correctly branded correspondence.

## System Coherence

The measure of a stationery system is whether all pieces look like they came from the same place. This requires more than using the same logo and colors — it requires consistent spatial logic, consistent typographic treatment, and consistent handling of supporting elements like addresses, registration numbers, and taglines across all formats.

That consistency is what turns a collection of print templates into a system.
