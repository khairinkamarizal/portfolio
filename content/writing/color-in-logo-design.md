Clients love full-color logos. The more colors, the more effort, the more value — or so the logic goes. My job is to explain why that logic is backwards.

A logo with five colors is a logo that requires five-color printing, fails when printed in one color, looks muddy when small, and costs more in every single physical application. A logo with two colors, or one color, is infinitely more flexible and often far more visually powerful.

## The Single-Color Foundation

Every logo I design starts in black and white. Not as a preliminary sketch — as the primary design condition. If the mark doesn't communicate clearly in a single color, it doesn't communicate clearly. Color can't fix structural problems in a logo; it can only mask them temporarily.

Once the mark works perfectly in black, I consider what color adds. Not what color makes it look good — what color adds meaning or functional value. Those are different questions.

## Color and Brand Personality

Color is one of the most direct channels for communicating brand personality. Warm hues — reds, oranges, yellows — communicate energy, warmth, and urgency. Cool hues — blues, greens, teals — communicate trust, calm, and clarity. These associations aren't universal across all cultures, but within the commercial contexts most of my clients operate in, they're reliable enough to design with.

I derive the color palette from the brand's intended personality and competitive context. If every competitor in the category uses blue, there's a strong argument for using a different hue — differentiation has value. If blue is the universal signal of trust in the category and the client's most important message is trustworthiness, there's a strong argument for blue.

## Primary, Secondary, Tertiary

I structure brand color systems in tiers. The primary color is the one that should be immediately associated with the brand — used most often, most prominently. Secondary colors support and complement the primary. Tertiary colors are used sparingly for specific functional purposes like alerts, highlights, or category coding.

Most brands need one primary, one or two secondaries, and optionally a tertiary. More than this creates system complexity without communication benefit.

## Pantone Matching and Consistency

For any client using physical printed materials, I specify Pantone colors alongside the CMYK, RGB, and hex values. The Pantone reference is the authoritative standard — when a vendor asks for the brand color, the Pantone number is the answer that ensures consistency regardless of printing process.

I test every color choice against the CMYK gamut before finalizing. Nothing is more disappointing than a vivid brand color that prints as a dull, desaturated approximation.
