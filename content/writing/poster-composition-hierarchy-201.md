## The eye doesn't wander — it follows

Every time I start a new poster, I remind myself of one thing: the viewer is not going to study it. They're going to glance at it. That means I have about two seconds to pull them in before they move on. Hierarchy is how I win those two seconds.

Hierarchy in poster design is not just about making the title big. It's about engineering a sequence — a deliberate path the eye travels from the most important element to the least. When it works, the viewer absorbs the information without realizing they're being directed. When it fails, everything competes and nothing wins.

## Starting with a single dominant element

My rule: one thing rules the poster. Not two. Not a group. One. That dominant element — whether it's a typographic headline, a face, an abstract shape — sets the entire visual weight of the composition. Everything else is subordinate to it.

I usually decide what that dominant element is before I open any software. It's a conceptual decision. If the poster is for a music event, is the dominant element the artist's name or the date? That depends on the context. A known artist who sells out shows on name recognition alone? The name dominates. A free community event where attendance matters more? The date and time might share the top tier.

## Building the three tiers

I think in three tiers: primary, secondary, and tertiary.

Primary is what you see from across the room. Secondary is what you read when you walk up to it. Tertiary is the fine print — venue address, ticket links, sponsor logos.

The mistake I see most often from designers coming up is treating secondary information with primary-level scale. Giving the supporting text too much weight collapses the hierarchy and turns the poster into visual noise.

## Size, weight, and space are your tools

The three main levers I use to build hierarchy are scale, typographic weight, and white space. Scale is the most obvious — bigger means more important. But weight (bold vs. regular vs. light) can do enormous work even at the same size. A bold secondary headline can feel like primary if the primary isn't heavy enough.

Space is the most underused tool. Isolating an element with generous white space around it automatically elevates it in the hierarchy — even without changing its size. I use this trick constantly with dates or venue names I want to feel considered and clean rather than crammed in.

## Reading the poster out loud

One exercise I do before finalizing any poster: I read it out loud in the order I think a viewer's eye will travel. If the sequence makes sense as a piece of communication — "Jazz Festival / June 18 / The Paramount / Doors at 8" — the hierarchy is working. If I catch myself reading things out of logical order, something needs to shift.

## Hierarchy is an argument

Ultimately, visual hierarchy is an argument about what matters. Making those decisions confidently — not hedging by giving everything equal weight — is what separates a poster that communicates from one that just decorates a wall.
