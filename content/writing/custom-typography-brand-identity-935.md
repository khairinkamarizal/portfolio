## When Off-the-Shelf Isn't Enough

For most brand identity projects, a well-chosen licensed typeface is exactly the right solution. There are thousands of excellent typefaces available, and the odds are good that one of them fits the brand well enough. Custom type is expensive, slow, and requires specialized skills that most graphic designers don't have in-house.

But for certain brands, at certain scales, with certain positioning goals, custom typography is the only right answer. I've worked on two custom typeface projects as design director — one for a national retail brand, one for a cultural institution — and in both cases, the decision was justified and the result irreplaceable.

## What Custom Type Provides

A custom typeface is exclusive. The retail brand I worked with had been using a popular geometric sans that three of their direct competitors also used. The typeface itself was excellent. But its ubiquity undermined the brand differentiation it was supposed to provide. Switching to a custom face eliminated that problem permanently.

Custom type is also exactly right. When you commission a typeface for a specific brand, every design decision in the letterforms can be made in service of that brand's specific character. The degree of contrast between thick and thin strokes, the shape of the terminals, the proportions of the x-height — all of these can be optimized for this brand rather than approximated from something designed for general use.

Finally, custom type creates an asset. A licensed typeface is a cost. A custom typeface is intellectual property that the brand owns, can use without ongoing licensing fees, and can license to others if it chooses.

## What Custom Type Costs

I won't give specific numbers because type design fees vary enormously based on scope, foundry, and market. But in general: a custom text typeface with three or four weights, Latin character set, and OpenType features is a six-figure investment minimum from a reputable type designer or foundry.

This is a significant but not irrational investment for a brand that will use the typeface across all its communications for the next decade or more. Amortized over that timeframe and the print and digital applications it covers, the per-use cost becomes very reasonable.

## The Exclusivity Alternative: Variable Licensing

For brands that want something more distinctive than a commodity typeface but can't justify full custom development, some foundries offer exclusive licensing arrangements. You pay a premium for the right to be the only brand in your category using a specific typeface.

This is a middle path that provides meaningful differentiation without the full custom cost and timeline.

## How I Work With Type Designers

When I direct a custom type project, my job is to be the clearest possible communicator of the brand's typographic needs. This means writing a detailed brief: the brand's personality, the use cases the typeface needs to serve, the existing typefaces that feel adjacent (and why they're not quite right), and any specific formal characteristics I want the letterforms to have.

The type designer interprets that brief through their own craft. My role after that is responsive direction, not micromanagement. Type designers are specialists. The best work comes from a clear brief, honest feedback, and room to do the craft.

## When to Recommend Against Custom Type

I actively talk clients out of custom type when the project doesn't justify it. A startup with limited runway doesn't need a custom typeface — they need a great licensed typeface chosen carefully. A regional business with a focused market doesn't need exclusive typography — they need consistent application of a good system.

Custom type is an amplifier. If the brand strategy is strong, it makes the brand stronger. If the strategy is unclear, it just makes the uncertainty more expensive.
