## Logos fail at the edges, not the center

A logo that looks perfect at the scale you designed it is a starting point, not a finished mark. Real-world logo use is brutal: the same mark appears on a 12pt business card footnote and a 30-foot building sign, on embroidered patches and on digital avatars. A mark that isn't tested at these extremes isn't finished.

Most logo failures I see happen at the extremes. Too small, the mark becomes an unreadable blob. Too large, the construction flaws that were invisible at design scale become obvious — curves that aren't quite smooth, spacing that's slightly off, stroke endings that look arbitrary.

## The size range I test

My standard test suite covers: 16px (favicon scale), 32px, 64px, 120px (app icon), 200px (business card size on screen), 400px (standard presentation scale), and a large-format print mockup at signage scale.

I don't just test the primary mark. I test every version of the logo: the primary horizontal lockup, the stacked version, the icon-only mark, and the wordmark in isolation. Each version may fail at different sizes in different ways.

## The favicon test is the hardest

At 16px, a logo is 16 square pixels. That's not much to work with. If the primary mark doesn't have a simplified favicon version, I design one. Not a scaled-down version of the primary mark — a purpose-built simplification that preserves the essential character of the brand while being readable at postage-stamp scale.

This usually means stripping details, increasing contrast, and sometimes reducing the mark to its single most distinctive element. A logo that's a wordmark plus an icon mark might have a favicon that's just the icon's core geometric form with no letterforms at all.

## The single-color test

Scalability and reproduction go together. Every logo needs to work in single-color black and single-color white. I test both before finalizing. A mark that relies on color to hold together — where two elements are only distinguishable from each other by their color — has a structural problem that no color specification can fix.

If the mark doesn't work in black and white, I redesign the form, not the color. Color is a layer on top of the structure. The structure has to hold without it.

## What small sizes reveal

Testing at very small sizes reveals two types of problems. The first is genuine detail problems — a letterform has a hairline element that disappears at 32px. These require redesign. The second is optical problems — the spacing that looks balanced at 400px becomes too tight at 64px because the negative space has compressed. These require size-specific spacing adjustments, not a full redesign.

I document these adjustments in the logo delivery package so that whoever is implementing the mark knows which version to use at which scale.
