## Spacing Is Personality

Two logotypes set in the same typeface at the same size can communicate completely different things based on their letter spacing alone. Tight tracking feels compressed, urgent, assertive. Loose tracking feels spacious, premium, considered. The spacing is a personality signal as direct as the typeface choice.

Most designers understand this intellectually. Fewer apply it with real intentionality — adjusting spacing to a specific point that reflects the brand's personality rather than leaving it at whatever default the typeface sets.

## The Default Is Never Right

Typefaces are designed to work at a range of sizes and contexts. Their default spacing is a general-purpose compromise that serves most contexts adequately. For a logotype — a single application where the design is permanent and the name will be seen thousands of times — "adequate" isn't the goal.

I always adjust logotype spacing from the default. Sometimes only slightly. Sometimes significantly. The adjustment is based on what the brand's personality requires, not on whether the default looks wrong. Even if the default is close to right, I interrogate it.

## What Tight Spacing Says

Tight tracking — bringing letters closer together than the typeface's default — creates density and compression. At extremes, individual letters start to touch or overlap.

This reads as: confident, focused, efficient. In its most aggressive form, it can read as urgent, bold, or even confrontational. Used well with the right typeface, tight spacing creates logotypes with authority and weight.

The risk: too tight, and the letterforms lose their clarity. Individual characters merge into each other and legibility suffers. Finding the tightest spacing that still reads cleanly takes careful evaluation.

## What Loose Spacing Says

Generous tracking — more space between letters than the typeface's default — creates openness and breathing room. At extremes, letters feel isolated from each other, more like individual objects than a unified word.

This reads as: premium, spacious, elevated. Luxury brands and fine goods almost always use generously tracked logotypes. The space signals that the brand isn't in a hurry, isn't crowded, has room to operate.

The risk: too loose, and the word falls apart as a unit. The reader has to mentally assemble the letters into a name rather than reading it as a unified word. There's a threshold beyond which generous spacing becomes a parsing problem.

## The Method I Use

I set the logotype at its primary use size — usually 100pt or larger — and adjust tracking in very small increments: 10 units at a time in InDesign's tracking scale. I evaluate at each increment.

What I'm looking for: the spacing where the brand's personality comes through most clearly, and where the letterforms maintain their individual clarity while reading as a unified word. Those two requirements can be in tension — find where they're both satisfied.

## Then Kern Individual Pairs

Global tracking sets the overall spacing; kerning fixes the pairs. After tracking is set, I go through every adjacent pair of letters and evaluate whether the spacing between them looks optically even.

The pairs that almost always need attention: A-V, T-a, Y-a, W-e, L-T. These create awkward visual gaps at the default metric spacing that global tracking doesn't address.

Global tracking creates the mood. Pair kerning creates the craft.
