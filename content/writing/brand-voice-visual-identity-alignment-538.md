## Two Languages, One Brand

A brand communicates in two languages simultaneously: visual and verbal. The visual language is the identity system — the mark, the colors, the typography, the imagery. The verbal language is the brand voice — the words, the tone, the rhythm of the copy.

When these two languages tell the same story, the brand feels coherent and trustworthy. When they contradict each other, the audience feels something is off — even if they can't articulate what.

I've worked on identity projects where the visual work was strong but the verbal voice was misaligned, and the brand felt confused despite the quality of the visual design. The fix wasn't more visual refinement. It was alignment.

## What Alignment Looks Like

A brand that describes itself as "honest and direct" should have visual design that's clean, unembellished, and gets to the point. Not layered with decorative elements, not using elaborate visual metaphors, not hiding behind complexity.

A brand that describes itself as "warm and personal" should have visual design that reflects that warmth — in the color temperature, in the texture, in the softness of the typography. Not a cold, precise geometric system.

A brand that's "bold and unconventional" should have a visual identity that takes risks. Not a safely constructed, middle-of-the-road design that could belong to any brand in the category.

The test is simple: read the brand's verbal positioning and look at the visual identity. Do they feel like they came from the same place?

## Where Misalignment Happens

The most common source of visual/verbal misalignment I see is disconnected processes. The brand strategy (including verbal voice) is developed by one team or consultant. The visual identity is developed by another. The two teams brief each other at the start and reconvene at the end, but the detailed work happens independently.

This process is common and it produces misaligned brands.

I insist on working from the brand strategy myself, or building the strategy alongside the visual design, so the two are developed in dialogue. When I see tension between the verbal direction and where the visual work is going, I address it in the design phase rather than discovering it in the presentation.

## Typography Is Voice

Typography is the visual element most directly connected to verbal voice. The typeface you set the brand's words in either amplifies or contradicts the voice.

A brand with a warm, conversational verbal voice set in a cold, precise geometric typeface will feel off. The words say one thing; the letterforms say another.

I treat typeface selection as a voice decision. Before I recommend a typeface, I read the brand's copy. I ask: does this typeface sound like the brand talks? That question sounds strange, but it produces good answers.

## Photography and Imagery

Photography and illustration style are the most flexible elements of a visual identity, and they're often where verbal/visual misalignment shows up most clearly.

A brand that talks about human connection running stock photos of generic business people. A brand that talks about craftsmanship using sleek computer-generated imagery. A brand that talks about sustainability using imagery that's glossy, pristine, and environmentally detached.

The imagery should show what the words say. If the verbal brand talks about real people, the imagery should show real people. If the verbal brand talks about process and making, the imagery should show making.

## The Alignment Check

At every major milestone in a brand identity project, I run this check: read the brand strategy out loud. Then look at the visual work. Does the visual work feel like the physical embodiment of those words?

If yes, proceed. If no, find where the gap is and close it. That's the job.
