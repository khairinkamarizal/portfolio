## Why Festivals Are a Special Case

A festival identity operates at a scale and complexity that most event design doesn't. You're dealing with a core brand that needs to be consistent year to year, a system that needs to accommodate dozens or hundreds of sub-events and stages, a range of applications from wristbands to billboards, and often a visual identity that gets refreshed annually while maintaining recognizability.

That's a genuine system design challenge, not just a logo design challenge.

## The Core Mark and Its Durability

The primary festival mark is the anchor of the whole system. It should be distinctive and memorable enough to build recognition across years of events. It also needs to be robust enough to accommodate the annual refreshes that keep the brand feeling current.

I think about festival marks as containers — they hold the festival's identity in a form that can absorb variation in color, texture, and graphic treatment without losing its essential character. The mark itself stays constant while its expression changes year to year.

## Annual Theming Without Identity Drift

The annual theme challenge is real: how do you make each year feel fresh and visually exciting while maintaining the brand equity built over previous years?

My approach is to distinguish between what's constant (the primary mark, core brand color, main typeface) and what can change (secondary colors, textural treatments, supporting graphic elements, illustration style). The constant elements anchor the brand. The variable elements carry the year's theme.

## Sub-Brand Logic for Stages and Events

A large festival typically has multiple stages, programming tracks, and sub-events that each need some degree of visual identity. Managing this requires clear brand architecture: which elements are shared across all sub-brands, and which elements are unique to each.

A practical system uses a consistent structural logic — same typeface family, same grid, same treatment of the main festival mark — with a differentiated color or graphic treatment for each stage or track. The family resemblance is clear; the differentiation within it creates navigational utility for the festival audience.

## Across Applications

Festival design touches an enormous range of applications: posters, programs, stage backdrops, signage, wristbands, merchandise, lanyards, digital screens, social media templates, staff uniforms, vehicle wraps. The identity system needs to work across all of these.

I build application mockups for this full range during the identity development phase. What works on a poster may not work on a wristband. Discovering these problems during design is much easier than discovering them in production.

## Building the Machine

The best outcome for a festival identity project is a design system so well documented and flexible that the internal team can produce all applications consistently, year after year, without needing to re-engage external designers for every deliverable.

That's the goal: build the machine, then hand over the keys.
