I set up a screen printing operation in my studio three years ago, partly out of curiosity and partly because I was getting tired of posters that looked like every other digitally printed poster. The process is slow, tactile, and completely unforgiving of bad design decisions. It's also the most direct connection between design and physical making that I've found.

## The Process Changes the Design

In screen printing, every color is a separate pass through the press. Two colors means two screens, two setups, two opportunities for registration error. Four colors means a day of production. This cost structure changes how you design — and it should.

Designing for screen printing means embracing flat color. No gradients, no transparency, no blends between colors. Everything resolves to solid areas. This limitation sounds severe until you see what it produces: a graphic clarity and physical presence that digital printing struggles to match.

## Registration as a Design Element

Screen-print registration — the alignment of one color layer to the next — is never perfect. Slight misregistration is inherent to the process. I've stopped fighting it and started designing with it.

A small deliberate offset between color layers creates a shadow-like depth effect. A wider offset between layers reads as a split-fountain effect. Designing for imperfect registration means the "errors" become part of the aesthetic rather than defects.

## Ink and Paper Interaction

In screen printing, the ink sits on top of the paper rather than absorbing into it. This creates an opacity and tactility that offset printing can't replicate. Printing a light color on dark paper is completely achievable — the ink covers the substrate rather than blending with it.

This opens the full range of paper color as a design element. I can print white on black, yellow on kraft, pastel on charcoal. The design possibilities are completely different from digital or offset printing.

## Halftone Mesh Counts

The mesh count of the screen determines the fineness of detail possible. High mesh counts (160+) can hold fine detail and thin lines. Low mesh counts (80–110) are better for heavy ink coverage and create a visible halftone dot pattern when printing photographic material.

I choose mesh count as a design decision, not just a technical one. A coarse 85lpi halftone on a 110 mesh screen creates a visible dot pattern that reads as texture — a graphic effect, not a reproduction limitation.

## Why It Makes Me a Better Designer

Designing for screen printing requires absolute commitment to decisions. There's no "fix it in Photoshop" when the ink is on the paper. The constraints push me toward cleaner, more resolved designs. And the physical result — a poster with three colors of ink sitting on its surface, slightly imperfect, unmistakably made — is something digital printing doesn't produce.
