## Learning by Dissection

Some of my best professional development has come from systematically dissecting major rebrands — not just looking at before and after, but working out exactly what changed, why those specific changes were made, and what the strategic logic was.

This exercise reveals patterns that apply directly to the work you produce yourself.

## What to Look for in Before/After

When I analyze a rebrand, I work through these questions: What stayed the same? What changed? Which elements clearly address a specific problem with the old identity? Which changes seem motivated by aesthetic preference versus strategic need? Does the new identity communicate something the old one couldn't?

The answers to these questions tell you whether the rebrand was strategic or cosmetic. Most cosmetic rebrands fail to produce lasting improvement. Strategic ones tend to stick.

## The Airbnb Case

The Airbnb rebrand from 2014 is worth studying in detail. The previous identity was corporate and generic — a lowercase wordmark in a forgettable blue that could have belonged to any travel booking service. The new identity introduced the "Bélo" symbol, a mark designed to be simultaneously a head, a heart, a location pin, and the letter A.

What changed: the mark became ownable and distinctive. The color shifted from corporate blue to a warm coral that stood alone in the category. The overall system conveyed belonging and human warmth — aligned with Airbnb's core promise — rather than transactional efficiency.

The strategic logic was clear and the design served it.

## What Often Gets Wrong in Analysis

The most common mistake in rebrand analysis is focusing on the aesthetic shift without asking what problem it solved. "The old logo looked dated" is an observation, not an analysis. What did the dating signify? Who was the brand losing with an outdated identity? What was the cost of that loss?

Connecting visual changes to business problems is what separates design analysis from design criticism.

## Building a Reference Library

I keep a documented library of rebrand case studies. Not just the images — the context, the strategy, the apparent reasoning. Over time, patterns emerge: what kinds of visual changes tend to accompany what kinds of strategic shifts, what execution decisions have proved durable versus what has aged badly.

This library informs new work. No rebrand project I do starts from scratch — it starts from an accumulated understanding of what works and why.
