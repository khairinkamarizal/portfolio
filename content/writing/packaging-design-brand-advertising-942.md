## The Package Is the First Experience

For consumer products, the package is often the first physical brand experience the customer has. Before they use the product, before they read the website, before they see any advertising — they pick up the package. Everything the brand wants to say needs to be communicated in that moment.

I've designed packaging for food products, personal care brands, specialty retail, and gift collections. The design challenge is consistent: the package has to work as a container, as a retail shelf piece competing for attention, and as a brand identity carrier.

## Shelf Context Changes Everything

A package design that looks beautiful in isolation on your desk often looks completely wrong in context. Retail shelves are dense, chaotic, and lit by whatever fluorescent fixtures the store has. Your package sits next to direct competitors, and the buyer is making their decision in about three seconds from two feet away.

I always design packaging with the shelf context in mind. I create mock shelf sets — images that show the package surrounded by competitive products at realistic scale. This test reveals problems that isolated design views never catch: the package that reads as distinctive on a white background disappears against the typical competitor palette; the clever type treatment that looked elegant at design scale is completely illegible in shelf conditions.

## The Front Panel Is a Poster

I design the front panel of every package as if it were a poster. The hierarchy should be immediate: brand name, product name, key differentiator. Everything that isn't essential to the initial purchase decision should be on the back or side panel.

Many packaging briefs include a wish list of front panel content that would require a spread of a magazine to accommodate comfortably. My job is to negotiate that list down to the essential. The front panel has one job: make someone pick this up.

## Typography at Package Scale

Packages are held at arm's length and examined closely. This is different from poster viewing distance, and it means small type is more readable than on a poster. But it also means type quality at close range is scrutinized.

I use custom letterspacing and careful size relationships on packaging. The hierarchy between brand name, product name, and variant is expressed precisely, because at close viewing distance these relationships are obvious to a careful shopper.

## Color in a Competitive Context

Brand color on packaging serves two functions simultaneously: it identifies the brand and it differentiates from competitors. These sometimes conflict. If your brand color is the same family as your primary competitor's color, you'll have visibility problems on shelf.

I research the competitive color landscape before specifying packaging colors. The goal is to own a piece of the shelf's color real estate that's distinctive — immediately recognizable as this brand, not that one.

## Unboxing as Brand Experience

For premium and direct-to-consumer brands, the interior of the package is increasingly important. The unboxing experience — what the customer encounters when they open the package — is a brand touchpoint that builds loyalty and drives social sharing.

I design interior printing, tissue paper, and inserts as part of the primary packaging project, not as afterthoughts. A beautifully designed exterior package that opens to reveal nothing inside is a missed opportunity. The moment of opening should feel like receiving a gift.

## Sustainability Specifications

Most brand clients now care about packaging sustainability. I stay current on substrate options — recycled materials, FSC-certified papers, plant-based inks, reduced-weight structures — and include sustainability recommendations in packaging proposals.

Design and sustainability aren't in conflict. The constraint of more sustainable materials often produces more considered, less overwrought design. Less material, more intentionality.
