## Wayfinding is Information Design

Signage and wayfinding systems exist to help people navigate physical environments. That purpose is non-negotiable. A wayfinding system that's visually elegant but functionally confusing is a failure. Before aesthetics, before brand expression, the system has to work.

This doesn't mean wayfinding can't be beautiful. The best systems are both functionally impeccable and visually excellent. But the sequence of priorities is unambiguous.

## The Scale of the Problem

Wayfinding design is complex because it operates across an enormous range of scales simultaneously. Overhead hanging signs visible from 20 meters. Directory signs read from 3 meters. Room identification signs read at arm's length. Each scale has different requirements for type size, contrast, symbol complexity, and information density.

A system that's been designed only for one reading distance fails at others. I design across the full range of scales from the beginning.

## Symbol Systems

Wayfinding relies heavily on symbols — icons that communicate room types, directions, amenities, and facilities without language. This makes the system accessible across language barriers and faster to process than text.

Symbol design for wayfinding follows strict standards for size, legibility at distance, and universality. I follow established symbol systems (ISO 7001, AIGA/DOT symbols) as a baseline and extend them with custom symbols only where standard options don't exist.

## Hierarchy and Sequencing

Wayfinding information has a natural sequence: you need the high-level orientation information first (which direction, which floor), then the mid-level confirmation (you're in the right area), then the room-level identification (this is the place). The signage system needs to support this progressive disclosure.

Signs placed at decision points — where someone has to choose a direction — are the critical functional elements. Signs placed in corridors confirm that the correct route is being followed. Signs on rooms confirm arrival. Each type has different informational requirements.

## Materials and Environments

Sign materials and finishes need to perform in their specific environments. Exterior signs face weather, UV, vandalism. Healthcare environments need antimicrobial surfaces. Industrial environments need impact resistance. The material specification is part of the design.

I specify materials and finishes explicitly and require manufacturer samples before approving production.

## Testing the System

Before full production, I test wayfinding systems with users who don't know the space. Nothing reveals gaps in a wayfinding system more clearly than watching someone unfamiliar with the building try to navigate using it. Those tests almost always surface things that looked fine on paper but fail in practice.

Test with real people. Fix what you find. Then produce.
