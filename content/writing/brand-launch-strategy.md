Designing a brand identity is one thing. Launching it successfully is another. I've seen excellent brand work land badly because the launch wasn't managed as carefully as the design. And I've seen competent-but-not-exceptional work succeed because the launch was executed well.

The design deliverables are necessary but not sufficient.

## The Transition Plan

Every brand launch requires a transition plan: how do you move from the old brand to the new one across every touchpoint where the brand appears?

For small organizations, the transition might be as simple as: update the website, print new business cards, update social media profiles. For larger organizations, the transition involves: phased rollout across business units, coordinated timing to avoid mixed signals, a timeline for retiring old brand materials.

I work with clients to build a transition plan as part of every identity project, not as an afterthought. The questions that need answering:

- Which touchpoints transition on day one, and which transition over time?
- What happens to existing printed materials — are they used until exhausted, or retired immediately?
- Who is responsible for each touchpoint transition?
- What's the communication plan for announcing the new identity internally before external launch?

## Internal Launch Before External

A brand launch that catches employees off guard is a brand launch that starts with internal chaos. Team members who don't know about the new brand can't represent it correctly in client interactions, and they may inadvertently use old materials in situations where new materials should appear.

I advocate for an internal launch — a communication to the full organization about the new identity, what's changing, why, and what each person needs to do — that precedes the public launch by at least a few days.

## The Launch Moment

A well-designed launch moment — a specific date and a coordinated update across key touchpoints simultaneously — creates a cleaner break from the old brand than a slow drift from old to new. Simultaneous updates to website, social media, email signatures, and any visible physical materials on a single day send a clear signal: this is a deliberate change, it's complete, and it's intentional.

The dramatic simplicity of a clean launch day is worth coordinating for.

## Asset Distribution

The brand launch is when the new brand assets get into the hands of everyone who needs them. This means: a clearly organized, accessible asset library; communication to all stakeholders about where assets live and how to use them; and a brief summary of the most important usage guidelines for non-designers.

Assets that are inaccessible or poorly organized get ignored. People use the old logo because they can find it. Making the new assets easier to find and use than the old ones is a practical launch requirement.

## Post-Launch Review

Six months after launch, I recommend a brand audit: reviewing how the identity is being used in practice, identifying drift or inconsistency that's developed, and making any necessary clarifications to the guidelines.

A brand system that's launched and never reviewed tends to drift. A system that's reviewed periodically and maintained stays strong.
