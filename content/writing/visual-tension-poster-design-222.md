Resolved compositions are safe. Every element sits comfortably, nothing pushes against anything else, the hierarchy is clear, the balance is satisfying. The poster works. It also has no energy.

I spent too long in my early career optimizing for resolved compositions. I was chasing technical correctness — good hierarchy, good balance, good contrast — and I was producing work that was competent but not memorable. The posters solved the brief without generating any excitement.

The shift came when I started studying poster work that made me feel something and asking what it was doing compositionally that my work wasn't. Almost universally, the answer was tension.

## What Tension Means in Composition

Visual tension is the sensation of unresolved energy in a composition. It's created by elements that push against each other, by near-collisions between forms, by asymmetry that feels unstable rather than settled, by scale relationships that seem slightly wrong.

Tension isn't the same as chaos. Chaotic compositions are just disordered — there's no intentional pushing, just noise. Tension requires intention: deliberate placement of elements in relationships that create energy rather than resolving it.

## Creating Tension Technically

Several compositional techniques reliably generate tension:

Near-collisions: elements placed very close to each other without actually touching. The eye anticipates contact and holds the energy of that anticipation. I use this between typographic elements and image edges, between headline words and compositional boundaries.

Asymmetric weight: placing a heavy visual mass off-center with insufficient counterbalance on the other side. The composition feels like it might tip. This is different from simply being asymmetric — many compositions are asymmetric and settled. Tension requires that the asymmetry feels unresolved.

Scale dissonance: placing elements at sizes that feel slightly mismatched — a very large element and a very small one with nothing in between. The gap in scale creates a visual jump the eye keeps crossing.

Compositional overflow: elements that push to or beyond the edges, implying extension beyond the frame. This creates the feeling that the composition isn't fully contained, that energy is escaping.

## Tension in Service of Concept

Tension isn't decoration. When I use it deliberately, it should serve the work's concept or emotional register.

A poster for a confrontational political artist should feel tense because that tension reflects the content. A poster for a calming wellness brand probably shouldn't have visual tension fighting the message.

The question I ask: does this tension reinforce what the poster is communicating? If yes, it belongs. If no, it's decoration at best and contradiction at worst.

## The Client Conversation

Presenting tense compositions to clients requires some framing. Clients often read visual tension as "unfinished" or "wrong" if they're not design-literate. I've learned to lead with the concept — why this tension serves the work — before showing the composition.

When they understand that the discomfort they feel is the point, not a problem to fix, most clients can engage with it productively. Some still ask me to fix it. When that happens, I have to decide whether the tension is essential to the work or whether I can find resolution without gutting the design.

## The Comfort Trap

Easy balance feels correct. That's exactly what makes it dangerous. A composition that feels completely comfortable usually isn't pushing hard enough.

When I review my own work and it feels too settled, I treat that as a signal to introduce some deliberate discomfort. Not randomness — targeted tension that gives the composition something to say.

Let it feel slightly wrong. That's usually when it starts being right.
