## Most text in print is never read

Studies of print reading behavior consistently show that most body copy in print materials — brochures, annual reports, marketing collateral — is never read. Readers scan, they read headlines and callouts, they look at images. They read body copy only when something has already given them a reason to.

This isn't a failure of the reader. It's a failure of the design to earn reading. Text that looks difficult, uninviting, or not worth the investment doesn't get read. Text that's well-structured, well-set, and clearly organized invites reading by communicating that the effort will be rewarded.

## The scanning layer

Before I design the reading layer of any print piece, I design the scanning layer. The scanning layer is everything that communicates to a reader in a 3-second glance: the headline, the subheads, the pull quotes, the captions, the call-to-action. If the scanning layer communicates the essential message, the reader who never reads the body copy has still received something valuable.

Many designers treat subheads and callouts as typographic decoration — styling decisions made after the body copy is placed. I treat them as primary communication elements. What does the subhead say? Does it advance the message, or just label the section? Pull quotes should be the most compelling sentence in the paragraph, not a randomly selected one.

## Line length and reading comfort

The most significant factor in reading comfort for body copy is line length — the number of characters per line. The research consensus is that 50-75 characters per line is optimal for comfortable reading. Lines shorter than 40 characters feel choppy; lines longer than 85 characters require the eye to travel too far and cause readers to lose their place when dropping to the next line.

I calculate character-per-line counts on every print layout I produce with substantial body copy. I set columns wide enough for comfortable line lengths at the specified type size, then adjust type size or tracking to hit the optimal range.

## Leading and reading rhythm

Leading — the space between lines — creates the rhythm of reading. Tight leading (less than 120% of the point size) creates density and visual heaviness. Generous leading (130-145%) creates openness and ease.

The appropriate leading depends on the typeface's x-height and the line length. Typefaces with large x-heights need more leading because their proportions create more visual density per line. Long line lengths need slightly more leading to help the eye return correctly to the left margin.

## The paragraph structure decision

How text is broken into paragraphs — and how those breaks are marked — significantly affects reading comfort. The two standard approaches are indented paragraphs (no vertical space between paragraphs, a first-line indent signals the break) and spaced paragraphs (a visible vertical gap between paragraphs, no indent).

I use indented paragraphs for long-form reading — magazine features, reports, essays — because they create a denser, more continuous reading experience with less visual interruption. I use spaced paragraphs for shorter copy in marketing materials where scanability is more important than reading flow.
