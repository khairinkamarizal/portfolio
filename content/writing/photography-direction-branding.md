Photography is often the component of a brand identity system that gets the least attention and produces the most inconsistency. A logo that's been carefully designed and specified can be undermined by photography that's inconsistent, generic, or tonally wrong.

Photography direction isn't about taste. It's about ensuring the visual communication of the brand is consistent across the element that takes up the most visual real estate.

## Photography as a Communication Layer

Photography communicates things the logo and typeface can't. Scale, texture, atmosphere, human presence, emotional temperature. A brand that uses cold, high-contrast, abstract photography communicates something completely different from the same brand using warm, intimate, candid imagery — even if the logo and color are identical.

The photography direction should be derived from the same brand character principles that drove every other visual decision. If the brand is "warm, direct, human," the photography should reflect that. If it's "precise, restrained, premium," the photography should reflect that instead.

## The Direction Has Several Dimensions

When I write a photography direction for a brand system, I address several dimensions:

**Subject matter:** What is typically shown? People, places, products, processes, abstractions? In what proportion?

**Perspective and proximity:** How close are we to the subject? Intimate close-ups, human-scale environmental shots, or expansive establishing views?

**Light quality:** Hard or soft? Warm or cool? Directional or flat? Artificial or natural?

**Color treatment:** Is photography used in full color, desaturated, with a color grade, or duotone treated with brand colors?

**Composition style:** Centered and formal, or candid and asymmetric? Generous negative space, or full-bleed and dense?

**Processing and retouching:** Natural and documentary, or heavily produced and retouched?

## Creative Direction vs. Art Direction

Creative direction defines the character of photography at a strategic level. Art direction applies that direction to a specific shoot with specific decisions about location, casting, styling, and lighting.

In brand identity work, I'm defining the creative direction — the standards and character that all photography should meet. A separate art director (or myself in a producing role) applies that direction when photography is actually captured.

The creative direction document needs to be specific enough for an art director to execute it correctly, but not so prescriptive that it eliminates creative judgment in the field.

## Stock Photography and Its Limits

Most small to mid-size brands rely heavily on stock photography. A well-written stock photography direction defines the specific qualities to look for in stock selection — the types of images that fit the brand character versus those that don't.

Stock photography has a generic quality problem: it often looks designed to appeal to the broadest possible audience, which means it often looks like every other brand's photography. The direction document should make clear which categories of stock imagery to avoid — typically anything that reads as "generic marketing imagery" rather than "authentic to this brand."

Distinctive stock photography choices, and especially pairing stock photography with brand-specific graphic treatments (color grading, cropping style, combination with graphic elements), can significantly reduce the generic quality problem.
