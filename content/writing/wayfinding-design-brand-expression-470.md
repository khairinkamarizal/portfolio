Wayfinding design sits at the intersection of graphic design, information design, and environmental design. It's one of the most demanding applications of a brand identity system, and one where the connection between brand character and practical function is most visible.

A wayfinding system that guides people through a space efficiently while expressing the brand's visual personality is excellent design. A wayfinding system that does one but not the other is incomplete.

## Wayfinding Is Always Brand Design

Every sign in a space communicates brand. The typeface on a directory sign, the color of a directional arrow, the finish on a room number plate, the material of a sign substrate — all of these are brand decisions whether they're made consciously or not.

Most wayfinding systems are designed by facilities teams or construction firms who have no brief for brand expression. The result is signage that functions adequately and looks generic. The brand identity that was carefully developed for marketing materials is absent in the space where customers actually experience the brand.

When I'm involved in brand identity projects for organizations with significant physical spaces, I push to extend the identity into wayfinding explicitly. This doesn't always happen — budget and organizational structure often mean wayfinding is handled separately. But when it does, the difference in the experience is significant.

## Legibility Constraints in Wayfinding Typography

Wayfinding typography operates under strict legibility requirements that override many brand preferences. Signs are read quickly, at distance, by people who are navigating rather than reading. The typography must be:

**Legible at the required reading distance.** A sign with a destination name at 25mm cap height is readable at approximately 15 meters. A sign with the same information at 10mm cap height is not. Physical scale is determined by reading distance, not by what looks balanced on the design mock-up.

**High contrast against the substrate.** Light type on dark background or dark type on light background, always. Mid-contrast combinations that look sophisticated in print become problematic when viewing conditions vary.

**Unambiguous in character differentiation.** The classic wayfinding type requirement: the uppercase I, the lowercase l, and the numeral 1 must be visually distinct. Many fonts that work well in other contexts fail this test. Wayfinding typefaces are designed specifically with these disambiguations.

## The Hierarchy of Information

Wayfinding information has a natural hierarchy: primary destination, secondary destination, direction. A pedestrian arriving at a junction needs to process this in order.

The design hierarchy should match this functional hierarchy: largest and most prominent for the primary destination name, smaller for secondary information, clear directional element. The viewer's eye should follow the path that mirrors the decision they're making.

## Materials and Durability

Wayfinding signs exist in physical space and face physical challenges: UV exposure, cleaning chemicals, physical contact, vandalism. Material selection is a design decision that affects both how the sign looks and how long it looks that way.

High-pressure laminate (HPL) is robust and available in many colors and finishes. Brushed aluminum works for premium applications and holds up well. Vinyl graphics on substrate can be cost-effective and easily updated. Each material has a visual character that should be appropriate for the space and brand.

I specify materials based on: the environmental conditions the sign will face, the brand's quality register (cheap materials in a premium brand environment create dissonance), the update frequency expected (signs that need frequent content updates need formats that support easy updating), and total lifecycle cost.

## The System View

Wayfinding has to be designed as a system, not as individual signs. Consistency across sign types, locations, and scales is what makes a wayfinding system comprehensible. A visitor should be able to identify a wayfinding sign as part of the system immediately, anywhere in the building.

That systemic consistency is the same design discipline as a brand identity system. Applied to wayfinding, it makes spaces navigable. Applied to brand communications, it makes brands recognizable.
