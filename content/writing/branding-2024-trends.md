Trend pieces are a strange genre. By the time you're reading about a trend, it's already been absorbed by the mainstream — which means the real leading edge has moved on. So instead of a listicle of things everyone's already doing, this is more of a field report: what I'm actually seeing in briefs, in the work I admire, and in the broader conversation around brand design right now.

## The Brutalist Turn Isn't Done

Brutalism in digital design has been "having a moment" for about four years now, which means it's no longer a moment — it's a direction. But the interesting thing is that it's evolved. Early brutalist web design was reactive: raw HTML aesthetics, visible structure, deliberately unpolished. A rejection of the clean, rounded, friendly design language that dominated the 2010s.

What I'm seeing now is something more considered. Brands are borrowing brutalist vocabulary — stark typography, high contrast, visible grids — but applying it with real craft. The rawness is intentional rather than accidental. It's brutalism that knows it's brutalism, which makes it something else entirely: a kind of editorial severity that reads as confident rather than unfinished.

For brand work this shows up as: heavy serifs at enormous sizes, black and white with a single accent, type that breaks out of expected containers. It works especially well for brands that want authority without warmth — finance, architecture, certain kinds of tech.

## AI-Generated Identity Systems: More Complicated Than the Takes Suggest

The discourse around AI and branding tends to collapse into two positions: "AI will replace designers" or "AI can't do real creative work." Both are boring. What's actually happening is more interesting and more specific.

Where I've seen AI tools genuinely change the workflow is in exploration. Generating fifty variations of a visual direction in an afternoon used to require a team. Now one designer can cover that ground alone. The strategic and curatorial work — deciding which direction is right, understanding why, communicating that to a client — is still entirely human. But the volume of raw material you can generate has shifted dramatically.

For brand identity specifically, the challenge with AI generation is consistency. A logo system needs to work across a business card, a billboard, and a favicon. It needs to survive one-colour printing and embroidery. Generative tools produce beautiful individual images but struggle with the systematic thinking that makes identity work actually useful. That gap is where designers still have a lot of runway.

What worries me slightly is the number of clients who've seen AI-generated concepts and now expect the exploration phase to be essentially free. The generation is fast; the judgment is not.

## Motion as Brand Language

Static logos feel increasingly incomplete. The brands doing the most interesting identity work right now are thinking about motion from the start — not as an afterthought animation, but as a core part of how the brand communicates.

This isn't new exactly, but the tools have caught up to the ambition. After Effects has always been there, but Lottie, Rive, and CSS animation have made it practical to ship complex motion at scale without a team of specialists. A single designer can now build a motion system that actually ships.

The trend I find most interesting here is identity systems where the motion *is* the logo. No static lockup — the brand only exists in motion. Spotify's audio-reactive visual system is the famous example, but it's filtering down to smaller brands now. It makes sense for digital-native companies that live entirely on screens.

For traditional brand work, the bar is lower: a well-crafted logo animation that works across social, presentations, and loading screens. Still surprisingly rare. If you're doing brand work and not thinking about at least this baseline, you're delivering half a system.

## The Backlash to Blanding Is Real

The homogenisation critique — all modern brands look the same, the Saans-serif everything, the muted palettes — has been building for years. In 2024 the backlash is visible in the actual work.

Clients are asking for distinctiveness more directly than they were three years ago. "Don't make us look like everyone else" is becoming a real brief rather than lip service. The brands that stand out are leaning into specificity: regional character, category-breaking aesthetics, personality that feels like it comes from actual people.

In Malaysia specifically, I've noticed a growing appetite for identity work that feels local without being clichéd. Not batik patterns plastered on everything, but a genuine visual sensibility that comes from this place, this culture, this context. That's harder to do than it sounds, and it's where I think there's the most interesting work to be done right now.

## Typography is Having a Proper Moment

Variable fonts have been technically available for years. 2024 feels like the year they've actually become mainstream in brand work — not just as a technical curiosity but as a genuine creative tool.

The ability to animate type along a weight or width axis opens up expressive possibilities that weren't practical before. A brand that uses type as its primary differentiator can now do things with that type that feel genuinely new. I'm watching this space closely — the brands that figure out how to use variable type well are going to look very different from everyone else.

Classic serif revivals are also everywhere. After years of geometric sans-serifs, there's a clear appetite for type with history and weight. The interesting examples are the ones that mix eras — a Victorian display face paired with a grotesque body, or a humanist sans alongside a contemporary serif. Contrast over harmony.

---

I revisit these observations regularly and some of them will look different in six months. That's the nature of it. But the through-line I keep coming back to is this: the brands doing the most interesting work right now are the ones that have a clear point of view and commit to it fully, whatever aesthetic direction that takes them. Trend-chasing is visible at a distance. Conviction isn't.
