## The year in review is a design tool

At the end of each year, I review my practice. Not the business metrics — those matter but they're not what I'm reviewing. The practice. What kinds of problems did I work on? What did I learn? Where did I struggle? What surprised me?

This review is not self-congratulation or self-criticism. It's data collection. The patterns that emerge from a year of practice tell me something true about where I am as a designer and where I want to be in the next year.

## What this year clarified

This year clarified something about restraint that I'd understood conceptually but hadn't fully internalized: restraint is not the absence of decision. It's a particular kind of confident decision — the decision to do less, to trust the design, to resist the addition of the element that seems like it would help but actually wouldn't.

I made work this year that was stronger for what I didn't add to it. A poster that stayed uncomfortable and sparse rather than being resolved into something more conventionally pleasing. A brand mark that stayed simple rather than being made more interesting. A color palette that stayed quiet rather than being enlivened with an accent color that would have undermined the whole register.

## The problems that didn't get easier

Some problems I've been working with for years and still find genuinely hard. Presenting identity work in a way that's confident without being defensive. Knowing when a mark is finished rather than just complete. Producing the first sketch of a concept that's genuinely original rather than the recombination of existing work I've studied.

These problems getting harder is a sign that my standards have risen, not that I haven't improved. The version of me from five years ago would have found these problems easy to solve badly.

## What I want to explore next

Every year of practice opens lines of inquiry that I don't have time to pursue fully in that year. This year opened questions about variable typography as a brand tool, about the relationship between motion brand language and static brand language in identities that live fully in both contexts, and about the design of brand systems for brands that are genuinely complex — that don't reduce to a single mark and a clear identity.

These questions will shape next year's practice. I'll be following them with genuine curiosity rather than already having the answers.

## The long game

Brand design, motion design, poster design — all of these are long-game practices. The best work comes from a deep understanding of visual principles that develops over years, combined with the specific knowledge of materials, processes, and contexts that can only be built through practice. There's no shortcut to the long game. You just have to keep working, keep studying, keep asking whether what you made is actually as good as it can be.

That question — is this as good as it can be? — is the only quality control that matters.
