An event poster lives in a hostile environment. It's competing with twenty other posters on the same wall, with ambient noise, with people who are moving and not looking for it. The typical viewer gives it about one second of attention before deciding whether to stop.

That reality shapes everything about how I design them.

## The One-Second Rule

If your poster can't communicate its primary message in one second, it has failed at its core job. I test this by standing across the room from a print and looking at it for exactly one second, then looking away. What did I get? Name of event? Date? Mood?

If I got all three, the hierarchy is working. If I got the visual mood but missed the date, I have a hierarchy problem. If I got nothing, I have a composition problem.

## Contrast as Survival

On a cluttered wall, the poster that survives is the one with the most visual contrast — against the wall, against its neighbors, against expectation. I ask myself: if this poster is on a wall covered in photographic posters with muted palettes, what would stand out? A flat color and bold type. If it's surrounded by typographic minimalism? An unexpected image.

I design for the likely competition. Which means I research the venue, the event type, and the visual culture around it before I start.

## Limiting Information

Event posters get overloaded with information because clients are nervous about leaving anything out. My job is to protect the communication by limiting what's on the primary poster. Name, date, location: that's the irreducible minimum. Everything else is negotiable.

The rest of the information — lineup details, ticket pricing, supporting acts — belongs on a secondary piece: a digital version, a flyer, a follow-up post. The primary poster's job is to create interest and urgency. It doesn't have to answer every question.

## Format and Scale

I always design event posters for at least two formats simultaneously: large-scale print (A1/A0) and a digital 1:1 square for social. These formats have completely different requirements. What reads as sophisticated and spacious on an A1 can look empty and unresolved on a 1080x1080 square.

Designing for both from the start forces better decisions. If an element only works at large scale, I question whether it should be in the design at all.

## Letting the Event Tell You the Design

The best event poster designs emerge from the event itself, not from a generic "poster template" with the name swapped in. A jazz night and a metal show and a classical recital shouldn't look like the same design with different fonts. The visual language should reflect the sonic language.

I spend time with the music, the artist's work, the venue's atmosphere before I touch the computer. The design should feel like it belongs to this event specifically, not to the category of events it belongs to.
