## Redesign is archaeology first

Before I change anything about an existing brand identity, I need to understand what's actually there. Not what the brand guidelines say should be there — what is actually being used, in the wild, across real touchpoints. These are often very different things.

The visual identity audit is how I do that archaeology. It's a structured inventory and evaluation of the brand as it actually exists, not as it was designed to exist.

## Gathering the real-world materials

The first step is collecting real-world brand materials — not design files or guidelines documents. Business cards that were actually printed. Marketing materials that went to market. Website screenshots. Environmental signage photos. Social media graphics. Merchandise. Anything that carries the brand in public.

This collection almost always reveals drift. A brand that has clear guidelines often has terrible adherence to those guidelines in practice. The marketing team used a different font because the approved one wasn't installed on their computers. The website has a different shade of the primary color because nobody communicated the hex value to the development team. The merchandise vendor matched the logo color to what was on screen, not to the Pantone specification.

## Evaluating what's working

The audit is not just about finding problems. I look equally hard for what's working — what elements of the existing brand have genuine equity, are immediately recognizable, and would be damaged by changing them.

Brand equity is real and it's fragile. A typeface that's been on a brand's storefronts for twenty years carries recognition value that a "better" typeface replacement doesn't have for the first five years of use. A color that customers associate strongly with the brand is an asset. Identifying these equities is as important as identifying the weaknesses.

## The gap analysis

After mapping what exists and what works, I produce a gap analysis — a structured comparison of the brand as designed versus the brand as deployed. The gaps tell me where the identity system has failed, and understanding why it failed tells me how to design a system that won't fail in the same way.

Common gap causes I see repeatedly: brand guidelines that are too complex to follow without design expertise; color specifications that don't hold up in digital contexts; typefaces that were expensive and difficult to license for non-design staff; logo files delivered in formats that distort when resized.

## Making recommendations that will actually hold

The audit findings shape my redesign recommendations in a very specific way: I design for the team that will actually implement the brand, not for an ideal team that perfectly follows every guideline.

If the client's marketing team produces their own social media graphics and they're not designers, the new brand system needs to be simple enough for non-designers to use without compromising its integrity. If the brand will be applied by vendors across different countries, the color specifications need to be robust enough to survive imperfect process printing conditions.

A brand identity that works beautifully in the design studio and falls apart in deployment is a design failure, regardless of its conceptual sophistication.
