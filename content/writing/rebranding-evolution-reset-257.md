Rebranding decisions are among the highest-stakes choices in brand management. Rebrand too cautiously and you miss an opportunity to address real strategic problems. Rebrand too dramatically and you abandon equity that took years to build. The designer's job in this conversation isn't to advocate for the most work — it's to diagnose accurately and recommend appropriately.

This requires being willing to tell a client that they need less work than they think, or more work than they want to hear.

## The Four Rebranding Scenarios

Through experience and client work, I've identified four scenarios that require different responses:

Strategic shift: the brand's positioning has fundamentally changed — new market, new audience, new product category. The existing visual identity was built for a different strategy and actively misrepresents the brand today. This warrants a full identity redesign.

Visual refresh: the brand's strategy is sound but the visual identity has aged poorly, looks dated relative to the competitive landscape, or has accumulated inconsistency over time. This warrants a systematic refresh — updating without abandoning.

System development: the brand has a mark and basic colors but no real system. There's no typography specification, no layout principles, no graphic language. The brief isn't to redesign but to build out what's missing. This is system design, not rebranding.

Execution problem: the brand identity is actually fine, but it's being applied inconsistently or incorrectly. This isn't a design problem — it's a process and governance problem. Redesigning the identity won't fix it.

Diagnosing which scenario applies is the first and most important work.

## The Equity Audit

Whatever the scenario, I assess existing brand equity before recommending any changes. Equity — the recognition and associations the brand has built — is real value that takes time and investment to create. Discarding it without strategic justification is expensive.

I measure equity informally through client conversations (what do customers consistently respond to?), market research if available, and by evaluating the brand in its category (what do people actually know and remember about this brand?).

High-equity elements — a distinctive mark shape, a recognizable color, a well-known name treatment — should be preserved and evolved, not replaced, unless there's a compelling strategic reason to abandon them.

## The Evolution Approach

For brands undergoing a visual refresh, I work with what exists rather than starting over. I identify which elements should be preserved (the equity-bearing elements), which should be updated (elements that have aged or aren't performing), and which should be added (system components that were missing).

Evolution is harder than starting fresh. It requires making new decisions that respect old decisions, creating coherence between what's staying and what's changing. But it's almost always the right approach for established brands because it preserves the equity investment while addressing the problems.

## The Reset Scenario

When the strategic shift is fundamental — new name, new market position, complete repositioning — evolution isn't sufficient. The brand needs to start over.

This is rare. Most apparent rebranding needs are actually refinement needs. I resist the designer's natural inclination toward the more expansive brief and honestly assess whether the reset is warranted by the strategic situation.

When it is warranted, I proceed with full conviction. A half-hearted reset produces the worst outcome: equity abandoned without a strong new identity replacing it.
