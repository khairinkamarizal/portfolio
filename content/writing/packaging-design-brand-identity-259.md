Packaging is the most demanding application in brand identity work. Unlike a poster or a business card, packaging has to work on six faces simultaneously, in three dimensions, at multiple distances, often surrounded by competing products in a retail environment. It's also the brand touchpoint the customer interacts with most physically — they pick it up, turn it over, open it.

Every design decision in packaging carries weight that it doesn't carry in any other medium.

## The Retail Context First

Before I touch any packaging design, I visit or research the retail environment where the product will be sold. What does the competitive set look like on shelf? What's the typical viewing distance? What's the lighting? How are products arranged — faced forward, stacked, hung?

This context determines the hierarchy of the packaging's visual elements. In a supermarket, a product has roughly two seconds to register from across an aisle. The most important element — usually the brand name or the product's primary visual — needs to be readable at that distance and in those conditions.

## Structure and Dieline as Constraints

Packaging design always starts with the physical structure — the box, bag, bottle, or can. This structure isn't just the canvas; it shapes every design decision. I get the dieline (the flattened template showing all faces) before I design anything and work within its constraints from the start.

The primary face gets the most design attention. Secondary faces get supporting content: product information, legal requirements, ingredients, barcodes. The way these elements flow between faces needs to be coherent when the package is assembled.

## The Hierarchy Is Different Here

Packaging hierarchy operates differently from poster or print hierarchy. In posters, hierarchy is usually: headline → supporting information. In packaging, hierarchy is typically: brand → product name → variant/flavor → supporting claim → functional information.

Each level needs to be distinguishable from the next while maintaining the brand's visual language. And the relative scale of these elements needs to work at retail display distance and at reading distance.

## Color as a Navigation Tool

In a product line with multiple variants — flavors, sizes, formulations — color becomes a navigation system. Each variant gets a distinct color treatment, but all variants need to read as part of the same family.

I build variant color systems from the brand's palette, assigning each variant a distinct primary hue while maintaining shared structural elements (typography, layout, graphic language) that establish family membership.

## The Unboxing Experience

Packaging is experienced sequentially. The customer sees it on shelf, picks it up, reads it, buys it, takes it home, opens it. Each of these interactions is a design moment.

The unboxing experience — what happens when the package is opened — is often under-designed. A thoughtful interior print, a tissue paper in a brand color, a structural detail that feels satisfying to open — these touches create memorable brand moments at a high-engagement interaction point.

## Print Production for Packaging

Packaging has more complex production specifications than most print work: flexographic printing for flexible packaging, lithographic for folding carton, screen printing for rigid containers. Each process has different color gamut, registration tolerances, and substrate properties.

I work with the packaging vendor early and often, getting production specs and requesting press proofs on the actual substrate. The visual difference between a screen design and a printed package can be dramatic without this collaboration.
