Cultural institution work is some of the most demanding identity design I do. The stakeholder complexity is intense — boards, directors, curators, donors, community members, all with opinions and investment in the outcome. The scope is broad — the identity has to work on a blockbuster exhibition catalog, a wayfinding system, a social media presence, and a printed membership card. And the identity has to last — institutions don't rebrand every five years the way commercial brands do.

## The Mission Is the Brand

Commercial brands are built around positioning — the unique value proposition in a competitive market. Cultural institutions are built around mission. The identity serves the mission, not the market position.

This means starting every cultural institution project by understanding the mission deeply and asking: what does this institution's visual identity need to communicate about what it stands for? Not what it sells, not how it differentiates, but what it is in the world.

## Neutrality as a Design Problem

Many cultural institutions want their identity to feel "neutral" — not to impose a specific aesthetic on the art or cultural material they present. This is a legitimate concern, but neutrality is not the same as no character.

My approach: build the identity around typographic and spatial systems rather than graphic flourishes. A strong typographic system — well-chosen typefaces, clear hierarchy rules, consistent spacing — gives the institution a recognizable voice without competing with the content it presents.

The Tate, the Walker Art Center, and the Guggenheim Bilbao all have identities that are immediately recognizable without being visually dominant. They achieve this through systematic consistency rather than graphic assertiveness.

## Longevity Over Trend

A cultural institution's identity needs to hold up for a decade, minimum. Many institutional identities run for twenty or thirty years. This demands conservatism about trend-driven choices.

I avoid anything that feels acutely contemporary — typefaces that are having a moment, spatial compositions that feel fashionable, color palettes that are culturally coded to a specific period. I want the identity to feel considered and intelligent, not timely.

## The Exhibition System

One of the unique challenges of cultural institution identity is the exhibition system — the sub-brand architecture for temporary and permanent exhibitions. Each exhibition has its own visual identity, but it needs to coexist with the institutional identity.

I build flexible systems that give exhibitions a canvas — a set of grid structures, type hierarchies, and color conventions — within which each exhibition can express its own character. The institution is the frame; the exhibition is the painting.
