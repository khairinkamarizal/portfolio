## What Service Brands Are Actually Selling

A product company sells a thing. You can hold it, use it, evaluate it before buying. A service company sells a promise: we will do something well on your behalf, at a future time, with outcomes you can't fully predict in advance.

That fundamental difference in what's being sold changes what the brand identity needs to do. A product brand can let the product speak. A service brand has to speak for itself.

The identity's job is to build enough credibility and trust before the service is experienced that the prospect decides to take the risk. That's a specific, high-stakes communication challenge.

## The Credibility Signals

For service businesses, I look for visual signals that communicate credibility in the specific forms that matter to the target client.

**Stability** — the brand looks like it has been around, or at least like it intends to be around. This comes from confident, settled design choices. Not trendy, not provisional.

**Expertise** — the brand looks like it belongs to people who know what they're doing. This comes from quality of execution: precise typography, confident color decisions, a system that holds together under scrutiny.

**Authority** — particularly important in professional services. The brand looks like a peer, not a supplicant. It doesn't over-explain or over-reassure. It presents itself with quiet confidence.

## The Problem With Generic Professionalism

The default visual language of professional services — deep blue, conservative serif or clean sans-serif, abstract mark, white backgrounds — communicates "professional" but communicates almost nothing else. It fails the distinctiveness test.

In a category where all the competitors look professionally credible, looking professionally credible doesn't differentiate. The question becomes: what makes this firm or practice distinct from all the others that also look professionally credible?

I push service business clients to identify their genuine differentiator — not "we're more experienced" or "we care more" but something specific about how they work, who they serve, or what outcomes they produce — and then design the identity to make that differentiator visible.

## Personality Matters More Than Category Signaling

In service businesses with strong personal brand elements — consultants, creative professionals, boutique practices — personality in the identity often matters more than category signaling.

A financial advisor who works with creative entrepreneurs benefits more from an identity that speaks to creative entrepreneurs than from an identity that signals "financial services." The category signal is less important than the audience signal.

This requires more strategic clarity about the target audience — who specifically, what do they value, what visual language resonates with them — and the courage to design for that audience even if the resulting identity looks less like a "typical" firm in the category.

## Proof of Work

For service businesses with strong portfolios or impressive client rosters, the identity system should create a natural home for proof of work. Case studies, client logos, before-and-after work — these are often the most persuasive brand assets a service business has.

I design visual systems that present this proof with appropriate emphasis: not buried in footnotes, not overwhelming the brand itself, but given the prominence of a supporting argument.

The brand makes the promise. The work keeps it.
