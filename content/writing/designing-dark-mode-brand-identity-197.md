## Dark Isn't Just Inverted

The most common mistake in designing for dark backgrounds is treating it as a simple inversion — reversing black and white, using light colors where dark ones were. This approach almost always produces unsatisfying results.

Dark background design requires its own logic: different color values, adjusted contrast relationships, reconsidered mark weights, and sometimes genuinely different design decisions.

## Why Colors Change on Dark Backgrounds

Colors that look vibrant against white can look garish against black. Subtle color distinctions that read clearly in a light context can flatten into visual similarity against dark backgrounds. The chromatic adaptation of the human visual system — the way our eyes adjust to the ambient light level — changes how we perceive color.

Practical consequence: brand colors need to be re-evaluated for dark background use. The hex or RGB values that work on light backgrounds often need adjustment for dark surfaces. The hue may stay the same; the saturation or brightness typically needs modification.

## Logo Mark Behavior on Dark Surfaces

A logo designed primarily for light backgrounds often needs a specifically adapted version for dark surfaces. This isn't just inverting colors — it may mean adjusting stroke weights (strokes can appear heavier on dark backgrounds), modifying color relationships within the mark, or simplifying elements that become visually confusing against dark.

I design dark-background logo versions as genuine design work, not as production derivatives.

## Type Legibility on Dark

Type set against dark backgrounds is subject to halation — the visual effect of light type appearing to bloom or glow against a dark field. This makes high-contrast white type on black slightly harder to read at body sizes than the equivalent dark-on-light setting.

Practical adjustment: slightly warm the "white" in dark-background body text — a very slightly warm off-white is easier to read for extended periods than pure white. Reduce font weight slightly if the typeface is available in multiple weights.

## The Full Dark Surface

Full dark surface design — where the entire composition is built on a dark field with light content — has a different atmospheric quality from light surface design with dark accents. It communicates differently.

For brands whose identity benefits from that atmospheric quality — premium, nocturnal, cinematic, contemplative — designing primary materials in dark mode is appropriate. For brands that communicate openness, clarity, and accessibility, dark as primary surface often works against those values.

## Brand Coherence Across Modes

When a brand operates in both light and dark contexts, the two modes need to feel clearly related — same brand, different environment — not like different brands. Maintaining shared typographic proportions, consistent mark character, and related (if not identical) color relationships across modes creates that coherence.
