## The Most Common Misconception

Nearly every new client who comes to me for brand work asks for a logo. What they need is a brand. These are not the same thing, and the confusion leads to underinvestment in the work that actually matters.

A logo is a mark. A brand is the total impression your organization makes on every person who encounters it. The logo is one component of the brand — an important one, but one component among many.

## What a Brand Actually Is

A brand is the sum of every experience, impression, and association a person has with an organization. It includes the visual identity (logo, colors, typography), but also the verbal identity (how the organization speaks and writes), the service experience (how people are treated), the product experience (what using the thing is actually like), and the cultural associations that build up over time.

Apple's brand isn't the bitten apple mark. It's the sum of every product someone has used, every store they've walked into, every ad they've seen, every time someone has said "I'm a Mac person." The mark is a symbol that activates those associations — but the associations are the brand.

## Why This Matters for Design Work

When clients think they need a logo, they often think the logo will do all the work. A beautiful mark and the brand problems are solved. In reality, a logo applied to inconsistent, poorly considered, or actively bad experiences doesn't build a good brand. It just gives the bad experiences a consistent face.

My job isn't just to design a logo. It's to help a client think about what impression they want to make, and then build the visual components that support that impression. The logo is often the smallest part of that work.

## The Verbal Identity Gap

One of the most underinvested areas of brand development is verbal identity — how the brand sounds. What words does it use? What's the tone — formal or conversational, precise or approachable? How does it describe its own products and services?

Visual and verbal identity need to be in alignment. A brand that looks premium but writes like a bureaucracy is incoherent. A brand that sounds human but looks cold and corporate is incoherent. I always address verbal identity alongside visual identity, even if just at the foundational level.

## What I Tell Clients

When clients come to me and say they need a logo, I tell them: yes, we'll design a mark, and we'll also build the visual system that makes that mark work in the world. And if they have budget and time, we should also talk about how the brand sounds and what it believes. Because the mark by itself is just a shape until everything around it gives it meaning.
