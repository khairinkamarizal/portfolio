When people talk about brand voice, they usually mean copy — the words, the tone, the way a brand uses language. But before anyone reads the words, they're reading the type. The typefaces you choose are communicating personality, authority, and attitude before a single word is processed.

Typography is brand voice made visual.

## What Typefaces Actually Communicate

Different type classifications carry different associations, reinforced by decades of use in specific contexts:

**Humanist serifs** feel learned, literary, considered. They suggest depth and tradition without stuffiness.

**Geometric sans-serifs** feel modern, precise, rational. They suggest clarity and forward-thinking.

**Grotesque sans-serifs** feel neutral, utilitarian, democratic. They get out of the way and let content speak.

**Slab serifs** feel sturdy, confident, direct. They suggest reliability and no-nonsense character.

**Display typefaces** feel specific — each one carries the particular associations of its design. A condensed display sans feels urgent and efficient; a wide, open display face feels relaxed and expansive.

These aren't rules. They're tendencies, shaped by use and culture. But they're reliable enough to design with.

## Building a Type System

A brand type system typically has two or three functional roles: display (for headlines and primary communication), text (for body copy and supporting content), and optionally a functional type (for UI, captions, or highly technical contexts).

The relationship between display and text type defines much of the brand's visual personality. A high-contrast pairing — a dramatic display face alongside a neutral body — creates energy and tension. A low-contrast pairing — display and body from the same family — creates harmony and cohesion.

## Licensing and Practicality

Type licensing is one of the most frequently overlooked aspects of brand identity work, and it's caused more than a few expensive problems for clients.

When recommending a typeface, I verify the license for every intended use: print, web (webfont license), apps (if applicable), and digital advertising. Some licenses are per-machine, some per-pageview, some per-installation. The typeface that costs $200 for a desktop license can cost thousands per year for a high-traffic webfont license.

I present license costs alongside type recommendations, not as an afterthought.

## Custom Type

For brands that need maximum distinctiveness and are willing to invest, custom type is the ultimate solution. A typeface designed specifically for one brand can encode the brand's character in every letterform.

I've worked with type designers on custom projects. The collaboration between brand designer and type designer — translating brand character into typographic form — is one of the most interesting creative processes in identity work. It's also expensive and time-consuming. For most brands, a thoughtfully licensed and customized existing typeface is the right answer.

## Consistency and Flexibility

A type system should be specific enough to be recognizable and flexible enough to work across every context. Over-specifying (every single use case explicitly defined) creates brittleness. Under-specifying (just a list of typefaces, no guidance) creates chaos.

The guidelines I write specify the type scale (a defined set of sizes for different hierarchical roles), the weight system (which weights are used when), and the hierarchy of choices (use X for headlines in most cases, Y when the context requires something different, here's why).
