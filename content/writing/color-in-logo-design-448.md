Before a viewer reads a logo's name or recognizes its mark, they've already perceived its color. Color is the fastest communicating element in visual design. That speed makes color selection one of the most consequential decisions in logo design.

And yet color is often treated as decoration — chosen because it looks good, matches the founder's taste, or references the category's conventional palette. That approach misses what color can do.

## Color Has to Work in Black and White First

Before I make any color decisions about a logo, I design it in black and white. This is non-negotiable.

A logo that only works in full color is a fragile logo. It will fail when reproduced in single-color on merchandise, when printed in black-and-white on a fax or a photocopied document, when used in contexts where color reproduction can't be guaranteed.

The structure of the mark has to work without color. Color then adds another layer of meaning and character on top of a mark that already works. This is the correct order of operations.

## The Competitive Context First

The first color question isn't "what does this color mean" but "what are competitors using, and where is there space to differentiate?"

Every established category has a color logic. Tech companies lean into blue, white, and electric accent colors. Natural food brands cluster around earthy greens and browns. Finance uses conservative blues and dark greens. Healthcare splits between clinical blues and warmer, more approachable palettes.

Knowing the category landscape tells me which colors are already occupied and where there's room to own territory. Sometimes differentiation means working against the category color logic entirely. Sometimes it means taking a color that everyone uses but expressing it with more specificity and distinction.

## The Specific Color Matters More Than the Hue Family

The decision isn't "blue" or "green." The decision is which specific blue, at what saturation, at what lightness. The difference between a muted sage green and a vivid emerald green is enormous, even though both are technically green. They communicate completely different things.

I use Pantone swatches to make final color decisions, because Pantone colors have precise specifications that survive reproduction across media. "A warm mid-value terracotta at about 60% saturation" is too vague to be a brand specification. Pantone 7526 C is precise.

## How Many Colors in a Logo

The logo itself should use one, possibly two colors. Three is the absolute maximum and should be rare. Every additional color adds complexity that creates problems in reproduction and dilutes the ownable color territory.

Single-color logos are usually the strongest. Coca-Cola red. UPS brown. Tiffany blue. The association between the brand and a single specific color is one of the most powerful brand assets an identity can have. Building that association requires consistency and commitment, which means not fragmenting the color strategy across multiple logo colors.

## The Full Color System

The logo color is the beginning of the brand color system, not the end. One or two logo colors need to be supported by a palette that handles the full range of brand communications: backgrounds, typography, secondary graphic elements, photography treatment.

That palette needs to be developed in relationship to the logo color. Not as a separate exercise, but as a unified system where everything works together. The logo color anchors the palette. Everything else serves it.

## The Documentation Requirement

Every logo color needs four specifications in the brand documentation: Pantone, CMYK, RGB, and hex. These aren't interchangeable — they're specifications for different reproduction contexts. A printer using offset CMYK needs the CMYK value. A web developer needs the hex. Provide all four, because you can't predict which specification a vendor will need.

Color is where the brand starts for most viewers. Make the decision deliberately.
