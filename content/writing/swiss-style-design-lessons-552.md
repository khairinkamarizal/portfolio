## A Style Built on Conviction

The International Typographic Style — Swiss Style — emerged in the 1950s from designers in Basel and Zurich who believed that design had a moral obligation to clarity. Not just an aesthetic preference for clean forms, but a genuine conviction that visual communication should be honest, ordered, and accessible.

I'm not a dogmatist. I don't think Swiss Style is the only valid approach to graphic design. But I think its underlying convictions are correct, and returning to them regularly makes my work better.

## The Grid as Ethics

Swiss designers didn't use grids because grids looked nice. They used grids because a grid imposes a discipline on the designer that serves the viewer. Arbitrary placement serves the designer's intuition. Gridded placement serves the reader's comprehension.

This is an ethical argument, not just an aesthetic one. The grid is in service of the viewer. Every design decision should be.

I think about this framing when I'm tempted to make a composition more complex or expressive at the expense of clarity. Who does this serve? If the answer is "me" — my desire to make something visually interesting — I question it. If the answer is "the viewer's understanding," I proceed.

## Objective Photography

Swiss Style favored photography over illustration because photography was considered more objective — a document of reality rather than an interpretation of it. This conviction is debatable (photography is always a point of view), but the underlying principle is useful: the imagery in a design should serve the message, not the designer's expressive impulses.

When I select imagery for a design, I ask: what does this image say about the subject? Is that what I want to say? Or did I select it because it's beautiful and I want to use it?

The most common version of this failure is selecting images that are visually interesting but communicatively vague. Beautiful texture, compelling light — but no clear relationship to the content. Swiss Style would reject this immediately.

## Sans Serif as Default

Swiss Style favored sans-serif typefaces — particularly Helvetica, Univers, and Akzidenz Grotesk — for their neutrality. The argument: a neutral typeface doesn't impose personality on the message; it serves the message without distorting it.

I don't follow this completely. Typefaces are never fully neutral, and sometimes the personality of a face is exactly what a message needs. But the principle of choosing typefaces for their suitability to the message — not for their trendiness or the designer's personal affection — is sound.

## Asymmetric Balance

Swiss designers moved away from centered, symmetric layouts toward asymmetric compositions held in balance by the deliberate positioning of elements. This was a philosophical statement: symmetry imposes a false equality on information that is inherently unequal.

I use asymmetric balance constantly. A dominant element in one corner, counterbalanced by a cluster of smaller elements in the opposite area. A strong text block counterbalanced by open space. The composition is in tension, which creates energy that symmetric layouts can't achieve.

## What to Take, What to Leave

I take the commitment to clarity, the systematic thinking, the viewer-first ethic. I leave the dogmatism. Swiss Style at its worst became a formula that produced competent but lifeless work.

Design should be clear and it should be alive. Those aren't in conflict — they just take more work to achieve together.
