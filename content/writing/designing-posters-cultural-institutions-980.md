## A Different Kind of Poster Problem

Cultural institution posters — museum exhibitions, gallery openings, concert series, theater seasons — sit in a different category from commercial event posters. They need to communicate cultural authority and aesthetic credibility while remaining accessible to broad public audiences. They represent organizations with established visual identities that the poster must align with. And they often need to work across multiple formats simultaneously: street-level outdoor, transit advertising, lobby display, print collateral, and digital.

Getting this balance right requires understanding both the institution's visual identity and the specific exhibition's or event's subject matter.

## The Institution Identity vs. The Exhibition Identity

One of the core tensions in cultural institution poster design is between the institution's brand and the content it's presenting. A natural history museum has an established visual identity. A temporary exhibition about deep ocean ecosystems has its own visual world. How prominently does each appear on the poster?

The answer varies by institution and context. Some institutions have such strong visual identities that all exhibition materials are clearly branded as that institution's work — the poster is a [Museum Name] communication that happens to be about deep oceans. Other institutions subordinate their identity to serve the content more completely, allowing each exhibition to develop its own visual personality within a loose institutional framework.

I establish this hierarchy in conversation with the institution before beginning design work. The answer shapes every design decision.

## Typography as Cultural Signal

Cultural institutions communicate authority and rigor through their typographic choices. A major art museum presenting a retrospective of a twentieth-century modernist painter uses typographic references to modernist print culture — clean, precise, historically informed — not the casual energy of a commercial event poster.

This doesn't mean cultural institution posters should be typographically conservative. The most compelling exhibition posters I've designed have been typographically bold and surprising. But the bold choices were made with deep knowledge of the design history relevant to the subject matter, so the surprise felt earned and informed rather than arbitrary.

## The Role of Exhibition Artwork

Major art exhibitions often feature the artwork being exhibited prominently in the promotional poster. This is understandable from a marketing perspective but creates design complications. The artwork has its own compositional logic that may not work as a background or featured element in a designed poster format.

I work with the artwork rather than around it. If I'm designing an exhibition poster that features a painting, I treat the painting's compositional elements — its dominant colors, its geometric structure, its figure-ground relationships — as design inputs for the poster. The poster should feel like it emerged from the artwork rather than just containing it.

## Format Planning for Multiple Applications

Exhibition campaigns typically need a full suite of formats: main exhibition poster (often A1 or larger), A3 lobby display, A4 and A5 flyer, digital horizontal (1200×628px), digital square (1080×1080px), and outdoor applications if budget allows.

I design the exhibition visual identity at the largest format first, then systematically adapt to each smaller and differently-proportioned format. Some elements work at large scale that need simplification at smaller scale. Some compositions work in landscape that need redesign for portrait.

Planning the full format suite at the beginning prevents the painful process of retroactively making a designed identity work in formats that weren't considered.

## Longevity in Exhibition Design

Exhibition posters often remain on display for the full duration of the exhibition — months, sometimes longer. The design needs to reward repeated viewing in a way that advertising materials don't. A piece that looks right at first glance but reveals more with familiarity fits this context better than one that makes its full impact immediately and exhausts itself.

I think about this as the museum visitor test: someone who visits the museum three times during the exhibition run sees the same poster each time. Does it hold up? Is there something to notice at the third viewing that wasn't visible at the first?
