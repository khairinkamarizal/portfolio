Custom logotype work sits at the border between typeface design and brand design. It demands enough understanding of letterform construction to build credible, optically sound characters, combined with enough brand thinking to ensure the resulting word belongs to this specific brand and no other.

I approach custom logotype work differently depending on the budget and the brief — from light customization of an existing typeface to drawing every letter from scratch.

## Light Customization: The 80/20 Approach

For most brand identity budgets, light customization of an existing typeface produces excellent results. I choose a commercial typeface that's close to the target feeling, then modify the most visible and distinctive letterforms — typically the first letter of the brand name, any repeated letters, and any characters with distinctive shapes in the specific word.

The goal is to move from "this is [typeface name] set as the brand name" to "this is a custom wordmark that started from [typeface name] but now belongs to this brand." The level of modification needed to cross that line is often less than people expect.

Typical modifications: adjusted proportions on key characters, terminal reshaping, stroke weight redistribution, counter space adjustment, and thorough optical spacing.

## Medium Customization: Systematic Modification

When the budget allows more, I extend the customization systematically across all characters in the wordmark. This means making consistent formal decisions — the angle of stressed strokes, the style of terminals, the proportion of counters — and applying them across every letter.

At this level, the wordmark no longer reads as a modified typeface at all. It reads as drawn letterforms that happen to have internal consistency. The result is more distinctive and more owned.

## Full Custom: Drawing From Structure

For brands that need a fully custom logotype with no typeface ancestry, I draw from structure rather than from existing letterforms. I define the formal parameters — weight, contrast, proportion, terminal style, construction logic — and build every character to those parameters.

This approach is slowest and most expensive but produces the most distinctive result. The letterforms belong uniquely to the brand because they were built for it.

## The Consistency Requirement

Across all levels of custom work, the consistency requirement is absolute. Every decision made for one letterform — a specific terminal angle, a specific treatment of the thin-to-thick transition — must apply consistently to every other letterform that shares the opportunity for that decision.

Inconsistency in formal decisions is the most reliable marker of amateur letterform work. The eye detects it immediately, even when the viewer can't articulate the problem.

## Testing Custom Work

I test custom logotype work rigorously before delivering:

- At multiple sizes from display to small application
- In all required color contexts (positive, reversed, single-color)
- With the same viewer-testing approach I'd use for any wordmark
- Against the original typeface source if applicable, to confirm the modifications have been thoroughgoing enough

The test is not "does it look good in the presentation?" It's "does it read as a designed object that belongs to this brand in every context it will actually appear?"
