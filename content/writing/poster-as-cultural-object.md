A poster is a strange object. It's designed to communicate a specific message in a specific moment, but the best ones outlive that moment and become cultural objects — collected, studied, reproduced, referenced long after the event they announced has passed.

Understanding this dual nature — the poster as immediate communication and as enduring cultural object — changes how I approach the work.

## The Poster's Ephemeral Context

A poster is born in a specific context: a concert, a political movement, an exhibition, a product launch. Its initial purpose is to announce, attract, or communicate in that specific moment. The vast majority of posters are read, discarded, and forgotten. They do their job and disappear.

This context shapes the design requirements: it needs to work quickly, communicate clearly at a glance, compete with its environment, and be apprehended by an audience that's not seeking it.

## When Posters Become Documents

Some posters transcend their original context and become documents of their time. Polish cinema posters from the 1960s. Anti-war posters from the late 1960s. Psychedelic concert posters from the same era. These objects tell us something about the visual culture, the political climate, and the design possibilities of their moment.

The designers who made these posters weren't thinking about posterity. They were solving the immediate communication problem. But their solutions were distinctive enough, well-crafted enough, and rooted in enough genuine cultural experience that they've earned a different kind of longevity.

## What I Carry from Design History

When I design a poster, I carry the history of the form. I know that Cassandre used bold flat color and geometric simplification to distill transportation and luxury into pure visual impact. I know that Wolfgang Weingart broke the Swiss grid to demonstrate that rules derive their authority from understanding, not convention. I know that the designers of the WPA posters used bold silhouette and flat color to communicate across economic and educational barriers.

This history isn't a constraint — it's vocabulary. I'm working in a form with a history, and understanding that history gives me access to a richer set of decisions.

## Craft as Respect

The best poster work I see today is made by designers who take the form seriously as a form — who care about the quality of the printing, the specificity of the typeface, the precision of the composition. They treat the poster as an object worth making well, not just as a job to complete.

This care shows. It's the difference between a poster that gets seen, photographed, and shared versus one that gets torn down and forgotten. The audience may not be able to articulate why one piece holds their attention and another doesn't, but they feel the difference between something made with serious intention and something made to satisfy a brief.

## The Work I Want to Make

I want to make posters that hold up. That do their immediate job and survive the moment. That a designer 20 years from now could look at and find something of value — not just a historical artifact, but a solution to a problem that's still interesting. That's a high standard. It's what keeps the work demanding and worth doing.
