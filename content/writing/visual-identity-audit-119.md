## Why Audits Matter

The worst brand redesigns I've seen started without an audit. The designer swept in, declared everything outdated, and replaced it all. Sometimes that's the right call. More often, it isn't. There's equity in existing brand elements — recognition built through years of consistent use — that doesn't show up in a design critique but absolutely shows up when you remove it.

The audit tells you what that equity is before you touch anything.

## What I Look For

An identity audit examines every touchpoint where the brand appears: the logo and its variations, the color palette, the typefaces, the photography style, the illustration approach, the iconography, the layout templates, and how all of these are actually used in practice.

I look for inconsistency (a signal that the system is unclear or poorly documented), for elements that have clearly aged (aesthetic choices that date the brand), for equity markers (elements that appear consistently and have built recognition), and for gaps (places where the identity provides no guidance and designers are improvising).

## Separating Sentiment from Equity

This is the trickiest part. Internal teams often have strong emotional attachment to brand elements. The original logo designed by a founder. The color that's been on everything since the company started. These feelings are real and worth acknowledging — but they're not evidence that the element has market value.

I try to distinguish between "this element has built recognition with customers" and "this element matters to people inside the company." Both are legitimate considerations, but they call for different responses.

## What's Worth Keeping

Elements with genuine customer recognition — a distinctive color, a well-known mark, a memorable visual device — have value that should be weighed against any redesign decision. I don't throw out recognizable elements without a clear strategic reason to do so.

The goal of most rebrands is evolution, not revolution. A brand that retains its recognizable core while modernizing the system around it tends to perform better than one that starts from scratch.

## Documenting the Audit

The output of an audit is a written document, not just a presentation. It maps the current state, identifies specific problems, notes where equity exists, and frames the design brief for the rebrand work that follows.

This document becomes the foundation for the project. Without it, the rebrand work starts from opinion. With it, it starts from evidence.

## The Practical Upshot

If you're inheriting a brand engagement rather than building from scratch, do the audit before you do anything else. It will save you from destroying things worth keeping and help you focus your effort on what actually needs to change.
