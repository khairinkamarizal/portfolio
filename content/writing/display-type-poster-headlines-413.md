A display typeface does something body copy typefaces don't need to do: it carries emotional weight at scale. When a headline is set at 120pt and takes up a third of the poster, the typeface isn't just delivering information — it's setting a tone, communicating a personality, establishing the register of everything that follows.

That's a lot to ask of a letterform. Which is why display type selection for poster work deserves more time than most designers give it.

## The Register Question

Before I open a type library, I think about register. Not style — register. What is this poster trying to communicate emotionally? Is it urgent? Elegant? Playful? Authoritative? Melancholic? Confrontational?

Each of these emotional registers has a typographic vocabulary. Condensed gothic types suggest urgency and power. Wide-set serifs suggest authority and permanence. Expressive script faces suggest intimacy and warmth. Geometric sans-serifs suggest modernity and precision. High-contrast transitional serifs suggest sophistication.

The problem isn't finding a typeface that looks good. It's finding a typeface whose inherent emotional register matches the poster's content. A mismatched register — even if both the typeface and the content are individually well-executed — creates a cognitive dissonance the viewer feels without being able to name.

## Historical Context Is Not Optional

Typefaces carry historical associations. Blackletter types reference German print tradition, medieval manuscripts, and — more recently — metal band aesthetics and hip hop culture. Art Nouveau display faces reference the turn of the 20th century. Swiss grotesques reference Modernism and International Style graphic design.

When I use a display face for poster work, I need to know what historical associations it carries and decide whether those associations work for the content. Sometimes I want the associations — a poster for a traditional craft fair might genuinely benefit from an Arts and Crafts typeface. Sometimes I want to work against them — using a strict Swiss grotesque for an emotional, human-interest poster creates a productive tension.

Ignorance of historical associations isn't neutrality. It's accidentally signaling things you didn't intend.

## What to Look For at Scale

Not all typefaces designed for display use actually perform well at display scale. Some things I check:

**Optical size appropriateness.** Some typefaces have specific display optical sizes with finer spacing, higher contrast, and more delicate details — designed specifically for large-scale use. These will always out-perform the same face used at sizes it wasn't designed for.

**Spacing at scale.** Default kerning from most fonts is optimized for moderate sizes. At headline scale, I need to kern manually. I'm looking for a typeface where the letter relationships can be tightened to feel unified rather than letterspaced.

**Contrast behavior.** High-contrast typefaces — those with significant variation between thick and thin strokes — look stunning at large scale and can become illegible at small scale. That's fine for a giant poster headline that only appears big. It's a problem if the same typeface will be used for body copy or captions.

**Detail resolution.** Some typefaces have subtle details — ball terminals, ink traps, bracketed serifs — that only become visible at large scale. I want to know what I'm getting at the sizes I'll actually use.

## The Wrong Reason to Choose a Display Face

The most common bad reason to choose a display typeface: it's trendy. Variable display faces with extreme axes. Brutalist experimental typography. Whatever is currently filling design Tumblrs and Pinterest boards. 

Trend-driven display type choices age fast and date the work. The poster that looked current in 2019 looks like a relic in 2024. I prefer typefaces with some proven staying power, or typefaces so deeply embedded in a historical tradition that they exist outside trend cycles entirely.

The second wrong reason: it's on the first page of every type library's "Display" category. Popular typefaces are overused. I'd rather spend an hour finding something less familiar that fits better than default to something I've seen on forty posters this year.

## The Right Question

The question I always come back to: does this typeface tell the truth about what this poster is about? If it does, the choice is right. If there's any gap between what the type says and what the poster means, I keep looking.
