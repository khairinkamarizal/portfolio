Most logo design education assumes Roman script. A single alphabet, left-to-right, with well-established conventions for wordmark design. The moment a brand needs to operate in Arabic, Hebrew, Chinese, or other scripts, a different set of challenges emerges.

Designing for multiple scripts is one of the most complex problems in identity design.

## The Problem With Transliteration

The instinct for many brands entering new markets is to transliterate the brand name into the local script — rendering the phonetic equivalent of the name in Arabic or Chinese characters. This often produces poor results.

Transliteration in non-Roman scripts doesn't always produce phonetically accurate results, because the phoneme inventory of the target language may not include all the sounds in the original name. More importantly, the transliteration is often phonetically approximate in ways that create unintended meanings or awkward constructions.

The better approach for major market entries is localization: either adapting the name to work meaningfully in the target language or designing a parallel identity that can coexist with the primary identity.

## Visual Harmony Across Scripts

When a brand needs both a Roman script wordmark and a wordmark in another script, the challenge is making both feel like the same brand despite the fundamental differences in letter construction and visual rhythm between scripts.

Arabic script is continuous and right-to-left; Roman script is discrete and left-to-right. Chinese characters are visually complex and occupy a square spatial envelope; Roman letters occupy variable widths in a linear sequence.

Achieving harmony doesn't mean making them look identical — that's neither possible nor desirable. It means finding the visual properties that can be consistent: weight, spatial density, the balance between geometric and organic, the overall tone. The two wordmarks should feel like siblings from the same family, not strangers who share a color.

## Working With Script Specialists

I don't design Arabic, Chinese, or other non-Roman scripts without involving a specialist. A Latin designer approaching Arabic typography without deep knowledge of the script makes mistakes that are invisible to them and immediately obvious to native speakers.

The relationship I develop is collaborative: I define the character and requirements of the identity, the specialist brings mastery of the script's conventions and aesthetics. The result is better than either of us could produce alone.

## Technical Considerations

Multi-script identities have technical complexities beyond the visual design. Font licenses need to cover all scripts in use. Type files for each script need to be specified and provided. Color and spatial specifications need to be consistent across all script versions.

The brand guidelines need to address how the different script versions are used — which version is primary in which markets, how they appear together when both languages are needed in the same context, and how the hierarchy between them is established.
