Every poster I've ever been embarrassed by had the same problem: I skipped the grid and went straight to placing things. It looked fine in my head. It looked like chaos on paper.

The grid isn't a constraint. It's the difference between a poster that holds together at three meters and one that falls apart the moment someone squints at it.

## What a Grid Actually Does

A grid gives your eye somewhere to land. When you establish consistent columns, gutters, and margins before you place a single element, you're building invisible infrastructure that every piece of type and every image will lean against.

I work with modular grids for most posters — usually a 12-column base that I subdivide depending on the complexity of the content. A concert poster might only use three of those columns. An exhibition poster might use all twelve in overlapping ways. The grid doesn't dictate what you do; it prevents accidents.

## Baseline Grids for Typography

The one grid most designers skip is the baseline grid. This is the horizontal rhythm — typically set to match the leading of your body text — that keeps every line of type optically aligned across columns. When you nail it, the poster has a pulse. When you ignore it, something feels off even to non-designers.

I set my baseline grid before I set my typeface. Pick your leading first, build your grid to match, then choose type that fits. It sounds backwards, but it forces discipline.

## When to Break the Grid

Breaking the grid only works if the grid exists. I've seen students "break the grid" when they never had one — that's just random placement. A real grid break is deliberate: you've established the expectation of order, and you're violating it in one specific place to create tension or emphasis.

One element off-grid with everything else locked in: that's a statement. Every element off-grid: that's noise.

## My Workflow

1. Define the format and safe zones first (bleed, trim, margin)
2. Set the baseline grid based on intended body leading
3. Build the column structure (I default to 12, then simplify)
4. Place hierarchy anchors — headline, primary image, key info
5. Fill in secondary content, letting it breathe against the grid

The grid is the first thing I build and the last thing I abandon. When a poster isn't working, I go back to the grid before I touch the type or color. Nine times out of ten, the grid is where the problem lives.
