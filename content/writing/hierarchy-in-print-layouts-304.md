When I hand off a print file, I want whoever picks it up to know exactly where to look first, second, and third. Not because I've told them, but because the layout makes it obvious. That's hierarchy doing its job.

## Hierarchy Is an Argument

Every print layout is making a claim about what matters most. The largest element says "start here." The secondary element says "then here." The body copy says "and here's the rest." When those claims are clear and consistent, the reader moves through the piece with no friction.

When the claims conflict — when everything competes for attention — the reader stalls. I see this constantly in client-supplied drafts: three things set at the same scale, all bold, fighting for dominance. The result is a layout that reads as shouting and says nothing.

## The Three-Level Rule

I default to three levels of hierarchy in most print work. Primary: the one thing the piece exists to communicate. Secondary: supporting context. Tertiary: detail and fine print. Everything maps to one of these levels.

I enforce scale ratios between levels. Primary to secondary is usually a 2:1 or 3:1 ratio. Secondary to tertiary is a softer step — often weight or color shift rather than size. This creates a clear ladder without making the small stuff invisible.

## Weight Before Size

A common mistake is jumping straight to size when you want something to stand out. Size is blunt. Weight is precise. A headline set in a heavy weight at a moderate size often reads with more authority than the same words set enormous in a light weight.

I test hierarchy by squinting at the layout until it blurs. The blur test shows you pure visual weight without the distraction of readable content. If the reading order holds up blurry, it holds up at normal viewing distance.

## Color as a Hierarchy Tool

Color can lift a secondary element above a larger primary one if you're not careful. I treat color as a multiplier, not a base hierarchy signal. Warm colors advance; cool colors recede. A small red element will always fight with a large gray one for visual primacy.

I assign hierarchy levels before I assign color. Once the scale-and-weight structure is right, I use color sparingly to reinforce it — not to compensate for it.

## Negative Space Is an Active Element

Surrounding your primary element with space amplifies it. The eye moves toward isolated elements. Pack everything tight and nothing stands out. I often solve hierarchy problems not by making the primary element bigger but by clearing the space around it.

This is counterintuitive for clients who feel like white space is wasted space. My job is to show them that restraint is the mechanism, not the compromise.
