## The Typographic Poster

A typographic poster makes a bold proposition: that letterforms, set with enough conviction and craft, don't need imagery to communicate. The type is both the message and the medium.

Some of the most powerful posters in design history are purely typographic. Jan Tschichold. Armin Hofmann. Herb Lubalin. These designers understood that type, treated as form rather than just as carrier of language, has extraordinary expressive range.

## When to Go Typographic

A typographic poster is the right choice when the words themselves are the subject — a quote, a call to action, a manifesto, a title. When the language is strong enough that imagery would compete with it rather than support it. When the budget doesn't include illustration or photography. When you want the design to feel direct, immediate, and confident.

The wrong choice: when the message is complex and needs illustration to explain, when the event or product has strong visual identity that should be represented, when the audience needs to be introduced to something they've never seen.

## Type as Shape, Not Just Language

The shift that makes typographic posters work: treating letterforms as visual shapes rather than as vehicles for language. The composition is built from forms that happen to be readable, not from readable content that happens to be composed.

This means thinking about the weight, the silhouette, the negative space of each letter as purely visual properties. A condensed bold headline creates a strong vertical rectangle. A single large lowercase letter creates an asymmetric form with counters and extenders. A word in spaced uppercase creates a horizontal band with rhythmic dark-light alternation.

When I'm designing a typographic poster, I sketch the letterforms as abstract shapes first, without reading them, and evaluate the visual structure. Then I bring the meaning back in and see whether the visual structure serves the linguistic meaning.

## The Rule of Maximum Scale

For a typographic poster to have presence, the type needs to be large. Not "headline size" — architecturally large. Type that fills the format. Type that runs to the edges. Type that commands the entire surface.

The instinct to scale type to a comfortable reading size and surround it with white space is wrong for this format. The type should use the space.

## Hierarchy Without Imagery

Without a dominant image to create visual hierarchy, type must do all the hierarchical work. This means:

- Massive scale difference between primary and secondary text
- Deliberate use of weight (ultra bold vs. thin)
- Strategic tracking (expanded vs. compressed)
- Color contrast between text levels
- Spatial isolation of the primary element

When these tools are deployed together with intention, a purely typographic composition can have hierarchy as clear and emphatic as any image-based poster.

## The Baseline as Anchor

In typographic posters without imagery, the baseline — the invisible line that letters sit on — becomes a compositional anchor. Multiple baselines create horizontal structure that holds the poster together.

I often design typographic posters around two or three deliberate baselines that create compositional layers. The primary message lives on one baseline. Supporting text lives on another. The visual relationships between these baselines organize the space.

Let type do everything. It's capable of it.
