## Two Colors and a White Sheet

Some of my favorite print work has been produced with exactly two spot colors on uncoated white stock. The constraint is real — no gradients, no rich blacks, no photographic imagery unless you're willing to halftone it — and it produces work that feels purposeful in a way that full-color printing often doesn't.

Designing for spot color forces you to make decisions. Every element in the composition has to be one of two (or three, if you count the paper) values. Nothing is soft, nothing is blended. Everything is chosen.

## Understanding Spot Color Behavior

Spot colors in print behave differently from CMYK process colors, and understanding this is essential for designing with them.

A Pantone spot color is mixed as a specific ink formulation and applied as a single print pass. This means the color is consistent, saturated, and accurate across the entire print run regardless of paper variation. A CMYK simulation of the same Pantone will vary slightly across the run and will never quite match the spot color's saturation on uncoated stock.

For brand-critical color applications — a logo color, a distinctive brand color — I specify spot colors. For general print work where budget is the priority, CMYK is appropriate.

## Overprint and Knockout

With two spot colors, you have a third visual value available: the overprint of the two colors. When two inks are printed on top of each other, the result is a mixed color. Red overprinting blue produces a dark brownish-purple. Yellow overprinting blue produces a green.

This is a design tool. I plan overprint areas deliberately to get a third tone without a third ink. The key is specifying in the file which areas should overprint and which should knock out — overprint behavior defaults vary by software and printer, and assuming it'll work out is a recipe for surprise.

## Halftoning as an Aesthetic

Halftoning a photographic image or creating tonal gradations in spot color work requires halftone patterns — dots of varying size that create the illusion of tone. This is a technical constraint that becomes an aesthetic when embraced rather than avoided.

I sometimes deliberately use coarse halftone patterns as a design element. A 20-line halftone on a portrait turns it into graphic texture rather than photography. Combined with a strong spot color, this treatment produces work that looks uniquely printed rather than generically reproduced.

## Choosing the Right Pantone

When I'm choosing spot colors for a project, I look at the Pantone books on the specified stock — coated and uncoated behave completely differently. A vivid orange on coated stock becomes a muted dusty orange on uncoated. A deep navy on uncoated can feel more interesting than the same color coated.

The coated/uncoated distinction catches people every time. Look at the swatch in the actual book for the actual stock. Checking a hex simulation on screen is not sufficient.
