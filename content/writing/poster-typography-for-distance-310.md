A poster is not a page. People don't hold it twelve inches from their face and read it carefully. They encounter it at a distance — across a room, on a wall at the end of a hallway, on a street pole in passing. The typographic decisions I make for a poster have to account for that physical reality.

## The Viewing Distance Calculation

The first thing I do when scoping a poster project is establish the primary viewing distance. An A2 exhibition poster in a gallery will be read at roughly two to three feet. A street poster on a bus shelter will be scanned at four to six feet. A large-format exhibition banner might need to read at twenty feet.

Every distance multiplies the minimum readable type size. What reads comfortably at 10pt at arm's length needs to be roughly 30pt to read at the same level of comfort at six feet. I use this calculation to set my minimum type sizes before I design anything.

## What Survives at Distance

At distance, fine details vanish. Hairline strokes become invisible. Fine serif details merge into noise. The counters in dense text settings close up. What survives is: macro contrast, large shapes, and bold forms.

This is why posters that rely on delicate, fine type at body-text scale feel illegible from across a room. The detail the designer sweated over in InDesign at 300% zoom simply doesn't exist at the intended viewing distance.

## Hierarchy Gets Simpler at Distance

A poster seen from across a room communicates one thing — the dominant element. That's the thing that pulls the viewer in close enough to read the secondary information. The hierarchy for a poster is: primary message at reading distance, everything else at arm's length.

I design poster hierarchy in two stages: what does this communicate at fifteen feet? And then: what does it communicate when someone steps up to read it? The answers should be consistent — the closer read should complete the story the distance read started.

## Testing by Stepping Back

I test every poster design by stepping back from my screen or, better, by printing a smaller version at accurate scale and pinning it at the other end of the room. What holds up? What disappears? What competes when it shouldn't?

This is not a comfortable test — it usually reveals that things I thought were working are lost at distance. But it's the only accurate test. The poster has to work in the room where it will live, not on a 27-inch display at arm's length.

## Letter Spacing at Display Size

Open tracking helps legibility at distance because it prevents letters from visually merging. All-caps headlines especially benefit from generous tracking at poster scale. I add tracking progressively as size goes up — larger text gets more open spacing, which might seem counterintuitive but matches how letterforms read at physical distances.
