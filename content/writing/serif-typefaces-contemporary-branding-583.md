## The Sans-Serif Default

For roughly fifteen years, the dominant aesthetic in startup and tech branding was geometric sans-serif. Clean. Neutral. Forward-looking. The thinking was that sans-serifs felt modern and serifs felt old.

The irony is that everyone reached for the same conclusion simultaneously, producing a category of brands that are indistinguishable from each other. When every fintech, every health app, and every B2B software company looks like a slight variation on the same sans-serif template, the choice that was supposed to signal "modern" now signals "generic."

Serifs are back — not as nostalgia, but as differentiation.

## What Serifs Actually Communicate

The association between serifs and "old-fashioned" was always overstated. What serifs actually communicate, when chosen well:

**Authority and credibility** — serifs have been the typefaces of publishing, law, and finance for centuries. Those associations are earned and real. For brands that need to communicate expertise and trustworthiness, serifs often do this better than sans-serifs.

**Care and craft** — serifs are more structurally complex than sans-serifs. They communicate that attention was paid, that someone designed this deliberately. In a landscape of template-built brands, that communicates something.

**Character** — the best contemporary serifs have strong personalities. They're not generic in the way that "clean sans-serif" has become. A distinctive serif is more memorable than a distinctive sans in many market contexts.

## The Contemporary Serif Landscape

It would be wrong to use serif = old. The typeface design industry has produced extraordinary serif work in the last decade — typefaces designed specifically for contemporary use, with high optical resolution, variable font axes, and design sensibilities that are completely of the moment.

These typefaces don't look like Times New Roman. They look like now, with historical depth. That combination is powerful for brands that want to feel rooted and contemporary simultaneously.

## When Serifs Are the Wrong Choice

Serifs at small sizes on screen can suffer from rendering issues, particularly at body text sizes on standard-resolution displays. Variable font serif options have improved this significantly, but it's still a real consideration for digital-first brands.

Serifs also carry associations that are genuinely wrong for some brands: a playful consumer product brand that needs to feel fun and light might find that any serif, however contemporary, adds unwanted gravity.

The question isn't "is serif modern or traditional?" — it's "does this typeface communicate what this brand needs to communicate?"

## A Recommendation

If your category is dominated by sans-serifs and your brand has genuine depth — expertise, heritage, craft, seriousness of purpose — consider a contemporary serif. Not to look retro. To look like a brand that has something real to stand behind.

That substance, expressed through a well-chosen serif, can be one of the most effective differentiators in a cluttered category.

Type choice is market positioning. Make it deliberately.
