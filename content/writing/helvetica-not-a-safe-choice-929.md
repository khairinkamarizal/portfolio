## The Default That Means Nothing

Helvetica has been called the most perfect typeface ever designed. It's also been used so extensively, across so many brands, institutions, and contexts, that it communicates almost nothing specific anymore. When you set a brand in Helvetica, you're not saying anything about who they are. You're saying: we couldn't decide.

I use Helvetica deliberately, rarely, and only when its specific visual qualities — its neutrality, its modernist precision, its institutional weight — are exactly what the brand needs. That's maybe five percent of projects. The other ninety-five percent need something with more character.

## Why Designers Default to It

Helvetica is genuinely excellent in many respects. It has a comprehensive weight range, excellent rendering at most sizes, and a visual neutrality that offends almost nobody. For a designer who is uncertain about a client's preferences, Helvetica is a safe bet.

That's exactly the problem. Safe is another word for forgettable.

Brand design is not about offending nobody. It's about connecting with someone — a specific audience with specific values and expectations. A typeface that offends nobody also moves nobody. It's the design equivalent of beige.

## What Helvetica Actually Communicates

When Helvetica does communicate something, here's what it says: Swiss modernism. Mid-century corporate rationalism. Institutional authority with Swiss German efficiency. That's a genuinely powerful communication — but it's the right communication for a very specific kind of brand.

It's right for New York City transit signage, which needs to communicate exactly that kind of rational authority. It's right for a certain kind of corporate brand that genuinely embodies those Swiss modernist values. It's wrong for a craft bakery, a creative studio, a sustainability brand, an artisan manufacturer, or almost anything that wants to feel warm, distinctive, or connected to a specific cultural moment.

## What I Reach for Instead

The typeface selection process should be driven by the brand's specific character, not by category defaults. Some of my more reliable alternatives depending on context:

For brands that need warmth and humanist quality without being decorative: the humanist sans-serifs — Gill Sans, Frutiger, Myriad, or the newer generation like Aktiv Grotesk or Forma DJR.

For brands that want the structural clarity of a neo-grotesque but with more personality: the current generation of neo-grotesks — Neue Haas Grotesk (which is Helvetica's actual DNA), Suisse Int'l, or Graphik.

For brands where typographic authority and tradition matter: a well-chosen serif like Canela, Freight, or Lyon.

For brands that want to signal cultural currency and design awareness: the right independent type foundry face for their moment, chosen with the brand's specific visual territory in mind.

## The Lazy Neutrality Trap

There's a broader version of this problem beyond Helvetica. It's the tendency to reach for visual neutrality as a default because neutrality feels safe and safe feels professional. It's not. Safe neutrality is a missed opportunity. Every brand communication is a chance to reinforce a distinctive identity. Defaulting to neutrality is choosing not to take that chance.

The best brand typography is specific, deliberate, and character-full. It says something true about who the brand is. Helvetica can say that — but only if it's actually true about this brand, which it usually isn't.
