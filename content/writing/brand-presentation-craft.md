Great work gets rejected in bad presentations. I've seen it happen, and I've done it myself. The design was right, but the presentation didn't build the context for the client to understand why it was right. They reacted to surface qualities without understanding the decisions behind them.

A brand presentation is a communication design problem. The audience is the client, the medium is the meeting, and the goal is shared understanding.

## Start With the Brief, Not the Work

Every presentation I give starts with a recap of the brief. Not as a formality — as a setup. I'm reminding the client what we agreed we were trying to achieve before showing them what I achieved.

This matters because clients come into presentations with expectations they may not have shared. Starting with the brief realigns everyone on the actual criteria for evaluation before the work is revealed.

## The Narrative Before the Reveal

I build a short narrative before showing the identity. What direction did I explore? Why? What is the central idea behind this approach? How does it connect to the brand strategy?

This narrative creates context for the visual. The client isn't just seeing a logo — they're seeing a solution to a problem they helped define. The narrative makes that connection explicit.

## Show One Direction Well

I show one direction per presentation, not three. Three directions signals that I don't know what's right, which undermines my authority as the designer. It also invites the client to mix elements from different directions, which produces incoherent hybrids.

If I have a genuinely strong alternate direction worth exploring, I show it as a secondary option — "here's the primary recommendation, here's an alternative worth discussing" — not as three equally weighted options.

## Mockups Do Real Work

Showing a logo on a white background at the end of a PDF is the least effective way to present it. Mockups — the logo on a business card, on a storefront, on packaging, on a website — show the identity in context and help clients visualize it as a real thing in the world.

Good mockups answer the question "what will this actually look like?" before it's asked.

## Handling Pushback in the Room

When clients react negatively to work in a presentation, my first response is always a question: "What specifically isn't working for you?" I want to understand the nature of the concern before responding to it.

Sometimes the pushback is about personal taste and isn't strategically relevant. Sometimes it reveals a genuine misalignment between my interpretation of the brief and the client's intent. These require completely different responses.

I don't defend work in presentations. I ask questions, listen, and then respond with clarity about what I'm willing to adjust and why. Defending design decisions with "that's what works best" is less effective than explaining "here's what this decision is doing for the brand."
