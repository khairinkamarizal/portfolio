## Print's role has changed, not ended

For twenty years, the design industry has been announcing the death of print. Every year, print's share of the media mix shrinks relative to digital. Circulations decline, print budgets get cut, clients ask "do we really need to print this?"

Print isn't dying. Its role is changing. When print was the primary communication medium, it had to do everything — inform, persuade, build awareness, drive action. Now that digital has taken most of those jobs, what's left for print is different and, in some ways, more powerful.

## Print as a deliberate choice

When a brand chooses print in 2022, it's making a deliberate statement. Print is slower, more expensive, and less trackable than digital. Choosing it signals that the communication is important enough to warrant that investment. The recipient understands this, even subconsciously.

A physical piece that arrives in the mail in an era of digital communication gets attention that an email never will. It gets picked up, held, examined. It stays on the desk. It gets passed to someone else. These behaviors are increasingly rare and therefore increasingly valuable.

## The premium positioning of physical objects

As digital has become the default, physical objects have acquired premium positioning by default. A beautifully printed brand identity system communicates investment and quality in a way that digital-only brands can't replicate. Luxury brands understand this intuitively — the shopping bag, the box, the tissue, the card are all designed with physical quality that communicates brand values through touch and material.

This premium positioning opportunity is available to non-luxury brands too. A small business that produces beautiful, thoughtfully designed printed materials communicates a seriousness and quality that positions it above competitors who went digital-only.

## Designing for a different relationship with time

Digital design is designed for immediate consumption and rapid turnover. A social media post has a half-life of hours. An email newsletter might be read once and deleted. Print design lives longer.

A poster might be on a wall for months. A book might be kept for decades. A well-designed brochure gets kept in a drawer and referenced months after it was received. This different relationship with time changes what good print design needs to do — it needs to reward extended looking and repeated reference, not just immediate scanning.

## The integration imperative

The most effective contemporary use of print integrates it with digital rather than treating the two as separate channels. A print piece that drives to a digital destination, supplemented by digital content that couldn't fit in print. A book that references a companion website. A brand identity system that's designed consistently across both physical and digital touchpoints.

Designing for this integration — where print and digital are complementary parts of a coherent brand experience — is the most interesting print design challenge of the current moment.
