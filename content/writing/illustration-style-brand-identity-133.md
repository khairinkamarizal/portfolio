## Beyond Photography and Icons

Most brand identities default to photography and icon sets. Photography is versatile and feels real. Icons are functional and scalable. Both are legitimate choices. But illustration offers something neither of them can: a wholly proprietary visual language that is unique to the brand and impossible to replicate.

When a brand's illustration style is strong enough, you can identify it before reading the name.

## Style as Signature

An illustration style that's been developed thoughtfully becomes a brand signature. The line weight, the color application, the character of the forms, the level of detail — all of these contribute to a visual voice that's as distinctive as the logo itself.

Mailchimp's quirky, hand-drawn characters. Dropbox's flat, conceptual illustrations. These styles are deeply embedded in how those brands are experienced. Remove them and the brand feels generic.

## The Brief for an Illustration System

Briefing an illustration system is harder than briefing a logo. The variables are more complex. I specify: what subjects or concepts will the illustrations depict, what level of abstraction is appropriate, what's the relationship between figure and background, how does color work within illustrations versus the broader brand palette, what line weight is standard, what's the minimum and maximum level of detail.

These constraints are what make a system reproducible by different illustrators without losing coherence.

## Custom vs. Licensed vs. Stock

Custom illustration is expensive and time-intensive. For brands with the resources, it's absolutely the right choice. For brands with tighter constraints, a licensed illustration library or a carefully curated stock illustration approach can work — as long as the selections are made with a consistent aesthetic eye.

The problem with stock illustration is that it's often used inconsistently: different illustrators, different styles, different color palettes assembled without a unifying sensibility. Used with discipline, stock can work. Used carelessly, it creates visual noise.

## Integration with the Wider System

Illustration doesn't exist in isolation. It needs to integrate with the type system, the color palette, and the photographic style (if any). The best identities feel like all their elements come from the same world. Illustration that looks visually unrelated to the other brand elements breaks that coherence.

I think about integration from the beginning. The illustration palette should draw from the brand color system. The line weight and form language should echo the type choices.

## When Illustration Is Wrong

Not every brand should use illustration. Some brands communicate best through photography, data visualization, or pure typography. Illustration suggests a certain character — playful, human, creative, unconventional — that doesn't fit every brand's positioning.

Match the tool to the communication need. Don't use illustration because it looks interesting. Use it because it's right for the brand.
