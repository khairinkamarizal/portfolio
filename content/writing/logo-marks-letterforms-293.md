The letterform-derived logo mark sits at an interesting intersection of wordmark and abstract symbol. It uses the geometry of one or more letters from the brand name as the basis for an abstract or semi-abstract mark — creating a symbol that has a logical connection to the name without simply being a letter in a circle.

Done well, this approach produces marks with built-in narrative (the mark is derived from the name) and strong visual logic (the form has a clear geometric origin). Done poorly, it produces marks that look like random geometric shapes with a tenuous letter connection invented after the fact.

## The Derivation Must Be Real

The most important principle: the derivation from the letterform must be genuine, not rationalized. The mark should clearly originate from the letterform's geometry — not just share one curve with it.

When I show a letterform-derived mark in presentation, I can trace the exact geometric origin. I show the letterform, the extraction or transformation I applied, and the resulting mark. The viewer should be able to follow the derivation and say "I see how that came from the letter." If they can't, the mark isn't truly letterform-derived — it's a shape I'm associating with a letter after the fact.

## Extraction Techniques

Several approaches produce genuinely derived marks:

Counter extraction: using the negative space (counters) inside a letterform as the primary positive mark. The enclosed space inside a 'B', 'D', or 'O' can become a distinctive shape when extracted and treated as a positive form.

Geometric reduction: breaking a letterform down to its underlying geometric skeleton — the circles, angles, and strokes that construct it — and finding a new composition within those geometric elements.

Character fragment: taking a distinctive portion of a letterform (a specific terminal, a curve section, a distinctive serif) and developing it into a complete mark. The fragment carries the letter's DNA without being legible as a letter.

Rotation and transformation: taking the letterform and applying a transformation (rotation, reflection, interlocking repetition) that creates a new composite form while the original letter is still discoverable within it.

## When the Letter Is Too Generic

Some letters are so geometrically simple — C, I, L, O — that marks derived from them tend to be too generic to own. A circle derived from 'O' isn't distinctive. A vertical stroke derived from 'I' communicates nothing.

For brands with names beginning in geometrically simple letters, I explore derivation from more distinctive characters elsewhere in the name, or use a combination of multiple letter geometries, rather than forcing a derivation from the initial that can't produce a distinctive mark.

## The Standalone Test

A letterform-derived mark needs to pass the standalone test: removed from any context that reveals its name connection, does the mark have sufficient visual distinctiveness and strength to function as a brand symbol?

If the mark only makes sense when paired with the wordmark — if it looks like a random shape without the name to explain it — then the derivation hasn't produced a genuinely strong symbol. The mark should be able to stand alone in contexts where the name isn't present, carrying the brand's recognition on its own.

The derivation is the origin story. The mark needs to be strong enough to tell its own story after that.
