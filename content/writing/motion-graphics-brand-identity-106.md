## When a Brand Starts to Move

For most of design history, brand identity lived in two dimensions. A mark, a color, a typeface — all static, all frozen. Then screens became the dominant medium, and suddenly brands had to exist in time as well as space.

Motion graphics changed brand design permanently. Now when I work on a visual identity, I think about how it moves from the beginning, not as an afterthought.

## What Motion Adds

Movement communicates things that static design cannot. It carries energy, rhythm, personality, and sequence. A logo animation is not just decoration — it can reinforce what a brand stands for.

A logo that assembles itself from fragments suggests construction, complexity, building something. A logo that flows and morphs suggests adaptability, creativity, change. A logo that snaps into place with mechanical precision suggests reliability, precision, strength.

These are not accidental readings. They're design decisions.

## Starting from the Static Identity

When I'm creating motion work for a brand, I start deep in the static identity. The logic of how a mark is constructed — the shapes that make it up, the relationships between elements, the color system — contains motion possibilities if you look for them.

The best logo animations feel like they reveal how the mark is made. They show you the geometry. They make the static version feel richer because now you understand its structure.

## Timing is Design

In motion graphics, timing is as important as form. The speed of an animation, the easing of a movement, the pauses between beats — these carry as much meaning as the shapes themselves. A fast, snappy animation reads as energetic and contemporary. A slow, deliberate movement reads as considered and premium.

I spend as much time on timing as I do on any visual element. Frame-by-frame work, adjusting curves in the graph editor, testing different eases — this is where motion design lives or dies.

## Consistency Across the System

One challenge in motion identity work is maintaining consistency when different team members animate different assets. A logo reveal, a lower-third, a transition, a social media post — if each animator makes independent timing and easing decisions, the brand loses coherence.

I build motion guidelines just like I build visual guidelines: documenting the easing curves, the timing values, the principles that should govern all motion in the system.

## The Practical Reality

Not every brand needs a full motion identity. But every brand that exists on video — which is most of them now — needs at minimum a resolved logo animation and some sense of how motion fits into the identity.

Getting that right elevates the whole brand. Getting it wrong is painfully visible.
