## The Most Common Branding Conflict

In founder-led businesses, the most common branding conflict is between the founder's personal aesthetic preferences and what the brand's audience and positioning actually require. The founder built the business from nothing; their instincts and tastes feel inseparable from the brand's identity. When a designer proposes something that doesn't match those instincts, the conflict can feel personal even when it isn't.

Navigating this well is one of the more delicate skills in brand identity practice.

## The Legitimate Role of Founder Taste

Founder taste is not irrelevant. For many young businesses, the founder's aesthetic sensibility is a genuine brand asset — it's part of what makes the brand feel human, distinctive, and consistent. Founder-led brands often have a coherence that committee-designed brands lack, precisely because one person's taste is running through all the decisions.

I never dismiss a founder's aesthetic preferences. I try to understand where they come from, what they're expressing, and whether they're pointing toward something true about the brand.

## When Taste and Brand Diverge

The problem arises when a founder's taste is pointing away from where the brand needs to go. A founder who built a premium service business might personally love bright, playful colors that communicate accessibility — which is at odds with the premium positioning the business needs to project to attract its target clients.

In these cases, I separate two questions that often get conflated:

**What is your personal aesthetic?** Legitimate and interesting, but not determinative for the brand.

**What does your target client need to see to trust this brand and choose you over competitors?** This is the design brief.

When these two questions have different answers, the second one wins. That's not disrespecting the founder's taste; it's doing the job.

## How I Navigate the Conversation

I've learned to make this separation explicit early in the project, before any design work exists to argue about. In discovery, I ask directly: "We're going to design for what your clients need to see, not necessarily for what you personally like. Are you comfortable with that distinction?"

Most founders who've thought seriously about their business say yes. The ones who aren't comfortable with that distinction are telling me something important about how the project will go.

## When the Founder's Taste Is Right

Sometimes the founder's intuition is actually pointing at the right answer and they just need someone to trust it rather than question it. Founders of companies with strong brand identities often have genuine design intelligence about their own brand — they've been living with it and building it for years.

My job is to evaluate both the strategic brief and the founder's instincts honestly, and to tell the truth about which is right. Sometimes the instinct is right. Sometimes the strategy is right. Occasionally they're the same, and that's the best outcome.
