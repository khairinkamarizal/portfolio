## The Problem with Trendy Color

Every year the design press announces the colors of the moment. Every year a wave of brands launches with those colors. Three years later those brands look dated in exactly the same way — they all got old together because they all arrived together.

I'm not opposed to using a color that's currently popular if it genuinely fits the brand's strategy. But I make the decision for strategic reasons, not because it's trending. And I tell clients honestly that colors with strong current cultural associations will carry those associations — including the temporal ones — for the life of the brand.

## The Longevity Framework

When I'm evaluating a potential brand color for longevity, I ask:

**Does this color have a specific cultural moment attached to it?** Some colors get indelibly associated with a particular era. Choosing them is a deliberate choice to be of that era — or a mistake if you're not thinking about it.

**How is this color used by competitors and adjacent categories?** A color that's ubiquitous in your category right now may still be ubiquitous in ten years. That's not longevity — that's being generic.

**Does this color require a specific level of technical precision to reproduce consistently?** Very vivid, highly saturated colors are often outside the gamut of standard CMYK printing, which means they'll vary across media. A color that reproduces consistently across all applications is more durable in practice.

**What associations does this color carry beyond the design conversation?** Red means stop, danger, passion — depending on context. These deep cultural associations are more stable than trend-driven ones.

## Specific vs. Generic Color

The most lasting brand colors are specific — not just "green" but a precise, unusual shade of green that belongs to one brand. Think of Tiffany's particular robin's egg blue, or Hermès orange. These colors are ownable because they're specific and because they've been used with discipline.

Generic versions of primary and secondary colors — the blue that means "corporate," the green that means "natural" — are available to anyone and own nothing.

## Saturation and Brightness Considerations

High-saturation, high-brightness colors look great in digital but age faster. There's something about ultra-vivid colors that marks a specific technological and cultural moment — they look current now and will look of-the-moment later.

Colors with slightly muted saturation and considered brightness levels tend to age more gracefully. They read as selected rather than rendered, which gives them a more permanent quality.

## The Test: Does It Look Like a Decision?

I ask this about every color palette before presenting it: does this look like a considered decision or like the output of a tool? A palette that looks like it came from a palette generator — even if it's technically well-constructed — communicates generic. A palette that looks like it was arrived at through specific choices about the specific brand communicates intention.

Intention ages better than trend.
