## Monograms are harder than they look

A monogram — a mark built from one, two, or three initials — seems like the simplest possible logo brief. Take the letters, make them look nice together. Done.

It's not done. The letterform mark is one of the hardest logo problems because the solution is visible in the brief. Everyone knows what the letters are. There's no conceptual surprise to hide behind. The quality of the work lives entirely in the quality of the letterform construction and the sophistication of the formal relationships between the letters.

## Starting with the letterform geometry

Before I combine anything, I study each letterform individually. What are its angles? What are its curves? Where does it have open space that another letterform could occupy? Where does it have structural weight that will anchor the mark?

The letters that naturally combine well share geometric properties. An H and an A share diagonal strokes that can be unified. An O and a C are both circular and can overlap cleanly. A B and a P share a vertical stem. I'm looking for these shared properties first — they're the natural connection points.

## The overlap decision

The central compositional decision in a monogram is overlap: do the letters share space, sit adjacent to each other, or interlock? Each approach produces a very different reading.

Adjacent letters — simply placed next to each other with careful spacing — produce a monogram that reads clearly but has less visual unity. It's two letters next to each other, not a single form.

Overlapping letters, where one occupies the negative space of the other, creates visual complexity and requires more careful construction. The overlap needs to be resolved — the viewer needs to be able to read the individual letters even where they share space.

Interlocking letters, where strokes literally share paths and forms interpenetrate, creates the most unified single shape but the most demanding construction problem.

## Weight and proportion decisions

Monogram marks almost always work better with consistent stroke weights across all letterforms. Mixing a heavy letter with a light one creates visual imbalance that's hard to resolve. If the letters in the name have very different inherent weights at a given size, I modify one to bring them into harmony.

Proportion is the other major decision. Should all letters be the same height? Should one be dominant? For initials-only monograms (JF, RBK), I usually keep all letters at equal height. For marks where one initial is more primary — a company named after a founder's last name, for instance — a dominant first letter with smaller secondaries can work well.

## When to abandon the letterform approach

Sometimes the letters simply don't want to become a mark. They don't share geometric properties, they don't overlap well, and forced combinations look labored. In those cases, I don't force it. I move to a different mark type — a symbol, an abstract form, a wordmark — rather than produce a monogram that doesn't work. The brief is for a logo that serves the brand, not for a monogram at all costs.
