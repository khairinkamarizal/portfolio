A wordmark is simultaneously the simplest and most demanding type of logo. There's no graphic symbol to carry meaning, no abstract mark to create intrigue. It's just the name, typeset with enough intentionality and craft that it becomes distinctive.

Done well, a wordmark is one of the most direct expressions of a brand's personality. Done poorly, it's a company name in a slightly customized font.

## Why Wordmarks Work

Not every brand needs a logomark. In fact, many brands are better served by a well-crafted wordmark than by a symbol that nobody can remember or connect to the company name without seeing them together.

A wordmark is the brand name literally made visible. The letterforms carry the personality. The custom typeface — or the thoughtfully modified existing typeface — is the mark. When someone can recognize your wordmark at a glance without reading every letter, you've succeeded.

## Starting With an Existing Typeface

I almost always start wordmark development with an existing typeface as a base, then modify it. Starting from scratch is usually unnecessary and often results in letterforms that look like existing typefaces anyway.

The starting typeface should already be close to the right character for the brand — the right weight, the right personality, the right technical properties. From there, I look at what modifications will make it distinctive and specifically suited to this name and this brand.

## Customization Strategies

The modifications that make a wordmark distinctive fall into a few categories:

**Letter spacing:** Custom tracking, particularly in display wordmarks, makes the name feel considered. Very open tracking suggests luxury or restraint. Tight tracking suggests confidence or modernity.

**Custom letterforms:** Modifying one or two distinctive characters — a unique lowercase 'a', a distinctive 'g', a customized terminal on the 's' — creates distinctiveness while maintaining the overall character of the typeface.

**Ligatures:** Custom ligatures between adjacent letters can create visual cohesion and make a wordmark feel designed rather than typeset.

**Connecting strokes:** Some wordmarks connect letters with custom strokes or modify baseline points to create a continuous, unified form.

**Proportion adjustments:** Expanding or compressing specific letters to improve the visual balance of the name — especially for names with awkward combinations of wide and narrow letters.

## The Legibility Non-Negotiable

Every customization I make to a wordmark gets evaluated against a single criterion: does it compromise legibility? A wordmark that requires explanation has failed. The name must be readable, even if the letterforms are unusual.

This is the constraint that separates wordmark design from calligraphy or lettering. A wordmark is a functional identifier first. Its aesthetic distinction has to exist within the bounds of readability.

## Testing the Wordmark

I test wordmarks in three conditions: at minimum viable size (usually around 50px wide for digital), at display scale, and at the specific applications the brand will use most. I also test it in black only, reversed white on dark, and in the primary brand color — because a wordmark that only works in one color condition isn't a complete solution.
