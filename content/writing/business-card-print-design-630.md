I still carry business cards. This sometimes surprises people who assume physical cards are obsolete. They're not. A business card that's beautifully designed and well-printed is a physical artifact of a brand — something the recipient keeps, notices, feels. No app replicates that.

The business card is also a design challenge that concentrates almost every principle of good print and brand design into a 85×55mm rectangle. Getting it right is harder than it looks.

## The Constraints Are the Brief

The business card brief is essentially: communicate identity and contact information at the highest quality in the smallest possible space. Those constraints drive every decision.

The identity has to be clear and immediate. The contact information has to be complete and legible. The print quality has to be excellent enough to communicate care and attention. None of these can be compromised for the others.

## The Front/Back Decision

Most business cards use both sides. The question is how to divide the work between them.

My default approach: the front carries the identity — logo, possibly a brand element, name and title. The back carries the contact information. This separation keeps the front clean and the back functional, and gives each side a clear job.

An alternative I use for clients with strong brand personalities: the front is a bold, almost image-like brand expression — a large pattern, a big typographic statement, a strong color — and the contact information is on the back in a smaller, functional treatment. This approach leads to a card that gets kept because it's beautiful, and the information is found on the back when needed.

## Typography at Business Card Scale

Business card typography is miniature precision work. The smallest text on the card — typically contact details — is usually set at 7-8pt equivalent. At this scale, typeface choice matters enormously.

Faces with high x-heights and generous apertures — the openings in letters like c, e, and a — are more legible at small sizes. Overly thin weights disappear. Complex letterforms become unreadable. I typically use a clean humanist sans for contact information on cards — something like Graphik or Aktiv Grotesk at a regular weight.

Leading at small sizes needs to be generous relative to type size. Tight leading at 7pt is a recipe for illegible stacked lines.

## Paper and Finish Decisions

The paper stock on a business card communicates immediately and powerfully. A thick, uncoated stock feels substantial and considered. A thin, gloss-coated card feels generic. The weight and coating are not neutral choices.

My defaults, depending on brand personality:

- **Premium, professional:** 400gsm duplex board with soft-touch lamination, no gloss
- **Creative, editorial:** 350gsm uncoated natural, possibly with a small letterpress element
- **Bold, brand-forward:** 350gsm with spot UV on the logo element on the front
- **Minimal, confident:** Single color on thick uncoated stock, clean and spare

## The One Thing People Remember

Every person who receives a business card handles it for a moment and then puts it somewhere. That moment of handling is the design's only chance. The tactile quality — the weight, the texture, the finish — is what registers in that moment. The information gets looked up later.

Design the card to win the moment of handling. Everything else is secondary.
