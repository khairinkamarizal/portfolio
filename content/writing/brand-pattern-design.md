A logo can't cover a tote bag. It can't wrap a product box without looking like a wallpaper of the same mark repeated. It doesn't fill the background of a poster in a way that adds richness and depth. This is where brand patterns come in.

A graphic pattern is a secondary visual language for the brand — one that's clearly related to the primary identity but extends it into territory the logo can't occupy alone.

## Patterns Derived from Identity Elements

The most coherent brand patterns are derived directly from the identity system. They might use:

- Geometric shapes drawn from the logo mark's construction
- The brand's typeface rendered as large graphic elements
- Forms derived from the brand's subject matter or industry
- Abstract representations of the brand's core idea or values

When a pattern is derived from identity elements, it reinforces the visual system and creates coherence across brand touchpoints. When a pattern is selected independently of the identity (a generic geometric pattern applied to a brand because it "looks nice"), it competes with the identity rather than supporting it.

## Scale Variation and Context

A good brand pattern works at multiple scales — from tight small-scale repeat to large, bold elements that function more as graphic devices than traditional patterns. I design patterns as a scale system, not as a single repetition unit.

At small scale, the pattern reads as texture. At large scale, it reads as graphic composition. The brand can use either register depending on context: texture for envelopes and bags, bold graphic for poster backgrounds and key art.

## Color Flexibility

Brand patterns need to work in multiple color combinations, not just one. I design patterns with specific color versions:

- Full color (using the complete brand palette)
- Two-color (primary brand color on neutral background)
- Single color (logo color on white or reversed on dark background)
- Monochromatic (a single color family at different values)

Each version should feel intentional and work in specific application contexts. The full-color version is richest; the single-color version is most flexible for production.

## Pattern as Texture, Not Wallpaper

The worst use of brand patterns is as plain wallpaper — the same element repeated mechanically across a surface with equal visual weight and no hierarchy. The result is visual noise.

Good pattern design creates visual rhythm through scale variation, directional tension, and density variation. The eye should find areas to rest and areas of more complex detail — a visual landscape, not a flat field.

## Application Testing

Patterns need to be tested in context before they're finalized. A pattern that looks great as a flat swatch can look overwhelming on a product box, claustrophobic on a banner, or too subtle on a page background.

I create mockups of the primary pattern applications before finalizing the design. The pattern's scale and density almost always need adjustment when moved from a swatch into a real application context.
