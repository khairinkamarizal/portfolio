A great title sequence is a piece of cinema before the cinema begins. It establishes tone, introduces themes, and creates the emotional context that the work that follows will inhabit. The best title sequences — Saul Bass's Vertigo, Kyle Cooper's Se7en, the HBO static — are studied as much as the films they precede.

## The Function of a Title Sequence

A title sequence does several things simultaneously: it presents the title and key credits, it transitions the audience from the outside world into the world of the film or show, and it communicates the emotional register and thematic concerns of the work.

The transition function is the one that most distinguishes title sequences from other motion design. The viewer is arriving from their daily life. The title sequence is the airlock — the chamber that changes the pressure before they enter the world being created.

## Abstraction and Implication

The most successful title sequences work through abstraction and implication rather than literal representation. They suggest the themes and emotional landscape of the work without showing it directly.

Saul Bass's design for Vertigo uses spirals and falling — shapes that embody the psychological experience of the film without depicting any specific scene. The sequence is not preview; it's induction.

I approach title sequence briefs by asking: what is the emotional world of this work, and what visual forms embody that world rather than illustrate it?

## Typographic Presence

Credits are the functional mandate of a title sequence. The typographic treatment of these credits is the sequence's voice. Heavy, bold type announces authority. Thin, delicate type suggests fragility or intimacy. Type that appears and dissolves without strong anchor suggests instability.

The credits in a title sequence aren't just information to be displayed — they're typographic events in an ongoing motion composition. Each credit arrival and departure is designed as part of the whole.

## Synchronization to Music

Title sequences are almost always built to a specific piece of music or sound design. The synchronization between motion and sound is the axis that everything else rotates around.

I edit the visual rhythm to the music first — establishing cut points, motion emphasis, and energy changes in relation to the track — before designing the detailed motion. The music is the director; I follow its lead and look for moments of alignment that create emotional intensity.

## The Opening Frame

The first frame of a title sequence is seen before the viewer knows what they're watching. It needs to immediately suggest that something deliberate and considered is happening — not a technical countdown or an unrelated holding card, but the beginning of an intentional experience.

I spend disproportionate time on the opening five seconds of any title sequence. That's where the audience decides whether to engage or disengage.
