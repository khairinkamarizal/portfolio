## Letters as Architecture

A monogram takes two or more letters and unifies them into a single graphic mark. At its best, the result reads simultaneously as the letters and as an abstract form — a mark that operates on two levels at once. At its worst, it's just letters crammed together in a way that reads as neither.

I've designed monograms for law firms, fashion brands, luxury goods, hospitality groups, and personal brands. The problem is always the same: how do I make these specific letters feel inevitable together?

## Starting with the Letters

Before I think about how the letters will combine, I spend time with each letter individually. What is its structure? What strokes extend past the other forms? Where are the natural intersections? What geometric relationships exist between the two letterforms?

I draw each letter at the same scale, in the same weight, and overlay them in multiple configurations: side by side, overlapping, nested, interlocked. I'm looking for the configuration where the two letters find a natural harmony — where the strokes flow into each other rather than awkwardly colliding.

## The Three Main Approaches

**Stacked or side-by-side** — the simplest monogram. Letters arranged in a clear spatial relationship without interlock. The work is in the proportions, spacing, and any shared visual element (a rule, a containing shape) that unifies them. This is often underestimated — a well-proportioned two-letter mark with a strong enclosing form can be extremely elegant.

**Interlocked** — letters share strokes or overlap in ways that create a genuinely unified form. The challenge is finding the interlocking that reads as intentional, not accidental. The intersection points must be clean, and the visual weight must balance across the combined form.

**Ligature-based** — letters connect directly, sharing strokes where they meet. This works best when the letters' forms naturally suggest connection points. Forcing letters to share strokes that aren't geometrically compatible produces awkward breaks and unclear reading.

## Weight and Proportion

Monograms are particularly sensitive to stroke weight. The same letterforms at different weights create completely different dynamics:

Thin monograms feel precious, refined, luxury-adjacent.
Medium weight monograms feel balanced and versatile.
Bold monograms feel confident and contemporary.

The weight should match the brand's personality — and it needs to work at the sizes the monogram will actually be used.

## The Readability Question

A monogram that's too clever to read defeats its purpose. The individual letters must remain identifiable even when interlocked. I test this by asking people who don't know the brand to read the monogram and tell me what letters they see.

If they can't read it, the combination is too complex. I simplify until the letters are legible without destroying the unified form. The sweet spot is a mark that reads as both the combined form and the individual letters — where the resolution of the puzzle feels satisfying rather than frustrating.

## When a Monogram Is the Right Direction

Monograms work best when:

- The brand name is long and a wordmark would be unwieldy
- The initials are inherently distinctive or form an interesting combination
- The brand has a personal or artisanal quality where a human touch is appropriate
- The brand needs a mark that can work in fine applications: embossing, engraving, embroidery

Monograms signal heritage and craft. They're the right tool when those are signals the brand needs to send.
