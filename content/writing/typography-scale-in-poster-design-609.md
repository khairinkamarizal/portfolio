The single most effective thing you can do to a mediocre poster layout is increase the scale of the primary typographic element. Not tweak the font. Not change the color. Make the headline bigger. Make it much bigger. The discomfort you feel when it seems "too big" is usually the moment it starts working.

## Why Scale Works

Scale creates hierarchy instantly. When one element is dramatically larger than everything else, the eye goes there first without any ambiguity. This is what posters need — an unambiguous entry point.

The problem with most amateur poster work is timidity. Every element is reasonably sized in relation to every other element. The headline is large, the subhead is medium, the body copy is small. This is technically hierarchical but visually boring. The steps between sizes are too modest to create tension.

I aim for at least a 3:1 size ratio between my primary headline and the next tier of information. Often more. When a single word or phrase fills most of the poster, and the supporting information is small and tight in a corner, that's when something interesting starts to happen.

## Contrast Is the Point

Scale only works in contrast. A huge headline surrounded by huge subheads and huge supporting information is just a loud poster — not a hierarchical one. The big thing needs small things around it to be read as big.

This is where a lot of designers get tripped up: they scale up the primary element but don't scale everything else down proportionally. The result is a poster where everything is fighting for attention. The solution is to be ruthless about what's secondary and tertiary, and then really commit to making those elements feel secondary and tertiary.

## Type as Image

At sufficient scale, type stops being text and starts being image. Individual letterforms become shapes. Counter spaces become compositional elements. The texture of a word set in a heavy extended typeface becomes visual mass that anchors the composition.

I regularly use type at sizes where it partially crops off the edges of the poster — where only part of a letter is visible. This treats the type as pure form. It's a technique borrowed from Swiss and Polish poster traditions, and it's one I return to constantly because it creates immediate graphic impact without requiring illustration.

## Practical Scale Guidelines

When I'm building a poster layout:

- The primary element should feel uncomfortably large during the design process. If it feels right at first, it's probably too small.
- Secondary information should be readable but clearly subordinate — I rarely go above 12-14pt equivalent for supporting text on an A-series poster.
- Never let the secondary elements compete with the primary. If something needs to be readable, make it readable through contrast and placement, not through size.

## Testing Scale

I always proof my posters at reduced size — thumbnail or phone photo from a distance. The hierarchy should be completely legible at 100px wide. If I can't tell what the poster is about from a thumbnail, the scale relationships need work.
