## The Showreel Is an Argument

A motion design showreel is not a highlight compilation. It's an argument for why a specific type of client should hire you. The best reels are built backwards from that argument — who am I trying to work with, what do they need to see, and how do I sequence my work to make the most compelling case?

A reel built without that argument is just a random selection of things that look good. It might attract random clients. It won't attract the specific clients you want.

## Length

The right length for a motion design showreel is between 60 and 90 seconds. Less than 60 seconds can feel thin; more than 90 seconds tests the patience of the decision-makers who watch hundreds of reels.

This length constraint forces curation. Every shot needs to earn its place. If you have 20 seconds of a piece that only has 5 seconds of good material, cut it to 5 seconds. The reel is evaluated on its weakest moment, not its best.

## Front-Loading Your Strongest Work

The decision about whether to keep watching is made in the first 10 seconds. The decision about whether to reach out is often made in the first 30. Your strongest, most representative work goes first — not as a reveal, not building up to it. First.

I've seen reels that save the best work for the climax. These reels lose the viewer before the climax arrives.

## The Music Problem

Music and motion are inseparable in a showreel. Bad music undermines good work; great music can make good work look better. But the most common music mistake is using well-known commercial tracks.

Well-known tracks carry their own associations that compete with the work. They also create music licensing issues for publicly distributed reels. I use either licensed music from sync libraries or work from emerging artists (with permission). The track should feel like it was selected for the reel, not borrowed from a playlist.

## What to Leave Out

Work that doesn't represent the level you want to work at. Work in categories you don't want to be hired for. Work where you weren't the primary creative — if you were executing someone else's design direction, the work shows their creative intelligence, not yours.

The discipline of leaving things out is harder than the work of including things. But a tighter, more intentional reel consistently outperforms a longer, more comprehensive one.

## The Case for Specialization

The reels that get the most targeted, high-quality response are often the ones that clearly specialize — brand motion, explainer animation, title design, broadcast graphics. A reel that looks like it can do everything often looks like it excels at nothing.

Specialization is counterintuitive because it seems to reduce opportunities. In practice it increases the quality and relevance of the opportunities it attracts.
