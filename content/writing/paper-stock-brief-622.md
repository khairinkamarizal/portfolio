I always ask about paper stock before I start a print project. Not during production spec, not during preflight — before I design. The paper is a design variable. It affects color, legibility, tactility, and tone. Designing for the wrong substrate is like designing without knowing the format.

## The Core Variables

Paper stock for print design has three primary variables: weight (expressed in GSM for European/international standards, lb for US), coating (uncoated, silk/satin coated, gloss coated), and color (white, natural/cream, kraft, colored).

Each of these variables has aesthetic and production implications that should inform the design.

**Weight** determines rigidity and feel. A 300gsm business card feels solid and premium. A 150gsm doesn't. For posters, 170-200gsm is my typical range for something that will be displayed; thinner than that feels cheap, heavier adds cost without clear benefit for most applications.

**Coating** is probably the most consequential variable for design. Gloss coated stock makes colors pop, photographs reproduce vividly, and fine detail is sharp. But it also looks slick in the literal sense — it can feel commercial in contexts where warmth or craft is the goal. Uncoated stock absorbs ink, softens colors, and feels immediately different in the hand — more natural, more editorial, more considered. Silk/satin sits between the two.

**Stock color** is often overlooked. Designing with a warm cream-colored background when your stock is natural white means the "background" is real — you're using the paper color. This is a legitimate and beautiful design decision. Designing a "white" background on natural stock means the white is printed, which is expensive and looks different than paper white.

## Colors Change on Different Stocks

I've seen designers who do beautiful work on screen have their print projects fall apart because they didn't account for how color shifts between coated and uncoated stock. On uncoated paper, CMYK inks absorb into the paper fibers and come out less saturated, slightly warmer, with reduced contrast.

That vivid brand red on a gloss coated business card becomes a slightly different, murkier red on an uncoated letterhead — not because the specification changed, but because the substrate behaves differently.

My standard practice is to have Pantone solid coated and solid uncoated swatches for every brand color I specify. I use the uncoated version for uncoated stock applications and the coated version for coated applications. The two chip numbers are often different colors — and that's correct; they're compensating for substrate behavior to achieve a consistent visual result.

## Special Finishes

When budget allows, special finishes transform print. Soft-touch lamination makes a surface feel like suede. Spot UV creates a glossy, raised texture on a matte surface. Foil stamping adds metallic sheen. Embossing or debossing creates dimensional form.

These finishes are expensive but they punch above their cost in perceived quality. A business card with a soft-touch laminate and a spot UV logo element communicates luxury through touch before anyone reads a word.

## The Brief I Give Myself

For every print project, I note before I start designing: the substrate (stock type, weight, color), the print process (digital, litho offset, screen, letterpress), and any special finishes. These constraints shape the design. Working within them produces better, more coherent work than designing first and specifying later.
