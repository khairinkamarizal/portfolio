## Colors Need Reasons

I've delivered brand color palettes with and without accompanying rationale. The difference in how clients treat them is significant.

A palette without a story is just swatches. The client likes it or doesn't, but they have no language to defend it internally. When the CEO or the board asks "why these colors?" the answer is "the designer chose them" — which invites second-guessing and scope creep.

A palette with a story gives the client language. They can say: "The deep blue references our fifteen-year history in maritime shipping. The copper accent reflects the precision metalwork that's at the heart of our manufacturing process. The warm off-white grounds the system in approachability." Now the CEO has something to react to beyond personal taste.

## What a Color Story Includes

A color story isn't a lengthy document. It's three to four sentences per color, explaining where the inspiration came from and what the color is meant to communicate.

The explanation should be honest — grounded in real connections between the color and the brand — not invented after the fact to justify an aesthetic choice. Clients can usually tell the difference, and invented rationale undermines trust.

I build the color story during the development process, not as a retrospective justification. As I'm making color decisions, I'm noting why. The source of inspiration. The emotional quality I'm reaching for. The competitive context I'm responding to.

## Sources I Draw Color From

Real color stories connect to real things about the brand. Sources I've used:

**The physical world of the brand** — the materials of a manufacturer, the landscape of a geographic region, the ingredients of a food product.

**The brand's history** — colors from archival materials, founding-era documents, original packaging.

**The customer's world** — the environments, objects, and experiences that the brand's audience already lives with.

**The competitive context** — a deliberate response to category conventions, either meeting or consciously breaking them.

**The brand's emotional territory** — colors chosen to produce a specific emotional response that matches the brand's personality.

## The Story in Presentation

When I present a brand color palette, I present the story before I show the colors. Not as a teaser, but because the context transforms how the colors are perceived.

If I tell you "these colors were inspired by the patinated copper and aged timber of the client's original 1920s workshop" before showing you a palette of warm copper, dark greenish-teal, and warm tan — you see something different than if you just see swatches. You see the workshop. The colors mean something.

That meaning is the work. The color story is the delivery mechanism.

## Colors Without a Story Are Vulnerable

When a brand goes through a leadership change or a strategic review, every design decision gets questioned. The identities that survive these reviews are the ones that can be defended on strategic grounds.

"We chose this color because it feels modern" is not a strategic defense. "We chose this color because it's distinctive in the category, it resonates with the emotional temperature our audience associates with our product category, and it's consistent with the brand's heritage while feeling contemporary" — that's a strategic defense.

The story is armor. Build it deliberately.
