Picking a typeface for a poster is not the same as picking one for a book or a website. The criteria are different. The failure modes are different. And the margin for error is smaller, because everything is big.

I've spent years refining how I approach this decision, and I've landed on a framework that saves me from the obvious traps.

## Display Sizes Expose Everything

At body text sizes, a mediocre typeface can hide. The eye integrates the imperfections and moves on. At 120pt on a poster, every quirk in the letterforms is architectural. The subtle ink trap that looked charming at small sizes becomes a gaping hole. The slightly uneven weight distribution that was imperceptible in running text now looks like a mistake.

Test every typeface candidate at actual poster size before committing. Set the headline, print it at scale, and look at it. Not on screen — printed, on paper, from a distance.

## Optical Sizes Matter

Many type families offer optical size variants — text, caption, display, poster. These aren't just stylistic variations. The display or poster cuts typically have tighter spacing, finer strokes, and more refined details specifically because they're designed to be seen large.

Using a text-optimized cut at display size is one of the most common mistakes I see in student work. The spacing is too loose, the contrast too low, and the result looks timid on the wall.

## Legibility at Distance

A poster needs to communicate at three distances: from across the room, from two meters, and up close. The primary information — the headline — needs to work at the farthest distance. That means high stroke contrast is a liability unless you're working at genuinely large format, because thin strokes disappear at distance.

I favor mid-contrast or low-contrast type for primary poster headlines unless the format is A0 or larger. High-contrast serifs are beautiful in editorial work, but they can be fragile on a poster seen from 10 meters.

## My Current Go-To Families

- **Grotesks** (Aktiv Grotesk, Neue Haas Grotesk): Reliable, versatile, strong at any scale
- **Geometric sans** (Futura, Neue Machina): Great for structured, modernist work
- **Condensed display** (anything with a true condensed cut): Space-efficient without compromising scale
- **Slab serifs** (Sentinel, Clarendon): Authority and warmth without the fragility of high-contrast serif

I revisit this list constantly. New releases change the options. But the evaluation criteria stays the same: how does it look on paper, at size, from a distance?

## The Pairing Question

My default answer to typeface pairing on posters is: don't. One family, multiple weights and widths, is almost always enough. The visual complexity of a poster — image, color, space — doesn't need more typographic variety on top of it.

When I do pair, I treat it as a conceptual decision, not an aesthetic one. Two typefaces should represent two different voices or registers in the content, not just two different looks.
