## The Gut Feeling Problem

Most typeface selection is done by feel. The designer looks at options, something feels right, and that's the one. This works when the designer's instincts are well-calibrated and when the brief is clear enough that their gut is responding to the right inputs.

It fails when the instinct is responding to aesthetic preference rather than brand appropriateness. A typeface the designer finds personally beautiful may be entirely wrong for a specific brand. A typeface the designer finds dull may be exactly right.

I use a structured framework to discipline my typeface selection process. The framework doesn't replace instinct — it gives instinct a productive channel.

## The Character Axis Framework

I evaluate typefaces against a set of character axes relevant to the brand. Each axis is a spectrum between two poles. I position the brand on each axis based on the brief, then look for typefaces that reflect those positions.

**Formal vs. Casual:** Where does the brand fall on this spectrum? A formal brand needs typefaces with more traditional construction, less quirk, more historical precedent. A casual brand can use more informal, gestural, or contemporary type.

**Precise vs. Organic:** Is the brand's world defined by technical precision and clean systems, or by natural growth, human craft, and imperfect beauty? Geometric typefaces feel precise. Humanist typefaces feel organic.

**Reserved vs. Expressive:** Does the brand communicate through understatement, or does it occupy space confidently and loudly? Reserved brands use typefaces that don't call attention to themselves. Expressive brands use typefaces with more personality and distinctive letterform qualities.

**Heritage vs. Contemporary:** Is the brand building on established traditions and history, or positioning itself as new and forward-looking? Typefaces carry historical period associations that can align or conflict with this positioning.

**Approachable vs. Authoritative:** Does the brand need to be seen as human and accessible, or expert and authoritative? This axis often maps to humanist vs. rational type design traditions.

## Applying the Framework

For each brand project, I mark the brand's position on each axis from the brief. Then I look at typeface candidates and assess where they sit on each axis.

The goal isn't a perfect match on every axis — typefaces have personalities that are more nuanced than a simple five-axis framework captures. The goal is to eliminate clear misalignments and identify the typefaces where multiple axes align well with the brand.

## Testing the Alignment

After identifying candidate typefaces through the framework, I test them with real brand copy. Not "The quick brown fox" — actual text from the brand's world. The brand name, a tagline, a headline about what the brand does, body copy describing a product or service.

Seeing the typeface in authentic brand language often surfaces alignments or misalignments that the abstract evaluation missed. A typeface that seemed right in the framework may look wrong when it's actually setting the words the brand uses. A typeface that seemed borderline may look completely right when it's setting the right content.

## The Final Gut Check

After the framework analysis and the copy test, I still run a gut check. Does this feel right for this brand? Have I arrived at a typeface that I'm selecting strategically rather than the one I actually believe in?

The framework is a discipline, not a substitute for judgment. It helps me check my instincts against a structured evaluation. But the final selection should be one I believe in completely — not just one that passes the checklist.

The best typeface selections are both analytically defensible and intuitively right. When I can only get one of those, I keep looking.
