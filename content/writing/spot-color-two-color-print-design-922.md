## Two Colors Is a Design Challenge, Not a Compromise

Some of my best print work has been two-color. Not because of budget limits — though two-color is cheaper — but because the constraint of exactly two inks forces design decisions that full-color work lets you avoid. You can't reach for a third color to solve a problem. You have to solve it with what you have.

Two-color print design is a specific discipline. It has its own set of techniques, its own production considerations, and its own aesthetic quality that full-color printing can't replicate.

## Choosing the Two Colors

The first decision — which two colors — determines almost everything else. The pair needs enough contrast to create clear visual hierarchy, enough compatibility to feel intentional, and enough character to carry the piece's personality.

My most reliable combinations:

Black plus one spot color. This is the most flexible two-color option because black provides all the contrast you need for type and dark graphic elements, and the spot color becomes pure accent. The spot can be any hue.

A dark and a light within the same hue family. Deep navy and a soft sky blue. Charcoal and warm cream. These combinations feel sophisticated and editorial.

Two complementary colors of similar value. This is the riskiest approach because colors of similar lightness can look muddy when overprinted, but when the proportions are right it creates a striking, unconventional palette.

## Designing With Overprint

Two-color printing produces three visual values: color A, color B, and the overprint of A plus B where both inks overlap. This third color is free — it's a bonus you get by designing with overprint in mind.

The overprint color's exact appearance depends on the specific inks. Blue plus yellow gives green. Red plus blue gives purple. Black plus any color gives a darker, richer version of that color. I always calculate the approximate overprint color before starting and incorporate it into my design palette as the third visual element.

Using overprint deliberately is what separates sophisticated two-color work from work that merely happens to use two inks.

## Halftone and Tint

Within each ink color, I can use tints — screened halftone patterns at different percentages that simulate lighter values of the ink. A 100% blue field, a 50% blue tint, and a 20% blue tint give me three distinct blues from one ink.

Combined with overprint, a two-color job can produce a remarkable range of visual values. The key is designing with these values explicitly rather than discovering them accidentally in the press proof.

I build my color swatches file for every two-color job to include: 100% ink A, 50% ink A, 100% ink B, 50% ink B, 100%A + 100%B overprint, 100%A + 50%B overprint. Eight deliberate visual values from two inks.

## Typography in Two-Color Print

Type should always print in the highest-contrast pairing available. On white paper, black type is the standard. The spot color handles headlines, accents, rules, and graphic elements.

Reversing type out of a spot color field works well only with bold, simply drawn typefaces. Fine strokes and small sizes trap ink and fill in. I don't set anything lighter than 8pt reversed out of a solid color in two-color print.

## The Craft of Registration

Two-color print requires two separate passes through the press. Registration — the alignment of the two passes — matters for any element that requires both inks to be precisely placed. A headline that uses both colors in a split treatment will look wrong if registration is off.

For registration-dependent effects, I design with a small overlap built in — each color extends a fraction past where it meets the other. This tolerance means a small registration variance doesn't create a visible gap between the colors.
