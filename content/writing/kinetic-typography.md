Kinetic typography — text that moves — has a long history of being done badly. The technique got popular with After Effects tutorials in the 2000s and spawned a decade of text flying, spinning, and bouncing in ways that added stimulation but subtracted clarity.

Done well, kinetic typography uses motion to add meaning to words. The motion isn't decoration — it's part of the communication.

## Motion Should Serve Meaning

The first question I ask before animating any piece of text is: what does the motion need to say? Motion carries emotional and semantic content. A word that drops forcefully into frame communicates something different than a word that drifts in slowly. A line of text that explodes apart communicates something different than one that gently dissolves.

Every motion choice should be answerable to the question: why does the text move this way? "It looks cool" is not an answer. "It feels like the force behind this word" is an answer.

## Legibility First

The fundamental constraint in kinetic typography is legibility. Moving text is harder to read than static text. Any animation that compromises the viewer's ability to read the text has failed at the primary job.

I test kinetic typography with fresh eyes: someone who hasn't seen it before watching at normal speed. Can they read every word? Do they catch every line? If they miss content because the animation is too fast or too complex, the design needs to change.

## Timing to Speech and Music

Kinetic typography almost always lives in relationship to audio — voiceover, dialogue, music, or some combination. The synchronization between type and audio is where the work either comes alive or falls flat.

For speech-driven kinetic typography, I work from a transcription with precise timing notes. The text needs to appear at or just before the word is spoken — appearing after creates cognitive dissonance. I typically aim for text to hit its final state about 100ms before the corresponding spoken word.

For music-driven kinetic typography, I work with the rhythm and energy of the track. The editing points, the beat emphasis, and the dynamic shifts in the music become the choreographic structure for the type.

## Scale and Emphasis

One of the most powerful tools in kinetic typography is scale. A word that grows as it speaks carries a different weight than a word that stays constant. Selective scaling — making one word in a sentence larger than the others — creates a typographic hierarchy that doesn't exist in the static source text.

The risk is overuse. When everything is scaled for emphasis, nothing is emphasized. I use scale as a precise tool, not a default behavior.

## Character Animation vs. Word Animation vs. Line Animation

Kinetic typography can be animated at the character level (each letter independently), the word level (each word as a unit), or the line level (each line as a unit). These produce very different results.

Character-level animation is detailed and potentially complex. Word-level animation is the most natural unit for speech-based work. Line-level animation is the most restrained and reads most like reading.

I choose the unit of animation based on the content and the desired energy. Complex, energetic pieces often use character-level animation. Contemplative, literary pieces use line-level.
