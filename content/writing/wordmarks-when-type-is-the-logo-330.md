A wordmark is the purest test of typographic skill in logo design. There's nowhere to hide. No icon, no mark, no supporting graphic. Just letterforms — and everything those letterforms communicate.

## Choosing the Base Typeface

Most wordmarks start from an existing typeface and modify it. Pure custom lettering is expensive and only justified for brands with the scale and longevity to amortize that investment. For most projects, a well-chosen commercial typeface as a starting point is the smarter approach.

The selection criteria are different from body text decisions. I'm looking for a face with strong, distinctive character at display sizes. Optical quirks that might cause problems in running text — an unusual 'a', a distinctive 'g', an idiosyncratic 'Q' — become assets in a wordmark. Unusual characters give me material to work with.

## Modifications That Matter

A wordmark built directly from an unmodified typeface reads as a typeface, not a logo. The modifications are what create distinctiveness and ownership. But the modifications have to be principled, not arbitrary.

The types of modifications I use: custom letter spacing that differs from the typeface's default (and is consistent throughout the mark), subtle alterations to specific letterforms to create unique character, ligatures between adjacent letters, and occasionally structural changes that create a visual motif unique to the brand.

The rule I follow: every modification has to look intentional and reinforce the brand's personality. A modification that just looks like a mistake is worse than no modification at all.

## Spacing Is Everything

Wordmark spacing is not the same as text tracking. In running text, you're optimizing for readability at small sizes across many characters. In a wordmark, you're making an aesthetic and characterological decision about how the brand presents itself.

I set wordmark spacing by eye at the intended use size, then check it at smaller sizes. I'm looking for optical evenness — not mathematical evenness. Rounded characters need more space than vertical ones. The 'AV' pair needs different treatment than 'HH'.

## All-Caps vs. Mixed Case

All-caps wordmarks read as formal, authoritative, and strong. They work well for institutional brands, financial services, architecture firms. The loss of ascenders and descenders flattens the rhythm of the letters into a more even, controlled register.

Mixed case feels more conversational and approachable. The variation in letterform height creates a more dynamic silhouette. For consumer brands and creative services, mixed case often reads better.

Lowercase-only marks have a specific quality — they feel relaxed, modern, and slightly countercultural. Used a lot in tech and DTC brands. When it fits the positioning, it's a strong choice. When it doesn't, it reads as trying too hard.

## Testing the Silhouette

I evaluate wordmarks as silhouettes — filled black shapes with no internal detail. The outer edge of the wordmark should have a distinctive profile that reads quickly and memorably. If the silhouette is blobby and undifferentiated, the wordmark needs more work.
