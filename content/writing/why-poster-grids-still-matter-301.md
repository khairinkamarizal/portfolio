I've seen a lot of designers abandon the grid in favor of "creative freedom." I get the impulse — grids can feel limiting when you're trying to do something expressive. But after years of poster work, I've come to believe that the grid isn't a cage. It's a launching pad.

## What a Grid Actually Does

A grid gives your viewer an unconscious path through the composition. When I set up a poster, the grid decides where tension lives, where the eye rests, and where the hierarchy begins. Without it, you're making arbitrary decisions at every step — and arbitrary decisions compound into visual noise.

I typically start with a baseline grid tied to my body copy size. If I'm setting body text at 9pt with 13pt leading, every major element snaps to multiples of 13pt. This creates a rhythm that the viewer feels even if they can't name it.

## Modular vs. Column Grids

For exhibition posters, I lean on a modular grid — rows and columns that create a matrix of cells. This gives me flexibility to break the grid intentionally. And that's the key word: intentionally. A headline that bleeds across three modules has visual weight precisely because everything else obeys the structure.

Column grids work better for event posters with a lot of text hierarchy — band names, dates, venue, supporting acts. A 12-column grid lets me slot information into natural widths without things feeling crammed.

## The Break Is the Point

Here's the thing nobody tells you in school: a grid is most powerful when you break it once. One element — a diagonal, a bleed image, an oversized initial cap — that violates the grid reads as a deliberate choice. Two violations start to look sloppy. Five violations and you've lost the grid entirely.

I keep a rule for myself: one structural break per poster. Everything else conforms. That single break becomes the poster's personality.

## Practical Setup

In my workflow, I build grids in InDesign before I touch any type or imagery. I set up margins, columns, gutters, and baseline grid all at once. Then I don't move them. The temptation to "just nudge this a little" is real, but I've learned to trust the setup. If something doesn't fit the grid, the problem is usually with the content decision, not the grid itself.

Print work punishes sloppiness in ways screen work doesn't. A misaligned baseline shows up on press. A gutter that's two millimeters too narrow causes text to optically crowd the fold. The grid prevents these problems before they start.

## Final Thought

Grids aren't about rigidity. They're about having a coherent visual argument. When someone looks at your poster, you want them to feel the logic even if they're only consciously reading the headline. That subconscious coherence is what grids give you — and it's not something you can fake your way into.
