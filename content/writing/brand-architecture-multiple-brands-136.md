## The Brand Architecture Problem

Most brand identity work focuses on a single brand. But many organizations operate multiple brands — a parent company with subsidiary brands, a product portfolio, a company that has grown through acquisition. How these brands relate to each other is a strategic question with major visual consequences.

The wrong architecture — or no architecture at all — leads to brand confusion, wasted equity, and visual incoherence.

## The Three Main Models

There are three foundational brand architecture models. The branded house puts one dominant brand over everything: Google with Google Maps, Google Drive, Google Meet. Everything is clearly Google. The house of brands creates distinct, independent brands that don't visibly connect to the parent: Procter & Gamble's portfolio of consumer brands, each with its own identity. The hybrid (or endorsed) model sits in between: parent brand is present but sub-brands have substantial independence.

Each model has strategic logic behind it. The right choice depends on the organization's goals and the nature of the brands involved.

## When the Branded House Works

If the parent brand has strong positive equity, bringing that equity to all products and services through a unified brand structure makes sense. The recognition and trust associated with the parent transfers to every offering.

The risk: a failure in one product area affects the whole brand. The parent brand's associations constrain what kinds of products can sit under it.

## When the House of Brands Works

Independent brands allow each product or service to be positioned distinctly for its target audience, without the constraints of the parent brand's identity. A luxury brand and a budget brand from the same company can coexist without one undermining the other.

The cost: building multiple distinct brands requires significantly more investment than maintaining one.

## Visual Expression of Architecture

Once the architecture is determined, the visual system expresses it. In a branded house, the visual identity is centrally controlled and consistently applied. In a house of brands, each brand has its own independent identity system. In hybrid models, a common visual device — a shared color, a structural element, an endorsement lockup — creates the connection while allowing individual expression.

The visual system is the architecture made tangible. Getting the design right requires understanding the strategy first.

## When Architecture Changes

Mergers, acquisitions, and brand consolidations all create brand architecture decisions. I've worked on projects where acquiring companies needed to integrate or retire acquired brands — decisions with significant emotional and economic complexity beyond the design itself.

In these situations, design follows strategy. I won't design a brand architecture without clarity on the strategic intent.
