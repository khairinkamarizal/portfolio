## What Patterns Do in a Brand System

Patterns serve a specific function in brand identities: they provide a way to fill space with brand-consistent content that isn't the logo. On packaging surfaces, merchandise, brand environments, digital backgrounds — a pattern extends the visual identity into contexts where a logo alone isn't sufficient.

Done well, a brand pattern is as recognizable as the logo. Louis Vuitton's monogram. Burberry's plaid. Hermès scarves. These patterns have become brand assets as powerful as any mark.

## Pattern Derived from the Mark

The strongest brand patterns have a direct relationship to the brand's primary mark. They're often derived from the mark's geometry, letterforms, or graphic motifs — extended into a repeating tile.

This derivation creates coherence. The pattern looks like it belongs to the same visual world as the logo because it literally shares its DNA. Patterns that feel arbitrary — geometric fills that could belong to any brand — miss this opportunity.

## Tile Structure and Scale

A pattern is built from a repeating tile. The tile's size relative to the application determines how the pattern reads — small tiles create texture, large tiles create graphic presence. Getting the scale right for the intended application requires testing: a tile that reads correctly on a business card may feel completely different on a tote bag.

I design patterns at multiple scale options and specify which scale is appropriate for which context.

## Color in Patterns

A brand pattern should work in multiple color versions: the full-color version, a one-color version (for single-color printing applications), and often an inverted version (light on dark). These variations extend the pattern's utility across the full range of brand applications.

The color combinations also need to be specified precisely. "Use the brand's primary and secondary colors" is not specific enough — which one is foreground, which is background, what are the proportions?

## Restraint in Application

The mistake I see most often with brand patterns is over-application. When a pattern appears on every surface, in every context, at every scale, it becomes visual noise. The pattern loses its distinctiveness.

Patterns are most powerful when used strategically — in moments where surface coverage adds something to the experience, not as wallpaper that fills empty space reflexively.

## Testing in Context

I never finalize a pattern without testing it on actual brand materials — the specific packaging shapes, merchandise types, or surface contexts it will inhabit. The geometry of the object changes how the tile repeats. A pattern that tiles beautifully on a flat surface can look awkward on a curved one.

Context testing before finalizing. Always.
