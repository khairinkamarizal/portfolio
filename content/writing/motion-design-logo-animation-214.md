## The animation serves the mark, not the other way around

When a client asks for a logo animation, the first question I ask is: what does the static mark already communicate, and how can the animation reinforce that rather than override it? A bad logo animation can actually damage a strong static mark by introducing movement that contradicts the mark's visual personality.

A geometric mark built on precision and grid logic should not animate organically and loosely — even if organic motion looks interesting in isolation. The animation needs to feel continuous with the mark's existing character.

## Start by studying the static mark's personality

Before I open After Effects, I spend time with the static logo. I describe its personality in words: precise, angular, confident, geometric. Or: organic, flowing, warm, craft-driven. Or: bold, compressed, urgent, energetic. These adjectives become the brief for the animation.

The personality words map to animation properties. Precise and geometric suggests mechanical timing, no overshooting, crisp starts and stops. Organic and warm suggests easing that has some give, a slight settle, perhaps some motion blur. Bold and urgent suggests fast timing, strong ease-in, decisive arrival.

## Building the reveal sequence

Most logo animations are reveals — the mark builds in from nothing to its full form. The key decision in a reveal sequence is order: which element appears first, and what does the sequence communicate?

For marks where the icon and wordmark are separate elements, I almost always animate the icon first, then the wordmark. The icon is the visual anchor; the name confirms it. Animating in the other order feels backwards — like saying the answer before you've established the question.

For wordmarks, I think about whether the letters should reveal simultaneously, cascade left to right, or build from a central anchor outward. These choices have personality implications. A simultaneous reveal feels confident and bold. A cascade feels sequential and measured. Building from an anchor feels grounded.

## The hold time matters as much as the animation

After the mark fully reveals, there should be a clear hold — a moment of stillness where the fully-formed logo sits without moving. This hold is what makes the reveal feel complete. Without it, the animation feels like it stops in the middle.

I typically time the hold at 0.5–1 second before any transition or cut. This gives the viewer's eye time to settle on the complete mark and actually perceive it as a resolved, stable thing.

## Deliver in the right formats

A logo animation needs to be delivered in multiple formats for practical use: a transparent-background ProRes or PNG sequence for broadcast compositing, a GIF or WebP for digital use, and often a Lottie JSON file for interactive applications. Each format has limitations — GIFs can't reproduce the color depth of ProRes, Lottie has constraints on certain animation effects.

I confirm the delivery format list with the client before finishing production, not after. The formats needed sometimes affect decisions made during animation.
