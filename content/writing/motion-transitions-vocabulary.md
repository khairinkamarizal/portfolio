The dissolve is the default transition in motion design the way Helvetica is the default typeface in graphic design. It's neutral, it works, and it's used constantly without much thought. That default isn't wrong — but using it exclusively signals a designer who hasn't developed a full motion vocabulary.

Every type of transition communicates something. Choosing deliberately means understanding what each one says.

## The Vocabulary

**Cut:** An instantaneous change from one state to another. No transition effect — just the edit. Cuts feel decisive, energetic, and editorial. In motion design, a sharp cut between graphic states creates urgency and confidence. Overused in slow, considered work, it feels jarring; used in fast-paced, bold work, it feels right.

**Dissolve/Crossfade:** The default. One state fades out as another fades in. Dissolves feel gentle, temporal, and slightly nostalgic. They communicate "time is passing" or "we're moving gently from here to there."

**Wipe:** One state wipes across the frame to reveal another. The direction, speed, and hardness of the wipe change its character significantly. A hard-edged wipe is graphic and deliberate; a soft-edged wipe is more gentle.

**Push/Slide:** One state slides off while another slides on. Communicates spatial relationship — the two states feel like they exist in the same space, with one replacing the other laterally or vertically.

**Scale transition:** An element grows or shrinks into or out of the frame. Growing into full screen feels like we're entering the element's world; shrinking to nothing feels like departure or conclusion.

**Morph:** One form smoothly transforms into another. This is the most complex transition type — it requires careful design to make the morph feel motivated rather than arbitrary. When it works, it can create powerful conceptual connections between two visual states.

**Reveal/Wipe-on:** An element appears by being revealed from behind a mask, rather than fading or pushing on. Reveals feel considered and intentional — like something being unveiled rather than simply appearing.

## Matching Transition to Content

The right transition should reinforce what the edit is communicating conceptually:

- Spatial relationships between elements: push or slide
- Temporal passage: dissolve
- Decisive change or parallel action: cut
- Conceptual transformation: morph
- Dramatic unveiling: reveal

When the transition fights the content — a slow dissolve on a moment that should feel decisive, a cut on a moment that should feel gentle — the audience feels the dissonance even without identifying it.

## The Value of Restraint

A motion piece that uses five different transition types usually feels more complex than it is. Establishing one or two transitions as the primary vocabulary and using them consistently creates a coherent motion language. Introducing a special transition type for a specific moment — a morph that connects two ideas that are meant to be related — makes that moment distinctive precisely because it departs from the established pattern.
