One of the most instructive exercises in poster design — and one I return to regularly as a discipline — is designing with a single color on uncoated stock. One ink. One paper. No gradients. No photography. Just typography, form, and the relationship between ink and substrate.

The constraints are brutal. The results, when they work, are some of the most powerful poster work I've produced.

## What Single-Color Forces

When you remove color as a design variable, everything else has to work harder. Typography carries more weight. Spatial composition becomes the primary tool for creating hierarchy. The density of ink on the page — heavy, solid areas versus open white space — becomes your only contrast mechanism.

This forces clarity in a way that multi-color work often doesn't. In full-color work, you can compensate for a weak composition with strong color contrast. In single-color, the bones of the composition are completely exposed. You can't hide behind a bold color combination.

## Choosing the Right Single Color

Not all single-color posters use black. A well-chosen spot color on the right paper stock can be extraordinarily expressive.

A warm red on cream uncoated stock reads as energetic and immediate. A deep navy on natural paper reads as considered and authoritative. A bright yellow on white coated stock reads as bold and graphic. The relationship between the ink color and the paper tone is the only color relationship you have, and it matters enormously.

I also pay close attention to the saturation and value of the spot color against the specific paper. Pantone colors behave differently on coated vs. uncoated stock — uncoated paper absorbs ink and the color reads slightly duller and warmer. I always request a printed proof on the actual specified stock before finalizing a spot color choice.

## Typography Has to Carry Everything

In single-color poster work, the type can't be supplemented by a strong image or a rich color palette. The typographic composition is the poster. This means every decision about typeface, size, weight, spacing, and arrangement is load-bearing.

I find that single-color work pushes me toward more extreme typographic decisions: type that's larger than I'd normally go, weight contrasts that are starker, spacing relationships that are more decisive. The type needs to be interesting enough to sustain attention by itself.

## The Texture of Type at Scale

One phenomenon that single-color print makes beautifully apparent: type set at large scale in a heavy weight creates a visual texture from the printing process itself. Letterpress printing on heavy uncoated stock, especially with deep ink impression, produces a tactile quality that is completely unlike digital output. The ink sits differently in the paper.

Even offset printing of dense black type on uncoated paper has a presence that digital reproduction can't replicate. The ink has weight. This is something worth designing for, not just accepting.

## The Exercise Value

Even if you primarily produce full-color work, designing single-color posters regularly is a practice worth maintaining. It builds compositional muscles that carry over into every other format. When you return to full-color work after serious single-color work, you use color more deliberately — as a tool with purpose rather than as decoration.
