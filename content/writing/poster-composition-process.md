The blank artboard is where most designers get stuck. The brief is in front of them, the references are in a folder, and nothing is happening. I've been there. Over time, I've developed a starting process that reliably gets me moving.

It doesn't guarantee great work. But it prevents the paralysis that comes from trying to design and evaluate simultaneously.

## Start With the Communication Hierarchy

Before a single visual decision, I write out the information hierarchy as a plain text list. First: the most important piece of information. Second: the next most important. And so on down to the least essential.

This list is the brief for the composition. The visual decisions about scale, placement, and contrast will all serve this information hierarchy. If I start with the hierarchy clear in my head, every subsequent decision has a criterion to be evaluated against.

## Find the Concept

Some posters are purely typographic — the type is the design. Others use a visual concept — an image, an illustration, a graphic metaphor — to communicate something the type can't. Before choosing a visual approach, I spend time with the content asking: what's the most interesting or unexpected way to represent this?

A concert poster could be a photograph of the performer. But what's the most interesting visual interpretation of this performer's music? That question is where concepts come from.

## Rough Composition Sketching

I spend time sketching thumbnail compositions before opening the computer. Small, fast, loose — 15–20 thumbnails exploring different spatial arrangements. Where is the primary information? What scale is the image relative to the type? What's the visual weight distribution?

Thumbnails at this stage are not about refinement. They're about exploring the space of compositional possibilities quickly and identifying the two or three directions worth developing.

## The First Digital Pass

When I open the design application, I have a compositional direction in mind. I block in the major elements at rough scale and position — not detailed type, not final images. I'm testing whether the composition works spatially before investing in the details.

This is the stage where I establish the grid (if I haven't already), the general color direction, and the rough hierarchy. Everything is placeholder quality.

## Zooming Out Repeatedly

The most common mistake in poster design is working too close. I set a habit of zooming to 25% or smaller every 20 minutes and evaluating the composition from a distance. Problems that are invisible at 100% — a visual weight imbalance, a hierarchy that doesn't read at distance, an awkward relationship between image and text — appear immediately at reduced scale.

The poster has to work from across the room before it works up close.

## Knowing When to Stop

Poster design has a completion problem. There's always something that could be refined, adjusted, or reconsidered. I stop when the communication hierarchy works, the composition is balanced, and the concept is clearly communicated. Not when every detail is perfect — when the work is complete.
