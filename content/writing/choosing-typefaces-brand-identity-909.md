## The Presentation Trap

A typeface that looks stunning in a brand presentation can be a nightmare in actual use. The display weight that made the mood board sing has no usable regular weight. The beautiful serif has terrible screen rendering at small sizes. The distinctive geometric sans has no italic, no tabular numerals, and no extended character support.

Choosing typefaces for a brand system requires thinking about every use case simultaneously, not just the hero applications in your deck.

## What I Actually Evaluate

I evaluate typefaces against five criteria before recommending them to a client.

The first is weight range. A brand system needs at minimum a light or regular, a medium or semibold, and a bold. Ideally it needs a matching italic for each. If the type family only comes in three weights with no italics, it's going to create problems the moment someone needs to write a press release or a long-form document.

The second is rendering quality. I test every candidate at 10px, 16px, and 24px on screen, and at 8pt and 11pt in print. Some typefaces are designed for display use and fall apart at text sizes. Others are optimized for text and look weedy at large scale. The brand needs to work at both.

The third is character set completeness. Does it include the diacritics you need for the markets you serve? Tabular numerals for pricing and data? Proper small caps if the brand uses them? Fractions? Missing characters get substituted with system defaults, which instantly breaks typographic consistency.

The fourth is licensing. Can the client use this typeface on their website, in their printed materials, in digital ads, and in documents they send to third parties? Font licensing is complicated and the answers are often no, or only at additional cost. I verify this before recommending anything.

The fifth is personality fit. The typeface needs to say something true about the brand. A humanist serif for a heritage brand. A clean geometric sans for a tech company. A slab serif for a brand that wants to signal robustness and confidence. I can tell you what a typeface communicates; it's my job to make sure what it communicates matches who the client is.

## The Pairing Logic

Most brand systems use two typefaces: a display or heading face and a text face. Sometimes these are from the same family (different optical sizes or weights). More often they're complementary typefaces that create contrast while feeling harmonious.

Contrast is the goal. A serif heading paired with a sans text is a classic combination because the contrast between the two is immediate and clear. Two sans-serifs can work if they're from genuinely different design traditions — a geometric sans with a humanist sans, for instance. Two serifs rarely work because the similarity creates competition without resolution.

## When One Typeface Is Enough

For many brands, especially smaller businesses and startups, one typeface with a strong weight range is the right answer. It simplifies the system, reduces licensing costs, and makes it harder for non-designers to break the brand with bad pairing choices.

The single-typeface constraint also forces me to find a family with enough internal variation to do all the work. Variable fonts have made this much more viable — a single variable font file can span the entire personality range the brand needs.

## The Test I Always Run

Before finalizing any typeface recommendation, I set a real text sample in it. Not "The quick brown fox" — actual brand copy: a headline, a subheading, a paragraph, a caption, a price, a date, and an email address. If it all looks right, the typeface is ready to recommend.
