## Practice as Infrastructure

The work that gets produced is a function of the practice that produces it. Designers who produce consistently excellent work don't just have talent — they have systems. Habits around how they engage with problems, how they make decisions, how they evaluate output. The practice is the infrastructure.

I've built my practice deliberately over time, through iteration and through examining what produces good outcomes versus what doesn't.

## Time Blocking for Deep Work

Design thinking requires sustained, uninterrupted concentration. Decisions made under fragmented attention — between emails, in 20-minute windows — are worse than decisions made in focused work blocks.

I protect morning blocks for design work and handle client communication and administrative tasks in the afternoon. This isn't rigid — real project demands shift things — but it's the default structure that produces my best work.

## The Sketchbook Habit

I keep a physical sketchbook and use it before touching software on any significant design problem. The sketchbook has no undo button, no grid snapping, no automation. It forces a quality of thinking that software discourages — committing to an idea and following it, even badly, rather than making endless reversible adjustments.

I'm not precious about what goes in the sketchbook. Most of it is bad. That's the point.

## Reference Collection as Practice

I maintain organized archives of design references — organized by category, style, and problem type — that I've built over years. When I start a new project, I draw from this archive to understand what exists and what's possible.

This reference collection is as important to my practice as any software skill. It's the visual vocabulary I work from.

## Project Reviews

After every significant project, I spend time reviewing what worked and what didn't. Not the client feedback — my own assessment. What design decisions produced the outcomes I intended? What didn't? What would I do differently?

This review practice is how the work improves over time. Without it, you repeat the same mistakes and the same approaches indefinitely.

## The Client Filter

My practice includes being selective about clients — not always possible early in a career, but increasingly possible and important as the practice matures. Working with clients who engage substantively with the design problem, who value the work, and who bring interesting challenges produces better work than working with anyone who'll pay.

The clients shape the work as much as I do. Choose them carefully.
