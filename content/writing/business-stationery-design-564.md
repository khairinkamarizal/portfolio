## The First Physical Impression

Before a client meeting, before a proposal, before a pitch — the business card. It's the most consistently produced brand touchpoint, the one that gets handed to people whose opinion matters, and the one that most designers underinvest in.

A well-designed business card is a physical argument for the brand's quality. A poorly designed one undermines everything the brand is trying to say.

## The Business Card as a Design Object

Business cards in most of the world follow a standard size (85x55mm or 3.5x2 inches in North America). That standard creates a level playing field where the design has to do all the differentiating.

I treat the business card as a miniature poster. Every element earns its place. The typographic hierarchy is clear at small scale. The color and finish choices signal the brand's positioning.

The most important decision: front/back. I almost always use both sides deliberately. The front carries the essential identity — logo, name, title. The back carries either additional contact information or is used as a strong graphic statement — the full-bleed brand color, a pattern, a key message.

A card used on both sides feels more generous and considered than one that leaves the back blank.

## Finish and Production as Communication

For premium brands, the production quality of the business card should match or exceed the brand's positioning. This means specifying production thoughtfully:

**Stock weight** — 400gsm or heavier for premium. 350gsm for standard quality. Under 300gsm reads as inexpensive.

**Coating** — soft-touch matte laminate feels different from standard gloss or uncoated. The tactile experience communicates before content is processed.

**Special techniques** — foil stamping on the logo, debossing the name, edge printing in a brand color. Each technique adds cost and adds memorability.

The brief for a startup on a tight budget and a brief for a luxury consulting firm should produce different production specifications, even if the design quality is equivalent.

## Letterhead Design

Letterhead is increasingly less common in a world of digital communication, but for industries where formal correspondence still happens — legal, financial, professional services — it matters.

Letterhead design requires careful thought about usable space. The header and footer can carry the brand, but the body of the letter needs clean, legible space for text. An overdesigned letterhead that competes with the letter's content is worse than no design at all.

I keep letterhead headers relatively compact — the brand needs to be present but not dominant. The page is about the letter, not about the brand.

## The Envelope

The envelope is the letterhead's first impression. A well-designed envelope with consistent branding signals quality before the letter is opened.

For premium correspondence, a bespoke envelope — custom color, custom liner, specific stock — elevates the entire communication. Most brands don't go this far, but the brands that do make a strong impression.

## System Cohesion

Business stationery works as a system. The business card, letterhead, envelope, and compliments slip should feel like they came from the same session, using the same grid, the same color applications, the same typographic hierarchy.

Inconsistency within the stationery set undermines the overall quality impression. If the business card is premium and the letterhead is generic, the system fails.

Design the system together and proof it as a set before approving any individual piece.
