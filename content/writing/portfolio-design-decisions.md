Every decision in this portfolio was deliberate. Not a single font pairing or layout choice was accidental. I want to walk through the thinking behind the major ones — because the process is often more interesting than the result.

## Why Full-Bleed

Most portfolios sit in a centered container, maybe 1200px wide, with comfortable padding on both sides. It's safe. It's predictable. It's also a little boring.

Full-bleed layouts force you to think differently. There's no safety net of a container to hide in. Every element has to justify its position on the page relative to the entire viewport. Sections become distinct zones. The grid becomes part of the design language.

I took direct inspiration from editorial print design — think magazine spreads where the content breathes all the way to the edge of the page. On a screen, this translates to sections that feel purposeful and weighty rather than floating in the middle of nothing.

The sticky header runs full width. The hero text bleeds edge to edge. The stats grid divides the page into even columns. It all reads as a coherent system rather than a collection of components.

## Why Space Mono for Display

I use two typefaces: Inter Tight for body and UI, and Space Mono for display elements — the hero name, the navigation, the stats.

Space Mono is a monospace typeface but it reads as something more interesting than a code font. It has personality. It has a slight rawness to it that feels technical without being cold. Paired with a tight uppercase treatment and generous letter-spacing, it creates a visual signature that's distinct.

Inter Tight handles everything else. It's neutral, legible, and works at every size. The contrast between the two — personality versus utility — creates a hierarchy that's felt even when it's not consciously noticed.

I went back and forth on this for a while. I tried a few display serifs, I tried GT Alpina, I tried keeping everything in one typeface. Space Mono won because it felt honest. It reflects the technical side of the work without performing it.

## Why Minimal Palette

Black and white only. No accent colors. No gradients.

This sounds like a constraint but it's actually a clarifying choice. When you have one color (or really two), every decision becomes about form, space, and typography. You can't fall back on a bright accent to draw attention to something — you have to earn attention through composition.

It also ages well. Color trends shift. What feels fresh in 2024 can feel dated in 2027. Black and white is timeless in a way that a carefully selected slate-based palette isn't.

The dark mode toggle isn't just a feature — it's part of the design. The site feels genuinely different in dark mode, not just inverted. The opacity-based layering means dark mode has its own quiet depth.

## The Editorial Approach

The influence throughout is editorial print — specifically the kind of design you'd see in a well-produced magazine or design annual. Large type, strong section breaks, whitespace used intentionally rather than by default.

This felt right for a design portfolio because it demonstrates the sensibility directly. I'm not just telling you I think about composition and hierarchy — the portfolio is the evidence.

The section labels are styled like newspaper section headers. The stats are presented as a full-width grid. The work entries are structured like editorial listings. These choices are all pointed in the same direction.

## What I'd Do Differently

Honestly, the image treatment is unresolved. The placeholder boxes are fine for now but they're clearly temporary. The challenge with a minimal palette is that photography introduces color — and color has to be handled deliberately, not just dropped in.

I'm leaning toward treating all project images as full-bleed, high-contrast, with a slight filter to pull them toward the monochrome palette. The visual tension between the raw color of real photography and a strict black-white system is something I want to explore.

The other thing I'd revisit is the mobile experience. The full-bleed approach works beautifully on desktop. On mobile, some of the type scale decisions are more aggressive than they need to be. That's refinement work, not rethinking — but it's on the list.

Every portfolio is a living document. This one is no different.
