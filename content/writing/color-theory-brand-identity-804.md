## Why Most Brand Colors Are Wrong

Most brand color decisions happen like this: the client says they want to feel "trustworthy and modern," the designer opens a palette generator, picks something blue-ish, and that becomes the brand color forever. It's not that blue is wrong. It's that the decision wasn't made — it was defaulted to.

Real color strategy in branding starts with competitive positioning, not with what the client finds attractive.

## The Competitive Audit First

Before I propose any palette, I map every major competitor's brand colors. Literally — I put their logos on a page and look at the color landscape of the category.

What I'm looking for: where is there visual space? If every player in a category is navy blue and forest green, a brand that owns warm amber stands out structurally, not just aesthetically. Standing out in a category is often more strategically valuable than matching the emotional associations of a color.

## Psychological Associations Are Real But Overrated

Yes, blue reads as trustworthy. Red reads as urgent. Green reads as natural or financial depending on context. These associations are real and worth understanding. But they're also so widely known that they've become clichés.

The smarter question is: what do these colors mean in this specific category context? In healthcare, blue is clinical. In technology, it's default. In food, it's appetite-suppressing. The same hue carries entirely different meaning depending on what surrounds it.

## Building a Palette That Works at Every Scale

A brand palette needs to function across a huge range of applications — from a tiny favicon to a building-sized billboard. This means I design palettes with deliberate hierarchy:

- **Primary color**: owns the brand, used most prominently
- **Secondary colors**: support the primary, provide flexibility
- **Neutrals**: the workhorses for backgrounds, text, and breathing room
- **Accent**: used sparingly, for emphasis and energy

I test every palette in actual contexts: on light backgrounds, on dark backgrounds, in single-color printing, in digital with varying display calibrations. A palette that only works on a perfectly calibrated monitor isn't a brand palette — it's a mood board.

## Pantone vs CMYK vs RGB

Every brand color I specify gets a Pantone reference, a CMYK breakdown, an RGB value, and a hex. All four. Because brand applications span print and screen, and if you only specify hex, the person printing the trade show booth is going to guess — and they're going to guess wrong.

The translation between these spaces is never perfect. A vibrant digital color often becomes muddy in four-color print. I factor this in during palette selection, not after.

## The Gut Check

After all the strategy and testing, I still run a final gut check: does this palette feel right for the brand? Color carries emotional intelligence that analysis can't fully capture. If I've done the competitive work, specified across color spaces, and tested in context — but something still feels off — I trust that instinct and dig deeper before presenting.
