## What the Client Doesn't See

When a client approves a logo, they're approving the visual result. They're not seeing the work underneath: the grid, the geometric relationships, the precise alignment that makes the mark reproducible across every output at every size.

That underlying structure is invisible when it's done right. It becomes visible when it's missing — when a logo comes back from a sign fabricator looking slightly off, or when the embroidered version doesn't quite match the print version, or when the mark has to be redrawn for a new application and nobody can figure out how it was built.

## Geometric Construction

I build every logo mark on a geometric foundation. This means defining the mark in terms of circles, squares, and lines with precise mathematical relationships rather than freehand curves.

The process looks like this: I start with a grid, define the primary shapes using exact dimensions, construct curves as arcs of defined radii, and ensure all tangent relationships are mathematically perfect. The result is a mark that can be perfectly reconstructed from its specifications without having the source file.

This is how the great marks of the 20th century were built. Before digital tools, logo construction documents described marks using geometric specifications that a craftsperson could redraw precisely at any size. That rigor is still valuable and most designers have lost it.

## The Optical Correction Layer

Here's the nuance: mathematically precise geometry doesn't always look optically correct. A perfect square looks slightly shorter than a perfect circle of the same width. A perfect geometric construction can have intersections that look slightly misaligned.

After geometric construction comes optical correction — making small adjustments to compensate for the eye's perception of form. A slightly oversized circle here, a fraction of extra weight on a thin element there. These corrections are small, deliberate, and invisible to most viewers. They're what make a mark feel right rather than merely correct.

## Documenting the Construction

Every logo I deliver comes with a construction overlay — a version of the file showing the underlying geometric grid and the key dimensions. This document serves as a rebuild specification. Anyone who receives it can reconstruct the mark precisely.

This documentation protects the client. If they change designers ten years from now, the new designer can work with the mark confidently rather than guessing at the designer's intent.

## Why This Matters for Reproduction

The practical reason for geometric rigor is reproduction quality across media. A mark built on precise geometry reproduces consistently whether it's:

- Output as a vector file for large-format print
- Rendered as a raster image for digital use
- Handed to a fabricator for dimensional signage
- Sent to an embroidery digitizer
- Engraved in metal or glass

Each of these processes requires interpreting the mark. A geometrically precise mark gives the interpreter unambiguous information. A mark built from organic, intuitive curves gives them room to guess — and they will guess, and it will drift.

The invisible foundation is what makes the mark last.
