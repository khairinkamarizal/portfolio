Packaging design has constraints that other design formats don't. The package doesn't exist in isolation — it lives on a shelf next to competitors, under specific lighting conditions, at a specific viewing distance, in the hands of a shopper who's making a decision in seconds. Designing without understanding that context is designing blind.

## The Shelf Context Is the Brief

Before I start any packaging design, I visit the retail environment where the product will live. I look at the category. What colors dominate? What's the visual noise level? How far are shoppers typically standing from the shelf? What's the lighting like?

This research changes design decisions. A warm organic color that reads beautifully in a studio presentation might disappear in a category where every competitor uses warm organic colors. A clean white package that stands out dramatically in the category might look clinical under the specific fluorescent lighting of that store type.

Designing for the shelf means designing for the actual competitive and environmental context, not a neutral background.

## The Two-Meter Test

Shoppers typically view a shelf from two meters or more before moving closer to inspect a product they're interested in. The packaging has to communicate something useful at that distance.

At two meters, fine detail is invisible. Small type is unreadable. Subtle color variation collapses. What reads at two meters: a strong silhouette or shape, a dominant color, a large primary element (brand name or product category name), and the overall feeling of the design.

I design packaging top-down: nail the two-meter communication first, then fill in the detail for close inspection.

## The Brand-Product Balance

Every packaging design has to balance two communication needs: the brand identity (this is our brand, with our brand values and personality) and the product identity (this is the specific product variant, with its specific attributes).

Too much brand, too little product: the range looks beautiful and coherent but shoppers can't distinguish between variants. Too much product, too little brand: each variant is distinct but the range doesn't read as a family.

Getting this balance right usually involves a fixed brand zone (consistent across all variants — same position, same treatment of the brand mark) and a flexible product zone (variant-specific color, imagery, or descriptor treatment). The fixed zone establishes family identity; the flexible zone enables variant navigation.

## Typography for Packaging

Packaging type faces two simultaneous requirements: it has to communicate the brand's typographic personality (usually through the primary headline type) and it has to be readable under imperfect conditions (usually through the body and fine print).

The fine print requirements on many product categories — ingredients, nutritional information, regulatory text, barcodes — are largely dictated by regulation and practical readability constraints. Getting creative with the fine print usually just makes it harder to read without adding communicative value.

I invest the typographic interest in the hierarchy's upper levels — the brand name, the product name, the key descriptor — and use neutral, highly legible type for everything below that. The contrast between the expressive headline type and the functional body type is itself a design statement.

## The 3D Reality

Packaging is three-dimensional. The design I produce needs to account for how the package looks in the hand, how the panels relate to each other as the package rotates, and how the design reads when multiple units are stacked or faced together on a shelf.

I always work in a 3D mockup, not just flat artwork, before finalizing any packaging design. The spatial relationships between panels reveal problems that flat artwork doesn't show.
