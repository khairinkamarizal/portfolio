## The Invisible Typeface

The best body text typeface is one the reader doesn't notice. They read for twenty minutes without eye fatigue, turn pages without conscious awareness of the type, absorb the content without the letterforms announcing themselves.

This is a completely different design goal from display type, where distinctiveness and personality are virtues. In body text, getting out of the way is the goal.

## What Makes Type Legible Over Long Reading

Several factors determine how comfortable a typeface is for extended reading:

**x-height** — typefaces with larger x-heights (the height of lowercase letters relative to capitals) are generally more legible at small sizes because the lowercase letters are larger relative to the overall type size. Small x-height typefaces require larger point sizes to achieve equivalent legibility.

**Aperture and counter size** — how open are the interior spaces of letters like a, e, c, s? Open apertures and generous counters help letterforms remain distinct even at small sizes or in less-than-perfect printing conditions.

**Stroke contrast** — the difference between thick and thin strokes. High-contrast typefaces (Didones, some transitional serifs) require high-quality reproduction to maintain legibility. Lower contrast typefaces are more forgiving. For newsprint, low-quality printing, or small text sizes, lower contrast is safer.

**Letter spacing defaults** — how the typeface is spaced by default. Type with generous default spacing is easier to read; type with very tight default spacing requires more processing effort.

## The Typeface Categories I Use for Body Text

For long-form print, I work primarily in three categories:

**Humanist serifs** — typefaces based on humanist writing, with warmth and variation in their letterforms. These are the workhorses of editorial and book design. They read naturally because their structure echoes hand-written forms the eye has been trained on since childhood.

**Transitional and Old Style serifs** — classical serif designs with moderate contrast and well-considered spacing. A long history of successful use in book printing means these have been refined over centuries for exactly this purpose.

**Humanist sans-serifs** — for contexts where serifs feel inappropriate. More legible than geometric or grotesque sans for long reading because the humanist variations create enough differentiation between similar letterforms.

## Leading for Long-Form Text

Leading — the space between lines — has an enormous effect on reading comfort. Too tight and the eye has difficulty finding the next line. Too loose and the eye loses the thread of the text.

My starting point for body text leading: 120–130% of the type size. At 10pt body text, that's 12–13pt leading. I adjust from there based on the specific typeface (high x-height typefaces need more leading), the measure (longer lines need more leading), and the overall visual texture of the page.

## Measure (Line Length)

The optimal measure for comfortable reading is generally 45–75 characters per line. Too short and the eye returns to the margin too frequently. Too long and it struggles to find the beginning of the next line.

I calculate measure in characters for any body text setting and adjust column width or type size to hit the target range.

## Testing Choices

I set a substantial block of actual text — at least a full page — in any typeface I'm seriously considering for body use. I read it. Does it fatigue my eyes? Does anything in the letterforms snag my attention and interrupt reading?

If I notice the typeface while reading, it's not the right choice. Invisible is the goal.
