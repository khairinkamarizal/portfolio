## What Hierarchy Solves

A designed piece without hierarchy is a collection of equally weighted elements. The viewer's eye doesn't know where to start, what to pay attention to first, or how to sequence through the information. This creates cognitive friction — the viewer has to work to extract what matters.

Good hierarchy removes that friction. It tells the viewer where to look without requiring them to think about it.

## The Three-Level Model

Most brand communications work with three levels of hierarchy: primary (the most important message — the headline, the brand name, the key call to action), secondary (supporting information — subheadings, key body content, clarifying information), and tertiary (supporting detail — legal text, contact information, dates and logistics).

Each level should be visually distinct from the others. The transition from primary to secondary should be obvious. The transition from secondary to tertiary should be clear. If the levels blend into each other, the hierarchy isn't working.

## Tools for Creating Hierarchy

Hierarchy is created through contrast: contrast in size, weight, color, spacing, and typeface. Multiple contrast dimensions can be used simultaneously or independently.

The most reliable hierarchy tool is scale. A primary headline significantly larger than secondary text creates immediate priority. Color contrast can reinforce scale hierarchy or create exceptions to it. Weight contrast — bold versus regular — is useful for creating hierarchy at the same or similar scale.

## The Danger of Over-Hierarchizing

Adding too many hierarchy levels causes the same problem as having none — the viewer doesn't know what's important. If everything is emphasized, nothing is. I limit myself to three visually distinct levels in most pieces, and create gradations within those levels rather than introducing additional tiers.

## Hierarchy Across a Brand System

In a brand identity system, hierarchy needs to work consistently across all brand communications. The same typographic scale relationships, the same color usage for hierarchy, the same spacing conventions — these should be constant enough that a viewer builds pattern recognition across brand materials.

When hierarchy conventions are inconsistent across a brand's materials, each piece requires the viewer to re-orient from scratch. Consistent hierarchy conventions mean each new piece feels familiar and navigable immediately.

## Testing Hierarchy

I use the blur test: view a layout at low resolution or blurred enough that the content is illegible. What registers? The shapes and tones that remain visible under blur are what the visual hierarchy is actually communicating. If what registers under blur doesn't match what should be primary, the hierarchy needs work.
