Photography is often treated as an afterthought in brand identity work — the visual system gets designed, the guidelines get written, and then someone says "oh, and we should say something about photography." But photographic style is one of the most powerful and immediately perceived elements of a brand's visual identity.

## What Photographic Style Communicates

The choices that define photographic style — lighting, color grading, cropping, subject matter, casting, setting, styling — collectively produce a distinctive visual voice. That voice communicates: what this brand values, who it's for, and what relationship it wants with the audience.

A brand that photographs its products on clean white backgrounds communicates: precision, clarity, confidence in the product itself. A brand that photographs its products in environmental settings with people communicates: lifestyle, context, aspiration. Neither approach is wrong — they serve different brand personalities.

## Specifying Lighting

Lighting is the single most determinative factor in photographic style. Hard directional light (natural window light, rim light, harsh shadows) creates drama and tension. Soft diffuse light creates approachability and warmth. Studio strobe creates control and precision. Golden hour natural light creates romance and nostalgia.

I specify lighting approaches in brand guidelines rather than leaving them to individual photographers' preferences. The specification doesn't dictate exact setups — it defines the quality of light (direction, hardness, color temperature) and the mood it should produce.

## Color Grading as Brand Expression

Post-production color grading is as much a brand decision as typeface selection. A warm, desaturated grade with lifted shadows feels completely different from a clean, neutral grade or a cool, high-contrast grade.

I specify color grading as a set of parameters: overall color temperature, saturation level, shadow behavior, highlight rolloff, and any specific color channel treatments that define the brand's photographic register. These specifications can be built into a Lightroom preset or a LUT (look-up table) for consistent application.

## Casting and Subject Direction

Who appears in brand photography, and how they behave, is an identity decision. Casting should reflect the brand's audience and values — not just in demographic representation but in how the subjects are directed. Are they engaging with the camera or unaware of it? Are they active or contemplative? Are they idealized or naturalistic?

I include casting and subject direction notes in photography guidelines. "Subjects should appear engaged in activity, not posed, photographed in candid moments rather than looking at camera" is direction that shapes the feel of the photography fundamentally.

## Building the Shot List

For art-directing brand photography shoots, I build a shot list that covers the primary applications: hero images for the website, social media content, print materials, and a library of supporting images. The shot list specifies not just subject matter but format (landscape/portrait/square), mood, and usage context.

A well-built shot list ensures the photography session produces everything the brand needs, not just impressive individual images.
