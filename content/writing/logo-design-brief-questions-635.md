A bad brief produces bad work. I've learned this the expensive way — taking projects with vague briefs, making assumptions, producing work that missed the mark entirely, and having to start over. The time spent on a thorough brief is always recovered in the design phase.

Here are the questions I ask before I start any logo or identity project.

## About the Organization

What does the organization do, specifically? Not the elevator pitch — the actual activity. Who are their customers or audience? What problem do they solve for those people?

What is the organization's position in its market? Are they the premium option, the accessible option, the innovative option, the trusted established option? Position shapes visual language.

Who are the main competitors, and what do their visual identities look like? I want to understand the category's visual conventions — not to follow them blindly, but to make informed decisions about when to align and when to differentiate.

## About the Brand Personality

If the brand were a person, how would you describe them? Not their job — their character. Adjectives like "confident, warm, direct" are more useful than "professional, innovative, dynamic" (which describe almost every company's aspiration).

What tone of voice should the brand communicate? Authoritative? Approachable? Playful? Technical?

Are there brands — in any category, not necessarily direct competitors — whose visual identity the client admires? And critically: why? The "why" is more useful than the reference. It tells me what feeling they're actually after.

Are there visual styles they specifically don't want? This negative space is as useful as the positive.

## About the Applications

Where will this identity live? I need to know the actual deployment context before I design. A brand that lives on screen has different requirements than one that needs to work on embroidered workwear, vehicle signage, or packaging.

What's the first application that needs to be produced after the identity is delivered? This helps me prioritize which configurations to develop first.

Are there any hard constraints? Pre-existing brand colors that must be retained? A parent brand's typography system that must be respected? Assets that must be incorporated?

## About the Process

Who has final approval authority? This question matters more than clients expect. Projects that require consensus across a committee behave very differently than projects with a single decision-maker.

What's the timeline and what's driving it? Is there an event or launch that sets a hard deadline?

What's the budget? I ask this plainly. A brand identity project has a range of possible scope, and knowing the budget lets me advise on what level of work is realistic and where to focus.

## Why Briefs Fail

Most briefs fail because they describe what the client wants to look like rather than what the brand needs to communicate. "Modern, clean, professional" is a visual description, not a communication goal. "Trustworthy to first-time buyers in the premium home goods market" is a communication goal I can design toward.

I push every brief toward communication goals and away from aesthetic descriptions. The aesthetic decisions are mine to make once I understand the goal.
