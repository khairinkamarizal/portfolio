## What Typographic Color Means

In typography, "color" refers to the overall visual density of a block of text — how dark or light, how dense or airy, it appears on the page. This has nothing to do with the color of the ink. Black text on white paper still has typographic color, and controlling that color is one of the most important skills in print typography.

A page with good typographic color has an even, consistent visual texture. A page with poor typographic color has dark patches and light patches — areas that are too dense and areas that are too open — creating an uneven rhythm that makes reading uncomfortable without the reader knowing why.

## What Affects Typographic Color

Several factors combine to determine the typographic color of a text block:

**Typeface** — different typefaces have different inherent weights. A heavy typeface at 10pt reads darker than a light typeface at the same size. The x-height also matters: typefaces with large x-heights appear denser than those with small x-heights.

**Size** — larger type is darker than smaller type at the same weight. Obvious, but worth stating.

**Leading** — the space between lines. Tighter leading makes text darker and denser. Looser leading makes it lighter and more airy. Leading is probably the most powerful tool for adjusting typographic color.

**Tracking** — overall letter spacing. Tighter tracking makes text darker. Looser tracking makes it lighter.

**Measure** — the line length. Short measures create more frequent line breaks (hyphens, rags), which can create dark/light variation. Very long measures can create even color but at the cost of comfortable reading.

**Word spacing** — the space between words. Most default word spacing is slightly too loose for optimal typographic color in print. Reducing it slightly creates more even color.

## Evaluating Typographic Color

The classic technique: step back from the page until the text becomes unreadable — until it's just a gray texture. Is the texture even? Are there darker patches and lighter patches? Horizontal rivers of white cutting through the text?

Those irregularities are the problems. The solution depends on which factors created them.

Uneven color from hyphenation and justification issues? Adjust the H&J settings. Uneven color from varying line lengths in rag-set type? Adjust the line breaks manually. Color that's too dark overall? Increase leading. Too light? Decrease it or increase weight.

## Typographic Color in Hierarchy

Different levels of a typographic hierarchy have different appropriate colors. Body text should be relatively light and even — it needs to be read for extended periods. Headlines can be darker and more emphatic. Pull quotes can be lighter, more airy.

These density differences are part of how typographic hierarchy communicates. A headline that's dark and emphatic against body text that's lighter creates visual contrast that signals hierarchy before content is processed.

## Practical Application

I set and proof body text at actual print size before finalizing any layout. Typographic color can look completely different at 100% screen zoom versus printed output.

When the print output looks different from the screen version, I trust the print. The goal is a page that reads well in its final form, not a screen render that looks consistent.

Color on the page is felt before it's understood. Get the texture right.
