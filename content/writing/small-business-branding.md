Small business branding is not corporate identity work at a smaller scale. The problems are genuinely different, the constraints are different, and the priorities are different. Applying enterprise identity methodology to a two-person bakery produces expensive overkill in some areas and missed essentials in others.

I've worked with both, and the discipline switch matters.

## What Small Businesses Actually Need

A small business owner needs an identity that:

1. Works on the materials they'll actually produce in the next 12 months
2. Can be applied consistently without a design team
3. Looks professional in the digital contexts their customers encounter (social media, Google Maps, website header)
4. Doesn't require a 50-page brand manual to implement

What they usually don't need: a comprehensive visual language system, a photography style guide, a motion design language, or guidelines for contexts they'll never encounter.

Delivering a corporate identity system to a small business client is a disservice. It adds cost and complexity without adding value.

## Prioritizing the Right Deliverables

For small business brand projects, I prioritize ruthlessly:

**Must have:** Logo in all necessary color variants, color palette with hex and print specs, one primary typeface with usage guidance, business card design, social media profile assets.

**Often valuable:** A small set of supporting graphic elements, email signature template, a simple document template (letterhead or proposal).

**Rarely necessary:** Secondary typeface system, motion guidelines, extensive photography direction, comprehensive usage manual.

The deliverable set should match the client's actual production capacity, not the complete theoretical identity system.

## Budget-Appropriate Type Choices

Type licensing for a small business needs to be budget-conscious. A typeface that costs $800 for a desktop license might be appropriate for a corporation; it's not appropriate for a startup that needs to keep costs reasonable.

I maintain a list of high-quality, affordably licensed typefaces for small business work. Google Fonts has genuinely excellent options. Several high-quality independent foundries offer very reasonable small business licensing. The quality of the design isn't determined by the cost of the typeface.

## Making It Self-Sufficient

A small business brand system needs to be self-sufficient — able to be applied correctly by the business owner or a general-purpose freelancer without specialized design knowledge.

This means providing template files they can edit, not just specifications. It means the brand system is simple enough that the rules are easy to follow. It means the color palette is small enough that it can be memorized.

## The Relationship Matters

More than in corporate identity work, small business branding is a personal relationship. The brand identity is often deeply personal for the owner — it represents them, their work, and their investment. Taking that seriously, being responsive, and communicating clearly about what each decision is doing and why produces better outcomes than treating it as a smaller version of a standard identity project.
