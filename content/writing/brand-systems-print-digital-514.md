## The Range Problem

A brand identity lives everywhere. It's on a 2-inch business card, a 40-foot building sign, a 400-page annual report, a social media avatar, an embossed leather cover, a screen-printed tote bag. Each context has different constraints: resolution, color reproduction, size, substrate, viewing distance.

Most brand system problems I see come from identities designed for one context and forced into all the others. The logo that was designed for a website doesn't translate to foil stamping. The color palette defined in RGB turns into muddy disappointment in CMYK.

Designing for range from the beginning avoids all of this.

## Start With the Hardest Constraints

I design brand systems starting from the most constrained context, not the most glamorous one. That means thinking about black-and-white reproduction, small sizes, and one-color applications before I think about full-color large-format work.

If a mark works embossed on leather — one color, no fill, texture only through debossing — it will work everywhere. If it only works on screen with full color and transparency effects, it will fail half the contexts it encounters.

This approach produces systems that feel restrained at the premium end but reliable at the practical end. That's the correct trade-off.

## Color Across Media

Color is where most brand systems break down between print and digital. I manage this by defining the brand palette in terms of its source of truth and then translating carefully.

For print-first brands, I define the palette in Pantone and CMYK, then derive the RGB and Hex equivalents. For digital-first brands, I define in Hex/RGB and then work with a print professional to find the closest Pantone match.

These translations are never perfect. A Pantone color that looks rich and saturated in print can appear muted on screen. A neon digital color has no Pantone equivalent. I document these compromises explicitly so clients understand why the printed piece looks slightly different from the screen.

## Typography Across Print and Screen

The typefaces I specify for a brand system have to work in both environments. Not all typefaces do.

Some typefaces are designed for print and fall apart at screen sizes — their optical sizes and spacing are calibrated for high-resolution offset printing, and they become blurry or unreadable on lower-resolution screens.

Some digital-native typefaces lose their character when printed — they rely on screen rendering for their crispness and look generic in print.

I look for typefaces that were designed with both environments in mind, or I specify different typefaces for print and digital applications within the same brand — same family, different optical size versions.

## Building Flexibility Into the System

A scalable brand system needs flexibility in the right places and rigidity in the right places.

Rigidity where it matters: the primary mark, the core color palette, the typographic hierarchy. These don't change.

Flexibility where it's needed: layout, proportion, color application, secondary typography, image style. These adapt to context.

I document this explicitly in the brand guidelines. A system that has clear rules and clear flexibility is one that non-designers can execute correctly without asking for help on every application.

## The Stress Test

Before I deliver a brand identity, I stress test it across five contexts: a business card, a large-format sign, a one-color stamp, a dark background application, and a very small digital application like a favicon.

If it holds up across all five, the system is ready. If it breaks in any one of them, I go back and fix the root cause in the system — not patch the specific application.

Designing for range isn't harder than designing for one context. It just requires asking the harder questions at the start.
