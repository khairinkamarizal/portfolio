## The tagline is a typographic design problem

When a brand has a tagline — a short phrase that encapsulates its positioning or personality — integrating it into the visual identity is a specific typographic design challenge. The tagline needs to work with the logo and wordmark in a lockup, appear in body copy contexts, and sometimes stand alone as a headline treatment.

Each of these contexts has different requirements, and a tagline typographic system needs to address all of them.

## The lockup relationship

In a logo lockup that includes a tagline, the tagline sits below the wordmark in a clearly subordinate typographic role. The size relationship is critical: if the tagline is too large relative to the wordmark, it competes for attention and undermines the hierarchy. If it's too small, it becomes illegible at the sizes the lockup will commonly be used.

My standard starting point is the tagline at 25-35% of the wordmark cap height. At this scale, the tagline is clearly secondary but legible at reasonable reproduction sizes. I adjust from this starting point based on the specific letterforms and their optical relationships at lockup scale.

## Weight and style choices

The tagline typeface decision depends on the brand's overall typography system. For a brand with a serif wordmark and a sans-serif text face, the tagline might use the sans-serif at a light or regular weight — lighter than the wordmark to reinforce the hierarchy. For a brand with a single typeface system, the tagline uses the same face at a notably lighter weight or smaller size.

What I avoid: setting the tagline in the same weight as the wordmark, which creates visual competition. Setting it in a third typeface that's not part of the brand system, which creates inconsistency. Setting it at a scale where it crowds the wordmark rather than supporting it.

## When the tagline should be optional

Not every brand application needs the tagline. Brand guidelines should specify which contexts include the tagline lockup and which use the mark without it. Generally: formal stationery and brand introductions often include the tagline; repeat-use applications (website header, app icon, small-scale merchandise) should drop it.

Including the tagline in every lockup creates a system that's inflexible and often too complex. The tagline adds value when it's communicating positioning in a context where the viewer has attention to read it — not when it's crammed into a 40px logo on a website header.

## Taglines as typographic design moments

In marketing materials, the tagline often appears as a standalone headline treatment — set at display size with the full typographic system behind it. These treatments give the tagline room to breathe and communicate its full emotional register.

I design tagline typographic treatments with the same care I give to headline type: scale, weight, tracking, color, and spatial context all considered together. A well-set tagline at full display scale can be one of the most powerful typographic elements in the brand system.
