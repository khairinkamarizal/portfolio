## Moving type should say something the static type can't

Kinetic typography — text that moves — is everywhere in contemporary motion design. Title sequences, social media content, lyric videos, explainer animations. Most of it moves without purpose. The text animates because it can, not because the motion adds anything to the communication.

The question I ask before animating any text: what does the motion say that the static type doesn't? If I can't answer that, the text shouldn't move.

## Motion as semantic amplification

The strongest kinetic typography uses motion to amplify the meaning of the words. A word like "expand" that grows in scale. A word like "chaos" that moves unpredictably. A word like "impact" that hits the frame hard with no ease-out. The motion is a second layer of meaning on top of the verbal meaning.

This semantic relationship between word and motion is what separates kinetic typography that communicates from kinetic typography that merely decorates. When the motion contradicts the word — "rush" that animates slowly, "calm" that vibrates anxiously — it creates confusion. When it reinforces the word, it creates emphasis and memory.

## Reading speed and timing

The most common technical failure in kinetic typography is text that moves faster than it can be read. I've made this mistake. It feels dynamic in the edit, but viewers miss the content.

A rough rule: at display sizes (40px and above), allow at minimum 50 milliseconds per character for reading, plus a 200ms buffer for the phrase as a whole. For a five-character word, that's 450ms minimum hold time. Shorter than that and you're asking the viewer to read faster than comfortable.

For kinetic typography where the text is meant to be felt rather than fully read — backgrounds, texture layers, abstract visual elements — these rules don't apply. But for any text that carries communication load, readability timing matters.

## The choreography of phrases

When animating multiple lines or phrases in sequence, the transitions between them are as important as the individual animations. A hard cut between phrases creates urgency and discontinuity. A crossfade creates flow and continuity. A wipe creates directionality.

I map the choreography of a full kinetic typography piece before I animate any individual elements. This gives me a sense of the overall rhythm — whether the piece has a clear arc from energy to resolution, or whether it maintains a consistent intensity throughout. That arc is an emotional design decision, and making it consciously produces more coherent work.

## Type as pure visual form

The most experimental kinetic typography treats letterforms as pure visual shapes rather than linguistic objects — they move, deform, combine, and fragment as graphic elements. This is beautiful when done well, but it requires honest acknowledgment that the text has become image.

I'm careful about this boundary. The moment I'm designing letterforms as pure visual elements, the readability rules don't apply — but neither does the expectation that viewers will read the words. Mixing readable and purely visual text in the same composition creates a confusing register that serves neither goal.
