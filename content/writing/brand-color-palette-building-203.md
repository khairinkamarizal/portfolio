## Color is not decoration

I've reviewed a lot of brand guidelines documents over the years, and a common failure mode is the color section that lists four hex codes and calls it a system. Four colors on a page is not a system. A system is a set of rules for how those colors work together, when to use each one, and how they behave across different contexts.

Building a palette that actually functions across touchpoints — print, digital, environmental, merchandise — requires thinking about color relationships, not just color selection.

## Start with one anchor color

Every palette I build starts from a single anchor color. This is the color that will become most associated with the brand. It needs to be distinctive enough to own territory in its market — a color that isn't already saturating the category.

For a fintech startup I worked with, the category was dominated by navy blue and dark green. We went with a warm amber — unusual for finance, but intentional. Distinctive color creates recall. The anchor color is the one that should appear most frequently and carry the most brand equity over time.

## Warm it with a neutral

Once I have an anchor, I build out a neutral palette. Not gray out of a color picker — a neutral that's been warmed or cooled to harmonize with the anchor. A warm amber pairs better with a warm off-white and a soft tan than with a cold grey.

Neutrals do the heavy lifting in most applications. Body copy, backgrounds, divider lines — these are almost always neutral. If the neutrals clash with the anchor, the whole palette feels unresolved.

## Add one high-contrast accent

The accent color is the one that does the work of emphasis and call-to-action. It should be high contrast against both the anchor and the neutrals, and it should appear sparingly — no more than 10-15% of any given layout. When the accent appears too often, it loses its power to direct attention.

I usually pick the accent color last, after the anchor and neutrals are locked. At that point I have a clear view of what's missing — often it's a pop of contrast or a complementary hue that gives the palette energy.

## Test in context, not in swatches

The step most designers skip is testing the palette in actual layout applications before presenting it to a client. Swatches on a white background lie. Colors shift dramatically when you place them next to each other at scale.

I build a simple set of mock layouts — a business card, a letterhead, a social media post, a presentation slide — and apply the palette before finalizing it. This almost always reveals problems: the neutral background that turns greenish under certain print conditions, the accent that vibrates against the anchor at small sizes.

## Document the usage rules

The palette isn't finished when the colors are selected. It's finished when the usage rules are written. Which color is the primary background? Which is the primary text color? Can the anchor be used as a background, or only as an accent? Are there forbidden combinations?

These rules seem tedious to write, but they're what prevent the palette from fragmenting when multiple people apply it across different touchpoints six months after the initial project.
