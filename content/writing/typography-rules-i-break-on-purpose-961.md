## Rules Exist, Then They Get Broken

Typography has rules. Don't set long passages in all caps. Don't use more than two typefaces. Don't set text in very light colors on white. Don't use a typeface at sizes smaller than its intended use range.

These rules exist for good reasons and I follow them in contexts where they apply. In poster work, brand display applications, and editorial design, I break many of them regularly and deliberately. Here's which ones and why.

## All Caps for Emotional Emphasis

All-caps text is notoriously hard to read in running body copy. The uniform top profile of capitalized letters removes the word-shape silhouettes that skilled readers use to process text quickly. This is a real legibility problem for text that needs to be read comfortably over paragraphs.

At display scale, in short quantities, for high-emotional-intensity content, all caps does something different. It communicates that this text is meant to be received as impact, not read as narrative. It creates visual weight and authority that mixed-case doesn't.

I use all caps for event poster headlines, for brand taglines at large scale, for section dividers in editorial layouts. Never for anything longer than seven or eight words.

## More Than Two Typefaces

Two-typeface systems are easier to manage and more reliably coherent than three-typeface systems. That's true. It's also a rule that limits the expressive range available to more complex design problems.

For concert posters and cultural event materials, I sometimes use three or even four typefaces. The faces are chosen for strong visual contrast and each has a clear role — the headline face, the secondary information face, the detail face, the accent face. The composition controls the chaos that the multiple typefaces introduce.

The risk is real. A multi-typeface layout designed without discipline looks like a ransom note. With discipline, it looks like a sophisticated editorial statement.

## Mixing Type Sizes Within a Word

Setting letters within a single word at different sizes is a classic rule violation. It disrupts word recognition and forces the reader's eye to adjust mid-word.

In display contexts, this disruption is a feature. An oversized initial letter. A word where one syllable is emphasized at three times the scale of the rest. These treatments draw attention to specific words and create visual interest within a typographic composition.

I use within-word size mixing sparingly — usually once per composition, on the most important word or element.

## Extremely Light Type on Light Backgrounds

Low-contrast type is a readability and accessibility failure for functional text. For decorative type elements that serve a compositional rather than informational purpose, low-contrast type creates subtle visual texture without competing with the primary content.

A pale watermark-style typographic element behind primary content can establish depth and visual interest. The key distinction: this type is not meant to be read easily. It's a visual element that happens to be made of letterforms.

## Why I Know When to Stop Breaking Rules

The reason rule-breaking works when it works is that the underlying rule is clearly understood and the violation is clearly intentional. Random rule-breaking looks unskilled. Informed rule-breaking looks authored.

The test I apply: can I articulate why I'm breaking this rule in a way that references the brief, the audience, and the design's purpose? If yes, it's a deliberate decision. If I can't explain it, it's probably an accident I should fix.
