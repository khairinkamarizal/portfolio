## The Computer Closes Options

There's something that happens when I open Illustrator at the start of a logo project: the options narrow. The tool has a logic — points, paths, transformations — and that logic pulls my thinking into certain grooves. I start solving the problem in terms of what the software makes easy.

Sketching by hand has no such pull. The pencil goes wherever I direct it. The problem stays open longer because I'm not yet negotiating with a tool.

That difference in the generative phase produces more diverse concepts, faster.

## What Sketching Actually Does

Hand sketching externalizes thinking. The act of drawing something forces a level of commitment that thinking alone doesn't — I have to make the form concrete, even roughly, to evaluate it. And rough is exactly right at this stage.

A sketch takes thirty seconds. An Illustrator construction takes five minutes. In the time it takes me to build one digital iteration, I can sketch ten. That speed difference at the ideation phase matters enormously for the quality of the final result.

I also find that my hand generates forms my brain wouldn't consciously produce. The slight wobble of a drawn line, the unexpected proportion created by an imprecise hand movement — these accidents are often more interesting than my deliberate intentions, and I wouldn't encounter them digitally.

## How I Structure the Sketching Session

I set a timer for thirty to forty-five minutes and commit to filling several pages with thumbnail sketches before evaluating anything. The rule is quantity over quality: I'm not allowed to stop and develop a promising sketch at this stage.

Thumbnails are small — roughly postage-stamp size. At that scale, I'm evaluating overall form and silhouette, not detail. A logo lives in many sizes and the silhouette is what registers at the smallest ones. If a thumbnail doesn't have a strong silhouette at postage-stamp size, it won't work as a logo regardless of how well it's executed.

After the timed session, I step back and evaluate. I circle the thumbnails that have strong silhouettes and some quality worth developing. Usually three to five emerge.

## Moving to Digital

The move to digital happens once I have a direction worth investing time in. Not before. I'm using Illustrator to refine and build, not to generate.

I keep the sketch visible as a reference while I work digitally. The sketch captures something about the proportion and energy of the form that's easy to lose in the precision of vector construction. When the digital version starts to drift from the sketch's character, I go back and look at why.

## The Trap of Premature Digital Work

The most common mistake I see in student and junior designer work: going to digital too early and getting attached to a direction before the concept is strong. The digital work looks polished, so it feels finished. But polished execution of a weak concept is just a well-made mistake.

Sketching keeps concepts cheap long enough to kill the bad ones. Once I've gone digital, a concept has accumulated investment that makes it harder to abandon. I want to do my abandoning cheap, in pencil.

## A Practical Habit to Build

If you're not sketching by hand before going digital: start tomorrow. Set a rule that you'll fill at least two pages of thumbnails before opening any design application. Do it on every project for three months.

After those three months, compare the quality of your digital first drafts to where you started. The difference will be significant. The sketching isn't slowing you down — it's making the digital work faster and better by giving it direction.
