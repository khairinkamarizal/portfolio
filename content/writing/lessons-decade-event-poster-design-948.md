## Year One: I Thought Decoration Was Design

When I designed my first event posters, I treated design as decoration. I had the information — name, date, venue, ticket price — and I arranged it on a surface with some visual elements around it. The visual elements were chosen because they looked good to me. They weren't chosen for any strategic reason.

The posters were fine. They communicated the basic information. They didn't stop anyone in their tracks, and I had no framework for understanding why.

## The Hierarchy Revelation

The first thing that changed my poster work was understanding visual hierarchy as a deliberate system rather than an intuitive arrangement. I realized that every poster I made needed to answer one question first: what's the most important thing on this surface?

Not the most visually interesting thing. The most important thing. For an event poster, that's usually the artist or event name — the thing that makes someone stop.

Once I had a clear hierarchy, everything else followed from it. The second most important thing, the third. Each piece of information had a clear place in the sequence, and I designed for that sequence rather than for general visual appeal.

## Learning to Trust Simplicity

My early poster instinct was to add. More visual elements, more colors, more typographic variation. The result was always too busy — posters where the eye had nowhere to settle.

The turning point was a poster I designed for a small music venue with a very tight deadline. I had no time to be elaborate. I set the band name in large type, the venue and date in small type below it, and sent it. It was the most direct, simple thing I'd done.

It was also the best poster I'd made to that point. People told me so specifically. The simplicity wasn't a limitation — it was a design decision that I'd arrived at accidentally and then made consciously from then on.

## The Importance of Format Research

Early in my career I designed posters for the format that felt natural — usually A3 or standard US letter. I didn't research where the poster would actually be displayed or how large the printing run would be.

This led to posters that didn't fit the actual display context. A poster designed for a door would get displayed on a corkboard, scaled down, and lose all its impact. A design sized for standard print would need to be stretched for a banner, breaking all its proportions.

Now the format conversation happens at the brief stage. Where specifically will this be displayed? What sizes are available? Is this digital display, physical print, or both? The answers change every design decision.

## Typography as the Primary Tool

After a few years, I moved toward typography-dominant poster work and away from illustrative and photographic approaches. This was partly a strategic decision — typographic posters are faster to produce — but it was also a genuine aesthetic choice.

Letterforms are inherently graphic. A well-chosen typeface set boldly and with intention creates visual impact that no stock photograph can match. Typography at scale is dramatic in a way that other visual elements rarely achieve.

Working primarily in type forced me to develop a much deeper relationship with typefaces and font families. I know what each face I use does well and poorly. That knowledge makes every typographic poster faster and better.

## What Ten Years Has Made Me Sure Of

The fundamentals don't change. Hierarchy, contrast, white space, and a single dominant idea — these are the foundations of every successful poster I've ever made.

Everything else — style, aesthetic direction, technique — is expression layered on top of those fundamentals. The fundamentals are what I check when work isn't landing. Usually one of them is missing.
