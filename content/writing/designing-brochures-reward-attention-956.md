## The Brochure Nobody Reads

Most brochures don't get read. They get glanced at, assessed in about five seconds, and either kept for follow-up or discarded. Designing for that reality is different from designing for the optimistic assumption that every reader will work through the content page by page.

I design brochures to work at three engagement levels simultaneously. Level one: the five-second scan. Level two: the selective reader who reads headlines and captions. Level three: the committed reader who goes cover to cover.

## Designing for the Five-Second Scan

The first thing a brochure needs to do is establish its quality and relevance in five seconds. Cover design, opening spread impact, and the overall physical quality of the printed piece determine this.

Cover design is not a template problem. I design every brochure cover as a standalone piece — using the same level of compositional ambition I'd apply to a poster. A brochure cover should be compelling enough to make someone open it. That requires genuine design effort, not just placing the logo and a headline.

The opening spread (pages two and three for a traditional brochure) is the next critical moment. If the cover worked, the reader opened it. Now they need to be convinced to stay. The opening spread should establish the brand's visual register, communicate the primary message, and signal that there's something worth reading inside.

## Structure for Selective Reading

Most brochure readers are selective. They read headlines. They read pull quotes. They look at captions. They follow visual elements to the text they describe. They read body copy only when a headline or image has already captured their interest.

I design the selective reading path explicitly. Headlines are sized to be read without commitment to the body copy. Pull quotes are chosen to be the most compelling single sentence in each section. Captions describe what's in images rather than repeating what's in the adjacent body copy.

When the selective reading path tells a complete story, readers who never engage with body copy still come away with the key messages. Body copy deepens and supports; it doesn't carry the primary communication burden.

## Type System for Multi-Page Work

A brochure needs a complete type system: a headline face and size, a subheading treatment, a body copy treatment, a caption treatment, and a pull quote treatment. These need to be consistent across all pages and sections.

I set these as definitive specifications before beginning layout. Headline: X typeface, Y size, Z leading. Body: A typeface, B size, C leading. No deviations. Consistency in typographic treatment is what makes a multi-page document feel like a unified piece rather than a collection of individual pages.

## Pagination and Visual Rhythm

Page-to-page rhythm matters in multi-page print. A brochure that opens heavily on every spread feels exhausting. One that opens lightly on every spread feels empty. The best brochures alternate between expansive and dense spreads, between image-dominant and text-dominant pages.

I plan the page-to-page rhythm before detailed layout begins. A spread plan — a simple diagram showing the relative density and image/text balance of each spread — helps establish this rhythm at the structural level before the detail work begins.

## The Back Cover

The back cover of a brochure is the last thing a reader sees and the most common location for contact information. It's also frequently underdesigned, treated as leftover space after the interior has been designed.

I design the back cover as a deliberate conclusion to the piece. The brand's primary call to action, contact information set with care, and a visual element that echoes the cover design. The piece should feel finished, not forgotten, at the back.
