## Hierarchy Is a Contract With the Reader

When someone picks up a printed piece, they make an unconscious decision in the first few seconds: is this worth reading? Typography hierarchy is what answers that question before a single word is read. Strong hierarchy says yes, there's a clear path through this, follow me. Weak hierarchy says everything is equally important — which means nothing is.

I've designed everything from six-page restaurant menus to 48-page annual reports to single-sheet exhibition catalogs. The hierarchy problem is always the same, and the solution always comes back to the same fundamentals.

## Size, Weight, Color, Position

These are my four levers. Every typographic hierarchy decision I make uses at least one of them, usually two or three in combination.

Size is the most powerful. A headline set at 48pt against body copy at 10pt communicates a relationship that needs no explanation. But size alone gets crude quickly. Once you have more than three levels of hierarchy, size differences become too large to be elegant.

Weight adds nuance. A bold heading and a regular subheading can be the same size and still read in clear sequence. Weight contrast lets me compress the size range while maintaining clear differentiation.

Color adds both contrast and personality. I use it selectively — usually only one hierarchy level gets a color departure from the primary text color. Overusing color as a hierarchy signal creates visual noise and dilutes each application.

Position tells the reader where to start. The top-left of a page is where Western readers look first. I put the most important element where the eye naturally lands.

## The Rule of Three Sizes

For most print pieces, three type sizes is enough. A headline size, a subhead or pull quote size, and a body size. Every typographic element on the page should slot into one of those three sizes, with weight and color providing the fine-grained differentiation.

When I see layouts with seven different type sizes, I know the designer was making local decisions without thinking about the system. Every font size decision should reinforce the overall structure, not respond to the specific text in front of you.

## Leading and Measure

Hierarchy isn't just about size — it's about density. Tight leading and a long measure creates a heavy, demanding block of text. Generous leading and a shorter measure feels more approachable. I use these differences intentionally to signal to the reader what kind of engagement each section requires.

Pull quotes get generous leading and a short measure. Body copy gets standard leading and a comfortable measure. Captions get tight leading and whatever width the image dictates.

## Testing Hierarchy Before Sending to Print

My test is simple: I ask someone who hasn't seen the piece to tell me the three most important things on the page, in order. If they get it right, the hierarchy is working. If they hesitate or pick something I didn't intend, I have a problem to fix.

This test costs nothing and catches issues that I've become blind to after staring at the layout for hours.
