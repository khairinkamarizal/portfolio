## Photography Is Part of the Identity

Most brand identity projects deliver a logo, colors, and typography. Photography direction is treated as separate — something the marketing team handles, the social media manager decides on, or the stock library determines by availability.

This separation is one of the most common sources of visual brand fragmentation. The designed identity is tight and considered. The photography is inconsistent, ranging from warm and soft in one campaign to cold and clinical in the next. The brand looks like two different organizations.

Photography direction belongs in the identity system, designed with the same intention as every other brand element.

## Defining the Photography Personality

I develop photography direction by defining three qualities: subject, treatment, and emotional register.

Subject defines what the photography shows. For a professional services firm, it might be people in environments of focused work, with specific exclusions — no staged handshakes, no fake laughter over laptops. For a food brand, it might be ingredients and finished dishes without people, or always with hands present. Subject definition creates a consistent point of view.

Treatment defines how the photography is technically executed. Color temperature (warm, neutral, or cool). Contrast level (high contrast and punchy, or low contrast and soft). Depth of field (shallow and selective focus, or deep and environmental). Lighting style (hard directional light, soft diffuse light, natural available light). These technical decisions create the visual fingerprint of the brand's photography.

Emotional register defines the mood and feeling the photography should consistently produce. Aspirational and elevated. Warm and approachable. Clinical and authoritative. Raw and documentary. The emotional register tells the photographer what the images need to make people feel.

## Developing Moodboards

I develop photography moodboards as part of the identity project, not after it. The moodboard is a collection of reference images that show the desired subject, treatment, and emotional register in combination. It's a visual brief that any photographer can interpret.

The moodboard is specific enough to be actionable, and general enough to leave room for photographic creativity. It shows direction, not a template.

## Stock vs. Original Photography

Many brands can't afford original photography for all their needs. For these clients, I develop stock photography direction — specific search parameters and selection criteria for finding stock images that align with the brand.

The criteria are specific: color temperature range, lighting style to accept and reject, image subjects that fit the brand's world and those that don't, compositions that work with the brand's design system. A designer following these criteria should be able to find consistent-looking stock even across different sources.

## Directing a Photo Shoot

When I direct original photography for a brand, the photography moodboard and direction documents become the brief for the shoot. I share these with the photographer before the shoot, discuss them, and establish shared understanding of what success looks like.

On set, I evaluate each setup against the defined qualities. Is the color temperature right? Does the lighting match the treatment direction? Does the image feel like the right emotional register? I'm not directing every technical decision — that's the photographer's expertise. I'm evaluating against the brand criteria.

## Documentation in Guidelines

Photography direction in brand guidelines needs to show examples, not just describe principles. For each direction quality, I include positive examples (images that fit) and negative examples (images that don't). The negative examples are often more useful — they show the client and team the things that would break the visual consistency.

A section that says "avoid overly posed or staged imagery" is more actionable when paired with a real example of staged imagery labeled as "don't use this" alongside a natural, candid example labeled "this is right."
