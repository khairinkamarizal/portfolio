## The presentation is design work too

How you present brand identity work is as important as the work itself. I've seen genuinely excellent design rejected because it was presented poorly — without context, without the story that explained why the decisions were made. And I've seen mediocre work approved because it was presented confidently and contextually.

Good presentation isn't spin. It's the accurate communication of design thinking to people who weren't present for the process that produced it.

## Setting up the decision correctly

Before I show any visual work, I restate the brief. Not as a recap of what I was told to do — as a statement of the problem I was solving. "The challenge is distinguishing you from four competitors who all use blue and sans-serif in the financial services category, while maintaining the credibility signals that matter to your conservative client base."

This reframing serves two purposes. It confirms alignment on the problem before I reveal the solution. And it establishes the criteria by which the work should be evaluated — criteria the client agreed to before they're looking at the work.

## The strategic layer before the visual layer

I present the strategic rationale before I reveal the visual work. Brand positioning, differentiation strategy, target audience insights, competitive landscape — the thinking that preceded the design. When a client understands the strategy, they evaluate the design as an expression of the strategy rather than as a purely aesthetic choice.

This also makes revision conversations more productive. When a client says "I want it to feel warmer," the conversation can happen at the strategic level: "Warmer signals a different positioning than what we agreed on. Here's what that tradeoff looks like." Without the strategic context, design feedback becomes purely personal preference.

## Show one direction, not three

Presenting three directions at once sends an implicit message: I'm not sure which is right. It also spreads the presentation energy across three concepts rather than concentrating it on one strong concept. Multiple directions put the client in the position of choosing between things rather than evaluating whether a solution is right.

I present one direction — the one I believe most strongly is right — and I present it with complete conviction. If I have reservations about aspects of the solution, I state them proactively and explain how they're addressed. What I don't do is present "option A if you want more conservative" and "option B if you want more modern" — that's not strategy, it's hedging.

## In-context presentation

The mark shown against a white background is the least informative presentation of a brand identity. I always show the work in context: business cards, stationery, signage, social media mockups, packaging (if applicable). In-context presentation makes the work legible as a functioning identity system, not as an abstract design exercise.

These mockups don't need to be elaborate photorealistic renders. Clean, clear contextual applications are enough to communicate how the system functions.
