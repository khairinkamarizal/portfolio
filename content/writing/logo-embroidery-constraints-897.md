## Embroidery Is Not Printing

Designers who treat embroidery as just another output format get consistently poor results. Embroidery is fundamentally different from printing — it's a textile construction process using thread, and it has constraints that printing doesn't.

Understanding those constraints before designing saves time, money, and the frustration of a logo that looks great on screen and terrible on a hat.

## The Thread Width Problem

Embroidery thread has a fixed physical width. Fine detail that's clearly legible in a printed logo — a thin rule, a hairline serif, a delicate letter at small size — becomes a blob of merged stitches in embroidery. The machine can't produce strokes narrower than a certain stitch width, and trying to forces the digitizer to make compromises that degrade the design.

My rule: anything I can't reproduce clearly with a marker at 50mm wide is going to be a problem in embroidery. If it looks thick and blocky at that scale, it'll hold up. If it looks delicate and fine, it won't.

## Minimum Text Size

Text in embroidery has a practical minimum size below which letterforms stop being legible and become textured shapes. The exact minimum depends on the typeface and the stitch density, but a rough guide: lowercase letters smaller than about 6–7mm cap height are usually at risk. Sans-serif typefaces hold up better at small sizes than serifs.

For any brand where embroidery is a primary application — uniforms, caps, bags — I factor this minimum into the type size decisions throughout the identity, not just for embroidery-specific applications.

## Color Limitations

Embroidery thread colors are matched to manufacturer thread ranges, not to Pantone or CMYK values. The closest thread match to a brand color is rarely exact, and some colors (vivid oranges, certain greens, highly saturated pastels) have poor thread analogs.

When embroidery is a brand-critical application, I check thread color availability for the intended brand colors before finalizing the palette. Discovering at the embroidery stage that the brand's primary color has no good thread match is an avoidable problem.

## The Simplified Mark

Most identity systems that include embroidery applications need a simplified version of the primary mark specifically for embroidery use. This isn't the full mark with details removed — it's a redesign of the mark for the embroidery environment, which may mean:

- Eliminating very thin strokes
- Minimum text size adjustments
- Removing fine detail that won't survive thread reproduction
- Adjusting proportions for the embroidery field dimensions

I specify when this simplified version should be used in the brand guidelines, alongside the standard minimum-size guidance for the full mark. Embroidery gets the simplified version at embroidery scales; larger embroidered applications may handle the full mark.
