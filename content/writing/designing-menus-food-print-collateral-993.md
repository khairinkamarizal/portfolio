## The Menu Is the Brand

A restaurant menu is one of the most important brand touchpoints in hospitality. It's held for minutes, sometimes longer. It communicates the restaurant's personality, price point, and identity more completely than any other single piece of collateral. And it has to function perfectly — unclear menu design costs restaurants revenue.

I've designed menus for casual dining concepts, fine dining establishments, cafés, and food halls. The design priorities are consistent: legibility first, brand expression second, but neither at the expense of the other.

## Information Architecture Before Layout

Menu design starts with information architecture. How many categories? How many items per category? Are there modifiers, allergen notations, descriptions? What's the price structure?

These questions determine the spatial requirements of the menu before any design decisions are made. A menu with eight categories of eight items needs a fundamentally different structure than one with three categories of four items. I establish the content inventory before I establish the format.

## Format as Brand Signal

The physical format of the menu communicates immediately. A single large-format card for a simple concept. A multi-page bound menu for an extensive wine and food program. A clipboard with an insert for a casual concept that changes its menu daily. The format choice should be motivated by both content requirements and brand character.

I always argue against standard off-the-shelf menu folders for brands that care about design. The generic vinyl folder with inserted pages communicates chain restaurant regardless of what's inside. Custom format — even a simple custom-size card printed on excellent stock — immediately signals that this place cares about its details.

## Typography for Menu Legibility

Menus are read in challenging conditions: low restaurant lighting, distraction, social context. The typography needs to be more legible than standard print, not less.

My menu type standards: body copy no smaller than 9pt, ideally 10-11pt for item names and 8-9pt for descriptions. High contrast between type and background (dark type on light backgrounds, or carefully tested reversed type where the background is truly dark enough). Generous leading — I typically set menu body copy at 140-150% leading to prevent lines from running together in low light.

I also limit the number of typographic weights and sizes. Complex typographic hierarchy in a menu becomes impossible to read quickly. Three sizes maximum: category headers, item names, item descriptions. Clear visual separation between categories.

## The Price Design Problem

How prices are displayed communicates unconsciously. Right-aligned prices in a column create a visual column of numbers that research consistently shows leads diners to order by price rather than preference. Prices that appear as part of the item description — after a period or em dash — keep the focus on the food rather than the cost comparison.

For fine dining and any restaurant trying to create a premium experience, I integrate prices into the flow of the item description rather than creating a right-aligned price column.

## Durability and Production

Restaurant menus endure hard use. They're handled hundreds of times, exposed to grease, moisture, and heat, and dropped. The production specification needs to account for this.

For permanent menus: lamination or coating for paper pages, heavy board for single-card formats, bound books with protective covers for extensive menus. For daily-change concepts: durable inserts in a quality frame, or high-quality paper that telegraphs "premium temporary" rather than "cheap temporary."

The design should look as good at month six as at day one.
