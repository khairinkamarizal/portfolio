The best logo marks look like they couldn't have been drawn any other way. They feel inevitable — as if the idea and the form arrived together, fully formed. That quality rarely happens by accident. It comes from geometric construction.

I build almost every logo mark I design on a geometric skeleton. Not because geometry is fashionable (it is, but that's beside the point), but because geometric construction produces marks with an internal logic that holds up at any scale and in any application.

## What Geometric Construction Actually Means

I'm not talking about the trend of showing circles and lines behind a logo to imply mathematical precision. I'm talking about genuinely deriving the form from geometric relationships: proportions tied to √2 or the golden ratio, curves derived from circle arcs of specific radii, angles that are multiples of a base unit.

When a mark is built this way, every dimension has a reason. When a client asks "why is this angle 30 degrees and not 25?" the answer isn't "it looked right" — it's "because it's a multiple of the base grid unit, which creates visual consistency with the other angles in the mark."

## Starting With the Grid Unit

My construction process starts with defining a grid unit — typically the stroke width of the mark's thinnest element. Every other dimension is a multiple of this unit. This creates a visual consistency where all the parts feel like they belong to the same system, even if the viewer can't articulate why.

If the base unit is 4px, strokes might be 4, 8, or 12px. Radii might be 8, 16, or 24px. The increments create harmony without uniformity.

## Circle Arcs Over Bezier Curves

Whenever possible, I build curves from circle arcs rather than Bezier handles. Circle arcs have a mathematical purity that Bezier curves don't — you can define them completely with a center point and a radius, and they produce optically smooth curves that look consistent across all sizes.

Bezier curves require careful craft to look smooth. Circular arcs are smooth by definition. For a logo mark that will be used at everything from 16px to 6 meters, the difference matters.

## Testing Geometric Integrity

After building a mark, I test its geometric integrity by zooming in on every curve and junction. Curves should be smooth and consistent. Junctions should be optically precise, not just numerically aligned. And I always check that the construction works as a vector — that the paths are clean, the nodes are minimal, and the file is built in a way that a production artist could modify without breaking the geometric logic.

A logo built on geometry should be reproducible from its specifications. The spec sheet is part of the deliverable.

## When Geometry Serves the Concept

Geometric construction is a tool, not a style requirement. Organic, expressive marks sometimes need gestural construction — starting from a sketch and refining by eye. The question I ask is always whether the mark benefits from geometric precision or whether precision would undercut its character.

A craft brewery's mark might intentionally be slightly imperfect — humanity and irregularity are part of the brand story. A technology company's mark almost always benefits from geometric rigor. The construction method should serve the communication, not the designer's preference.
