## Social media is not broadcast television

When motion design moved from broadcast television to social media, many designers made the mistake of treating the new context as a smaller version of the old one. Same principles, smaller screen, shorter duration. This misses most of what's actually different about motion design for social media.

Social media motion exists in a radically different consumption context: no sound by default, tiny screen, infinite scroll, random entry point, extremely short attention window, and viewing in full daylight on a phone screen at arm's length. Each of these differences changes the design requirements substantially.

## Designing for silent viewing first

The most important adaptation for social media motion: design for no audio. The majority of social media video is viewed without sound. A motion piece that relies on music or voiceover to communicate its meaning will fail to communicate for most viewers.

I design social media motion to work completely silently. Information is communicated visually. Emotional register is communicated through color, pacing, and visual energy — not through music. Text is used to carry information that would be spoken in a broadcast context.

This doesn't mean audio doesn't matter — a piece that also works beautifully with sound is better than one that doesn't. But silent-first is the design requirement.

## The first-frame hook

In an infinite scroll feed, the first frame of your animation is the only frame most users will see. The animation only plays if the first frame stops the scroll. Designing the first frame as a hook — an image that demands the viewer keep watching — is as important as designing the animation itself.

I design first frames explicitly, asking: if this were a static image, would it stop a scroll? If the answer is no, I redesign the first frame before I continue with the animation.

## Format and aspect ratio planning

Social media has fragmented into multiple aspect ratios: square (1:1) for feed posts, vertical (9:16) for stories and reels, horizontal (16:9) for YouTube and some ad formats. A motion piece designed for one format rarely works unchanged in another.

I design social media motion with format adaptation in mind from the start. Key visual elements stay within the center safe zone that works across all aspect ratios. Critical text never touches the edges where it might be cropped. The composition reads as a square and as a vertical crop simultaneously.

## Duration and the attention curve

Social media platforms favor short duration: 6-15 seconds for most ad formats, 30 seconds maximum for most organic content. Within that window, the animation needs to establish context, communicate its message, and resolve — all with enough visual impact to justify the viewer's time.

I structure social media animations with the same discipline I'd use for a 30-second broadcast spot: a hook in the first 2 seconds, a middle section that delivers the message, and a clear end frame that completes the thought and (for commercial work) includes the brand mark and call to action.
