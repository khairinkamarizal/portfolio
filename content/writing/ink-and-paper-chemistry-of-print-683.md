Most designers treat paper as a container for ink. The right way to think about it is as a partner in a chemical and physical relationship that determines what the printed piece actually looks like. Understanding that relationship — even at a basic level — changes how you make paper and production decisions.

## How Ink Deposits on Paper

Offset lithography works through a process where ink is transferred from a plate to a blanket cylinder to the paper, building up layers for each color channel. The ink is a relatively viscous, oil-based material that deposits on the paper surface and then dries through oxidation and absorption.

How much the ink absorbs into the paper vs. staying on the surface is determined primarily by the coating status of the paper.

**Coated paper** has a mineral coating (clay, calcium carbonate) applied to fill the surface pores. Ink sits primarily on top of this coating and dries through oxidation, producing sharp dots with high color density and saturation. Coated paper is what makes photographic printing look vivid and detailed.

**Uncoated paper** has an open, fibrous surface. Ink deposits and then partially absorbs into the paper fibers through capillary action — a process called ink holdout. This absorption spreads the ink dot slightly (dot gain) and reduces the apparent density and saturation of the color. The result is softer, slightly muted colors with a different surface texture.

## Dot Gain

Dot gain is the increase in the size of printed halftone dots between the digital file and the printed result. All printing processes have some dot gain, but it's most significant on uncoated paper.

If a file specifies a 50% ink coverage for a tint, the printed result on uncoated paper might be 65-70% because the ink dot has spread. This is why uncoated paper colors look darker and duller than their screen representations — the dots are physically larger than specified.

Print suppliers compensate for dot gain through press calibration and color profiles. But when I'm preparing files for uncoated paper production, I'm aware that colors will shift and I test proofs on the actual stock.

## Paper Whiteness and Color Rendering

Paper "white" is not a single color. Paper whiteness varies from blue-white (optically brightened papers with UV fluorescent agents) to warm cream-yellow (natural uncoated papers without brightening agents). This baseline affects how every color looks printed on that paper.

A warm brand orange printed on blue-white paper looks different from the same specification printed on warm natural paper. The blue-white paper makes the orange appear cooler; the warm natural paper makes it appear warmer.

I account for paper baseline color when evaluating print color proofs. If I'm proofing on a different paper than the final production stock, the comparison is limited.

## Ink Chemistry Considerations

Most print jobs use standard offset inks, but there are contexts where ink chemistry matters:

**Food packaging:** Requires food-safe inks with specific formulations that won't migrate through packaging into food.

**UV-resistant inks:** For outdoor signage and packaging exposed to sunlight. Standard inks fade significantly with UV exposure; UV-resistant inks maintain color significantly longer.

**Metallic inks:** Aluminum pigment in the ink binder creates metallic appearance. Shine and metallic quality vary with the paper surface — coated papers produce better metallic results than uncoated.

**Fluorescent/neon inks:** Available as Pantone spot colors. These are genuinely outside the CMYK gamut and can only be achieved as spot inks, not simulated in process color.
