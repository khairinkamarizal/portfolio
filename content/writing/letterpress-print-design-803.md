## Touch Is Part of the Message

I've had clients ask me why a business card would cost four times the usual price. The answer is always the same: because when someone picks it up, they feel something. Letterpress printing debosses the paper surface — the ink doesn't just sit on top, it presses in. That physical quality communicates craft and intention before the reader has processed a single word.

In a world where almost everything is a screen, a card that has physical texture is remarkable. Remarkable gets remembered.

## What I Actually Send to Letterpress

Not everything belongs on a press. I use letterpress selectively:

- Business cards for clients where premium positioning is core to their brand
- Event invitations where the paper becomes a keepsake
- Limited-run posters where the impression itself is part of the collector appeal
- Certificates and awards where permanence matters

For anything high-volume or where cost is the primary constraint, I go offset or digital. Letterpress is a tool, not a religion.

## Designing for the Process

Letterpress has real constraints that change how I design. Fine hairlines collapse under press pressure. Reverse type out of dark ink gets muddy at small sizes. Dense areas of coverage can cause impression inconsistency.

This means my letterpress work tends toward:

- Bold, confident type at generous sizes
- High contrast between type and background
- Minimal elements — the process rewards restraint
- Paper selection as a real design decision

The paper is half the design. I typically spec something thick — 600gsm or heavier for cards, often cotton stock. The soft surface takes impression beautifully and the weight alone signals quality.

## Working with the Printer

The best letterpress printers I've worked with treat every job as a collaboration. I send them a file and we talk about what's achievable. They'll tell me when a font choice is going to cause problems, or when a color mix isn't going to behave the way I expect on the stock I've specified.

That relationship is worth cultivating. I've learned more about how type behaves under physical printing conditions from letterpress printers than from any design book.

## The Real Reason I Keep Coming Back

Honestly? It makes me slow down. Designing for letterpress forces deliberate decisions. Every element has to earn its place because the process costs too much to be casual. That discipline — the kind that comes from working within real physical constraints — makes the work better. And sometimes that rigor bleeds back into my digital work too.
