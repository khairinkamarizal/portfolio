There's a school of thought in brand design that says the wordmark is the safe choice — the fallback when you can't crack a mark. I think that's completely wrong. A great wordmark is harder to design than a great mark, it's more exposed, and it demands a level of typographic rigor that most designers aren't equipped to give it.

## What a Wordmark Actually Is

A wordmark is a logotype — the brand name set in a custom or carefully adapted typeface, with every letterform and spacing decision considered and locked. It is not a name typed in a font. The difference is everything.

When I design a wordmark, I start with a typeface as a structural foundation, then I pull it apart and rebuild it. I adjust the spacing between every letter pair optically, not metrically. I modify specific letterforms — a more distinctive terminal here, a slightly opened counter there — to make the wordmark feel custom rather than typeset.

The goal is a set of letters that form a unified, coherent whole that can only belong to this brand.

## Spacing Is the First Battleground

I've reviewed wordmark concepts from junior designers where the spacing was set in the software and never touched. Automatic kerning is a starting point, not a solution. Every letter pair in a wordmark needs to be set by eye, and the rhythm needs to feel intentional — either tight and locked, or open and airy, but consistent.

My process: set the word. Print it large. Walk away, come back, look at it fresh. Every gap that draws my eye is a spacing problem. Keep adjusting until the word reads as a single, unified object rather than a sequence of separate letters.

## Custom Letterforms vs. Off-the-Shelf Type

I can spot a wordmark built from an unmodified commercial typeface from across the room. The letterforms don't belong to the brand — they belong to the foundry. Anyone who buys that typeface can set the same word in the same face and have something that looks like the logo.

The level of customization I apply depends on budget and the brand's needs. At minimum, I'll modify the most distinctive letterforms — usually the first letter, and any letters with high frequency in the name. At full custom, I'll draw every letter from scratch if the brief demands something that doesn't exist in the typeface library.

## Testing Across Contexts

A wordmark has to live everywhere. I test every wordmark at:

- Large format (signage scale, top of a building)
- Medium format (business card, letterhead, packaging)
- Small format (favicon, social media profile icon, stamp)
- Reversed (white on dark)
- Single color (black only, or brand color only)
- Embossed (no color, only form)

If it fails any of these tests, something needs to change. Usually it's the weight — wordmarks need more optical weight than most designers initially give them, because they need to read at small sizes and in reversed contexts.

## The Longevity Question

Great wordmarks last decades. The ones that don't last are usually those that chased a typographic trend — the thin-weight-everything era of the early 2010s, the slab-serif revival before that. I think twice before designing anything that feels very now, because now becomes dated.

The wordmarks I look to as models — Coca-Cola, IBM, Levi's — are rooted in a consistent, ownable typographic voice. They've been refined over time, not redesigned. That's the goal.
