We don't remember colors in isolation. We remember them in relationship to other things — other colors, surfaces, contexts. "Tiffany blue" isn't a precise spectral description; it's a specific blue in a specific context with specific associations. The same hue on a different brand would carry none of those associations.

This has important implications for brand color design. You're not just choosing a color — you're choosing a relationship.

## The Ownership Question

The most strategically important question about a brand color is: can this brand own it in its category? Ownership isn't absolute — colors can't be trademarked — but it's perceptual. When consumers see that specific shade of red, do they think of Coca-Cola? When they see that particular yellow, do they think of Caterpillar?

Perceptual ownership is built through consistent, high-frequency exposure over time. A brand that uses its color consistently across every touchpoint for years begins to own it in its category. A brand that uses its color inconsistently or intermittently never builds that ownership.

## Color Combinations as Identity

Most brands are identified not by a single color but by a color combination. McDonald's is red and yellow. Ikea is blue and yellow. The combination is more distinctive than either color alone and harder for competitors to approach without the combination reading as derivative.

When I design brand color systems, I think about the primary combination — two or three colors that will frequently appear together — as the core identity signal. The primary combination should be tested against competitors in the category to ensure it doesn't read as derivative.

## Tonal Relationships

Within a color, tonal relationships create depth and sophistication. A brand that uses only the saturated primary color can feel flat and graphic. A brand that uses the primary color in combination with lighter tints and deeper shades has more visual range.

I design tonal relationships deliberately. The tint of the primary color often works better on large backgrounds than the saturated primary itself. The shade of the primary creates depth in shadows and supporting elements. The full saturated color serves as the accent — used sparingly for maximum impact.

## Color and Lighting Behavior

Brand colors appear in wildly varying lighting conditions: sunlit outdoor signage, fluorescent-lit retail environments, candlelit hospitality settings, backlit screens. Some colors shift dramatically across lighting conditions; others are more stable.

I test brand colors in multiple simulated lighting conditions before finalizing them. A deep teal that looks distinctive in natural light can look almost black under warm tungsten lighting. A bright orange that works beautifully on screen can look muddy under fluorescent retail lighting.

## Seasonal and Cultural Shifts

Color associations have seasonal dimensions. Certain greens feel like spring. Certain palettes feel autumnal. A brand color that works against its seasonal associations — a warm harvest gold for a brand that wants to feel fresh and current — will fight its context.

I'm not suggesting brands should shift their colors seasonally. But understanding these associations helps predict where a color will feel at home and where it will feel in tension with its environment.
