## Illustration as Brand Language

Photography and type can carry a brand identity, but illustration adds something neither of those does well: a distinctive visual voice that is fully ownable, infinitely scalable, and completely controllable.

A brand with a custom illustration style has a visual asset that no competitor can replicate without obvious imitation. The style belongs to the brand in a way that a typeface choice or a color palette never fully can — because those are available to anyone.

## When Illustration Makes Sense

Custom illustration style is worth the investment when:

**The brand needs to explain complex or abstract ideas visually.** Illustration can make the invisible tangible in ways photography can't. A financial services brand explaining portfolio diversification has no good photographic option. A well-designed illustration system can.

**The brand serves a creative or expressive category.** Publishers, cultural institutions, creative agencies, consumer brands in design-forward categories — these clients benefit from a distinctive visual voice that illustration provides.

**The brand needs warmth and humanity.** Illustration carries inherent warmth that photography often doesn't, especially when photography would require stock imagery. An illustration style tuned to a brand's personality can create emotional connection that stock photos never achieve.

## Defining the Style Parameters

An illustration style for a brand isn't just "the illustrations." It's a defined set of parameters that a range of illustrators could work within consistently. These parameters include:

- Line weight and stroke style (geometric vs. organic, thick vs. thin)
- Color application (full palette, limited palette, specific combinations)
- Level of detail and complexity
- Character representation if applicable (proportions, features)
- Perspective and viewpoint conventions
- What the style includes and explicitly excludes

A style that can only be executed by one illustrator is a vendor dependency, not a brand asset. Good illustration style documentation enables multiple illustrators to produce consistent work.

## Integration with the Broader System

The illustration style needs to feel like it belongs to the same brand as the typography and color. This means aligning the illustration's visual weight and personality with the typographic choices — a light, airy illustration style fighting with heavy compressed type creates visual incoherence.

I design illustration style and typographic system simultaneously, checking their compatibility throughout development rather than treating illustration as an add-on to a finished typographic system.

## The Scalability Problem

Custom illustration style is expensive to build and requires ongoing investment to maintain. Before recommending it to a client, I ask honestly: does this brand have the budget to maintain an illustration library over time, and does the illustration add enough value to justify the ongoing investment?

For many clients the honest answer is no. A well-chosen photography style and a clean typographic system may serve them better than an under-resourced illustration system that stagnates.

When the answer is yes — when the brand genuinely benefits and has the resources to maintain it — illustration style can be the most distinctive element in the entire identity system.
