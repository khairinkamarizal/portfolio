## The Case for Print in a Screen-Saturated World

Every few years someone declares print dead. Every few years, I walk past a wall plastered with posters and feel that declaration age badly. The poster is one of the oldest forms of mass communication we have, and it refuses to go away — not because designers are sentimental, but because it does something screens cannot.

A poster is a fixed, physical object. It doesn't compete for attention with notifications. It doesn't autoplay. It just stands there and demands you look at it. That's a design challenge I find genuinely exciting.

## What a Poster Forces You to Do

When I design for screens, I can layer in interactivity, hide complexity behind clicks, and rely on motion to carry meaning. A poster strips all that away. You get one surface, one moment of contact, and a viewer who may be walking past at speed.

That constraint is clarifying. Every element has to earn its place. The hierarchy has to be so clear that someone can absorb the core message in under two seconds. The color has to do emotional work. The typography has to be both readable at distance and interesting up close.

I've learned more about visual hierarchy from designing posters than from almost anything else.

## Scale Changes Everything

One thing digital work doesn't prepare you for is designing at true physical scale. A headline that looks bold on screen can feel timid at A1. A color that pops in your browser can go flat when printed on uncoated stock. You have to think about how ink sits on paper, how the human eye reads a surface from across a room.

I always print test sheets. Always. Even small proofs tell you things your monitor cannot.

## The Poster as a Cultural Object

There's also something worth acknowledging about the poster's cultural weight. A gig poster, a film poster, a protest poster — these become artifacts. People frame them, collect them, photograph them. The best ones outlive the events they were made for.

That's a different kind of stakes from a banner ad that runs for two weeks and gets replaced. When I'm working on a poster, I think about whether it could stand on its own as an object. Not just whether it communicates the message, but whether it's worth keeping.

## What I Keep Coming Back To

The discipline of poster design keeps me honest as a designer. It's too easy in digital work to over-complicate, to add one more element, to lean on animation to cover up weak composition. A poster doesn't let me hide.

I keep coming back to it because it makes me better at everything else.
