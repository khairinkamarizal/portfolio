## The Intimacy of Packaging

A customer holds packaging in their hands. They open it, handle it, keep it or discard it. This physical intimacy is something no other brand touchpoint achieves. A logo on a screen is seen. A product in your hands is experienced.

That difference in intimacy is why packaging is one of the most powerful branding investments a product company can make — and why getting it wrong has disproportionate consequences.

## Structure and Graphics as Partners

Packaging design operates at two levels simultaneously: structural (the form of the package — its shape, opening mechanism, materials) and graphic (the visual design applied to the structure).

The best packaging design treats these as integrated rather than sequential. The structural choices create constraints and opportunities that the graphic design should work with. A graphic design that ignores the structure it lives on produces work that fights its container.

## The Shelf Environment

Packaging is designed for a specific context: the shelf, the display, the retail environment. Understanding that environment before designing is essential. What's surrounding the product? What contrast is needed to stand out? How does the brand's shelf presence read as a family when multiple SKUs are displayed together?

I always research the retail context before designing packaging. A package designed in isolation misses the actual conditions where it has to perform.

## Material and Finish as Communication

The material a package is made from and the finish applied to it communicate before the design is read. Matte paperboard feels different from glossy plastic. Recycled kraft signals environmental values. Soft-touch laminate signals premium quality. Embossing and debossing add tactile richness.

These material choices are design decisions, not production afterthoughts. I specify them as part of the design, not as a post-design production question.

## Hierarchy in a Three-Dimensional Context

Unlike flat print design, packaging has multiple faces — the front, back, sides, top, and bottom. The hierarchy needs to work across all of them, with the primary selling face designed for maximum impact and the other faces structured to provide supporting information logically.

The hierarchy also has to account for the fact that the product may be displayed in different orientations — flat, upright, stacked — and the most important information needs to be visible in all common display positions.

## The Unboxing Experience

For brands where the product is shipped directly to consumers, the unboxing experience is a designed touchpoint. The inside of the packaging, the packing materials, the sequence of reveal — these can be designed to create a memorable moment that reinforces the brand.

This is not decoration. For premium and direct-to-consumer brands, the unboxing experience is a genuine differentiator.
