## Why Storyboard at All

The temptation in motion design work is to start animating as soon as you have a concept. The tools are good enough that you can produce results quickly, and there's a certain satisfaction in watching things move.

But problems that cost minutes to solve in a storyboard cost hours to solve in After Effects. Structure problems, timing problems, compositional problems — these are visible in a storyboard and invisible until late in the animation process. The storyboard is where you catch them cheaply.

## What a Motion Design Storyboard Contains

A storyboard for motion design work includes: a series of key frames showing the major states of the animation, annotations indicating what's moving and how, timing notes (approximate durations for each section), and direction arrows or other indicators showing movement direction and magnitude.

It doesn't need to be beautifully drawn. A clear storyboard communicates; a pretty one is nice but not required. The goal is to communicate the sequence and logic of the animation, not to produce finished artwork.

## The Animatic Step

After the storyboard, an animatic — a rough cut of the storyboard frames timed to the actual duration — adds temporal understanding. Seeing the frames progress at the actual timing reveals pacing problems that a static storyboard can't show.

I make animatics as a matter of course for any motion project longer than a few seconds. They're quick to produce and they surface problems that would otherwise only become visible in full animation.

## Getting Feedback on Structure Early

The best time to get client or creative director feedback on a motion piece is at the storyboard or animatic stage, not at the rough animation stage. At storyboard stage, changes cost almost nothing. At rough animation stage, changes may require rebuilding significant work.

I present storyboards as a standard client checkpoint. The conversation about structure and sequence should happen before I start animating.

## Storyboarding for Complex Sequences

For complex sequences — multi-scene motion pieces, explainer videos, title sequences — storyboarding is the primary organizing tool for the project. It establishes the visual language, the pacing rhythm, and the narrative structure before a single keyframe is set.

Working through the full sequence in storyboard form also reveals structural problems — a section that runs too long, a transition that doesn't work, a moment where the visual logic breaks — that would derail the production phase if discovered there.

## Building the Habit

Storyboarding requires time upfront. The payback is dramatically smoother production, fewer late revisions, and better final work. I've never regretted time spent storyboarding. I've often regretted skipping it.

Build the storyboard. Then animate.
