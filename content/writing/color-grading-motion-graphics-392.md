Color grading is the last step in most motion graphics workflows — which means it's the step that most often gets rushed or skipped. That's a mistake. Color grading is where the emotional register of a piece is finalized, and a motion graphic that isn't graded looks unfinished, regardless of how sophisticated the animation is.

## What Grading Does

Color grading adjusts the tonal and color characteristics of the rendered output. It can warm or cool the image, adjust contrast, lift shadows, pull down highlights, push specific color channels, and apply creative looks that couldn't be built into the original design.

The distinction between correction and grading: correction brings footage to a neutral baseline (correct exposure, neutral white balance, proper contrast). Grading applies an intentional look to that corrected baseline. Both are necessary.

## The Mood Vocabulary

Specific grading approaches carry consistent emotional associations:

Warm, lifted shadows with slightly desaturated highlights: nostalgic, intimate, slightly romantic. This is the Instagram filter family.

High contrast, cold shadows, vivid highlights: modern, high-energy, clinical. Common in tech and sports content.

Muted saturation across the full range, soft contrast: contemplative, premium, editorial. Common in luxury and documentary contexts.

Pushed color in the shadows (a color cast in the dark areas): cinematic depth. Orange shadows suggest warmth and narrative. Teal shadows suggest cool tension.

## Lumetri and After Effects

In After Effects, Lumetri Color is the primary grading tool. The workflow: correction in the Basic panel first (exposure, contrast, whites, blacks), then creative grading in the Creative panel (look, vibrance, saturation) and the Color Wheels (shadow/midtone/highlight color balance).

I apply Lumetri as an adjustment layer at the top of the composition stack. This means the grade applies to everything underneath — changes to the grade affect the whole piece simultaneously, not layer by layer.

## LUTs as Style Anchors

A LUT (Look-Up Table) is a preset color transformation that can be applied across multiple pieces of work for visual consistency. For motion brand systems, I create a custom LUT that encodes the brand's color style. Every piece in the system gets the brand LUT applied first, then individual adjustments.

This creates visual coherence across a body of work — the pieces look like they belong together even when they were produced by different people.

## The Export Test

Color grading is monitored on a calibrated display, but the output will be viewed on a wide range of uncalibrated consumer screens. I always test graded exports on multiple devices: a laptop screen, a phone, and ideally a television. What looks perfect on a graded monitor can look too dark or too warm on consumer displays.

Targeting a slightly lighter midtone range than looks correct on a calibrated monitor usually produces better results across the range of consumer displays.
