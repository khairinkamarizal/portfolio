A billboard is read from a moving vehicle at 60mph from a distance of 50–100 meters. The viewer has approximately two seconds. The constraints this creates are the most extreme in graphic design — more demanding than any other format I work in.

Designing for billboards has taught me more about hierarchy, legibility, and the essential communication of a message than any other format. When you strip away everything that doesn't survive those constraints, what's left is the irreducible core of the message.

## The Seven-Word Rule

The working rule in outdoor advertising: never put more than seven words on a billboard. Most effective billboards use fewer.

This isn't an arbitrary constraint. It's based on human reading speed, viewing angle, and the cognitive load of a moving viewer. Seven words is roughly what a viewer can read and process at highway speed in a two-second window while also processing driving information.

Breaking this rule consistently produces billboards nobody can read. You can fill the space with copy. The viewer will see a busy image and not read any of it.

## Visual Processing at Distance

At large scale and significant distance, the visual processing hierarchy is: shape first, then color, then text. The viewer sees the overall composition as a shape before they read anything. That shape needs to communicate the category and tone of the message before any text is processed.

A billboard for a fast food brand should read as fast food before the viewer reads a word. The color, the composition, the image — all of it should signal the category. The text confirms the specific message.

## Scale Changes Everything

Elements that look correct in a standard layout comps fail at billboard scale. Thin type disappears. Fine details in images read as texture noise at distance. Subtle color differences collapse. Light values become invisible against sky glare.

I design billboard comps at a scaled-down ratio but evaluate them at the ratio that simulates the viewing distance. If a billboard is 14 feet tall and viewed from 100 meters away, I scale the comp to the relative visual angle and check legibility. What reads clearly at that scale is what survives.

Type sizes that work: headlines at minimum 36 inches physical height for a standard 14'x48' billboard. Secondary information at no smaller than 18 inches. Any smaller and it becomes unreliable at typical viewing distances.

## Contrast for Outdoor

Outdoor environments have variable lighting conditions: full sun, overcast, dawn, dusk, artificial lighting at night. A billboard design needs to work across this entire range.

High contrast is mandatory. Dark on light, or light on dark, with no ambiguity. Pastel-on-pastel color combinations that might work in a controlled interior environment fail outdoors where contrast is the primary visibility factor.

For illuminated billboards (vinyl lit from behind or LED digital), color can be more saturated and luminous than in print. Dark backgrounds work because the illumination creates its own contrast with the environment. For non-illuminated vinyls, I'm cautious about dark backgrounds that may underperform in low-light conditions.

## The Phone Capture Problem

Many viewers who notice a billboard will photograph it on their phone. That photograph will be viewed on screen, shared, printed. Designing for the phone photograph as a secondary deliverable is increasingly sensible.

A billboard that reads clearly in a phone photograph taken from a moving car window is a well-designed billboard.

## Legibility Checklist

Before I submit a billboard design:
- Can it be read in under three seconds by someone unfamiliar with it?
- Does it work at the simulated viewing distance?
- Does the hierarchy communicate the most important message first?
- Are there fewer than eight words?
- Does it work in strong direct sunlight?
- Does it work on a cloudy day at reduced contrast?

If yes to all: submit. If no to any: revise.
