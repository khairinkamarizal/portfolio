## The Diminishing but Important Form

Annual reports are produced in smaller numbers than they once were, and increasingly in digital formats. But the premium printed annual report — where it still exists — remains one of the most demanding and rewarding print design projects.

When a company commits to a high-quality printed annual report, they're saying something about their relationship to materiality and permanence. The design should live up to that commitment.

## The Strategic and Narrative Structure

An annual report is a narrative document — it tells a story about the organization's year. That narrative structure should be visible in the design. The report isn't a data dump; it's an argument for why the organization is worth believing in.

I work with the writer and strategy team to understand the narrative arc before designing anything. The structure of sections, the sequencing of information, the placement of key data points — these are design decisions that need to be made in partnership with the content.

## Integrating Data Visualization

Annual reports are full of numbers. How those numbers are visualized is a design decision with significant communication consequences. A data visualization that accurately represents the data but is visually dull is a wasted opportunity. A visualization that looks beautiful but misrepresents the data is unacceptable.

The standard is accuracy first, clarity second, visual quality third. These can almost always coexist.

## Paper and Printing Choices as Expression

For premium printed reports, paper and printing choices are genuine design elements. Different stocks for different sections create physical differentiation that communicates section weight. Special finishes — spot gloss varnish, soft-touch laminate, foil — add sensory richness that digital can't replicate.

Every special treatment should be justified by the communication goal. Finish for its own sake feels arbitrary. Finish that reinforces the message feels inevitable.

## The Cover as Object

The cover of a printed annual report is a designed object that people will handle and observe before reading a word. It needs to function as a standalone piece — to communicate something essential about the organization and to feel worth opening.

I spend disproportionate time on the cover relative to its page count. The first impression is too important to treat as one element among many.

## Digital Companion

Most printed annual reports now have digital counterparts — PDF versions, interactive web versions, or both. Designing these in parallel rather than as afterthoughts ensures that the digital version is genuinely appropriate for its medium rather than a scan-like reproduction of the print version.

Digital and print versions of the same content should share a visual language but make format-appropriate choices.
