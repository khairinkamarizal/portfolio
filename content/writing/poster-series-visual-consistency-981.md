## Series Design Is Systems Design

A single poster is a composition problem. A series of posters is a systems problem. The individual poster must be excellent on its own terms. The series must be coherent as a set while remaining visually interesting across all its entries.

Getting this balance right — enough consistency to feel systematic, enough variation to stay interesting — is the core challenge of poster series design.

## The Fixed vs. Variable Framework

I design every poster series around an explicit fixed/variable framework. Some elements are fixed across all posters in the series: they establish the visual identity of the series itself. Other elements vary from poster to poster: they distinguish individual entries and prevent the series from feeling repetitive.

The fixed elements create cohesion. The variable elements create interest.

Typical fixed elements: overall format and proportions, primary typeface and size for consistent information (date, venue, series title), color palette or palette structure, grid system and margins, logo or series mark position.

Typical variable elements: featured color or color combination within the palette structure, featured imagery or typographic treatment for each specific event, secondary typographic elements specific to each entry's content.

## The Series Mark

Many poster series benefit from a series mark — a visual element that appears consistently on all posters in the series and identifies them as belonging together. This might be a wordmark for the series title, a graphic device, or a structural element that functions as a visual frame or signature.

The series mark should be distinct from the organizing institution's logo while relating to it. It operates at the series level, below the institution level but above the individual event level in the identity hierarchy.

## Color Systems for Series

A well-designed poster series uses color variation in a systematic way. The most common approach: a fixed neutral or anchor color that appears on every poster, plus a variable accent color that's distinct for each entry.

This system creates immediate visual differentiation between posters while making them clearly related. At a glance, you can see they're from the same series. You can also identify the individual poster by its specific color combination.

The variable accent colors need to be chosen as a set, not individually. They should work together when posters are displayed side by side, creating a palette that's coherent as a collection.

## Typography Across a Series

Typographic variation within a series can be a powerful differentiator if the variation is structural rather than stylistic. The same type family at different weights, or the same compositional structure with different text content creating different visual rhythms, creates variation within clear formal consistency.

What doesn't work: changing typefaces from poster to poster. A series needs a committed typographic identity. Typeface variation undermines the sense that these posters come from the same authorial voice.

## Designing the Series Before the First Poster

A mistake I see often: designing one poster and then trying to make a series from it afterward. The series structure needs to be designed before the first individual poster is finalized.

I work out the fixed/variable framework, the color system, the typographic approach, and the grid structure as a series design document. Then I design three posters simultaneously — the first, one in the middle, and one at the end — to test whether the system works across the range of the series. Only after this system test do I finalize the individual posters.

This upfront investment saves significant rework when a system developed for one poster turns out not to scale to the others.
