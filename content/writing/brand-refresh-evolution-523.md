## The Worst Thing You Can Do to an Established Brand

Walk in and redesign everything from scratch.

I've seen it happen. A new marketing director arrives, hires a new agency, and announces that the brand needs a "fresh start." Six months later, the brand has a completely new logo, a completely new palette, and completely new typography. Existing customers don't recognize it. The brand equity built over ten years is gone.

This is a failure of strategic thinking masquerading as creative boldness.

## What a Brand Refresh Actually Is

A brand refresh is a targeted intervention. You identify what's working — what has equity, what audiences recognize, what communicates correctly — and you preserve it. You identify what's broken — what reads as dated, what doesn't reproduce well, what contradicts the brand strategy — and you fix it.

The result is a brand that feels current and coherent while maintaining continuity with its history. To loyal customers, it looks like the brand grew up. To new customers, it looks like a confident, considered identity.

## How I Assess What to Keep

I start a refresh project with an audit. I collect every use of the brand I can find — business cards, website screenshots, signage photos, printed materials, merchandise. I look at what actually exists, not what the brand guidelines say should exist.

Then I ask three questions about each element of the identity:

Does it still communicate what the brand stands for? Some elements were designed for a version of the brand that no longer exists. Those can go.

Does it have audience recognition? If a specific element — a color, a shape, a typeface — is something loyal customers associate with the brand, it has equity. Changing it has a cost.

Does it work technically? A logo designed in 2003 might not scale to digital environments correctly. A typeface that looked contemporary then might read as dated now. Technical failures are worth fixing regardless of recognition.

## The Logo Continuity Problem

Logo refreshes require the most care. Audiences recognize logos at a near-unconscious level. Small changes preserve recognition. Large changes break it.

The principle I follow: if you can show the before and after side by side and a loyal customer says "same thing, but better," you've succeeded. If they say "what happened to your logo?" you've gone too far.

This means cleaning up construction inconsistencies, improving scalability, updating proportions to feel more contemporary — while preserving the essential character of the mark. The silhouette should be recognizable. The personality should be continuous.

## Typography and Color Are More Flexible

Typography and color can absorb more change than the logo because they operate more in the background of brand recognition. Audiences feel the brand's typography and color more than they consciously notice it.

This is where I make more significant interventions in a refresh. Updating a dated font to a contemporary typeface in the same genre. Adjusting color values to work better in digital environments. Adding secondary colors that give the system more range.

These changes modernize the brand without disrupting the top-level identity.

## Presenting a Refresh

When I present a brand refresh, I show it in context — existing touchpoints like a business card or website header, shown in the old system and the new system side by side.

The context makes two things clear: how much the brand has improved, and how much continuity has been maintained. Both matter. The client needs to trust that the update is an evolution, not an erasure.

The goal is always: recognizably the same brand, clearly better.
