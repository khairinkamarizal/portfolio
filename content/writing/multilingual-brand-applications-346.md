Designing brand identity systems that work across multiple writing systems is one of the most technically demanding areas of typographic design. Latin letterforms, Arabic script, Chinese characters, Devanagari, and Cyrillic all have different optical properties, different proportional systems, and different design conventions. Making them feel like the same brand requires both technical knowledge and design judgment.

## The Cross-Script Harmony Problem

The fundamental challenge is creating visual harmony between scripts that have completely different formal properties. Latin letterforms sit on a baseline with ascenders and descenders. Arabic script runs right-to-left with connected letterforms and no uppercase/lowercase distinction. Chinese characters fill a square optical space with no baseline system.

Harmony across scripts is achieved through shared optical weight, shared spatial density, and proportional relationships between the type sizes used for each script. It is not achieved by trying to make one script look like another — that approach produces letterforms that are wrong in both scripts.

## Type Selection for Multi-Script Systems

The foundation of a multi-script typographic system is choosing type families that were designed to work together across scripts. Several foundries have invested in multi-script type families: Monotype's Neue Frutiger has Arabic, Hebrew, and Devanagari companions. Google Fonts' Noto project provides consistent optical weights across all major scripts.

I research type families that were designed with cross-script harmony in mind rather than choosing the best Latin face and then finding the least-bad Arabic companion. The optical weight matching in purpose-built multi-script families is far more accurate.

## Size Calibration

At the same nominal point size, different scripts appear at different optical sizes. Arabic at 12pt reads as significantly smaller than Latin at 12pt, largely because of Arabic's more delicate stroke structure and smaller x-height equivalent. Chinese at 12pt appears larger than Latin at the same size.

I calibrate sizes visually for equal optical weight rather than using identical point sizes. In practice, this often means Arabic running 1–2 points larger than the Latin equivalent, and Chinese running 1 point smaller.

## Brand Mark Translation

When a wordmark needs to exist in a non-Latin script, the challenge is creating a version that feels like the same brand — same weight, same character, same positioning — while being correct in the target script.

This work requires collaboration with a typographer who specializes in the target script. The Latin wordmark designer often doesn't have the deep knowledge of the target script's conventions to make good judgments about what feels native and what feels wrong.

## Testing With Native Readers

A multi-script system should be tested with native readers of each script. What looks correct to a Latin typographer may feel wrong or awkward to an Arabic reader or a Hindi speaker. Native reader feedback is not optional; it's the only reliable test for whether the non-Latin components of the system actually work.
