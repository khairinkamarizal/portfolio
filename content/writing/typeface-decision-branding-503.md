## Type Is Voice

When I pick a typeface for a brand, I'm not decorating. I'm casting a voice. The letterforms carry personality before a single word is read. A client who says "just pick something clean" is actually asking me to make a very specific personality decision on their behalf — and I take that seriously.

I've been burned by typeface choices that seemed safe and turned out to be wrong. A geometric sans that felt modern in isolation but cold when applied to a healthcare brand. A serif that looked authoritative in a pitch deck but stuffy when the brand matured. Type decisions compound over time, so getting them right early matters enormously.

## The Four Questions I Always Ask

Before I open a type library, I answer four questions about the brand:

**What is the brand's temperament?** Warm or cool. Playful or serious. Familiar or aspirational. This narrows the field dramatically.

**What is the brand's era?** Not just "timeless" — that's a dodge. Every typeface has a historical moment baked into its DNA. Knowing whether the brand leans classic, contemporary, or forward-looking tells me where in history to look.

**What does the brand say most often?** A brand that lives in long-form editorial text needs something completely different from one that appears primarily in headlines and display settings.

**Who are the competitors?** I want to know the category conventions so I can either meet them or deliberately break them.

## Categories I Actually Use

I don't organize typefaces by classification the way textbooks do. I organize them by feeling.

**Authority serifs** — these have been around a long time and carry weight. Think of old-style and transitional typefaces. Good for legal, finance, heritage brands.

**Contemporary serifs** — optical size designs, high contrast, slightly editorial. Good for fashion, publishing, premium consumer goods.

**Humanist sans** — the workhorse. Warm, legible, approachable. A safe choice that can still be a good choice if the brand warrants it.

**Geometric sans** — precise, minimal, tech-adjacent. Often overused in startups. When it's right, it's very right.

**Grotesques** — my personal favorite category. Neutral but characterful. The best ones have quirks that reward close reading.

## Pairing Without Overthinking

I rarely use more than two typefaces in a brand system. One for display, one for text. Sometimes the same family handles both at different weights and sizes.

When I do pair, I look for contrast in category but harmony in era. A contemporary serif headline paired with a humanist sans body is a reliable combination because they feel like they're from the same cultural moment even though they're structurally different.

## The Test I Run on Every Choice

I set the brand's actual copy in the typeface at the sizes it'll actually be used. Not a generic Lorem ipsum paragraph. The real words the brand uses every day.

If the typeface makes that copy feel true, I keep it. If it feels like a costume, I cut it and move on.

Typefaces aren't decorations. They're decisions. Make them deliberately.
