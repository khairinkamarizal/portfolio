## Title sequences set the emotional contract

A title sequence for a live event or conference does one thing above everything else: it sets the emotional contract with the audience. Before a speaker walks on stage, before a presentation begins, the title sequence tells the audience what register they're in — whether this is serious and weighty or playful and energetic, intimate or epic, formal or casual.

Getting this wrong undermines everything that follows. A title sequence that's tonally mismatched to the event it introduces creates a jarring transition that the audience spends the first minutes of the event recovering from.

## The asset inventory problem

Title sequences for events need to work with a collection of assets that weren't designed for motion: logos in PDF format, photography of speakers in mixed quality, sponsor logos in whatever format the sponsors provided. The design challenge is building a motion system that can accommodate these imperfect inputs and produce a coherent visual experience.

My approach: build the sequence around a motion system — a set of typographic and graphical elements that are fully designed and controlled — and treat photography and external assets as content that slots into that system. The system provides quality consistency; the content provides specificity.

## Looping vs. linear sequences

Event title sequences typically need two modes: a pre-show loop that plays on the screen while the audience is arriving (sometimes for 30-60 minutes), and a main title sequence that plays immediately before the opening of the event (typically 60-90 seconds).

The pre-show loop needs to work at ambient attention levels — it's background visual content while the audience is talking, finding seats, settling in. It should be calming rather than demanding. The main title sequence needs to demand attention and build to an emotional peak.

Designing both modes with coherent visual language but different pacing and energy levels requires deliberate planning from the start of the project.

## The speaker introduction template

Most conferences need a speaker introduction graphic — a lower-third or full-screen treatment that introduces each speaker by name and title. These need to be consistent, fast to produce (conference producers often have speaker information changing days before the event), and clearly readable on various screen sizes from close seating to back-of-room viewing.

I design speaker introduction templates as proper motion design assets with animation, not as static graphics with text fields. The animation elevates the presentation quality and distinguishes professional event production from amateur.

## Technical delivery requirements

Event title sequences are typically played through a presentation system (Resolume, Disguise, or simply PowerPoint/Keynote) by an AV technician who may not be a designer. Delivery formats need to accommodate this: H.264 video files with specific codec and bitrate requirements, clearly labeled and organized files, and simple playback instructions.

I always deliver event motion design with a technical rider — a document specifying playback requirements, file organization, and any special instructions. This prevents last-minute problems when I'm not on site.
