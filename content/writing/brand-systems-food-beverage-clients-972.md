## Food Brands Live and Die on Shelf

The food and beverage sector is one of the most visually competitive environments in brand design. A brand on a supermarket shelf competes with dozens of category competitors for three seconds of shopper attention. The visual system doesn't just represent the brand — it's doing frontline sales work.

I've designed brand identities and packaging systems for craft beverage companies, artisan food producers, restaurant groups, and specialty food retailers. The constraints are consistent: maximum shelf impact, clear appetite appeal, and a visual system that communicates the brand's quality story at a glance.

## The Appetite Appeal Problem

Every food brand design has to answer the appetite appeal question: does this make the product look and feel desirable? This sounds obvious, but it's easy to get wrong when you're deep in the execution of a sophisticated visual system.

I test appetite appeal with non-designer audiences. I show the packaging or brand materials to people without design training and ask: does this make you want to try this product? The responses are direct and useful in a way that design-peer feedback isn't.

Appetite appeal isn't just about the food photography or product illustration. It's about whether the overall visual register — color, type, material quality — communicates that this is something worth eating or drinking. A brand that feels cold and clinical doesn't make food feel desirable regardless of how good the product imagery is.

## Color as the Primary Category Signal

In most food categories, color does more immediate work than any other design element. Color communicates category (a green palette for health-focused products, warm earthy tones for craft and artisanal, bold brights for snacks and convenience foods), price position (premium palettes use restraint; value palettes use density and primary colors), and brand personality.

I research the competitive color landscape for every food category I work in. Color that's distinctive in the competitive set creates shelf differentiation. Color that blends in with the category norm creates invisibility.

## The Flavor and Variant System

Most food and beverage brands have multiple products or flavors. The visual system needs to accommodate variants while maintaining brand coherence. This requires a structure that the brand can populate with new variants without external design involvement every time.

I design variant systems where the primary brand identity elements (logo, structural layout, primary type treatment) are fixed, and a single variable element — usually color, flavor illustration, or flavor name type treatment — differentiates variants.

The system needs to be simple enough for non-designers to follow and distinctive enough that each variant feels specific rather than generic.

## Typography That Works at Package Scale

Food packaging typography needs to work at very small sizes (ingredient lists, nutritional information) and very large sizes (hero brand name across the front panel). The type system must be tested and optimized for both extremes.

I almost never use a single typeface across all packaging typographic roles. The brand name usually gets a distinctive display treatment. Callout copy (organic, artisanal, fresh, etc.) gets a secondary face that complements the brand name treatment. Body copy (ingredients, nutrition, instructions) gets a functional, highly legible face optimized for small sizes.

## Regulatory Requirements as Design Constraints

Food packaging is heavily regulated. Nutritional information must be presented in specific formats. Allergen information must meet legibility and size requirements. Country-of-origin labeling, weight declarations, and certification marks all have mandatory specifications.

I familiarize myself with the applicable regulatory requirements at the start of every food packaging project. These requirements aren't constraints on the design — they're the design parameters within which the creative work happens. Ignoring them and addressing compliance at the end of the project produces expensive revisions and sometimes delays to market.
