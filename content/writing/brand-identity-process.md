# The Brand Identity Design Process

Brand identity work has a reputation for being mystical — the designer retreats for a week and emerges with a logo. The reality is more methodical. A good identity process is structured enough to be reliable and open enough to let good ideas emerge. Here's how I run mine.

## The Brief

Everything starts with a brief, and a good brief is worth more than anything that follows. Before touching Figma, I need to understand:

- What does this organisation do, and for whom?
- What do they want people to feel when they encounter the brand?
- Who are they competing with, and how do they want to be differentiated?
- Where will the identity live — digital, print, merchandise, signage?
- What does success look like, and how will it be measured?

I send clients a questionnaire before our first call. Not because the answers are always accurate — clients often discover what they think during the process — but because making them articulate their position in writing surfaces assumptions early. Surprises at the end of a project are expensive.

## Research and Audit

With a brief in hand, I spend time understanding the landscape. This means:

**Competitive audit** — reviewing the identities of direct and indirect competitors. Not to avoid copying, but to understand the visual language of the category. Fintech tends toward blues and geometric sans-serifs. Health brands reach for greens and soft curves. Fashion favors black and minimalism. These conventions exist for reasons — clients can choose to work within them or against them, but they should make that choice consciously.

**Reference gathering** — building a mood board that captures the emotional territory the brand should occupy. I use a broad net here: architecture, photography, fashion, editorial design. The goal is to establish a feeling, not a style.

**Audience research** — understanding who the brand is actually speaking to. A brand for 55-year-old financial advisors should feel different from one targeting 22-year-old creators, even if both are in "professional services."

## Concept Development

This is where the actual design work begins. I typically develop two or three distinct directions — not variations on a theme, but genuinely different ideas with different underlying rationales.

Each direction starts with a concept statement: a sentence or two explaining the idea, what it communicates, and why it's right for the brand. A concept without a rationale is just a picture; a rationale without a concept is just text. Together they form a legible design decision.

I sketch quickly and digitize selectively. Most ideas don't survive contact with Illustrator, and that's fine. The goal at this stage is divergence — generating enough territory to find something genuinely interesting.

## Presentation

I present concepts with context. Each direction gets:

- The concept statement
- Primary logo and variations
- Color palette with rationale
- Typography selection
- One or two applied mockups (business card, social post, whatever is most relevant)

Mockups matter because clients struggle to evaluate a logo in isolation. Seeing it on a letterhead or a phone screen makes it real.

I present the concepts without editorializing about which one I prefer — at least initially. Clients often surprise you, and leading with a preference shuts down genuine feedback.

## Refinement

After presentation, we select a direction to develop. Rarely does a client pick a concept unchanged — there will be adjustments, sometimes significant ones. This is normal and good.

The refinement phase is iterative. I make changes, share, get feedback, repeat. I set a clear scope upfront: typically two or three rounds of refinement are included, with additional rounds billed separately. This prevents endless iteration and keeps both parties focused.

The most common refinement requests: scale adjustments, color tweaks, simplification. Clients almost never ask for more complexity. Almost universally, editing leads to stronger work.

## System Development

A logo is not a brand identity. After the mark is locked, I build out the system:

- Extended color palette (primary, secondary, neutrals, semantic colors for digital)
- Typography system (primary and secondary typefaces, scale, usage guidelines)
- Iconography style (if needed)
- Photography/illustration direction
- Layout principles

The system is what makes a brand consistent across touchpoints. It's also where a lot of budget disappears if scope isn't managed — a full system for a large brand can be a multi-month project. For smaller clients, a focused system covering the main use cases is often more valuable than an exhaustive one that never gets used.

## Handoff

Final deliverables vary by client, but the baseline package I always provide:

- Logo files in SVG, PNG (transparent background, multiple sizes), and PDF
- Color codes in HEX, RGB, and CMYK
- Font files or license information
- A one-page usage guide — do's and don'ts, minimum sizes, clear space rules

For clients who will be managing the brand themselves, I also include a brief training session on how to use the files. The best identity system fails if the client recreates the logo in Word because they couldn't find the right file.

The process is predictable, but the outcomes aren't. Good process creates the conditions for good work; it doesn't guarantee it. That uncertainty is part of what makes this kind of work interesting.
