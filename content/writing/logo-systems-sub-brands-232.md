Single-brand logo design is straightforward compared to designing logo architecture for organizations with multiple sub-brands, divisions, or product lines. When a parent brand has five divisions, each with different audiences and positioning, you're not designing one logo — you're designing a system that has to work across all of them while maintaining coherent family identity.

This is architecture work, not just mark-making.

## The Brand Architecture Decision

Before I design anything, I clarify the brand architecture model. There are three common structures:

Monolithic (branded house): all sub-brands use the parent brand's identity. Every division is presented as part of the same entity with descriptors distinguishing them. Apple, FedEx. Simple to manage, strong master brand.

Endorsed: sub-brands have their own identities but are visually connected to the parent through an endorsement element — "A Company Company" or a shared graphic device. Marriott Hotels, Nestlé. More flexibility for sub-brands, moderate parent visibility.

Pluralistic (house of brands): each sub-brand has an independent identity with little or no visible parent connection. P&G, Unilever at the consumer level. Maximum flexibility per sub-brand, no parent visibility.

The architecture model determines almost every design decision that follows. I won't start mark development until this is clear.

## Building the Master Brand Identity First

In monolithic and endorsed architectures, the master brand identity needs to be fully developed before sub-brand expressions. The sub-brands derive their identity elements from the master — they don't establish independent directions that the master then has to accommodate.

This sequencing matters because the system design requires starting from the center and expanding outward, not designing independently and then trying to reconcile.

## The Differentiation Range

In a monolithic system, sub-brands are differentiated through secondary elements: a distinctive color from the brand palette, a differentiated descriptor treatment, sometimes a simple geometric element. The core mark stays constant.

The challenge is creating sufficient differentiation that each sub-brand feels like a distinct entity while maintaining sufficient family resemblance that the parent relationship is always clear.

I test this with a simple side-by-side: lay all sub-brand marks together and evaluate whether the differentiation reads clearly enough to distinguish between them, and whether the consistency reads clearly enough to mark them as family.

## Color Architecture

Color is usually the primary differentiator in multi-brand systems. I assign each sub-brand a unique color from the master palette, then ensure the color assignments are distinctive enough to differentiate without being so varied that they break the palette's coherence.

The master brand color — the primary brand color — usually stays with the parent and isn't reassigned to a specific sub-brand. Sub-brands use secondary and tertiary palette colors as their distinguishing hue.

## Scaling the System

The true test of a multi-brand logo system is whether it can scale. If the parent acquires a new division or launches a new product line, can the system accommodate it without requiring the existing marks to change?

I build this scalability in explicitly: a defined range of available differentiation colors, a clear structural template for how new sub-brand marks are constructed, and rules that prevent future additions from destabilizing existing marks.

Systems designed for change from the start age much better than those retrofitted after the fact.
