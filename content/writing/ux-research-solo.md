# Doing UX Research as a Solo Designer

UX research in large organisations has its own infrastructure: dedicated researchers, participant panels, usability labs, analysis tooling. As a solo designer, you have none of that. What you do have is proximity — you're usually closer to the product, the stakeholders, and often the users than anyone on a large team.

The challenge is being disciplined enough to do research at all when you're also responsible for design, prototyping, and sometimes development. Here's how I make it work.

## Calibrate the Method to the Question

The first mistake in solo research is over-engineering. You don't need a six-week study to answer most questions. Before choosing a method, get precise about what you actually need to know.

Research questions fall roughly into two categories:

**Generative** — you're trying to understand a problem space, form hypotheses, or find opportunities. Use interviews, observation, diary studies.

**Evaluative** — you have a design and want to know if it works. Use usability tests, first-click tests, surveys.

The method should fit the question. A survey can't tell you why someone made a choice; an interview can. A usability test can't tell you how someone's daily workflow actually looks; observation can.

## Guerrilla Testing: Lightweight and Underrated

Guerrilla testing — approaching people in public spaces or reaching out to existing contacts — has a bad reputation in some circles. "Not rigorous enough." In practice, for most product questions, five informal sessions with relevant users will surface more actionable insight than three highly formal sessions with a screener full of criteria.

The key is recruiting well. "Guerrilla" doesn't mean random. You still need people who match your user profile. The difference is you find them informally rather than through a panel.

Cafes near university campuses, coworking spaces, Slack communities in your product's niche, LinkedIn outreach to professionals in the target role — these are all legitimate recruitment channels. Offer a small incentive: a coffee, a gift card, 20 minutes of your time to give feedback on their work in return.

## Interview Structure for Solo Sessions

Running an interview solo is harder than it looks. You're facilitating, taking notes, listening, and following up on interesting threads simultaneously. A few things that help:

**Record with permission.** A simple screen recording with audio lets you focus on the conversation rather than note-taking. Review the recording after — you'll catch things you missed live.

**Have a topic guide, not a script.** A list of themes to cover, not questions to read verbatim. The conversation should feel natural. Follow tangents when they're interesting.

**Warm up slowly.** Start with easy, contextual questions before getting into the specific topic. "Walk me through your typical day" before "How do you currently manage X?" The first few minutes of an interview are about building enough trust for honest answers.

**Five-second rule for silence.** Don't rush to fill silences. Count to five before speaking again. Users often give their most honest answers after the initial response, when they've had a moment to think.

## Synthesising Alone

Analysis is where solo research risks falling apart. It's tempting to half-process the findings and move straight to design. Resist this.

My process after a set of sessions:

1. Transcribe or write up session notes within 24 hours, while it's fresh
2. Extract observations as discrete, atomic notes — one insight per note
3. Group notes by theme, looking for patterns across sessions
4. Write two or three "how might we" questions based on the most interesting patterns

This takes time. For five sessions, budget four to six hours of synthesis. It's the work that converts raw data into design direction — skipping it means you might as well not have done the research.

## Working with Stakeholders

As a solo designer, you often report to a client or a non-design stakeholder. Research findings need to be communicated in terms that land with a non-research audience.

Insights without evidence are opinions. Always pair a finding with a quote or observation: "Three out of five participants couldn't find the export button — they looked in the settings menu, not the toolbar" is more convincing than "the export button is hard to find."

A short research readout — two or three slides, key insights, supporting evidence, design implications — is usually more useful than a long report. People read short things.

## When to Skip Research

Research has a cost: time, money, and sometimes stakeholder patience. Some decisions don't need research. If you're choosing between two shades of grey for a divider, you don't need to test it. If you're making a navigational structure decision that will affect every user's daily workflow, you do.

The question to ask: what would I do differently if I knew the answer? If the honest answer is "probably nothing," the research isn't necessary. If the answer is "I'd make a meaningfully different design decision," do the research.

Solo research done well is a competitive advantage. Most designers skip it because it's hard to fit into a solo workflow. Doing even a small amount — five interviews, a handful of usability sessions — consistently produces better outcomes than designing purely from assumption.
