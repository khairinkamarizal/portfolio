Visual hierarchy is the invisible architecture of every great interface. It's the reason your eye knows where to look first, what to read next, and what to skip entirely. When it's working, users don't notice it. When it breaks, everything feels confusing — and designers often can't name why.

These are the principles I return to constantly.

## Scale Is the Most Powerful Tool You Have

Size communicates importance more directly than any other property. A large element says "start here." A small element says "this is supporting context."

The mistake most designers make isn't using scale wrong — it's using scale too conservatively. Interfaces where everything is roughly the same size are flat. There's no hierarchy because there's no contrast. The eye doesn't know where to land.

Go bigger on the things that matter. Much bigger than feels comfortable. A headline that's 4x the size of body text has genuine visual authority. A headline that's 1.5x body text is asking to be noticed but not demanding it.

Scale also works in reverse. Making secondary information genuinely small — not just slightly smaller, but small — creates the contrast that makes primary information feel important.

## Contrast Does the Work Scale Can't

Scale creates hierarchy between elements at different levels. Contrast creates hierarchy within a single level.

The most useful form of contrast in typography is weight: bold versus regular, medium versus light. A bold label next to regular body text creates instant separation without any change in size. Your eye parses them as different kinds of information.

Opacity is a subtler form of contrast that I rely on heavily. In a black-and-white palette, the difference between full-black text and 50% opacity text is enormous. Primary content sits at full opacity. Secondary content sits at 60%. Supporting context might be at 40%. The hierarchy is clear without any change in size or weight.

Color contrast works similarly but introduces more complexity — accessible contrast ratios, brand considerations, dark mode implications. When in doubt, use weight and opacity before reaching for color.

## Spacing Is Syntax

White space isn't empty — it's a structural tool. The spacing between elements communicates their relationship.

Elements that are close together are perceived as related. Elements with significant space between them are perceived as distinct. This is the gestalt principle of proximity, and it operates automatically in the viewer's perception before any conscious reading happens.

What this means practically: group related elements tightly, and separate distinct sections generously. Don't split the difference with uniform spacing across an entire layout. Uniform spacing creates uniform ambiguity.

The other spacing principle worth internalizing is the difference between padding and margin. Internal spacing (padding) determines the breathing room within a component. External spacing (margin) determines the relationship between components. These serve different purposes and should be sized accordingly.

## Alignment Creates Invisible Structure

A layout where elements align to a consistent grid feels ordered even when that grid is never explicitly visible. The alignment is the structure.

Left-aligned text with a consistent left margin creates a reading axis — a vertical line the eye can return to after each line break. Break that axis and reading becomes work. Restore it and reading becomes effortless.

Alignment also communicates hierarchy. A header aligned to the left margin of its content section feels like it belongs to that section. A header that breaks out of that alignment — wider, or offset — signals that it exists at a higher level in the hierarchy.

The practical rule: align everything to something. Nothing should float arbitrarily. If an element doesn't align to the grid, it should align to another element. Intentional misalignment can be powerful. Accidental misalignment is always destructive.

## Typography Does More Than Communicate Text

The choice of typeface, the size relationships, the line height, the tracking — these all contribute to the character of an interface before a single word is read.

A generous line height (1.6–1.8 for body text) signals calm and space. A tight line height (1.2–1.3) signals density and urgency. Neither is right by default — it depends on what you're building.

Uppercase text with wide letter-spacing reads as a label, a category, a structural element. It's the visual equivalent of a section divider. Use it for navigation, metadata, and section headers. Don't use it for long-form reading — it's too effortful at length.

The pairing of two typefaces — one for display, one for body — creates the visual contrast that makes a typographic system feel designed rather than default. The contrast should be genuine: a strong serif paired with a neutral sans-serif, or a monospace display face paired with a humanist body face. Pairing two similar typefaces creates confusion rather than harmony.

## Hierarchy Is a System, Not a List of Rules

The principles above don't operate in isolation. Scale, contrast, spacing, and alignment work together as a system. Strengthen one and you can afford to reduce another. Over-apply all of them at once and the hierarchy collapses into noise.

The test of a hierarchy is simple: cover the screen and reveal elements one at a time. If the order in which you reveal them matches the order in which they should be read — if the most important things are most visually prominent — the hierarchy is working.

If it doesn't, something in the system is out of balance. Usually it's contrast — there isn't enough difference between levels. Make the differences more extreme, and the hierarchy clarifies.

Visual hierarchy is a skill that improves with deliberate practice. Look at work you admire and ask specifically what's doing the hierarchy work. Look at interfaces that confuse you and ask what's broken. The more precisely you can name what you see, the more precisely you can control what you make.
