## Zines are where design fundamentals live without polish

A zine — a small, self-published print piece, typically photocopied or risograph printed in tiny editions — strips away everything that can hide weak design fundamentals: high production values, expensive printing, professional photography. What's left is pure composition, typography, and concept.

I've been making and collecting zines for years, and I recommend them to any designer who feels like their work is getting too polished, too safe, too removed from the raw problem-solving that makes design interesting.

## The format is the brief

Zine design starts with a decision about format: how many pages, what size, how folded, how bound. These decisions are the brief. An eight-page quarter-fold zine has different compositional logic than a 16-page saddle-stitched half-letter booklet.

I like starting with a constraint I've not worked with before. A single folded sheet that creates eight panels. A long narrow format that forces a different reading rhythm than standard page widths. The constraint doesn't limit creativity — it focuses it.

## Typography under lo-fi conditions

Zines are often reproduced on photocopiers or risograph presses, both of which have limitations: photocopiers crush shadows and blow out highlights; risograph has limited color registration and distinctive ink behavior. Typography needs to be designed for these conditions.

Very small text, very thin weights, and fine serif details all suffer in photocopier reproduction. I design zine typography with generous x-heights, robust stroke widths, and enough tracking to maintain legibility after the tonal compression of photocopying.

## Riso printing and its specific aesthetic

Risograph printing has developed an aesthetic language of its own: deliberate misregistration between colors, ink that's slightly transparent (allowing overlapping colors to mix), halftone dots at visible frequency, and a warmth and texture that digital printing can't reproduce.

Designing for riso means embracing these qualities rather than fighting them. Registration should be treated as approximate — design elements that require perfect color alignment will always look slightly wrong in riso. Design that treats misregistration as a visual feature looks right precisely because it anticipates the process.

## What zines teach

The lessons from zine design transfer directly to professional practice. Working with strong constraints produces more decisive design. Low production budgets push concept to the center. The directness and confidence of the best zine typography is something I try to carry into my professional work.

Every designer should make a zine. Not for the portfolio, not for a client — just to experience what design feels like when you have nothing to hide behind except the quality of your thinking.
