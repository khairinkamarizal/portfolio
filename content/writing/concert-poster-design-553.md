## The Concert Poster's Job

A concert poster has about two seconds to stop someone walking past it. It needs to communicate what, who, when, and where — but before it communicates any of that, it needs to create a feeling. The feeling comes first. The information follows.

This is different from most graphic design briefs. Most information design prioritizes clarity above all. Concert poster design prioritizes atmosphere first and uses clarity in service of that atmosphere.

## The Visual Language of Live Music

Concert posters have a visual vocabulary built from the culture of live music. Certain visual elements carry specific associations: hand-rendered type signals authenticity and DIY culture. Dense layering signals maximalist energy. Dark palettes signal the intimacy of a venue at night. High contrast signals intensity and volume.

I don't use these elements by formula. But understanding what they communicate helps me make deliberate choices about which associations to invoke and which to subvert.

## Artist Name Hierarchy

The most important typographic decision in a concert poster is almost always the scale of the artist's name. It dominates. Everything else supports.

For a single-artist show, the hierarchy is clean: name, venue, date. For a multi-artist bill, the headline act gets the dominant treatment, supporting acts get secondary treatment, and door times, ticket prices, and venue details get tertiary treatment.

The scale difference between headline and supporting acts is itself information. A 10x size difference communicates "this is a headliner with support." A 2x difference communicates something different. The hierarchy is editorial as much as typographic.

## Color and the Night

Concert posters often use dark backgrounds — black, deep navy, dark purple — because music is a nighttime activity and the poster should feel like it belongs to that world. The darkness also gives colors more luminosity: neon and saturated tones glow against dark backgrounds in a way they never do on white.

When I design on dark backgrounds for music work, I lean into that luminosity. Saturated colors at high contrast. Elements that feel like they're lit from within. The visual equivalent of the experience of being in a room when a spotlight hits the stage.

## The Role of Image

Many concert posters include a photograph of the artist, but the most interesting ones treat the image as a raw material rather than as documentation. Heavy duotone treatment. Strong cropping. Integration with typographic elements. The image becomes part of the graphic system rather than a placed photo surrounded by text.

When I work with artist photography for a concert poster, I ask: how can this image be pushed to feel like it belongs to this design, rather than sitting on top of it?

## Urgency and Impermanence

Concert posters have built-in urgency — the event happens on a specific night and then it's over. Good concert poster design can carry that urgency in its visual character.

This might mean a composition that feels slightly raw, slightly unpolished. Hand-rendered elements. Ink textures. Design decisions that feel made under pressure rather than deliberated over.

The roughness isn't sloppiness. It's a signal: this matters right now, not in a carefully produced future. The ephemeral quality is honest. It belongs to the format.

## Format and Production

Concert posters traditionally live in a few standard formats: 11x17, 18x24, 24x36. I design to these formats and think about the physical production — screen printing, offset, digital — because the production method is part of the aesthetic. A screen-printed two-color poster on newsprint looks and feels different from a full-color digital print, even with the same design. Know your production and design for it.
