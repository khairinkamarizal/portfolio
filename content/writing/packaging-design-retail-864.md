## Packaging Is the Product

For consumer goods brands, the packaging is the first physical experience of the product. In a retail environment, the package is making the sale — the brand promise is delivered or betrayed before the product is ever used.

This stakes packaging design differently from most other brand touchpoints. The business consequences of getting it wrong are direct and measurable.

## The Shelf Context First

Before I design anything, I go to the shelf. Literally — I visit the retail environments where the product will be sold and look at the competitive set in context. What does the shelf look like? What colors dominate? What structures dominate? Where is there visual space?

A packaging design that looks great in isolation can look generic or confused on the actual shelf. The shelf is the real context for evaluation, not the client's conference room or a presentation PDF.

## Structure and Graphics as One System

The structural design of packaging (the shape, the opening mechanism, the material) and the graphic design are most effective when developed together. A flat graphic treatment applied to a structural form that wasn't designed for it will always feel like a graphic applied on top of a box.

When I have input into structural design — on custom packaging projects — I work with a packaging engineer alongside the visual development. When I'm working with a standard structural form, I design the graphics specifically for that form's geometry and how it's held, opened, and displayed.

## Regulatory and Legal Requirements

Packaging for most consumer product categories carries mandatory regulatory content: ingredients, nutritional information, certifications, legal disclaimers. These are not negotiable, and they occupy real space on the package.

I get the full regulatory content brief before starting design, not after. Discovering at the final stage that a mandatory ingredient list needs to go where you've placed the hero image is a design revision that could have been avoided.

## Production Constraints

Packaging is produced in volume with real manufacturing constraints. The number of print colors affects cost. Die-cut complexity affects cost and lead time. Emboss and foil add cost and require specific structural considerations. Surface treatment affects how inks print and how finishes adhere.

I design packaging with the production method in mind from the start. A design developed without regard for manufacturing costs may be unbuildable at the target price point, which wastes everyone's time.

## The Unboxing Experience

For D2C brands and premium consumer products, the inside of the package is increasingly a designed brand experience. Tissue paper in brand colors, a branded insert, a personal note. These elements extend the brand moment and create the "unboxing" experience that generates social sharing.

The inside of the box isn't afterthought territory. It's where the brand experience is often most concentrated.
