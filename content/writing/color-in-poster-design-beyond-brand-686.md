Brand identity work puts color in a constrained context: the palette is defined, the usage rules are specified, deviation is by definition off-brand. But certain poster work — editorial, cultural, speculative — operates with more color freedom. When the brief doesn't specify a palette, or when the client is open to expressive color choices, the decision space expands enormously.

Working in wider color freedom requires a different set of instincts and processes than working within a defined palette.

## Starting With Emotional Register

When I have color freedom on a poster, my first decision is emotional register: what feeling should the color communicate? Not what colors I like, and not what colors are currently trendy — what feeling does this specific work need to produce?

A poster for a retrospective exhibition of 1970s architecture might use a warm, slightly desaturated palette that evokes that era. A poster for a contemporary club night needs colors that feel alive, present, electric. A poster for a memorial event calls for something quiet and considered.

The emotional register comes from the content, not from personal preference. I write the feeling in words before I make any color decisions: "warm but not nostalgic," "electric but not frivolous," "somber without being morbid." The words are imprecise but they create a frame.

## The Color as a System Within the Poster

Even without a brand palette, the colors in a poster need to work as a system. Two or three colors that relate to each other are more effective than five or six colors that don't. Within the poster, each color should have a job: the dominant color that sets the overall feeling, the secondary color that provides contrast and emphasis, the neutral that gives the eye a place to rest.

This internal color logic applies regardless of whether the specific colors are brand-defined or project-specific.

## Color Relationships and Contrast

The most important color decision in a poster isn't the hue — it's the relationship between the dominant color and the secondary color. High contrast between dominant and secondary creates visual energy and legibility. Low contrast creates sophistication and intimacy.

I think about this relationship before I've identified specific colors: do I want a high-contrast or low-contrast color relationship? The answer shapes the entire subsequent color selection.

## Historical and Cultural Color References

Colors carry cultural histories that can be used deliberately. The colors of the Italian Futurist movement. The neon palette of early post-punk graphics. The earth tones of 1970s design. Soviet constructivist red-and-black. These aren't costumes to wear but references to be aware of and to invoke or avoid deliberately.

For cultural institution work especially, the color references you evoke are part of the conceptual content of the poster. Using constructivist red-and-black for a contemporary political art poster is a legible reference. Using it for a children's event poster is a confused one.

## Testing Under Printing Conditions

Even in poster work with full color freedom, the printing conditions constrain what colors are achievable. Screen printing, which is common for limited-edition cultural posters, uses premixed inks — the color selection is limited to what's achievable as a mixed ink or Pantone equivalent. The design freedom is real but the production constraints are also real.

Testing color choices against the intended print process, not just against the screen display, is always part of my process.
