## Posters Need Different Images

Photographs that work in editorial contexts often don't work in poster contexts. The requirements are different in ways that are easy to underestimate when you're selecting or directing images without the poster format in mind.

The main differences: posters require images with enough compositional space to accommodate text, often at the scale where the image occupies the full poster and type must sit within or alongside it. The image needs to hold up at A1 or larger print scale without showing digital noise or compression artifacts. And the image needs to communicate in a fraction of a second — the longer read that works in editorial is a liability on a poster.

## The Text Zone Problem

The most common reason a photograph fails for poster use is no space for text. A tight portrait crop, a landscape that fills the frame edge to edge, a subject that occupies the center with no breathing room — these are beautiful images that don't work for posters because there's nowhere to put the essential information.

When I'm selecting photography for a poster, I'm looking for images with compositional breathing room: sky, wall, open space, depth — areas with relatively consistent tone where type can sit legibly. When I'm directing a shoot for poster use, I brief the photographer specifically for this.

## Resolution and Scale

Print at large format requires images with sufficient resolution to reproduce without visible pixelation or digital noise. The minimum I work with for A1 poster output is an image that can be reproduced at 150–200ppi at print size.

Before committing to an image in a poster layout, I check the resolution at the intended print size. An image that looks crisp on screen at reduced scale can reveal noise, compression artifacts, or softness at 1:1 view for print. This check takes thirty seconds and catches problems before they become expensive production mistakes.

## Color in Photography for Print

The photographic color treatment should be designed alongside the poster's graphic language rather than applied independently. A heavily stylized photograph in a cool, desaturated palette can look wrong against warm typographic elements unless the palette relationship is designed.

I approach photographic color treatment as part of the poster design, not as a pre-production step applied before layout begins. The image's color character and the poster's color system need to be in dialogue.

## When to Shoot vs. When to License

Original photography shot for a specific poster gives far more compositional and directorial control. Licensing existing stock is faster and cheaper but requires finding an image that fits rather than creating one that fits.

For most event poster work, stock photography serves well enough and is cost-appropriate. For brand posters where the image is a primary brand asset, original photography is almost always worth the investment.
