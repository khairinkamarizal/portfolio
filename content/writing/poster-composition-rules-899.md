## Rules Worth Knowing Before Breaking

The compositional conventions of poster design — centered layouts, clear hierarchy, generous margins, type in the lower third — exist because they work. Viewers have learned to read posters through these conventions. A design that follows them makes the viewer's job easier.

Breaking these conventions creates friction. Sometimes that friction serves the poster's purpose — it stops the eye, creates energy, demands engagement. More often, friction just makes the poster harder to read without delivering anything in return.

The difference between productive rule-breaking and counterproductive rule-breaking is understanding what the rule was doing before you removed it.

## The Centered Layout Convention

Centered layouts communicate symmetry, balance, and formality. They're common in concert posters, exhibition announcements, and contexts where the subject has a certain gravitas.

Breaking centering — moving the primary element significantly off-axis — creates tension and asymmetric energy. This works for subjects that are themselves asymmetric, energetic, or unconventional. It works against subjects that need to project authority or balance.

If I break the centered convention, I break it decisively. A composition that's slightly off-center looks like a mistake; one that's dramatically asymmetric looks intentional.

## The Hierarchy Convention

Standard poster hierarchy runs: primary message (largest), secondary information (medium), fine print (smallest). This order maps visual size to information importance.

Inverting hierarchy — large secondary information, small primary message — creates surprise and can create conceptual depth when the inversion itself communicates something. A poster for a whisper-quiet acoustic performance might use tiny type for the artist's name against a vast expanse of silence.

This works only when the inversion is conceptually motivated. Inverting hierarchy for aesthetic novelty produces posters that are confusing rather than interesting.

## The Margin Convention

Generous margins create breathing room and signal that the designer was confident enough not to fill every pixel. Breaking margins — letting elements bleed to or off the edge — creates energy, scale, and a sense that the content is larger than the page.

Bleeding a primary element dramatically off one edge while keeping the rest of the composition within standard margins maximizes the contrast between the constrained and the liberated. This contrast is often more effective than treating the whole poster as bleed territory.

## The Test for Any Break

For every compositional rule I consider breaking, I ask: what was this rule doing, and what replaces its function? If I can't answer that question clearly, I'm not ready to break the rule. If I can answer it, I break the rule with confidence.
