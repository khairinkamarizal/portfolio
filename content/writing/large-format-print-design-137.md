## Where Large Format Begins

Large format print — billboards, building wraps, trade show graphics, exhibition banners — operates under different design constraints than standard print work. The viewer's relationship to the piece is fundamentally different. They're not holding it or sitting near it. They're often looking at it from tens of meters away, frequently while in motion.

These conditions change every design decision.

## Resolution and Viewing Distance

A common misunderstanding: large format prints don't need to be high resolution because they're viewed at a distance. This is partially true. A billboard viewed from 50 meters can be printed at 15-30 DPI at the final size. But this doesn't mean low-resolution files will work — at 15 DPI at full size, the actual file dimensions are still enormous.

I always work with print vendors to establish the correct resolution requirements for the specific application and viewing distance. Getting this wrong produces muddy print.

## Simplicity Scales

The most important principle in large format design is simplicity. Details that look refined and considered at screen scale disappear at distance. Fine typography, intricate illustration, subtle color gradations — these become visual noise when viewed from far away.

Effective large format design uses bold, simple forms. Type with weight. High contrast. Clear hierarchy. The composition has to read from the opposite side of a road or across a trade show floor.

## Type Size for Distance

There's a practical formula for legibility at distance: roughly 1 inch of type height for every 25 feet of viewing distance. A headline meant to be read from 100 feet needs to be at least 4 inches tall in the final print.

I calculate type sizes for large format work rather than eyeballing them. Getting readable at the actual viewing distance is the requirement; aesthetic preferences come after.

## Color on Backlit Surfaces

Some large format print is backlit — light boxes, illuminated signs, exhibition displays. Backlit color behaves very differently from reflective (non-illuminated) print. Colors appear more saturated and vibrant. Dark tones become much lighter. Colors designed for standard print can look completely wrong when backlit.

I always ask whether the final display is backlit or reflective before designing the color system, and I adjust the values accordingly.

## The Viewing Environment

The viewing environment for large format work is never neutral. A billboard exists against sky, buildings, and surrounding landscape. A trade show banner competes with dozens of other stimuli. An exhibition print shares space with other works.

I design with the actual environment in mind. What background will this piece appear against? What else will compete for the viewer's attention? What color and contrast choices will make this piece stand out in that specific context?

Context isn't decoration. It's a constraint that shapes the design.
