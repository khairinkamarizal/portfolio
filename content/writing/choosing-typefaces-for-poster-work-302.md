Typeface selection for posters is nothing like choosing fonts for a website. On screen, readability at small sizes drives most decisions. On a poster, you're working at a scale where type becomes image — where the shape of a letter carries as much meaning as the word itself.

## Start With the Tone

Before I open a type catalog, I ask one question: what is this poster supposed to feel like? A film festival retrospective feels different from a punk show. A gallery opening for minimalist sculpture has different demands than a neighborhood block party.

Tone comes first because type communicates emotionally before it communicates linguistically. The viewer reads the feeling of the letterforms before they read the words. Get the tone wrong and the poster argues with itself.

## Display vs. Text in Poster Context

At poster scale, the distinction between display and text type almost disappears — everything is display. But the design history of a typeface still matters. A face designed for setting long text (like Garamond or Caslon) carries a different cultural charge than one designed purely for headlines (like Knockout or Acumin Condensed). I use that charge intentionally.

If I want something to feel literary, I reach for a face with text-setting heritage. If I want raw impact, I reach for something purpose-built for size.

## Weight and Width Are Part of the Message

A heavy, wide face dominates. A light, condensed face implies urgency and density. A medium-weight face with generous spacing says calm authority. These aren't rules — they're tendencies. But tendencies are useful.

On most posters I'm setting a single typeface in multiple weights and widths. This keeps the composition unified while giving me the contrast I need for hierarchy. I'd rather master one type family than introduce four different foundry releases that fight each other for attention.

## Licensing for Print

This is where a lot of designers get caught. Web fonts and desktop licenses are not the same thing. If you're sending files to a commercial printer, check your EULA. Most reputable foundries offer print/PDF licensing, but the terms vary. Buried in the fine print is often a page-count or print-run limit. Know what you're working with before you invoice the client.

## My Current Shortlist

The faces I keep returning to for poster work: Grotesk families like Aktiv Grotesk and Neue Haas Grotesk for structural work. Slab serifs like Clarendon and Sentinel when I want warmth with muscle. Display faces like Canela and Cardinal Photo when the brief calls for something editorial. And a rotating cast of variable fonts for when I need something that moves across a weight range within a single composition.

The catalog grows slowly. I add a face when I have a specific need it solves, not because it showed up in my feed.
