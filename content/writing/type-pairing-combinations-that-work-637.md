I've been asked more times than I can count how I choose typeface combinations. The honest answer is that I've internalized a set of principles through years of experimentation and observation that now operate somewhat intuitively — but the principles are articulable, and they're more useful than "I just know what works."

## The Core Principle: Contrast With Relationship

A good typeface pairing has contrast — the two faces are clearly different from each other — but also relationship — they share some quality that makes them feel like they belong in the same design.

If two typefaces are too similar, the combination looks like a mistake: why use two different fonts if they read as practically the same? If two typefaces have no relationship at all, the combination looks like two different designs occupying the same space.

The relationship can be:
- Shared proportions (x-height, cap height)
- Shared historical period or design influence  
- Complementary contrast (high-contrast serif + no-contrast sans)
- Shared designer or type family extended range

## Serif + Sans: The Classic Move

The most reliable pairing structure is a serif for display or primary use and a sans-serif for body or secondary use (or vice versa). This works because serifs and sans-serifs have inherent contrast — the presence or absence of serifs is an immediately visible difference — while their underlying letter structure can be closely matched.

The failure mode: pairing a serif and a sans-serif that have opposite proportions. A tall, narrow serif with a wide, short sans will never feel related. I look for faces with similar x-height ratios and similar overall proportions even across the serif/sans divide.

## Pairings I Return To

Some combinations I keep coming back to because they consistently work across a range of applications:

**Editorial and refined:** A sharp-contrast didone (Canela, Freight Display) with a neutral humanist sans (Söhne, Graphik). The high contrast of the serif and the clarity of the sans create an editorial luxury feeling.

**Contemporary and functional:** A geometric sans-serif for display (Neue Haas Grotesk, Aktiv Grotesk) with the same face at a different weight for body copy. Not technically a pairing but works consistently for clean contemporary work.

**Warm and cultural:** A humanist serif with ink-trap details (GT Sectra, Tiempos) with a humanist sans (Gill Sans, Freight Sans). Shared humanist construction creates warmth; the serif/sans contrast creates visual interest.

## Testing the Pair

I test every potential pairing by setting actual content — a headline, a subhead, a paragraph of body copy — and reading it. Not evaluating it aesthetically first. Reading it. If the reading experience is smooth and the visual rhythm feels natural, the pair is working. If I'm aware of the type rather than the content, something is wrong.

## When One Face Is Enough

Some design work doesn't need two typefaces. A project that uses a typeface with an extended family — multiple weights from thin to black, condensed and extended variants — can create the full hierarchy range within one type family. This approach has a purity and consistency that can be very effective.

I reach for single-family solutions for brand identity work especially, where simplicity in the type system makes the identity easier to apply consistently.
