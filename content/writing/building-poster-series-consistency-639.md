Designing a single poster is one problem. Designing a poster series — where each piece needs to be individually strong and read as part of a family — is a substantially harder problem. I've designed enough series work to have developed a consistent approach.

## Define the System Before You Design the Posters

The most common mistake in series design is designing the first poster to completion and then trying to extract a system from it. This approach produces a system that's really just "the same as the first one" and subsequent pieces feel like copies rather than siblings.

The right approach: define the system first, then design within it. The system consists of:

**Fixed elements:** Things that are identical across every piece. A specific color, a specific typeface and its sizing, a structural grid, a compositional logic (where the headline always lives, where the supporting information always lives).

**Variable elements:** Things that change from piece to piece to give each poster its own identity. Could be a dominant image, a dominant color from a rotating palette, a rotating graphic element, the specific typographic composition within the fixed rules.

The ratio of fixed to variable determines how tight or loose the series feels. More fixed elements → tighter, more uniform. More variable elements → more diverse, but potentially incoherent.

## Testing the System Before Committing

Before I design any final poster in a series, I build rough versions of three to four pieces using the system I've defined. This is the test: can I produce distinct, interesting posters within these constraints? Or does the system produce sameness without giving me room to work?

If the system is too tight, every poster will feel like a template. If it's too loose, they won't read as a series. I adjust the ratio of fixed and variable elements until I find the right balance.

## The Role of the Fixed Element

The fixed element is the anchor — it's what makes the series a series. Viewers who encounter multiple pieces should immediately recognize they belong together because of this anchor. It might be a typographic treatment that's unmistakably consistent. A structural color block in the same position every time. A layout logic that's clearly systematic.

The anchor should be strong enough to be immediately recognizable but not so dominant that it overwhelms the variable elements that make each poster its own piece.

## Making Each Poster Stand Alone

A series poster that only makes sense as part of the series has failed at half its job. Each piece needs to work as a standalone artifact — to communicate clearly, to be visually resolved, to have a reason to exist independently.

The test: isolate one poster from the series. Does it work? Is it a complete design? Does it communicate what it needs to communicate without the context of the other pieces? If yes, it's doing both jobs.

## Series Evolution

Long-running series — annual season posters, monthly event series — have the interesting challenge of maintaining continuity while allowing for evolution. A series that's exactly the same every year becomes stale. One that changes too much loses its series identity.

I think of long-running series as having a core identity that's invariant (the fundamental structural logic, the primary typeface, the overall visual language) and a surface treatment that can evolve over time within those constraints. The series grows and develops without losing what makes it recognizable.
