## When Correct Looks Wrong

Mathematics and optics don't always agree. A circle and a square of identical height will look wrong — the circle will appear slightly shorter, even though the measurements say otherwise. A horizontal stroke and a vertical stroke of identical thickness will look wrong — the horizontal will appear heavier.

These optical illusions are consistent and predictable, which means they can be corrected. The corrections are small, often fractions of a percent of the element's size. But they're visible, and the difference between corrected and uncorrected work is felt even by viewers who can't name it.

## The Overshoot

The most fundamental optical correction is the overshoot: round and pointed forms should exceed the flat cap height and baseline slightly. In well-designed typefaces, the O, C, and G sit slightly above the cap height and slightly below the baseline. The point of an A or V exceeds the baseline.

Without overshoot, round and pointed forms look too small relative to flat-topped forms. With overshoot, they look the same height.

This applies in logo mark design as well as typography. A circle inscribed in a square at mathematically equal dimensions will look smaller than the square. To look equal, it needs to be slightly larger.

## Stroke Weight Compensation

Horizontal strokes appear heavier than vertical strokes at the same mathematical width. This is a consistent optical effect that typeface designers address by making horizontal strokes slightly thinner than vertical strokes — even in supposedly monolinear designs.

In logo mark construction, I check horizontal and vertical stroke weights visually rather than relying on measurements. If they look the same, they're probably right. If a horizontal element looks heavier, I reduce it until it looks balanced.

## Spacing in Caps

All-caps type requires more tracking than mixed-case type because capital letters have more complex shapes that create varied spacing between pairs. What reads as correct spacing for lowercase text reads as too tight for all-caps settings.

In poster headline work set in all caps, I always add tracking — typically 5–15% of the type size for display headlines. The exact amount depends on the typeface, but some positive tracking is almost always needed.

## Vertically Centering Type in Boxes

Mathematical vertical centering puts the type at the geometric center of a container. It almost always looks too low because the visual weight of a line of type is in its cap height, not its geometric center (which includes descenders).

To visually center type in a button, container, or box, adjust the top padding to be slightly less than the bottom padding. The exact amount varies, but a ratio of roughly 45:55 (top:bottom) is a starting point that looks correct in most cases.

These corrections are small but consistent. Building them into your practice produces work that looks considered rather than merely constructed.
