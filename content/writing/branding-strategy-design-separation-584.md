## The Handoff Problem

Here's a scenario I've seen repeatedly: a brand strategy firm develops a thorough positioning document, hands it to a design agency, and the agency produces a beautiful identity that has almost nothing to do with the strategy.

Not because the designers were careless. Because the handoff was the process. Strategy in one room, design in another, a brief connecting them that was too thin to bridge the gap.

The result is a brand where the strategic thinking and the visual thinking never actually met. The positioning says "approachable and human." The identity is cold and geometric. Nobody is lying. The processes just never collided.

## Why This Happens

Strategy and design require different modes of thinking. Strategy is verbal, analytical, research-based. Design is visual, intuitive, form-based. Organizations often separate these by assigning them to different teams or agencies, believing the brief is sufficient to transfer context.

It isn't. The brief transfers conclusions, not understanding. The designer who reads "approachable and human" in a brief understands those words differently from the strategist who wrote them after months of customer research.

The understanding — the full context — only transfers through conversation and collaboration. Not a brief handoff. An ongoing dialogue.

## How I Structure the Process

When I work on brand identity projects, I insist on being involved in the strategy development, not just the design execution. I attend customer research sessions when possible, or review the research outputs in detail. I ask the strategists questions until I understand not just the conclusions but the evidence behind them.

When I have that depth of understanding, the design work becomes much more accurate. The decisions aren't based on my interpretation of words in a brief — they're based on direct engagement with the strategic thinking.

## When Separation Is Unavoidable

Sometimes the strategy comes from a different firm or has already been completed before I'm engaged. In those cases, I invest extra effort in the onboarding:

I read the strategy document multiple times. I list every question the document raises. I meet with the person who owns the strategy and ask those questions. I treat the strategy as the raw material for a design brief I write myself, synthesizing the strategic inputs into visual direction.

This process takes longer than reading a brief and opening Illustrator. It produces better work and fewer revisions.

## The Common Enemy

Strategy without design is a PowerPoint that nobody executes. Design without strategy is decoration that doesn't communicate. The brand identity that works is the one where strategic thinking and design thinking inhabited the same conversation from the beginning.

That conversation is the job. Everything else is just the output.
