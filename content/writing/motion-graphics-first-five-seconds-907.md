## The Attention Window

In motion design, the first five seconds are everything. That's not hyperbole — it's a measurable reality. If a piece doesn't establish its visual language, emotional register, and purpose within those five seconds, most viewers have already disengaged. They might still be watching, but they've stopped actually seeing.

I've produced motion graphics for broadcast, social, and installation contexts. The contexts are wildly different, but the first-five-seconds problem is identical across all of them.

## Establishing the Visual Language Immediately

The opening moments should answer the question: what kind of thing is this? Is it bold and kinetic? Quiet and contemplative? Precise and informational? The aesthetic language should be clear before the first major content beat arrives.

I do this through the treatment of the very first frame and the very first movement. A piece that opens on a single typographic element entering with a slow ease-in-out tells the audience something completely different from one that opens with a fast, staccato cut sequence. Both can be right — but the wrong opening creates a mismatch between expectation and delivery that the rest of the piece spends its runtime fighting.

## Music and Motion Locking

If the piece has music — and most of mine do — I lock the motion to the music before I do anything else. Not loosely timed, but structurally locked. The first major visual hit lands on the downbeat. Scene transitions correspond to phrase boundaries. Typographic reveals sync to melodic peaks.

This isn't about making something feel "flashy." It's about creating the sense that the visual and audio elements are a single unified piece, not two separate things that happen to coexist. Audiences feel this as coherence even when they can't articulate why.

## The Reveal Sequence

My default structure for the opening five seconds is: establish the container, introduce the first element, establish the rhythm. The container might be a full-bleed color field, an abstract texture, or a live-action background. The first element is usually type or a key icon. The rhythm is the speed and easing of that first element's motion.

Once the rhythm is established, everything else in the piece inherits from it. Subsequent elements enter at the same tempo or at a deliberate multiple of it. Deviation from the established rhythm feels jarring unless it's intentional — a moment of emphasis, a structural shift, a climax.

## What I Avoid in Opening Sequences

Long fades to black at the start. An animated logo reveal that takes three seconds before any content appears. Slow type reveals that make the audience wait to know what the piece is about. These are habits borrowed from broadcast television pacing that don't translate to contemporary social and digital contexts.

I also avoid opening with the most complex composition in the piece. Start simple, build complexity. The opening should feel like an invitation, not a wall.

## The Storyboard Discipline

Every motion piece I work on starts with a storyboard, even for short pieces. The storyboard forces me to make compositional decisions before I'm inside the animation software, where the temptation to add, adjust, and complicate is constant.

The first five frames of the storyboard get the most scrutiny. If those frames don't tell a clear visual story, the animation won't save them. Strong bones, then movement.
