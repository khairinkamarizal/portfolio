Stationery design — business cards, letterhead, envelopes, compliment slips — is one of the oldest and most codified areas of graphic design. The formats are standardized, the content requirements are predictable, and the purpose is clear. And yet stationery is persistently under-designed, treated as a box-ticking exercise rather than a brand expression opportunity.

The best stationery I've seen treats these standard formats as constraints to work within creatively, producing pieces that communicate brand character through precise formal decisions within tight parameters.

## The Business Card as Brand Flagship

The business card is the smallest possible canvas for brand expression, and it gets more tactile scrutiny than almost any other brand asset. It's held, examined, sometimes kept. It represents the brand at the moment of first professional contact.

I treat the business card as a creative brief with high standards, not as a template to fill. Format questions: standard size or custom? Portrait or landscape? Single-sided or double-sided? These aren't defaults to accept — they're design decisions.

A business card that uses standard format in a standard way communicates nothing distinctive about the brand. A business card with an unusual material, a considered use of space, a typographic approach that clearly expresses the brand's character — that communicates something meaningful about what this company cares about.

## Hierarchy Within Stationery Constraints

Business cards and letterheads have fixed content requirements: name, title, contact information, logo. The hierarchy of this information — what the eye is drawn to first, what comes second — is a design decision.

Most business cards default to: name big, everything else small. This isn't wrong but it's also not the only option. A brand that puts its mark first and the name at equal size with other content makes a different statement — the brand is primary, the individual is part of it.

I develop the hierarchy based on the brand's character and the card's primary function. Is this card primarily about the individual (service businesses, personal brands) or the organization (institutional, corporate)? The answer shapes the hierarchy.

## The System Logic

Stationery works as a system — the business card, letterhead, and envelope share a visual language that creates coherence across the set. This doesn't mean they look identical: a letterhead has different proportions, different content requirements, and a different use context than a business card.

The system is established through consistent use of: the logo and its configuration, the color palette, the typographic treatment, and the spatial/margin logic. These common elements create family resemblance without requiring identical layouts.

## Production as Part of the Design

Stationery is experienced physically, which makes production specification part of the design. A business card on standard 350gsm stock communicates differently from the same card on 600gsm triple-laminated board. Matte laminate communicates differently from soft-touch. Letterpress on cotton stock communicates differently from digital printing on coated paper.

I specify the production as part of the design brief, not as a procurement decision. The paper weight, finish, and print method are as much a part of the brand communication as the color and typography.

## Digital Stationery

Email signatures, digital letterhead templates, email headers — the digital equivalents of print stationery — need the same design attention. They appear with equal frequency and carry equal weight in professional contexts.

I always include digital stationery specifications in brand stationery work: email signature dimensions and specifications, digital letterhead templates compatible with common word processing software, and guidance on how the brand adapts to digital contexts where exact replication of print specifications isn't possible.
