## The Off-the-Shelf Problem

Using a commercially available typeface for a wordmark is a practical starting point, but it carries a risk: the exact same letterforms are available to anyone with a font license. Your brand name set in Futura Bold is Futura Bold. Another company can do the exact same thing.

That's not necessarily fatal — many successful brands use lightly modified or even unmodified commercial typefaces. But if distinctiveness matters (and it almost always does), the solution is customization: modifying the letterforms until they're proprietary to the brand.

## What Customization Actually Means

Logotype customization exists on a spectrum. At one end: selecting a typeface with strong inherent character that reads as distinctive even without modification. At the other end: drawing all letterforms from scratch.

Between those extremes are the modifications I do most often:

**Adjusting spacing** — tightening or expanding the overall tracking, then kerning individual pairs. This alone can transform a generic wordmark into something that feels considered.

**Modifying specific letterforms** — opening a closed counter, changing a terminal from curved to straight, adjusting the leg of an R or the tail of a g. Small changes to two or three letters create a custom feel without requiring a complete custom drawing.

**Creating ligatures** — connecting two or more letters where they naturally flow into each other. A well-designed ligature reads as obviously custom and adds visual interest to the wordmark.

**Changing weights or stroke relationships** — adjusting the contrast between thick and thin strokes, or modifying the weight of specific strokes to create a different visual rhythm.

**Extending or truncating elements** — a crossbar that extends beyond the letterform, a descender that drops lower than normal, a stem that rises above the cap height. These extending elements create a distinctive silhouette.

## When I Modify vs. When I Don't

I customize when the brand needs proprietary typography and the budget supports it, when the category requires distinctiveness, or when the chosen typeface is common enough to be recognizable to design-literate audiences.

I don't customize when the typeface is already distinctive and uncommon enough to read as proprietary, or when the brief calls for accessibility and clarity over distinctiveness.

## How I Approach the Work

I always start with the existing typeface at the size the wordmark will primarily be used — usually 100pt or larger for a full-brand wordmark. I look at the letterforms for 10 minutes without touching anything.

Then I ask: what bothers me? What feels generic, awkward, or inconsistent with the brand personality? What feels like it's trying to say something the brand doesn't actually say?

Those observations become my modification brief. I make each change conservatively — less than I think I need — and then evaluate. Overmodification produces letterforms that look designed-at rather than designed-well. The best custom letterforms look inevitable, like the type was always this way.

## The Ownership Question

Modified letterforms need to be outlined and locked in the final files. The logotype should never depend on the original font being installed. Once the modifications are made, the letterforms are independent of the source typeface — they're a proprietary drawing, not a font application.

I document this clearly in file handoffs: these outlines are the brand asset. The source font is not the asset.

That's the practical goal of all this work: turning a generic font into something that belongs exclusively to the brand.
