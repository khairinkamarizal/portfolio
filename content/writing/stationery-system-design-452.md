Business stationery is a category that gets underinvested in almost every brand project. The logo gets months of attention. The website gets a full design engagement. The business card is an afterthought, sized and printed at minimum viable spec.

That's backwards. Physical stationery is often the first tangible brand touchpoint a prospect encounters. It sits on desks, gets passed between colleagues, and persists in files long after digital communications have been forgotten. A beautifully produced card communicates brand values in a way no website can replicate.

## The Business Card as Primary Brand Artifact

The business card is the most concentrated brand design challenge in the stationery set. Standard size is 85mm x 55mm (or 3.5 x 2 inches in the US). In that space, you need to communicate: who this person is, what the company is, how to reach them, and what kind of company this is.

The last item is usually neglected. The card communicates brand character through every decision: paper stock, print process, typeface, layout density, the presence or absence of a distinctive design element. A card on thin, white coated stock in a default system font communicates something. A card on thick, uncoated cotton stock with a letterpress impression communicates something very different.

I design business cards with the print specification in mind from the start. A card that will be letterpress-printed on 600gsm cotton stock needs a different design than one that will be digitally printed on 350gsm coated stock. The production process is part of the design.

## Stock and Finish Decisions

The paper stock is the first decision I make for any stationery project. It sets the register for everything that follows.

**Coated stock** produces vivid color and sharp edge definition. It feels smooth and clinical. Appropriate for brands that want to feel polished and precise.

**Uncoated stock** absorbs ink and produces colors with a matte, slightly muted quality. It feels more textured and organic. Appropriate for brands that want to feel approachable, craft-oriented, or understated-premium.

**Cotton or rag stock** is the highest-end option. The texture is distinctive, the weight is substantial, and it responds beautifully to letterpress and foil. Appropriate for law firms, financial advisors, high-end design studios, any context where premium tactile quality is the brand message.

Weight matters too. Cards below 300gsm feel flimsy. 350gsm is a good standard for professional applications. 500-700gsm is for maximum impact.

## The Complete Stationery Set

A complete stationery system typically includes:

- Business cards (front and back)
- Letterhead (print and digital/Word versions)
- Compliment slip
- Envelope (DL and sometimes A4)
- Email signature template

Each item has different design requirements. Letterhead needs a generous area for correspondence content, which means the brand elements need to be restrained and peripheral, not dominant. Envelopes often get the least design attention and the most handling, which means they benefit from distinctive design more than any other element.

## The Digital Letterhead Problem

Most correspondence happens digitally now. The Microsoft Word version of the letterhead template is used more than the printed version. This creates a design problem: a letterhead designed for professional print looks wrong or breaks when opened in Word.

I design digital letterhead templates explicitly for Word (or Google Docs, depending on the client's environment). The design is simpler than the print version, constrained to what the word processor can handle: JPEG or PNG header image, standard system fonts, basic layout grid. The goal is a template that non-designers can actually use without it falling apart.

## Getting the Print Right

Stationery is high-visibility print work. A production error on a brochure might affect a limited audience. A production error on business cards affects every person who receives one. I request printed proofs before approving full runs, and I check proofs against the physical substrate, not the screen representation.

The investment in quality stationery production pays for itself in brand credibility.
