## The Software Trap

The moment you open Illustrator, you start making software decisions instead of design decisions. You pick a tool. You choose a stroke weight. You start nudging anchor points. All of that activity feels productive, and none of it is thinking.

I made this mistake for the first two years of my career. I'd open a new document, start playing with shapes and type, and produce five or six polished-looking directions that were all variations on whatever the software made easy. I wasn't designing — I was executing. The brief was just a prompt to start clicking.

## What Sketching Actually Does

Paper removes the feedback loop that makes software seductive. When you draw a mark on paper, you get no immediate reward. The line is just a line. It doesn't snap to a grid, it doesn't show you the color fill, it doesn't autocomplete into a shape. The only thing that matters is whether the idea is good.

This constraint forces clarity. On paper, I have to understand what I'm trying to draw before I draw it. I can't discover a direction by accident. I have to commit to a concept, however rough, and put it down.

## The Volume Game

The other thing paper does is enable volume. I can fill a page with thumbnail sketches in twenty minutes. The same twenty minutes in Illustrator produces one half-refined mark that I'm already emotionally invested in.

Volume matters because logo design is partly a numbers game. The more distinct concepts I generate, the higher the probability that one of them is actually right. I aim for at least fifty sketches before I evaluate any of them. Most are immediately discardable. That's fine. The five that aren't are worth the other forty-five.

## What I'm Looking For

I'm not looking for finished marks in the sketch phase. I'm looking for conceptual clarity and formal potential. Does this idea have a core that's interesting? Is there a visual pun or an unexpected connection that could be developed? Does the rough form suggest something that a more refined version could elevate?

Some of my best logo marks started as genuinely ugly sketches. The concept was right and the execution was terrible. That's exactly what the sketch phase should produce — ideas worth pursuing in a better medium.

## The Translation Problem

Moving from paper to screen is where most designers lose the energy of the sketch. The rough, gestural quality of the paper mark gets smoothed away in the process of vectorizing. The result looks cleaner but feels more generic.

I try to preserve the specific quality that made the sketch interesting. If the sketch had an interesting weight relationship between two elements, I measure that ratio and preserve it. If there was a tension or an unexpected angle, I keep it even when the software wants to regularize it.

## When I Skip Paper

I don't always sketch by hand. For wordmark work that's primarily typographic — where the design question is about letterform modification and spacing rather than symbolic form — I work directly in software. The tools are genuinely better for that problem.

But for any mark involving symbol, icon, or abstract form, paper is always first. The habit has saved me from more bad software directions than I can count.
