Typographers talk about "typographic color" — not color in the chromatic sense, but the visual density or grayness of a block of set type. This concept is one of the most useful frameworks I've encountered for thinking about text in print design, and it's almost never discussed in general graphic design education.

Understanding typographic color helps explain why some text feels easy to read and visually settled, and why other text feels either too light and insubstantial or too heavy and oppressive.

## What Typographic Color Actually Means

Hold a printed page at arm's length and blur your eyes slightly. The text blocks become fields of gray — not individual letters, just areas of visual density. Some are dark and heavy; others are light and open. The variation in darkness is the typographic color.

This gray value is affected by: typeface choice (some typefaces are inherently heavier or lighter at the same point size), weight (regular, light, bold), size, tracking (letter-spacing), leading (line-spacing), and column width.

Controlling typographic color means understanding how these variables interact to produce the overall density of a text block.

## Even Color as the Standard for Body Text

For continuous body text, even typographic color — consistent, medium gray without dark spots or light gaps — is the standard to aim for. Uneven color creates visual turbulence that the reader registers as discomfort or difficulty even without consciously noticing specific problems.

Common causes of uneven color: inconsistent word spacing caused by full justification without hyphenation (rivers of white run through the text), inconsistent letter spacing caused by poor kerning or automatic tracking, and mixed weights or sizes within a paragraph.

I read body text proofs specifically looking for color evenness — areas that look darker or lighter than the surrounding text. These almost always indicate a setting problem worth addressing.

## Using Typographic Color Intentionally

Once you understand typographic color, you can use it as a design tool. A bold subheading creates a dark band of color that visually separates sections. A lighter weight secondary column creates a tonal contrast with a heavier primary column. A pull quote set in large, widely tracked capitals creates a field of open, light color against the denser body text.

These contrast relationships — using typographic color to create visual hierarchy and rhythm — are distinct from tonal hierarchy created by chromatic color or size. They're subtle and powerful.

## The Optical Weight of Different Typefaces

Two typefaces set at identical specifications — same size, weight, leading, tracking — can have completely different typographic color because they have different design dimensions. A typeface with a large x-height, tight apertures, and high stroke contrast will produce darker color than one with a small x-height, open apertures, and low stroke contrast.

When I switch typefaces in an established layout, I always recheck the typographic color and adjust leading or tracking to re-establish the same visual density if necessary. The specifications that worked for one typeface may produce completely different color with another.

## Training Color Perception

Reading for typographic color is a skill that develops with practice. I recommend a specific exercise: print the same paragraph in several different typefaces at the same nominal specifications, step back, and compare the grayness. The variation will be striking.

Then adjust the settings — add leading here, reduce tracking there — until they all produce equivalent gray values. The adjustments required teach you the relationships between the variables more directly than any theoretical explanation can.

Text that looks right usually has good typographic color. When text feels wrong but you can't identify why, check the color first.
