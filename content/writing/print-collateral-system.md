A business card, a letterhead, an envelope, a brochure — these pieces are rarely designed together, and they're rarely designed with each other in mind. The result is a set of print materials that share the same logo and colors but don't feel like a family.

Designing print collateral as a system rather than as individual pieces is a discipline that most design briefs don't explicitly request, but every client benefits from.

## Define the Grid First

A print collateral system starts with a shared grid — a set of spatial proportions and column structures that apply across all pieces, scaled to each format. If the business card, the letterhead, and the brochure all use a consistent margin-to-format ratio and a consistent column structure, they'll share a spatial quality that feels unified even when the content is completely different.

I define this grid at the beginning of a collateral project, before designing any individual piece. It's the invisible architecture that makes disparate pieces feel related.

## The Repeated Visual Elements

Beyond the logo and color palette, a system needs a vocabulary of graphic elements that recur across pieces: a distinctive treatment for dividing lines, a specific way of handling captions, a characteristic use of white space as a layout element, a consistent approach to imagery or illustration.

These elements don't have to be prominent — they can be subtle. But when someone picks up the business card after reading the brochure, they should register (even unconsciously) that these objects come from the same visual world.

## Paper Stock Decisions

For a premium print system, paper stock is a brand decision. Using the same paper family across business cards, letterhead, and brochure creates physical consistency — the pieces feel alike in the hand, not just to the eye.

This requires coordinating with a paper supplier early in the project to confirm that the same family is available in the weights needed for each application (thicker for business cards, lighter for letterhead, medium for brochure).

## The Business Card as Distillation

I use the business card as the design challenge that distills the whole system. It's the smallest format, the most constrained, and the most frequently handled. If the brand system works beautifully at business card scale, it'll work at every other scale.

I design the business card first and let the solutions I discover there inform the larger pieces.

## Handling Print Specifications Across Vendors

Large print systems are often split across multiple vendors — business cards at one printer, letterhead at another, brochures at a third. Each vendor has different color calibration, different paper handling, and different production tolerances.

Specifying Pantone colors throughout the system (rather than relying on CMYK matching) is the most effective way to maintain color consistency across multiple vendors. The Pantone specification is an instruction that any competent printer can follow regardless of their specific equipment.
