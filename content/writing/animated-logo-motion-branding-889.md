## The Logo Animation Is Not Optional Anymore

A few years ago, an animated logo was a bonus — nice to have for digital contexts but not essential. Now it's a basic brand asset. Any brand with a digital presence needs a considered animation for the logo, whether that's a full motion reveal or a simple hold with a settled entrance.

Clients who don't think they need this are surprised to find their static logo placed between animated content looking like a forgotten relic.

## The Concept Before the Keyframes

The single biggest mistake in logo animation is going straight to After Effects without a concept. The result is animation that moves without communicating — logo elements that scale in for no reason, rotate because rotation is available, flash because the effect exists.

Before I open any animation software, I define: what does this animation communicate? What feeling does the brand need to create in the moment the logo appears? The animation is serving the brand, not demonstrating software capability.

## Types of Logo Animation

**Reveal**: the logo builds from nothing to its complete form. Can be element-by-element, stroke-by-stroke, or as a complete reveal from a graphic device. The most common approach.

**Signature**: the logo appears as if being drawn or written. Works particularly well for script or hand-drawn logotypes. Creates a sense of personal craft.

**Assemble**: logo elements come together from disparate positions to form the complete mark. Communicates unity and cohesion. Works well for marks built from distinct components.

**Pulse**: the complete logo appears and makes a subtle motion — breathes, pulses, shimmers. A hold animation rather than a reveal. Works for contexts where the logo is present throughout rather than introduced once.

**Transform**: the logo emerges from or transforms into another graphic element. The most conceptually complex approach. Works when there's a clear conceptual relationship between the starting state and the logo.

## Duration and Context

Logo animations need to be appropriate to their context. A brand video bumper might have 3-5 seconds. A loading screen might have 2-3 seconds. A transition element might have under 1 second.

I design the core animation for the primary context and then create simplified, shorter versions for secondary contexts. An animation designed for 4 seconds cannot simply be time-compressed to 1 second and remain effective — it needs to be redesigned for the shorter duration.

## Delivering Multiple Versions

A complete animated logo delivery includes multiple versions:

- Full-length reveal for primary brand contexts
- Shortened version (under 2 seconds) for secondary uses
- Loop version for sustained-display contexts
- On dark and on light background versions

Each version is delivered as a rendered file (typically ProRes or H.264 with alpha channel) and with the After Effects source file for future modification.
