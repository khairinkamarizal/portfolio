Building a brand color palette is one of the most visible parts of identity design, which makes it one of the most likely to go wrong. Clients have strong feelings about color. Trends pull in specific directions. And the gap between "colors I like" and "colors that work as a system" is wider than most people realize.

Here's how I actually build a palette — from the first strategic conversation to the final specifications.

## The Brief Before the Colors

I don't look at swatches until I've answered a set of brand positioning questions. What market position is this brand claiming? Who are the competitors? What emotional register does the brand need? What physical environments will the brand appear in?

These questions aren't just due diligence — they're the actual design brief for the color system. A color palette that doesn't answer a strategic question is just decoration.

## Competitive Audit

Once I have the positioning context, I map the category. I pull color profiles of every significant competitor and lay them out as a grid. This tells me three things: where the category conventions are (so I can decide whether to follow or deviate), where the overcrowding is (colors to avoid because differentiation is impossible), and where the gaps are (colors that have no strong owner in the space).

This audit often surfaces the most important insight in the whole color process. Sometimes a client thinks they want blue but the audit shows every single competitor is blue. That's the conversation that reframes everything.

## Primary Color First

I establish the primary brand color first and spend the most time on it. This is the color that will appear most frequently, anchor the system, and become the color people associate with the brand. It needs to work across print, screen, environmental, and merchandise applications.

I typically develop three to five primary color candidates — different hues or different saturations/values of a single hue — and present them with rationale. I'm not asking the client to pick their favorite. I'm asking them to evaluate which one best serves the strategy.

## Building the System Around the Primary

Once the primary is confirmed, I build outward. The secondary palette typically includes one or two colors that complement or contrast the primary in a way that adds range and flexibility without competing.

Then I build a neutral family — usually two to four values from near-white to near-black with temperature that aligns with the primary palette. Warm primaries get warm neutrals. Cool primaries get cool neutrals.

Finally, I add a functional accent color if the system needs one — something high-contrast for call-to-action elements or data visualization. This color often breaks the temperature logic deliberately to create snap.

## Specifying for Reality

Every color in the system gets full specifications: Pantone (coated and uncoated), CMYK, RGB, and hex. I also test every color in print (proofed on actual paper stocks), on screen (across different monitors and in light/dark environments), and in any specialty applications relevant to the brand.

Colors shift dramatically between screen and print. I've had primary colors that looked perfect in Figma print as a completely different hue on uncoated stock. Specs need to account for this — sometimes the Pantone value and the RGB value are visual equivalents rather than direct conversions.

## Documenting the Logic

The palette isn't done when the colors are chosen. It's done when the usage logic is documented. Which color is primary? What's it used for? When do secondary colors lead versus support? Are there combinations to avoid?

A palette without usage rules is a collection of colors. A palette with usage rules is a system. The system is what clients actually need.
