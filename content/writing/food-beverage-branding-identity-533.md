## The Appetite Appeal Problem

Food and beverage brands have to do something most other brands don't: they have to make you want to consume something before you've consumed it. The brand identity does this work before the product even opens.

When I first started doing food and beverage work, I treated it like any other brand identity project. I focused on distinctiveness, strategy, appropriate personality. That produced competent work. What I was missing was appetite appeal — the specific quality that makes a brand feel delicious before the product is experienced.

Appetite appeal is real, it's designed, and it's a learnable skill.

## Warmth and Texture as Brand Tools

The most appetite-appealing food brands I've worked on or studied share a few visual qualities: warmth in the color palette, texture in the materials or illustration style, and a sense of craft or care in the typography and layout.

Warmth comes from the color temperature — creams, terracottas, warm tans, rich browns, deep reds. These are the colors of food itself: bread, spice, fruit, earth. They signal edibility at a sensory level.

Texture — either in the material (textured paper, uncoated stock, screen printing) or in the illustration style (hand-drawn elements, watercolor, imperfect marks) — signals artisanal production. It says someone made this carefully, not that a machine produced it at volume.

## Shelf Presence Is a Different Problem

Retail packaging and brand identity intersect at shelf presence: can this brand be spotted from eight feet away while someone is moving down an aisle? That's a completely different design problem from "does this logo look good on a website."

Shelf presence requires high contrast between the brand and its competitors, distinctive silhouette, and a color that reads from a distance. I study category shelf sets before designing for any food brand — I take photos of the actual shelf where the product will live and design with that context in mind.

The mistake is designing for how a product looks on a white background in an e-commerce photo. That's not where consumers make the decision.

## The Typeface Question in Food

Food and beverage branding has well-established typographic conventions that are worth knowing even if you plan to break them.

Artisanal and craft products often use hand-lettering, historical type revivals, or condensed serifs. Premium and luxury food brands often use editorial typefaces with high contrast. Mass-market food brands tend toward bold, legible grotesques with maximum readability at small sizes.

Within these conventions, the room for differentiation is in how you handle the type: the specific choice, the sizing, the spacing, the combination with other elements.

## Where Clients Get It Wrong

The most common mistake I see food and beverage clients make is over-designing the brand to look impressive in a brand deck and under-considering how it will look applied to actual packaging.

Beautiful logos printed in offset lithography on uncoated kraft paper look very different from the same logos on screen or on coated card. I always request physical material samples before finalizing a food brand identity.

The second most common mistake: treating the product as separate from the brand. The brand identity for a food product should feel continuous with the product itself — the color should feel like something you'd find in the product, the texture should feel like the product's texture, the typography should feel like the brand's personality embodied in food.

When the brand and the product feel like they came from the same world, the identity is working.
