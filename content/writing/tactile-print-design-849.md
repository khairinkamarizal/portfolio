## Touch Before Reading

When someone picks up a printed piece — a brochure, a business card, a book — they touch it before they read it. The physical experience of the material creates an impression in the first second that colors everything that follows.

This means paper selection and surface treatment are design decisions with real communicative consequences, not production details to be delegated to the printer.

## Paper as Message

Different paper stocks communicate different things before a word is read:

**Uncoated stock** communicates naturalness, warmth, and craft. The surface absorbs ink slightly, creating soft edges and a matte finish that feels human and considered. Used extensively in artisan, cultural, and editorial contexts.

**Coated gloss stock** communicates precision, vibrancy, and commercial quality. Colors are vivid, images are sharp, the surface reflects light. Used in commercial catalogues, fashion imagery, and contexts where production value is the message.

**Silk or satin coated** sits between the two: good color reproduction, reduced glare, softer feel. Versatile and underused — many designers default to full gloss when silk would serve better.

**Cotton and fine papers** communicate premium positioning and permanence. The weight and texture are distinctive enough to create immediate sensory impact. Business cards, invitations, and certificates that need to feel important.

## Texture and Embossing

Texture added to a surface — through stock selection, varnishes, or embossing — creates visual and tactile interest that flat printing can't replicate.

A soft-touch matte laminate transforms a cover into something irresistible to touch. A blind emboss adds a hidden detail that rewards close inspection. Spot UV on a matte surface plays light in a way that creates surprise and delight.

These treatments cost more. They also create the quality impression that makes a printed piece feel worth keeping rather than discarding.

## The Envelope and the Outer Shell

For direct mail and invitation work, the envelope or outer packaging sets the expectation before the piece is opened. A cheap envelope undermines a premium inner piece. An unexpected texture or format creates curiosity before the reveal.

I design the full physical experience — not just the primary printed surface, but everything the recipient touches from first contact to final read.

## When Restraint Is the Right Call

Not everything benefits from elaborate finishing. Sometimes a single well-chosen uncoated stock, cleanly printed in two colors, is more elegant than a complex multi-process production. The message and the material need to be in conversation — the material should reinforce the message, not overwhelm it.

The question is always: what does this piece need to feel like to serve its purpose? The answer sometimes requires elaborate production. It sometimes requires radical restraint. Both require the same quality of decision-making.
