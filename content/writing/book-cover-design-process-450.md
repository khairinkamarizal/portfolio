Book cover design is a discipline I came to from brand identity work, and the skills transfer more directly than you might expect. In both disciplines, you're compressing complex meaning into a small visual field, designing for a viewer who is making a quick decision, and working within a system where the visual language has to be consistent with the content's character.

The differences are significant too. A brand identity is a permanent system. A book cover is a single object. The constraints are tighter, the concept work is deeper, and the typography carries more of the entire communication load.

## The Cover's Job

A book cover does three things: attracts, identifies, and positions. It attracts the right reader's attention in a field of competing covers. It identifies genre and category so the right reader recognizes this as their kind of book. And it positions the book relative to other books — premium vs. accessible, literary vs. commercial, debut vs. established author.

A cover that does all three for the right audience is successful, even if it doesn't win design awards. A cover that wins design awards but attracts the wrong readers, or misidentifies the genre, has failed the book.

## Starting With the Manuscript

When I take on a book cover project, I read the manuscript or at minimum the first fifty pages plus a synopsis. This is non-negotiable. I cannot design an honest cover for a book I don't understand.

What I'm looking for: the dominant emotional register, the visual world the prose creates, the central tension or question, the thing that makes this book different from similar books. These inform the concept, not the literal content.

I'm not looking for an image to illustrate the plot. The worst book covers are literal: they show a scene from the story, or they render the title's metaphor visually. These look fine and communicate nothing distinctive.

## Concept Before Execution

The cover concept is the idea that precedes the design. Not "I'll use a photograph of the ocean" but "this book is about the way grief makes familiar things unrecognizable, so the cover should present something familiar in a way that feels subtly wrong."

That kind of concept drives distinctive work. It gives every design decision a purpose: why this image, why this type treatment, why this color, why this composition. The concept is the brief I'm designing to.

## Typography as the Primary Design Element

In literary fiction and narrative nonfiction particularly, typography is often the most expressive element on the cover, not photography or illustration. The selection of the typeface, its size, its treatment, how it relates to any image — these decisions carry the majority of the cover's meaning.

I approach book cover typography with the same rigor as display type in any other context. The typeface selection needs to match the book's register. The size and weight needs to create the right emphasis. The relationship between title and author name needs to communicate hierarchy without making either element feel secondary.

## The Spine and Back Cover

Book cover design includes the spine and back cover, not just the front. The spine is what the reader sees on a shelf — often the only surface visible before they pull the book out. It needs to work on its own: legible title at small scale, visually distinctive in a row of other spines.

The back cover needs to work differently from the front: less visual drama, more information (copy, author bio, endorsements), but still within the same design language as the front.

I design all three surfaces as a unified object, not as three separate pieces.
