## Two Different Processes

Offset lithography and digital printing are fundamentally different processes that produce different results and have different constraints. Most designers treat them as equivalent — "just printing" — and end up surprised by production outcomes that could have been predicted.

Understanding the basic differences makes you a better print designer and a better production partner for your clients.

## How Offset Works

In offset lithography, ink is transferred from a plate to a rubber blanket to the substrate. Each color in the design gets its own plate. A four-color job uses four plates (CMYK); a two-color job uses two plates; adding a Pantone spot color adds another plate.

Because the plates are cut for the job, there are setup costs — plate-making, press make-ready, waste paper for press calibration. Offset becomes cost-effective at volume. Below a certain quantity (typically 500–1000 depending on the piece), the fixed setup costs make offset uncompetitive with digital.

## How Digital Print Works

Digital printing transfers the design directly from a file to the substrate using either inkjet or electrophotographic (toner-based) technology. No plates, minimal setup. The cost per unit is relatively consistent regardless of quantity.

This makes digital printing ideal for short runs, variable data printing, and jobs where the quantity is uncertain. It also allows printing on demand, which reduces inventory risk.

## Quality Differences

For most print applications, the quality difference between well-run digital and offset is not significant to a non-print-specialist eye. Both produce professional, high-quality output.

Where offset has real advantages: very large runs where consistency across the run matters, applications requiring exact Pantone color matching, work on unusual substrates or with specialty inks, and the highest-quality reproduction of fine detail and tonal range in photography.

Where digital has real advantages: short runs, variable data, quick turnaround, and projects where exact color matching to Pantone isn't critical.

## File Preparation Differences

Both processes require CMYK files, embedded fonts, correct bleed, and PDF/X output. The key differences:

**Color profiles**: offset uses press-specific ICC profiles (FOGRA39 for European offset, SWOP for North American). Digital print equipment has its own profiles. Ask your printer which to use.

**Spot colors**: offset handles Pantone spot colors as separate ink channels. Most digital printers simulate Pantone values with CMYK, which may not match exactly. If exact Pantone matching is essential, offset is usually the right process.

**Image resolution**: both processes want 300ppi at print size, but high-quality offset can justify 350–400ppi for detailed photographic work.

## The Production Conversation

The best outcome comes from having this conversation with your printer before designing rather than after. Tell them what you're trying to achieve, what quantity and timeline you're working with, and what your budget is. They'll recommend the right process. That recommendation should inform your design decisions, not the reverse.
