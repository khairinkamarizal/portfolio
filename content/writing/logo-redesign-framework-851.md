## The Redesign Is Not a Blank Slate

Every logo redesign brief contains an implicit instruction that clients often don't articulate: preserve what works. A logo redesign that throws out everything the existing mark has built — recognition, association, emotional equity — destroys value before it creates any.

My first task in every redesign brief is to audit what exists before proposing what should change.

## The Equity Audit

I evaluate existing logos against several dimensions:

**Recognition**: does the target audience know this mark? Can they identify the brand from the mark alone? Recognition is built over time and is expensive to rebuild.

**Association**: what do people associate with the existing mark? If those associations are positive and relevant to the brand's positioning, they're worth preserving. If they're negative or irrelevant, they're worth discarding.

**Structural quality**: is the existing mark well-constructed? Is it scalable? Does it reproduce cleanly? Marks with good structure may just need visual modernization; marks with structural problems need more fundamental work.

**Flexibility**: does the existing system have the lockups and color versions needed for current applications? A mark designed before social media may not have the square or small-format versions the brand now needs.

## What "Modernization" Usually Means

Most logo redesigns I'm asked to do aren't conceptual changes — they're modernizations of an existing visual approach. Tightening up the geometry, updating the typeface, cleaning up inconsistencies, improving scalability.

In these cases I keep the core recognition elements — the basic form, the color family, the general typographic personality — and refine the execution. The mark looks better; the brand still looks like itself.

## When the Concept Needs to Change

Sometimes the existing concept is wrong — not just dated, but conceptually misaligned with the current brand. This is rare and requires evidence beyond "it looks old." The evidence I look for: the brand has genuinely repositioned, the existing mark communicates something the brand actively wants to move away from, or the mark has accumulated negative associations that can't be addressed through visual refinement.

In these cases a more fundamental redesign is justified, but even here I look for threads of continuity — color family, structural approach, proportional logic — that can carry recognition through the change.

## Presenting the Redesign

When I present a redesign, I always show the before-and-after together. I want the client to see clearly what has changed and what hasn't, and I explain the rationale for each significant change.

This prevents the "why didn't you do more?" or "why did you change so much?" reactions that come from presenting the new work in isolation. The comparison makes the decisions legible.

## The Long View

The most successful logo redesigns are the ones that, five years later, feel like they were always what the brand looked like. The transition was handled so thoughtfully that the new mark feels like an evolution rather than a disruption.

That's the goal: change enough to serve the brand's current needs, preserve enough to honor what's already been built.
