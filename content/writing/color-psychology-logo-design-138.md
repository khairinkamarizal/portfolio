## The Problem with Color Meaning Charts

You've seen the charts. Blue = trust. Green = nature. Red = energy. Yellow = optimism. These associations are frequently cited in design writing and client presentations.

They're not wrong exactly. But they're dangerously oversimplified. The meaning of any color in a design context depends heavily on the specific hue and saturation, the context it appears in, the culture of the audience, and the other colors it's used with. A simple equivalence between a color and a meaning doesn't capture any of that.

## Context Modifies Meaning

The same blue can mean corporate reliability (IBM's navy), medical trust (pharmaceutical brand blues), or tech dynamism (Facebook blue) — depending on how it's used and where. The blue does work, but the work varies enormously based on context.

This is why applying the associations chart directly ("my client wants to feel trustworthy, so we use blue") produces generic, category-average work. The chart tells you what's already being done. It doesn't tell you what's distinctive.

## Cultural Variation Is Real

Color associations vary significantly across cultures. White is associated with purity and weddings in Western contexts and with mourning in many East Asian contexts. The saturated red associated with luck and prosperity in Chinese culture reads differently in other markets.

For brands operating across cultural contexts, this matters. I always research color associations in the specific markets where a brand will operate.

## What Research Actually Shows

The more rigorous research on color in branding focuses less on specific color meanings and more on fit — whether the color is appropriate for the brand's category and positioning. Research by Labrecque and Milne (and others) suggests that appropriateness and distinctiveness matter more than following supposed universal meanings.

A color that's different from competitors in the same category draws attention and builds recognition — even if it doesn't fit the "meaning" typically associated with that category.

## My Actual Process

In practice, I think about color for logo work in terms of: what the brand needs to communicate (personality, positioning, values), what the competitive landscape looks like (what colors dominate, what's available), what associations the proposed color carries in the relevant cultural contexts, and how the color performs technically across all the applications it needs to serve.

The chart is a starting point for a conversation, not an answer. The real color work is contextual and strategic.
