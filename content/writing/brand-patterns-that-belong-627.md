I've audited brand systems where the pattern was clearly bought from a stock pattern library, recolored in brand colors, and called done. Sometimes the pattern doesn't even conflict with the brand; it's just completely disconnected from it. It could belong to any company that bought the same stock file. This is a significant missed opportunity.

A well-designed brand pattern is one of the highest-recognition identity elements a brand can have. It extends the brand's visual presence into contexts where the logo can't go — packaging interiors, textile prints, environmental graphics, background treatments. When the pattern is right, it's as recognizable as the logo.

## Where Brand Patterns Come From

The best brand patterns derive from elements already present in the identity system. Geometric forms from the logo mark are an obvious source — if the mark uses a particular geometric motif, that motif can be extracted, scaled, repeated, and arranged into a pattern that feels continuous with the logo without replicating it.

Letterforms are another source. A monogram or an initial letterform extracted from the wordmark and arranged as a pattern has the dual function of being brand-decorative and continuously referencing the brand name. This is a classic approach for good reason.

Brand category or product-derived imagery can also work. A botanical brand that uses leaf forms in its identity can derive a pattern from abstracted versions of those forms. A construction company whose identity uses angular geometric forms can derive a pattern from those angles.

The test: if you removed the brand colors and showed someone the pattern, could they connect it back to the brand mark or wordmark? If yes, the pattern is doing identity work.

## Scale and Density Decisions

Pattern design involves two key decisions beyond the motif itself: scale and density.

Scale — how large the pattern unit is relative to the surface — dramatically affects the feeling of a pattern. A large-scale pattern of the same motif reads as bold, confident, almost architectural. A small-scale version of the same motif reads as textural, refined, almost like fabric weave.

Density — how tightly the units are packed — affects the visual weight of the pattern and how it interacts with content placed over it. A dense pattern competes with content. An open pattern supports it. I usually design patterns at two or three scales for different applications.

## Color Variants

Every brand pattern needs to work across the brand's color palette. I typically build:

- Brand color on white
- White on brand color
- Brand color on brand secondary color (if applicable)
- Black on white / white on black for single-color applications

Testing the pattern in all of these combinations early reveals which color relationships work best and ensures the pattern is flexible enough to be useful.

## When to Foreground, When to Background

Patterns work in two modes: as background treatments (always subordinate to content placed over them) and as foreground elements (pattern as the primary visual on a surface with no competing content). The pattern design should work in both modes, but the scale and density that works best for each mode is often different.

For background use, the pattern needs to be quiet enough to support content above it without competing. For foreground use, it needs to be bold enough to carry a surface on its own. These two demands often require two different scale versions of the same underlying pattern.
