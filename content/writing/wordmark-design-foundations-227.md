A wordmark is a logo that is entirely typographic — the brand name, set in a typeface that may be custom, modified, or selected from existing fonts, as the complete identity mark. No symbol, no icon, just letters.

When it works, a wordmark is one of the most powerful forms of brand identity. The name and the mark are the same thing. Recognition of one is recognition of the other. Think of Coca-Cola, Google, FedEx, Calvin Klein. The word itself is the visual identity.

When it doesn't work, a wordmark is just a name in a font — a layout decision, not a design decision. The distinction matters enormously.

## Typeface Selection as Brand Decision

In a wordmark, typeface selection is the primary design decision. Everything else — spacing, any modifications, color — amplifies or refines the choice, but the typeface establishes the mark's fundamental character.

I approach wordmark typeface selection the same way I approach any strategic design decision: competitive landscape first, then brand positioning, then formal criteria. What are the competitors doing typographically? What does the typeface need to communicate? What formal qualities (weight, contrast, historical reference, geometric or humanist construction) serve the brand's positioning?

I present three to five typeface directions in early stages, each representing a distinct positioning choice, before narrowing to one for development.

## Modification and Customization

Using an unmodified typeface for a wordmark is a starting point, not a finished product. I always make targeted modifications — adjusted spacing, refined curves, custom terminals, modified specific characters — to transform a typeface selection into a proprietary mark.

The depth of modification depends on the budget and the strategic need for distinctiveness. A startup with limited resources might get carefully optimized spacing and one or two character tweaks. A major brand with a long time horizon might warrant fully custom letterforms drawn from scratch.

Even small modifications matter. Adjusting the crossbar of the 'A', customizing the ear of a lowercase 'g', or tightening the spacing beyond what the typeface's metrics suggest can give a wordmark a feeling of intentional craft that off-the-shelf type never has.

## Spacing is Everything

Of all the wordmark refinements I make, optical spacing adjustment is the one that most consistently transforms a mark from amateur to professional. Default typeface metrics are designed for body text at moderate sizes. At logo scale, these metrics often produce visually uneven spacing — some pairs looking too tight, others too loose.

I set every wordmark using optical rather than metric kerning, and then manually adjust pair by pair, zoomed to large scale, until the rhythm is even. This process takes time. It's worth every minute.

## Testing at Extremes

Wordmarks need to perform at extreme scales. I test every wordmark at 16px width (favicon scale), at 48px width (app icon), at standard logo size (approximately 200px), and at large format scale (minimum 1 meter wide).

Different things go wrong at different scales. Very thin strokes disappear at small sizes. Open letterforms can close up. Counter spaces become unclear. These problems need solutions built into the mark itself — optically adjusted weights, simplified details — not workarounds applied case by case.

## The Case Question

Uppercase, mixed case, or lowercase? This choice carries significant brand meaning. All-caps reads as authoritative, classic, and formal. Mixed case (title case) is balanced and approachable. All-lowercase is casual, modern, and often digital-native.

I evaluate case options as part of the typeface exploration rather than treating it as a secondary decision. Sometimes a brand's positioning makes the answer obvious. Often it's one of the most productive conversations in the design process.
