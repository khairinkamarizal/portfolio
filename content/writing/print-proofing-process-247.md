Print proofing is the step that separates designers who understand print from those who've just learned to save PDFs. I've talked to designers who've sent projects to print based solely on screen review and then been surprised when the output didn't match expectations. The surprise is the problem — the proofing process exists precisely to eliminate it.

## Why Screen Review Isn't Sufficient

Screen displays and print output are fundamentally different reproduction technologies with different color gamuts, different resolutions, and different physical properties. A color that looks correct on a calibrated monitor will often look different when printed, because:

The RGB color space of a screen is larger than the CMYK gamut of most printing processes. Colors that are possible on screen aren't possible in print — they get mapped to the nearest printable equivalent, which may look noticeably different.

Different paper stocks absorb ink differently. The same CMYK values on coated stock and uncoated stock produce different visual results. Screen can't simulate this.

Screen light is emitted; print light is reflected. Our eyes perceive these differently, particularly in darker colors and subtle color relationships.

## The Soft Proof

Before a physical proof, I use soft proofing — on-screen simulation of the print output using ICC profiles for the specific paper and press. In InDesign and Photoshop, View > Proof Colors with the appropriate profile enables this.

Soft proofing catches the most obvious color gamut problems without requiring physical output. If a color is dramatically outside the print gamut, it will look visibly different in the soft proof. I correct these before sending anything to print.

Soft proofing is not a substitute for physical proofing. It's a first filter.

## Types of Physical Proofs

Contract proof: a high-quality output on proofing paper using calibrated equipment, certified to simulate the actual print press output. This is the standard for commercial print work where color accuracy is critical — brand materials, packaging, high-quality marketing print.

Inkjet proof on matching stock: an inkjet print on the actual paper stock the job will print on. Less precisely calibrated than a contract proof but very useful for evaluating how design decisions look on the actual material.

Press proof: running actual job files on the actual press with the actual paper. The most accurate proof and the most expensive. Appropriate for very high-value jobs or situations where ink color matching is critical and a contract proof isn't sufficient.

## What I Check on Proofs

Color accuracy against brand specifications — particularly any Pantone colors being simulated in CMYK, which should be as close as possible to the Pantone physical swatch.

Image quality — whether photos have reproduced with adequate detail and without visible banding or posterization.

Type legibility — whether small type is sharp and readable, particularly in reverse (white on color) which is more prone to ink fill problems.

Tonal relationships — whether the color palette relationships I designed on screen are maintained in print.

Overall impression — does the printed piece feel like the brand? Sometimes everything is technically correct but the sum doesn't produce the right impression.

## Proofing Is Client Service

Proofing isn't just technical quality control — it's client service. A client who approves a proof and then receives print output that matches it has no grounds for complaint. A client who never saw a proof and receives output they didn't expect has every grounds for complaint.

I include proofing as a standard project phase in print work. It's not optional and it's not free, but it's far less expensive than reprinting a job.
