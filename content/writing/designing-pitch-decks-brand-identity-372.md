Pitch decks and presentation design are the most frequently produced and least carefully designed brand materials in most organizations. The logo appears in the corner, someone chooses a theme that vaguely matches the brand colors, and the result is a presentation that looks like it came from the same template library everyone else uses.

A presentation is a brand touchpoint. Investors, partners, and clients sit with it for thirty to sixty minutes. It shapes their perception of the organization as much as any other material.

## Why Presentations Look Bad

The core problem is that presentation software — PowerPoint, Keynote, Google Slides — is built around templates and themes that optimize for ease, not for design quality. The defaults are mediocre. The constraints are real. And the design systems built for print and screen don't translate automatically to slide format.

I approach presentation design by ignoring the software defaults entirely. Master slides are built from scratch to match the brand system. Type sizes are set to match the brand typographic scale. Colors are taken from the exact brand palette values.

## The Slide Grid

Presentations need a grid as much as print work does. I build a slide grid with consistent margins, clear content areas, and a baseline grid for type alignment. Every slide template is built on this grid.

This creates consistency across the deck — each slide feels like it belongs to the same system rather than being composed independently. The viewer's eye adapts to the layout conventions and can focus on the content rather than re-orienting to a new composition on each slide.

## Type Hierarchy for Slides

Slide type hierarchy is different from print. The primary reading distance is ten to fifteen feet. Minimum readable body text is 24pt; I prefer 28–32pt. Headlines should be 40–54pt. Anything smaller than 20pt is a caption or a label, not body content.

The single most common presentation design mistake: putting too much text on a slide in too-small type. If a slide has more than forty words, it's probably two slides.

## Data Visualization Consistency

Charts and graphs in presentations should use the brand color palette and typographic system. The default chart styles in presentation software are universally ugly and off-brand. I redesign every chart: custom colors from the brand palette, brand typeface for all labels, removed chartjunk (unnecessary grid lines, borders, backgrounds).

## Delivering the Template

A well-designed presentation template is only useful if the people who create presentations can use it. I deliver templates with explicit instructions, locked master slides for non-negotiable elements, and a library of pre-built slide types that cover the most common content needs. The goal is that any team member can create an on-brand presentation without design support.
