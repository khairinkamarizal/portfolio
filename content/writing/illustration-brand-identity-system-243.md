Illustration is one of the most powerful and most misused elements in brand identity work. At its best, a distinctive illustration style becomes a brand's most memorable visual signature — more recognizable than the logo in some contexts. At its worst, brand illustration is generic stock art applied inconsistently, doing nothing to differentiate the brand and much to make it look generic.

The difference between these outcomes is almost entirely in how the illustration system is conceived and specified.

## Why Illustration Works as Identity

Illustration carries personality in a way that photography can't. Photography documents reality; illustration creates a specific, stylized view of reality that is inherently the creator's interpretation. A brand illustration style is therefore inherently distinctive in a way that photography — which can only be art-directed toward existing visual traditions — often isn't.

The best brand illustration styles are recognizable even without any brand name or color present. The line quality, the character design, the color palette, the level of abstraction — these together create a fingerprint that belongs to the brand.

## Commissioning vs. Building

There are two ways to develop a brand illustration system: commission it from an illustrator whose existing style aligns with the brand's visual needs, or develop a custom style in-house or through a design process.

Commissioning from an established illustrator with a distinctive style is faster and often produces a result with more genuine character — the style has been developed over years by someone with real artistic skill. The risk is dependency on one person and the question of whether you own the style or just licensed it.

Building a custom style through a design process gives the brand ownership and the ability to have the style executed by many artists consistently. The risk is that custom-developed styles can feel designed rather than genuinely artistic.

I evaluate both approaches for each brand context and recommend based on the strategic needs, budget, and timeline.

## Style Specification

A brand illustration style needs to be specified with enough precision that multiple artists can execute it consistently. This means documenting: line weight and quality (thick/thin variation, rough or smooth, tapered or consistent), level of detail and abstraction, color palette and how it's applied, character design principles if figures are involved, perspective and spatial approach, and what the style explicitly excludes.

Reference illustrations — both examples of the correct style and examples of what's explicitly wrong — are essential. Abstract descriptions alone aren't sufficient to produce consistent execution across multiple artists.

## Integration with the Visual System

The illustration style needs to integrate with the rest of the visual identity — sharing the color palette, harmonizing with the typography, and working compositionally with the layout principles. An illustration style that was developed independently and then integrated with an existing brand identity often has seams — places where the illustration and the rest of the identity feel like they come from different sources.

The ideal is developing the illustration style in parallel with the broader visual identity, with both informing each other throughout the process.

## Knowing When Not to Illustrate

Not every brand needs illustration. Brands where precision, documentary reality, or corporate authority are primary values may be better served by photography or pure typography. Forcing illustration onto these contexts produces work that contradicts the brand's positioning.

Illustration is right when the brand genuinely benefits from its specific qualities: personality, warmth, imaginative interpretation, distinctiveness. When those qualities are relevant to the brief, illustration can be the most powerful tool available.
