The business card is the most concentrated design problem in print. You have a 85mm x 55mm surface (or 3.5 x 2 inches) to communicate who someone is, how to reach them, and what kind of organization they represent. The physical constraints force every element to justify its presence.

Most business cards are mediocre by default. They use a system font, a standard layout, and 350gsm coated white stock because that's what the online template allowed. The result is a card that communicates nothing except that no one made a deliberate decision about it.

A well-designed business card does the opposite. Every decision is deliberate.

## What the Card Must Communicate

At minimum: name, title, company/brand, and at least one contact method. That's the non-negotiable content set. Everything else is optional.

What I encourage clients to remove from cards: every piece of contact information that isn't their preferred contact method. If they primarily communicate by email, the card needs an email address. It probably doesn't need a fax number, a postal address, a LinkedIn URL, a Twitter handle, a website, and a phone number. Each additional piece of information reduces the emphasis on the most important one.

Restraint in content makes the card easier to use. The person who received the card can immediately identify how to reach you.

## Front and Back

Most cards are designed as front-only, with the back left blank or used only for a design element. This wastes half the surface.

The back can carry:
- A second language version of the information
- A visual element that gives the brand more presence
- A QR code that links to the contact's portfolio, LinkedIn, or the company website
- An extended contact set if the front is minimal
- A short statement of the company's purpose

If the back is blank, it should be blank by choice for a reason, not because nobody thought to use it.

## The Paper Stock Is Part of the Design

I've said this before and I'll say it again: the paper stock communicates brand character in the same way the typography does. A card on heavy cotton stock with a letterpress impression feels different from a card on standard coated stock with a digital print. Both might carry the same information and the same logo. They communicate very different things about the organization.

The stock specification is a design decision that should be made by the designer, not defaulted to whatever the printer offers as standard.

## Type Size and Legibility

Contact information on a business card needs to be legible. This sounds obvious but I regularly see cards where the designer prioritized visual minimalism over legibility — 6pt type in a light weight, reversed out of a complex background.

My minimum: 8pt for contact information, Regular weight minimum, with adequate contrast against the background. The card can be sophisticated and still legible. These aren't competing goals.

## The Hierarchy

The visual hierarchy on a card should answer, in order: who is this person? what's their role and organization? how do I reach them?

The name is the most important element and gets the most emphasis — weight, size, or color. The organization/title cluster is secondary. Contact information is tertiary.

This hierarchy is inverted from what many people intuitively want. Clients often want the logo and company name to dominate because they're thinking like a brand, not like a card recipient. The recipient needs to remember the person, not be reminded of the company. The person's name should win.

## Production Quality Over Production Quantity

For most professionals, a run of 250 or 500 cards on premium stock is better than 2,000 cards on standard stock. Cards on exceptional stock get kept. Cards that feel like every other card get discarded.

The business card is a physical leave-behind that represents the brand at every human interaction. Make it count.
