Photography is often the most visible element of a brand's visual identity in practice — it covers more surface area in marketing materials than the logo does. Yet most brand guidelines treat photography as a secondary consideration: a few adjectives ("authentic," "warm," "candid") and some good/bad examples. This is not enough to produce consistency.

Photography consistency requires art direction. And art direction requires specific, actionable guidance.

## What a Photography Style Actually Consists Of

A brand's photographic style is defined by a set of concrete variables: lighting approach, color treatment, subject matter and inclusion, composition conventions, post-processing approach, and what types of imagery are explicitly excluded.

"Warm and authentic" is not a photography brief. "Natural light, slightly warm color grade, people in authentic activity rather than posed, slightly loose composition with breathing room, no stock photography poses" is a photography brief.

The specificity of the brief determines the consistency of the output.

## Lighting as Brand Language

Lighting is one of the most immediately recognizable elements of a photographic style and one of the most difficult to fake without understanding it explicitly.

Hard directional light creates drama and precision — appropriate for fashion, luxury, and certain technology contexts. Soft, diffused light creates approachability and warmth. Available light creates authenticity and realness. Studio light with carefully controlled environments creates polish and control.

I specify lighting approach in brand photography guidelines not as a technical prescription (unless working with professional photographers who need that level of detail) but as a clear stylistic description with multiple reference examples.

## Color Grading Consistency

Color grading — the post-processing treatment applied to photographs — is probably the most controllable element of photographic style. A consistent color grade can make photographs taken in completely different conditions by different photographers feel like they belong together.

I develop a reference grade for brand photography and document it as specifically as possible: color temperature, white balance direction, shadow and highlight treatment, saturation adjustments, and any specific hue shifts.

For brands with in-house teams doing their own photography, I create a Lightroom or Capture One preset that produces the brand's reference grade. This converts a complex post-processing decision into a single click.

## Composition Conventions

Compositional habits — how subjects are framed, how much breathing room surrounds the primary subject, where the horizon sits, whether compositions are centered or dynamic — create recognizable visual fingerprints.

I document these as positive examples of the brand's compositional approach along with brief explanations. "We frame people loosely, not tight headshots" communicates clearly. "We leave space in the direction of movement or gaze" is specific and actionable.

## The Exclusion List

What a brand's photography should NOT include is often as important as what it should. Explicit exclusions prevent common mistakes: no stock photography clichés (handshakes, thumbs-up, generic teamwork poses), no certain color treatments that undermine brand positioning, no subject matter inconsistent with brand values.

The exclusion list catches the defaults that photographers and marketing teams will reach for if not guided away from them.

## Maintaining Consistency at Scale

For large organizations with photography happening across many markets and teams, consistency requires ongoing art direction, not just a style guide. I build photography review processes — regular audits, feedback loops, updated reference galleries — into the brand governance recommendation.

Style guides don't maintain themselves. Consistency is an active practice.
