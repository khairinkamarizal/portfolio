I've been collecting logos since I started in design. Not saving them to a Behance board — actually collecting: printed ephemera, old packaging, trade show programs from the 70s. The ones that have lasted fascinate me. And the qualities they share are consistent.

## Reduction, Not Addition

The logos that age well are the ones with nothing removable. Not because they're simple for simplicity's sake, but because the designer has found the minimum number of elements needed to express the idea. Every line, every curve, every negative space is load-bearing.

The logos that date themselves are the ones that add — gradients, shadows, bevels, textures — to compensate for an idea that isn't strong enough on its own. Decoration has a cultural timestamp. Structure doesn't.

## Geometric Confidence

Most enduring marks are built on geometric primitives: circles, squares, triangles. Not because geometry is inherently beautiful, but because geometric forms have a visual confidence that complex organic shapes lack. A circle is completely itself. An irregular blob is always negotiating with your eye.

This doesn't mean every mark should be a circle. It means every mark should have the same kind of certainty — where every curve and angle is exactly where it needs to be.

## The One-Idea Rule

Great logo marks express one idea. The Nike swoosh expresses speed and motion. The Apple mark expresses the name. The FedEx mark hides an arrow. One idea, clearly expressed.

When I see a mark that's trying to express the company's three brand values simultaneously, I know it's going to fail. Not because multiple ideas can't coexist visually, but because a mark trying to do too much ends up doing nothing legibly.

## Negative Space as Positive Design

The best marks use negative space actively. The FedEx arrow. The hidden bear in the Toblerone mountain. The arrow in the Amazon smile. These negative space elements create depth and cleverness without adding visual weight.

I think about negative space in every mark I design. What is the shape I'm creating *between* the positive elements? What does that shape say? Sometimes the negative space turns out to be more interesting than the positive form, and I flip my thinking entirely.

## What "Timeless" Actually Means

Timeless doesn't mean no aesthetic sensibility. Every enduring mark was contemporary when it was made. What it means is that the aesthetic sensibility is grounded in form relationships that don't depend on current trends. The IBM stripes could have been made in any decade because they're built on proportion and rhythm, not on a stylistic tic of a particular moment.

When I design a mark, I ask: will the idea still make sense in twenty years? If the answer depends on anyone currently knowing a specific cultural reference or aesthetic trend, the answer is probably no.
