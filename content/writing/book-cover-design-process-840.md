## The Book Cover Is a Poster

I treat every book cover brief like a poster brief. The same principles apply: a dominant visual element, clear typographic hierarchy, an emotional register that matches the content. The difference is that the format is constrained — typically a vertical rectangle — and the context is specific: it will be seen at thumbnail scale on a retail site, at actual scale on a bookshelf, and in someone's hand.

All three contexts matter, and they have different requirements.

## Reading the Book (or at Least Enough of It)

I read enough of any book I'm covering to have a direct experience of it. Not necessarily the whole thing — a novel might only need the first 50 pages and a summary to give me a real sense of voice and world — but enough to form my own relationship with the material rather than working purely from the author's or editor's description.

The distance between how a book is described and how it actually feels is often significant. A description might emphasize plot; the actual experience might be driven by a very specific emotional register that's more distinctive than the plot. The cover I design from direct engagement will typically be truer to the book's experience than one designed from a synopsis.

## The Concept Before the Execution

Before I start compositing, I develop the concept in writing: what is this cover trying to communicate, and through what visual strategy? One clear sentence per concept option.

"The cover communicates the claustrophobia of the domestic world through a close, uncomfortable crop on a mundane object" is a concept. "Dark and literary" is not a concept.

Concepts give me something to evaluate decisions against during execution. When I'm choosing between two image treatments, the concept tells me which one is right.

## Type on Covers

Typographic decisions on book covers carry enormous weight. The title treatment — size, weight, typeface, color — is often the most visible element from thumbnail distance and contributes heavily to genre signals.

Genre is a real constraint. A literary fiction cover and a thriller cover have different typographic vocabularies, and departing from those vocabularies creates confusion in retail contexts. Within genre expectations there's still enormous room for distinction, but the expectations need to be understood and deliberately navigated rather than ignored.

## The Thumbnail Test

I check every book cover design at the standard Amazon product image thumbnail size — roughly 150px wide. This is where most readers will first encounter the cover, and it's where many covers fall apart.

What reads at thumbnail: bold value contrast, simple composition, large type. What disappears: subtle detail, delicate type, fine-line illustration, complex image content.

If the cover doesn't communicate effectively at thumbnail scale, the retail performance will suffer regardless of how it looks at full size.

## Spine and Back Cover

A complete cover design includes spine and back cover. The spine is a different design problem — it's a thin vertical strip that needs to be legible horizontally when the book is on a shelf. The back cover is where design gives way to editorial content, but the typographic and color system from the front needs to carry through.

Designing all three elements as a unified system rather than three separate surfaces produces covers that feel like designed objects, not just designed fronts.
