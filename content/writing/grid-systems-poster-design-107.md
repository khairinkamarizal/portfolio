## The Grid Gets a Bad Reputation

People who don't understand grids think they make design look the same. People who do understand grids know that they're actually what makes design feel purposeful. A grid doesn't constrain creativity — it gives creativity something to push against.

When I lay out a poster without any structural thinking, the result tends to be timid. Elements float, nothing feels anchored, the composition lacks tension. When I work within and against a grid, things get interesting.

## Why Posters Need Structure

Posters deal with a fundamental challenge: lots of visual elements, limited space, zero interactivity. Everything has to be resolved in a single surface. Without structural logic, that complexity collapses into chaos.

A grid gives me a framework for making consistent decisions. It determines where elements can live, how they relate to each other, what proportions feel right. Once that framework exists, I can break it selectively — which is where the energy comes from.

## How I Build a Grid for a Poster

I start with the format. A portrait A1 poster needs a different grid than a square event flyer. The format's proportions suggest certain grid structures more than others.

From there, I decide on column count and margins based on the content. Complex posters with multiple elements — event listings, image, headline, sponsors — need more columns to accommodate the variety. Simpler posters with a dominant typographic element might work on a looser, coarser grid.

I almost always work with a baseline grid as well, particularly when there's body copy involved. The vertical rhythm it creates is subtle but felt.

## The Art of Breaking the Grid

The most visually exciting thing you can do on a grid is break it deliberately. An element that bleeds off the margin, a headline that spans columns unexpectedly, an image that sits at an unexpected angle — these moments work because the grid gives them context.

Without the grid, the break means nothing. With it, the break creates tension and interest.

## Communicating the Grid to Clients

Clients don't always care about grid systems. That's fine — they don't need to. What I do try to communicate is the intentionality behind the composition. "This is structured deliberately, not randomly assembled" is a useful thing for a client to understand, even if they don't need the technical details.

Good design has underlying logic. The grid is often that logic.
