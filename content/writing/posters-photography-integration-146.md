## The Problem with Photo Dumps

The most common failure mode for photography-heavy poster design is what I think of as the photo dump: an image placed on the canvas, text overlaid on top, done. The image and the type exist in separate visual worlds. The composition has no integration — the design is just text floating over a photo.

This approach is everywhere and it produces mediocre work almost without exception.

## What Integration Looks Like

Integrated poster design treats photography and typography as parts of a unified composition. The image doesn't just provide a background — it interacts with the type, the layout, the color. Text and image create meaning together rather than coexisting independently.

Some techniques that create integration: type that bleeds into the image, crop choices that create space specifically for type elements, image treatment (duotone, cut-out, halftone) that brings the photo into the same visual language as the typography, compositional alignment between photo and type elements.

## Image Selection Is Design Work

Choosing which photograph to use is not a production decision separate from design — it's a design decision. The framing, the tonal range, the orientation, the amount of visual complexity in the image all determine what the composition can do.

An image with strong visual weight distributed evenly doesn't leave room for type hierarchy. An image with clear subject and empty background gives the composition something to work with. I think about compositional requirements before selecting images.

## Treating Photography to Belong

Unmodified photography often exists in its own visual world, disconnected from the brand's graphic language. Treatment — duotone, monotone, halftone, high-contrast black and white, color grading — brings photographs into the brand's visual universe and helps them integrate with other graphic elements.

I default to some form of treatment on photography used in poster design, because untreated photographic realism usually sits uncomfortably next to flat type and graphic elements.

## When Photography Works Against the Poster

Sometimes the right choice is not to use photography. Complex images compete with text hierarchy. Photographs with too much tonal range or visual complexity can make typographic clarity impossible. If the hierarchy has to fight the image to be read, the image is wrong for the application.

A strong typographic poster or an illustration-based design will often outperform a photography-based one precisely because it has compositional control that photography doesn't offer.

## The Crop as a Design Tool

The crop — what portion of an image is shown, at what scale — is one of the most powerful compositional tools in photograph-based design. A tightly cropped fragment of an image can be more visually powerful than the full frame. Scale changes meaning. I use the crop aggressively.
