## Inverting the Typical Process

The standard approach to logo design is to work large — sketch big, build big, refine big — and then check that it works at small sizes. I've flipped this. I now think about small-size performance first and work backward.

This sounds counterintuitive. You'd think designing something complex requires space to see it clearly. But designing at small sizes first forces constraints that produce stronger marks at every size.

## What Small Size Reveals

At 32 pixels or at a postage-stamp thumbnail, you can only see a few things: overall silhouette, dominant contrast, whether the mark reads as a distinct shape or blends into a neutral blob.

Every detail you added at large scale — the fine inner stroke, the subtle texture, the delicate serif — disappears. What remains is the essential form. If that essential form is strong, you have a logo. If it isn't, you have decoration.

Starting small forces you to design the essential form first, before you add any detail. All the detail you add later is genuinely secondary — it enriches the mark at large sizes rather than constituting the mark at all sizes.

## The Favicon Test

I use a 32x32 favicon as my primary early test. I drop the mark into a 32x32 pixel box and ask: does this read as a shape? Does it have a recognizable silhouette? Can I identify it at a glance?

If it fails at 32x32, I don't proceed. I go back to the sketch phase and find a form that passes.

This sounds harsh. In practice it's fast. Marks that fail the favicon test usually have the same underlying problem: too many similar-weight elements competing for attention. The fix is to simplify — increase the contrast between the primary form and any supporting elements, or remove the supporting elements entirely.

## What Gets Revealed Is What Matters

The forms that survive small-size scrutiny are almost always better marks at large size too. Not because simplicity is always superior — complex marks can be excellent — but because the simplification process forces you to identify what is essential about the mark.

Once you know what's essential, you can build complexity on top of that foundation and every additional element is genuinely adding something rather than just filling space.

## The Counterargument

Some logos are designed for large-format use only. A stage backdrop, a building sign, a billboard. These contexts genuinely don't require small-size performance.

But even here, I start small as a design discipline. If the mark doesn't have a strong silhouette at small size, it won't have a strong presence at large scale either. Scale amplifies both strength and weakness.

## A Habit Worth Building

Next time you're developing a logo, after the first round of Illustrator construction, paste the mark into a document and scale it down to 32x32 pixels. Look at it. Really look at it.

What does it look like? Does it read? Does it have presence?

If not — and this is the important part — don't try to fix the small version. Go back to the large version and fix the essential form. The small version is a diagnostic, not the problem. Fix the diagnosis at the source.

Start small. Build from strength.
