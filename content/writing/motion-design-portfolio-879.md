## Portfolio as Positioning Statement

A motion design portfolio is not an archive of everything you've made. It's a curated argument for a specific type of work with a specific type of client. Every piece included should advance that argument.

Before assembling or updating a portfolio, I write down: what type of work do I want to be doing more of, and what type of client do I want to be doing it for? The portfolio is then built backwards from those answers.

## What to Include

Include work that represents the level and type of work you want to do next — not necessarily the level and type you've been doing. If you want to move into brand motion but your recent work has been explainer videos, include any brand motion work you have even if it's personal projects or spec work, and position it prominently.

Include work where you were the primary creative decision-maker. If you executed a design direction set entirely by someone else, the work demonstrates your technical execution, not your design thinking. Both matter, but be clear about which the work demonstrates.

## Case Studies Over Compilations

For a website portfolio, case studies — pages that walk through the problem, the approach, and the result for individual projects — communicate far more about how you work than a gallery of final outputs.

A thirty-second animation in a showreel tells a prospective client you can execute animation. A case study showing the brief, the storyboard, the concept rationale, and the final piece tells them you can think and communicate as well as execute. The latter is more persuasive for most clients.

## Showreel vs. Portfolio Site

Both serve different purposes in a motion design practice:

The showreel is for quick evaluation — someone scanning for a vendor, an agency looking for a freelancer, a creative director reviewing candidates. It should be short, strong, and fast-starting.

The portfolio site is for deeper evaluation — a client who's already interested wanting to understand your work and approach. It should include case studies, written context, and process material.

Many designers have one but not both. Both are worth maintaining.

## Updating Frequency

A portfolio that includes work from more than two or three years ago, without current work to anchor it, can create an impression of stagnancy. I update my portfolio at least twice a year — adding new work, retiring pieces that no longer represent my current level, and updating the overall framing.

The framing matters as much as the individual pieces. How you describe your work, what problems you say you solve, and what clients you say you work with all shape how a portfolio is read. These should evolve as your practice evolves.

## Personal Projects

Personal projects — work made outside client constraints, for your own creative exploration — are among the most valuable portfolio inclusions because they're the fullest expression of your actual design thinking without the compromises inherent in client work. Include them. Context them clearly as personal work. They often become the most interesting conversations in a portfolio review.
