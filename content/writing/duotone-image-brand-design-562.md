## Why Duotone Works

Duotone — mapping an image through two colors instead of a full spectrum — does something that color photography can't do: it locks the imagery into the brand's color world. A full-color photograph can look beautiful, but it brings its own palette with it. Every image introduces new colors that compete with the brand system.

Duotone photography has no competing palette. The colors in the image are the brand's colors. The photograph becomes part of the identity rather than something that sits alongside it.

## How Duotone Is Constructed

A duotone replaces the full color range of a photograph with a gradient between two selected colors. The shadows of the original image map to one color; the highlights map to the other.

The most common approach: shadows map to a dark color, highlights map to a light color. This preserves the image's tonal structure while coloring it entirely in brand terms.

But the interesting work happens when you push beyond the obvious. Mapping shadows to a brand color and highlights to white. Mapping shadows to black and highlights to a saturated brand color. Mapping shadows to one brand color and highlights to a second, creating an unexpected color chord in the midtones where the two blend.

## Selecting Images for Duotone Treatment

Not all images duotone well. The images that respond best have:

**High inherent contrast** — strong darks and lights in the original. A flat, low-contrast image becomes a flat, low-contrast duotone. The tonal structure of the original determines the tonal structure of the treatment.

**Simple, readable content** — duotone removes color detail, so images that depend on color differentiation to read (a colorful market scene, a landscape with complex foliage) can become confused after treatment. Images where form and light carry the content work much better.

**Clear focal subject** — a single strong subject against a simpler background. This ensures that after color information is removed, the image hierarchy is still clear.

## Duotone in Brand Systems

When I use duotone treatment in a brand system, I document the specific settings: the shadow color (Pantone or hex), the highlight color, and any curve adjustments. This makes the treatment replicable by anyone working with the brand, not just the original designer.

Consistent duotone settings across all brand photography creates visual unity that's difficult to achieve otherwise. A set of twenty photographs from different photographers, locations, and times can feel like a cohesive body of work when they're all processed through the same duotone system.

## The Extension to Tinted and Monotone

Duotone is part of a family of similar treatments:

**Monotone** — the image mapped to a single color plus white. Simpler than duotone but effective.

**Tinted** — a color overlay applied to a grayscale or desaturated image, often at less than full opacity. Less dramatic than duotone but useful for subtle brand integration.

**Tritone** — three-color mapping. Rare in brand work but occasionally the right tool for rich, complex tonal work.

All of these treatments serve the same purpose: subordinating the image's native palette to the brand's palette. The goal is always integration — photography that feels native to the brand world, not imported from outside it.
