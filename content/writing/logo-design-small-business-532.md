## The Small Business Assumption

There's a widespread assumption in the design industry that small business logo work is somehow lesser — simpler briefs, lower standards, quicker turnarounds, less interesting problems. I've never found this to be true.

Small business clients often have clearer personality and more specific audience than large corporate clients. The restaurant owner who has been in the neighborhood for twenty years knows exactly who their customer is. The independent bookshop knows exactly what they stand for. The clarity of the brief is often better, not worse.

What changes is the budget, the timeline, and the production complexity. The standard of craft should not change.

## What "Right-Sized" Means

Working well with small business clients means calibrating scope, not quality. A small restaurant doesn't need a 300-page brand bible. They need a strong mark, a clear type treatment, a defined color palette, and enough guidance to use it consistently.

That's right-sized. It's a smaller deliverable, but every element of that deliverable should be as strong as the equivalent element in a large corporate identity project.

The mistake is using the reduced scope as a reason to cut corners on the thinking. The strategic foundation — who is this brand, who is the customer, what's the competitive context — should be just as rigorous at any budget.

## Simplicity Is Not Simplistic

Small business logos often need to be simpler than enterprise identity work because of the production contexts: low-cost printing, embroidery, hand-painted signs, simple digital templates. A complex mark breaks in all of these.

But designing simply is not the same as designing carelessly. A simple mark requires more precision, not less. With fewer elements, every proportion, every weight, every spacing decision is more visible and more consequential.

I find small business logo work keeps me sharp precisely because there's no complexity to hide behind. The mark has to stand on its own.

## The Production Constraint Advantage

When I design for a small business, I often know in advance what the primary applications will be: a sign, a business card, social media profiles, a simple website header, maybe embroidery on uniforms. That's a finite, known list of contexts.

That constraint is useful. I can design the mark specifically for those contexts and test it against each one before finalizing. The result is a mark that's demonstrably fit for purpose, not generically flexible.

## Having the Strategy Conversation

The part of small business brand work I value most is the discovery conversation. Small business owners often haven't been asked the strategic questions: who are you for, what makes you different, what feeling do you want people to have?

These questions open up the brief in ways that make the design work much richer. The initial request might be "I just need something that looks clean and professional." But after the discovery conversation, the brief becomes something more specific and interesting.

That conversation is worth having regardless of the budget. It produces better work and makes clients better informed about what they're getting and why.

## The Deliverable That Actually Helps

For small business clients, I've standardized a practical deliverable set: primary logo, reversed logo, favicon version, a defined color palette with print and digital values, and a one-page style sheet with basic usage rules.

That one-page style sheet is the most underrated deliverable I produce. It's a single reference document the client can share with any vendor, printer, or web designer. It prevents the most common misuses without requiring anyone to read a full brand guide.

Small scale doesn't mean small care. Bring your full attention to every brief.
