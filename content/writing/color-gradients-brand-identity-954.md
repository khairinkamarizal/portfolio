## Gradients Have a Reputation Problem

Gradients went from ubiquitous in the skeuomorphic design era to aggressively avoided in the flat design period, and they've been making a complicated comeback ever since. The Instagram rebrand in 2016 brought them back into conversation. The subsequent flood of gradient-heavy brand identities has made them feel trendy in a way that raises legitimate longevity concerns.

My position: gradients are a valid and sometimes excellent brand tool when they're chosen for the right reasons and designed with the right constraints. They're a problem when they're chosen because gradients are having a moment.

## What Gradients Do Well

Gradients create visual depth in a flat medium. A well-constructed gradient gives a surface a sense of light and dimension that solid colors can't. For brands that want to communicate energy, dynamism, or a sense of the ineffable — technology companies describing complex systems, entertainment brands conveying immersive experiences — this depth can be exactly right.

Gradients also create color transitions that allow more visual richness than a flat palette. A brand limited to three solid colors can create many more visual possibilities with gradient combinations of those same three colors.

## The Gradient Constraints I Impose

I never use a gradient that goes outside the brand's established color palette. The gradient should move between defined brand colors or from a brand color to white, black, or a defined neutral. Gradients that introduce new color territory undermine palette discipline.

I design gradients with specific angle and proportion parameters in the brand guidelines. A "blue-to-purple diagonal gradient" is not a reproducible specification. "Linear gradient at 135 degrees, 0% brand navy to 100% brand violet, with the midpoint shifted to 60%" is.

I also limit the number of gradient permutations. Two or three defined gradient treatments is a system. Ten undefined gradient possibilities is chaos.

## Reproducing Gradients in Print

Gradients in print require careful management. A gradient that transitions smoothly on screen can produce visible banding in print if the color change is too subtle or the gradient too long. The fix is to ensure the gradient has enough color differential across its range and to test the print output before specifying.

For important applications — packaging, large-format print, premium stationery — I always proof gradient treatments. Screen previews are not reliable for gradient print quality.

## When Gradients in a Logo Mark Go Wrong

A logo mark that requires a full-color gradient to work has a fundamental problem. As established in the one-color test: if the mark doesn't work in black, the structure is dependent on color. A gradient-dependent mark fails in embroidery, engraving, one-color print, fax, and most merchandise production contexts.

I design logo marks to work without gradients, then allow a gradient-enhanced version for specific digital and full-color print applications. The gradient is an enhancement, not a requirement.

## The Longevity Question

Will gradient-heavy brand identities look dated in five years? Some of them, yes. The ones that look dated will be the ones that used gradients because gradients were trendy, applied indiscriminately across all brand surfaces, and made gradients the central visual personality of the identity.

Gradients that are used as one tool among several, constrained within a clear system, and chosen because they serve the brand's specific character will age the same way any other considered design decision ages: relative to the rest of the visual culture, with enough brand-specific distinctiveness to feel like a choice rather than a trend response.
