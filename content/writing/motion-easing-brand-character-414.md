I've watched brand presentations where the identity system was meticulous — custom typeface, rigorously specified color palette, precise logo construction — and then the motion guidelines said "ease in and out." As if easing is a technical default rather than a design decision.

Easing is a design decision. It's one of the most communicative decisions in motion design, and it shapes brand character in ways that are felt even when they're not consciously noticed.

## What Easing Actually Is

Easing describes how an animated value changes over time. A linear ease moves at constant speed from start to end — mechanical, robotic, unnatural. An ease-in starts slowly and accelerates — like an object gaining momentum. An ease-out decelerates — like an object coming to rest naturally. An ease-in-out does both.

But the standard named easings are just the beginning. The actual character of a motion is defined by the specific shape of the easing curve — how steep the acceleration, how early or late it peaks, whether there's overshoot (the element goes slightly past its destination before settling back) or anticipation (it pulls back slightly before moving forward).

These details matter enormously. An ease-out that decelerates gradually reads as gentle and organic. An ease-out that decelerates very quickly reads as crisp and precise. Both are "ease-outs" but they feel completely different.

## Matching Easing to Brand Character

The easing language of a brand should reflect its personality.

**Brands that feel precise, technical, modern:** I favor easing curves with minimal overshoot, crisp deceleration, short duration. Elements arrive at their destination cleanly and quickly. Nothing lingers. Think instruments, software tools, financial systems.

**Brands that feel warm, organic, human:** I favor easing with gentle acceleration, natural-feeling deceleration, and sometimes slight overshoot that settles back. Elements move like they have a small amount of physical weight. Think lifestyle brands, food brands, wellness.

**Brands that feel energetic, youthful, expressive:** More dramatic curves. Faster overall timing. More obvious overshoot or anticipation. Bounce physics. Think entertainment, sports, consumer tech.

**Brands that feel established, authoritative, premium:** Slower overall timing. Easing that emphasizes the arrival rather than the departure. Elements settle into place rather than snapping in. Think luxury goods, institutional brands, heritage brands.

## Defining Motion Guidelines

When I'm developing motion guidelines as part of a brand system, I specify:

- **Timing range:** what's the shortest acceptable animation duration, and the longest?
- **Primary easing curve:** the default curve for most transitions, defined by bezier control points
- **Enter vs. exit easing:** elements often feel better entering with an ease-out (arrives naturally) and exiting with an ease-in (departs with intention)
- **Overshoot:** yes or no, and if yes, how much (typically 2–8% beyond the target value)
- **Anticipation:** yes or no, and if yes, how early does it begin

These aren't just for the motion designer. They're for anyone animating brand content — the social media manager doing Instagram stories, the marketing team building slide decks, the developer adding page transitions to the website.

## The Consistency Argument

Inconsistent easing across brand touchpoints creates a subliminal sense of incoherence — like a person who's confident and measured in one context and jittery and nervous in another. You trust someone whose movement quality is consistent. You're unsettled by someone whose behavior is erratic.

Brand motion should feel like it all comes from the same source. Same physics. Same tempo. Same character. That consistency is what makes the accumulated motion experience of a brand feel like something coherent rather than a collection of separate pieces.

Get the easing right and it becomes invisible in the best possible way — it just feels like the brand.
