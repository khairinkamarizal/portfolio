## The RGB to CMYK Problem

Every designer who's transitioned from screen to print work has had the experience: the vibrant, saturated colors that looked electric on screen come back from the printer looking muddy, dull, or completely wrong. It's one of the most discouraging experiences in print work.

The root cause is simple: screens emit light, and ink absorbs it. These are fundamentally different color models. A color that's achievable in light can be impossible in ink.

## What CMYK Can't Do

The CMYK color gamut — the range of colors achievable through four-color process printing — is significantly smaller than the RGB gamut. The most saturated cyans, magentas, and especially electric blues and vivid oranges are largely outside what CMYK can reproduce.

If I design a poster in RGB and then convert to CMYK at the end, some of my colors will shift. The extent of the shift depends on the specific colors involved. Neon greens, vivid oranges, and electric blues are particularly vulnerable.

## Designing CMYK from the Start

My approach for print work is to design in CMYK from the beginning, not convert at the end. This means I'm working within the gamut I'll actually be printing in. What I see on screen (calibrated as best as possible) is an approximation of what I'll get in ink.

This is still an approximation. Screen calibration can get you close, but there's no substitute for physical proofs. I always pull proofs on the actual stock before finalizing colors on any print project.

## When to Use Spot Color

For work where specific colors are non-negotiable — a brand's primary color, a particularly vivid tone that CMYK can't hit — I use spot colors (Pantone or equivalent). Spot colors are pre-mixed inks that give you precise, consistent color across a print run.

Spot colors cost more. They require separate printing plates. But they're the only way to guarantee that a specific color lands exactly right. For a brand logo on packaging or a special event poster, that guarantee is worth the cost.

## Paper Stock Affects Color

Color also behaves differently depending on the paper it's printed on. Uncoated stock absorbs ink more, making colors appear softer and slightly darker. Coated stock (gloss or matte) reflects more, making colors appear more saturated and vivid.

I always specify the stock when designing for print, and I keep in mind how my color choices will be affected. What looks great on coated gloss can look flat on uncoated kraft.

## The Calibration Baseline

None of this is solvable without a calibrated monitor. If your screen isn't calibrated, you have no reliable starting point for predicting how colors will print. Calibration isn't expensive and it doesn't take long. It's not optional for professional print work.

Print color is a craft, not a setting. The more you do it, the better your instincts get.
