## The Expensive Way to Learn

Early in my motion work I went straight from brief to After Effects. The reasoning seemed sound: the software is faster than drawing, you can iterate in real time, why add a step?

What I was actually doing was solving the wrong problems. I'd spend three hours pushing keyframes, get frustrated that something felt off, and eventually realize the compositional logic was wrong from the start. The motion was fine; the concept was broken. Three hours wasted because I skipped thinking.

## What Storyboards Force You to Do

Drawing frames — even rough, scratchy boxes with stick figures and arrows — forces you to make decisions that software defers. Where does the eye go first? How does this composition transition to the next one? What is the kinetic arc of this sequence from the first frame to the last?

These are story and composition decisions, not technical ones. Making them on paper, where you can sketch five alternatives in ten minutes, is fundamentally faster than making them inside a motion compositing tool.

## My Storyboard Process

I work in a ring-bound sketchbook with a fine-liner pen. No pencil — if I'm committed enough to ink it, I'm committed to the idea. Each frame is roughly the aspect ratio of the output (16:9 for most work, sometimes 1:1 for social).

I annotate each frame with:
- Camera or POV notes (if applicable)
- Key animation direction (arrows, motion paths)
- Duration estimate
- Sound or music notes if relevant

I'm not trying to produce a beautiful document. I'm trying to capture the sequence quickly enough that I can see the whole thing on two pages and evaluate it as a complete experience.

## When I Skip It (and When That's a Mistake)

I'll sometimes skip storyboarding for very simple animations — a logo reveal with one element, a lower-third that builds in three moves. For those I have enough experience that the concept is fully formed before I open the software.

But for anything with multiple scenes, narrative logic, or complex timing — anything over about 15 seconds — skipping the storyboard almost always costs me time. I've learned this the hard way enough times that I just do it now.

## From Board to Animatic

After the storyboard, I'll sometimes build a quick animatic — the storyboard frames sequenced in time with rough timing and the audio bed if there is one. This catches pacing problems before any real animation exists. A beat that reads fine as a static frame can feel rushed or sluggish when you actually play it at speed.

An animatic can be built in 30 minutes and can save days of revision. For any client-facing motion work, I include an animatic approval step in my process. Clients can evaluate timing and sequence in a way they simply can't from a storyboard. It also reduces surprise at delivery, which keeps everybody calm.

## The Honest Truth

Storyboarding is a habit that feels like it slows you down and actually speeds you up. That tradeoff is hard to explain to someone who hasn't experienced it, but once you've seen the difference it makes, you can't go back.
