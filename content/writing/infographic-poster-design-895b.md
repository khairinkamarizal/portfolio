## Two Disciplines, One Surface

An infographic poster sits at the intersection of two distinct design disciplines: information design and graphic design. Information design prioritizes accuracy, clarity, and efficient communication of data. Graphic design prioritizes visual impact, hierarchy, and aesthetic quality.

These disciplines can conflict. The most accurate visualization of a dataset might not be the most visually compelling poster element. The most dramatic visual treatment of data might distort its meaning. Navigating this tension honestly is the central challenge of infographic poster work.

## Data Integrity Is Non-Negotiable

The visual treatment of data can never compromise its accuracy. A bar chart where the bars don't start at zero, a map where geographic scaling has been distorted for compositional purposes, a percentage displayed with visual elements that don't accurately represent the proportion — these are not design choices. They're misrepresentations.

I apply this standard rigorously. If the accurate representation of the data doesn't serve the composition, I find a different visual approach or a different data representation, not a distorted one.

## The Visual Entry Point

An infographic poster needs a visual entry point — an element compelling enough to stop a viewer who wasn't necessarily seeking out this information. A striking statistic at large scale, a visually arresting chart, a strong image that contextualizes the data.

This entry point is designed to create curiosity. The viewer who stops to look at the large number or striking visualization then reads deeper into the supporting data. Without the entry point, the carefully designed data visualization never gets seen.

## Hierarchy in Data

Not all data is equally important. Part of designing an infographic poster is deciding what the poster's thesis is — the one or two things it most needs to communicate — and giving those elements visual primacy. Supporting data lives in a secondary tier.

An infographic poster that presents all data at equal visual weight is an undifferentiated data dump. A poster with a clear visual hierarchy that leads the reader from the key insight through supporting evidence is a communication.

## Typography in Information Design

Type in infographic contexts needs to do something it doesn't always need to do in other contexts: be precisely legible at small sizes, often alongside dense visual information that competes for attention.

I select typefaces for infographic work specifically for legibility at small sizes: generous x-height, open counters, clear character differentiation. The expressive display typefaces I might use in other poster work often aren't the right choice here. Clarity takes precedence over personality at the scale where data labels, axis markers, and supporting copy live.
