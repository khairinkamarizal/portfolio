## Scale Is the Real Test

A wordmark that looks beautiful in a presentation deck can completely fall apart on a 16×16 pixel favicon or stretched across a trade show backdrop. Scale testing isn't a finishing step — it's a design constraint that should inform every decision from the beginning.

I've refined wordmarks for companies ranging from boutique hospitality brands to national retailers. The ones that hold up across all contexts share a set of specific structural characteristics. The ones that don't almost always fail for the same predictable reasons.

## Letterform Weight and Optical Spacing

Most off-the-shelf typefaces need modification before they work as a wordmark. The letterforms were designed for text use — running copy at 10 to 14 points. At logo scale, those same letterforms reveal problems: inconsistent stroke weights, optical spacing that reads as uneven, and details that add noise at small sizes.

I start every wordmark by setting the name in the chosen typeface, then immediately viewing it at 20px and at 300px side by side. At 20px, I'm looking for letters that bleed into each other, counters that close up, and overall weight that reads as too light or too heavy. At 300px, I'm looking for inconsistencies in the letterforms that would feel amateurish at large scale.

## The Counter Problem

Counters — the enclosed or partially enclosed negative spaces in letters like O, C, B, P, and e — are the first thing to fail at small sizes. As the wordmark shrinks, counters close up and the letter becomes an unreadable blob.

My solution depends on the typeface and the client's brand character. For brands that need elegance, I might open up the counters manually by modifying the letterforms in Illustrator. For more robust applications, I choose or recommend a typeface with naturally open counters to begin with. Geometric sans-serifs with large apertures scale much better than humanist typefaces with narrow counter forms.

## Letter Spacing at Different Sizes

Spacing that looks right at one size rarely looks right at all sizes. Type set loosely for a billboard reads as gappy on a business card. Type kerned tightly for print feels cramped on screen at small sizes.

The solution is to deliver wordmarks with size-specific spacing recommendations. For most brand systems, I produce three spacing variants: a tight version for large-scale applications, a standard version for mid-range use, and a slightly looser version for anything below about 80 pixels wide. These aren't dramatically different — sometimes just two to four units of tracking variation — but the difference is visible and matters for the brand's quality perception.

## When to Modify and When to Replace

I used to spend a lot of time trying to modify typefaces that fundamentally weren't suited to wordmark use. The open apertures were too narrow, the stroke contrast too high, the overall weight too light. I'd fight the letterforms for days trying to make them work at small sizes.

Now I cut that process short. If a typeface needs more than optical corrections and minor weight adjustments to scale properly, I find a better typeface. The brand brief should be driving the type choice, not my attachment to a particular font.

## Delivering a Wordmark That Survives Client Hands

The wordmark I deliver isn't just a file — it's a system. I include SVGs optimized for every major use case, PNG exports at relevant sizes, and clear documentation on when to use each version. A wordmark that only works when I'm in the room isn't a professional deliverable.

The mark should be robust enough that a junior marketing coordinator can drop it into an email template and have it look right. That's the real test.
