I've seen strong logo concepts rejected because they were presented poorly, and weak concepts approved because the presentation was compelling. This is frustrating but it's also instructive: the presentation is part of the design work, and treating it as an afterthought will cost you.

Here's how I structure logo presentations to give concepts their best chance of being evaluated on their actual merits.

## Context Before Mark

I never open a logo presentation by showing the mark. I open with the strategic context: what problem this logo needs to solve, what the brand needs to communicate, who the audience is, and what the competitive landscape looks like. This frame gives the client a way to evaluate the mark against something other than personal preference.

When clients evaluate marks without strategic context, they default to "I like it" or "I don't like it." When they have strategic context, they can ask "does this serve the strategy?" That's a more productive conversation.

## Three Directions, Not Two, Not Six

Three is the right number of directions for an initial logo presentation. Two forces a binary choice and doesn't show adequate exploration. Six overwhelms and produces indecision. Three gives the client a genuine range while keeping the conversation focused.

Each direction should be meaningfully different — different conceptual territory, different formal approach. Presenting three variations of the same idea is not showing three directions.

## The Rationale Comes First in Each Direction

Before I show each mark, I briefly explain the concept — one or two sentences about what idea the mark expresses and why that idea serves the brand. Then I show the mark.

This sequence matters. When the client sees the mark first, their immediate visual reaction forms before they have context. Once a first impression is formed, rationale becomes defensive rather than explanatory. Concept first primes the eye to look for something specific rather than react generically.

## Mockups Are Essential

Abstract marks in a white field tell a client very little about how a logo will actually feel in the world. Mockups — the mark on a business card, on signage, on a product, in a document header — show the mark functioning.

I use realistic mockups, not renders so polished they look like a different business than the client's. The goal is to help the client see the mark as a real thing they'll actually use, not as a beautiful design object.

I also show the mark at multiple scales and in multiple contexts — large and small, on light and dark backgrounds, in single color and full color — because a mark that only looks good in one configuration isn't finished.

## What to Do With Feedback

Client feedback on logo presentations is often a mix of genuine strategic insight and personal preference dressed up as strategic concern. "The symbol feels complex" might mean it genuinely has a scalability problem, or it might mean the client doesn't personally like abstract marks.

My job is to separate these. I ask follow-up questions: "Is the complexity a concern in terms of how it will reproduce, or is it more about the visual character you want the brand to have?" The answer tells me whether I'm addressing a real design problem or managing a preference.

I don't capitulate to preference feedback when the design choice serves the strategy. I explain why the choice is right. If the client still wants the change, I make it — but I note my recommendation in writing.

## Never Present Work You Don't Believe In

The easiest way to lose a logo presentation is to include a direction you can't defend. Clients can feel when you're uncertain about something you're showing. If a concept isn't strong enough to present with full conviction, don't present it.
