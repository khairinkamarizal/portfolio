## What Was the International Style?

The International Typographic Style — sometimes called Swiss Design — emerged from Switzerland in the 1950s and spread globally through the 1960s and 70s. It was defined by rigorous mathematical grids, objective photography and imagery, sans-serif type (especially Helvetica and Univers), and a commitment to clarity over decoration.

It wasn't just an aesthetic movement. It was a design philosophy: the idea that visual communication should be as clear, universal, and functional as possible.

## Why It Still Matters

I study historical design movements not for stylistic reference but for the underlying thinking. The Swiss grid isn't just a visual style — it's a system for making rational decisions about space, proportion, and hierarchy. Those decisions don't go out of fashion. They're as applicable to a contemporary poster as they were to a 1960 corporate brochure.

The grid-based thinking, the typographic rigor, the preference for structure over decoration — these remain essential tools regardless of the current aesthetic zeitgeist.

## The Grid as Universal Language

What the Swiss Style codified is that the grid creates a shared visual language across a body of work. Multiple pieces by different designers, produced at different times, feel like they belong together because they share a structural logic.

For any client producing a significant volume of print materials, this coherence through shared structure is enormously valuable. The grid is the infrastructure that makes consistency possible without making every piece look identical.

## Helvetica's Legacy

Helvetica is the typeface most associated with the Swiss Style, and it's worth understanding why. It was designed to be maximally neutral — to carry content without imposing its own personality. The idea was that type should get out of the way of the message.

That philosophy is contested today. Many designers prefer typefaces with more personality. But the argument for neutrality in certain contexts — information design, signage systems, complex documents where multiple types of content coexist — remains sound.

## How I Apply These Principles

I don't design in a period-accurate Swiss Style. I apply the underlying principles: structure before decoration, hierarchy through scale and space rather than ornament, type choices justified by function rather than fashion, grids derived from the content rather than imposed on it.

These principles make my work more rigorous. They give me a framework for making decisions that I can explain and defend. That's the real legacy of the International Style.
