Redesigning an existing brand identity is fundamentally different from designing a new one. With a new brand, you're building on a blank slate. With an existing brand, you're inheriting a history — of decisions made, of equity built, of associations formed. The first step is understanding what you've inherited before you touch anything.

A brand identity audit is how I do that.

## What an Audit Examines

A thorough brand audit looks at the identity from three perspectives: the designed system (what exists in the guidelines and approved assets), the implemented reality (how the brand actually looks in the world), and the perceived brand (what audiences actually associate with the brand).

These three rarely align perfectly. The gap between the designed system and the implemented reality tells you about execution discipline — or the lack of it. The gap between the implemented reality and perceived brand tells you about communication effectiveness.

## Collecting the Material

I start by collecting everything: all versions of the logo in use (there are almost always more than the official guidelines acknowledge), brand guidelines if they exist, examples of the brand in every significant application (web, print, environmental, merchandise), and any brand research the client has done.

I also do my own sweep: searching for the brand online, visiting physical locations if relevant, finding third-party applications of the brand identity.

The purpose is to understand the complete picture of how the brand currently presents itself, not just the idealized version in the guidelines.

## The Consistency Analysis

I evaluate consistency across the material I've collected. How consistently is the logo applied? Are there multiple versions in use that should be the same? Is the color consistent, or are there variations that suggest the color hasn't been properly specified or followed?

Inconsistency patterns are usually instructive. If the logo is consistent but the color isn't, the problem is probably in color specification or accessibility of the correct color files. If nothing is consistent, the problem is probably a lack of guidelines, a lack of buy-in, or a guidelines document that nobody uses.

## Identifying the Equity

Every brand with any history has equity — elements that audiences recognize and associate with the brand. This equity is valuable and should be preserved through a redesign unless there are compelling strategic reasons to abandon it.

I identify the equity elements explicitly: the mark shape, the color, the typeface, the name itself. I note which elements have the strongest recognition and which are the weakest or most problematic.

The equity analysis shapes the redesign strategy. A brand with strong color equity might need a complete logo redesign but should retain the color. A brand with an outdated mark but strong wordmark equity might need a new mark while keeping the typographic treatment.

## The Strategic Fit Assessment

Finally, I assess whether the existing identity fits the brand's current strategy. Brands evolve. Sometimes a logo designed ten years ago for a company in one market is now representing a company in a completely different position. The visual identity needs to reflect the current strategy, not the historical one.

When there's a significant gap between what the brand is now and what its visual identity communicates, that gap is the creative brief for the redesign.

The audit doesn't tell you what to design. It tells you what you're working with, what's worth keeping, and what needs to change. That's the foundation for any redesign decision worth making.
