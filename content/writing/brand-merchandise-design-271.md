Branded merchandise — t-shirts, tote bags, caps, notebooks, water bottles, pins — is often treated as a marketing afterthought. Someone in marketing requests "something with the logo on it" and the result is a logo slapped on a white t-shirt in the same configuration it appears on the website.

This is a missed opportunity. Done thoughtfully, branded merchandise creates genuine brand equity — objects people choose to own, carry, and wear because they want to be associated with the brand. Done carelessly, it creates clutter that ends up in the bottom of a drawer.

## Merchandise as Brand Expression, Not Just Brand Reproduction

The first shift in thinking: merchandise isn't about reproducing the logo. It's about expressing the brand's character through objects.

The most successful branded merchandise I've designed came from asking: what would an object look like that this brand might genuinely make? Not "where do we put the logo" but "what kind of object would this brand create if it made physical products?"

A typography-led design studio might create notebooks with unusual grid layouts printed inside. A nature-focused outdoor brand might create merchandise with detailed botanical illustrations. A cultural institution might create editions that rethink how their visual identity applies to a specific object type.

## Choosing Objects That Fit the Brand

Not every object is right for every brand. A tote bag suits a bookshop or cultural institution. A high-quality notebook suits a design studio or consulting firm. Technical gear suits an outdoor or sports brand.

The object choice communicates as much as the design on it. A cheap pen with a luxury brand's logo creates dissonance — the object quality contradicts the brand positioning. A beautifully produced ceramic mug with a simple mark can elevate a relatively simple brand identity through production quality.

## Adapting the Identity to the Object

Most brand identity elements need to be adapted for merchandise rather than directly applied. The primary logo on a t-shirt is rarely the most interesting solution. A pattern derived from the identity, a typographic treatment designed specifically for the garment, an illustration in the brand's visual language — these create more compelling merchandise than a simple logo reproduction.

I design for the object specifically: the material, the print method, the viewing context. A t-shirt design that works at chest placement may need to be designed differently than the same brand expression on the back of a cap.

## Print Method and Object Quality Alignment

Production quality needs to match brand positioning. Screen printing on quality blank t-shirts is appropriate for most brand contexts. Embroidery on premium materials signals a higher tier. DTG (direct-to-garment) printing allows more complex designs but has quality tradeoffs worth understanding.

I specify the entire production chain for merchandise — object quality, print method, colorway — as a brand decision, not a procurement decision. The production specification is part of the brand expression.

## Limited Edition Logic

Scarcity creates desire. Merchandise produced in limited editions — or created specifically for events, milestones, or collaborations — generates more engagement than a perpetually available product.

I recommend the limited edition approach for brands that want merchandise to feel like something worth having, not just promotional material. The design investment per item is higher because the item needs to stand alone as a desirable object.

Make merchandise that people keep. That's the standard.
