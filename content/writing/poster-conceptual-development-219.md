## The poster is the last step

The most common mistake I made early in my career was opening design software too quickly. I'd get a poster brief, feel the urgency of the deadline, and start arranging type and images before I'd actually thought through what the poster needed to say.

The poster is the final form. The concept — the idea that the poster is expressing — is developed before the poster. When the concept is strong, design decisions become easier and more confident. When the concept is weak or absent, you're making arbitrary aesthetic choices and hoping they cohere.

## What a concept actually is

A concept is not a mood or a style. "We want it to feel modern and energetic" is not a concept — it's an aesthetic direction. A concept is a specific idea, a tension, a reframing that gives the poster something to say.

For a festival poster, the concept might be: this is the event that turns strangers into neighbors. Every design decision — the warmth of the color palette, the overlapping typography, the community-focused imagery — expresses that specific idea rather than just "festival vibes."

For an activist poster, the concept might be: the absence of action is itself a choice. That idea leads naturally to a design that uses negative space and restraint — what's not there says as much as what is.

## The research phase

Before I conceptualize any poster, I do contextual research. Where will this poster appear? Who will see it and in what mental state? What else is competing for their attention in that environment? What does the audience already believe about this subject, and what do I want them to believe after seeing the poster?

This research is not always elaborate. For a small local event poster, it might be twenty minutes of thinking through the venue, the likely attendees, and the specific action I want the poster to drive. For a major campaign, it might be days of research. The scale varies; the questions are the same.

## Generating multiple concepts

I generate concepts before I generate designs. Three to five distinct conceptual directions, each described in a sentence. Not sketches yet — words. "The poster will use a single image at full bleed with no text other than the event name, communicating through pure visual impact." Or: "The poster will be entirely typographic, using the text of the event description itself as both content and form."

I then evaluate these conceptual directions against the brief before committing to one. Which concept best serves the communication goal? Which has the most potential for visual impact? Which can I execute brilliantly in the time available?

## The discipline of commitment

Once I choose a concept, I commit to it. I don't hedge by designing "a bit of both directions." Conceptual compromise produces design work that's trying to say two things and saying neither clearly. The strength of a poster often comes from its decisiveness — from a designer who had a clear idea and followed it through completely.
