There are two routes to a custom lettered logo that doesn't rely on an existing typeface: hand lettering and custom type design. They sound similar. They're genuinely different disciplines, and choosing between them should be deliberate rather than default.

## What Hand Lettering Is

Hand lettering is the art of drawing letters for a specific use. The letters are illustrations — each one drawn for its specific position in a specific word. A hand-lettered wordmark isn't a typeface. You can't set other words in it. It's a drawing of a specific word, refined until it works as a mark.

Hand lettering is appropriate when:
- The brand needs a bespoke quality that signals craft and individual attention
- The letterforms need to do something that no existing typeface does (specific connections, unusual combinations, unique rhythms)
- The budget and timeline support the extensive drawing process
- The brand will have a limited set of lettered applications (primarily the wordmark and perhaps a few secondary elements)

Hand lettering is not appropriate when:
- The brand needs the same lettering style applied across many different words or text elements
- The production workflow requires digital typeface files
- The letterforms need to be highly legible at small sizes

## What Custom Type Design Is

Custom type design is the creation of an actual typeface — a systematic set of letter drawings that form a consistent, scalable type system. A custom typeface can be set in words, sentences, paragraphs. It has consistent spacing, works across sizes, and produces predictable results.

Custom type is appropriate when:
- The brand needs a distinctive typographic voice applied across all text, not just the wordmark
- The organization has sufficient scale to justify the investment (custom type development is expensive)
- The brand's differentiation depends substantially on typographic character
- Long-term exclusivity of the type system is strategic

Custom type is overkill for most brand projects. It's the right choice for large brands with significant typographic communication across many media — global consumer brands, major media organizations, large tech companies.

## The Modified Existing Typeface Option

Between hand lettering and custom type there's a more accessible option: selecting an existing typeface and modifying specific letterforms for the wordmark. Unique enough to be distinctive, practical enough to be manageable.

This is what I recommend for most brand projects. A well-chosen typeface, modified at three to five key letterforms, produces a wordmark that feels custom without requiring the investment of either full hand lettering or custom type design.

The modifications should be meaningful, not arbitrary. A modified stem terminal that echoes a mark in the logo. An adjusted letter proportion that creates better rhythm in the specific word. A unique connection between specific letters that makes the word read as a unified form. Changes for their own sake just make the typeface look broken.

## Evaluating Quality

The test for hand lettering: does each letterform feel drawn or does it feel selected? Does the drawing have an internal logic that connects all the letters? Does it read cleanly at the sizes it will be used?

The test for custom type: does it have consistent internal rhythm and spacing across all letter combinations? Does it hold up in extended text setting, not just showcase words? Is the system complete enough for the use cases it needs to cover?

The test for modified existing type: do the modifications feel like they belong to the typeface? Do they strengthen the mark or just make it look different from the original?

Quality in all three routes means the letterforms feel inevitable, not arbitrary.
