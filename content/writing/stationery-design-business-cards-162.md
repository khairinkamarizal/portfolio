## The Physical Signal

Handing someone a business card is a deliberate physical act. The card passes from your hand to theirs. They hold it, look at it, put it somewhere. That physical transaction is different from sharing a digital contact, and the card's quality communicates something about the person and brand it represents.

A poorly printed, carelessly designed business card is a brand liability. An exceptional card is a brand asset that continues working after the conversation ends.

## What "Exceptional" Means

An exceptional business card doesn't necessarily mean expensive finishing or unusual paper. It means: designed with the same rigor as any other brand touchpoint, produced at a quality level appropriate for the brand, and carrying all the information someone needs without being cluttered.

Exceptional is sometimes a clean, well-printed card on good stock. Exceptional is sometimes a letterpress-printed card on thick cotton stock with a spot color. The bar is appropriate to the brand — not universally extravagant.

## Information Hierarchy on a Small Surface

A standard business card is 90×55mm — a tiny canvas for communicating a brand and a person. The information hierarchy needs to be precise: name, role, and contact information need to be quickly scannable without any element competing for attention inappropriately.

I use type sizes in business card work that feel very small in isolation but are calibrated for the reading distance of a held card (roughly 40-50cm). What feels too small at 100% on screen often looks right in print.

## Both Sides Are Real Estate

The back of a business card is free surface that most brands waste on a white void or a repeated logo. A well-designed back can carry additional brand personality — a pattern, an illustration, a key message, a brand element that doesn't appear on the front.

I always design both sides, even if the back is simpler.

## The Finishing Conversation

Finishing options for business cards are extensive: different paper stocks, rounded corners, die cuts, spot UV varnish, letterpress or foil stamping, double-thick construction, painted edges. Each has cost implications and communicates something about the brand.

I have a deliberate conversation about finishing as part of the design process, not as a production afterthought. The finishing is part of the design.

## Template vs. Custom

For brands with multiple team members, the business card design needs to work as a template — the variable information (name, role, contact) slots into a fixed structure without requiring a designer to produce each person's card. A well-designed template is an act of systems thinking, not a compromise on craft.
