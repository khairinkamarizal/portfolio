Title sequences are one of the most formally ambitious forms of motion design. The best ones — the Saul Bass sequences for Hitchcock films, Kyle Cooper's work for Seven, the network identity sequences of the 1970s — are complete artistic statements that set the tone and context for everything that follows. They do in ninety seconds what a novel's opening chapter does in several pages.

Designing a title sequence is fundamentally different from designing a logo animation or an explainer video. It's storytelling work as much as it's motion design work.

## The Function of a Title Sequence

Before designing any element, I need to understand what the title sequence is supposed to do. The functions vary significantly by context:

A film title sequence establishes tone, period, and emotional register. It creates an expectation — the specific kind of experience the viewer is about to have.

A TV series title sequence builds recognition and anticipation across repeated viewings. It becomes part of the show's identity — something viewers associate with the experience of watching.

A branded video series title sequence communicates brand personality, establishes series identity, and signals that what follows is valuable content worth watching.

These different functions require different design approaches. A film sequence can be more challenging, more abstract, and more willing to make the viewer work. A TV series sequence needs to reward repeated viewing without becoming tedious. A branded sequence needs to align with the brand's visual identity system.

## Research Before Concept

Title sequence design requires understanding the content it precedes. For film or video content, this means reading the script or treatment, watching reference footage if available, and understanding the director's vision and aesthetic intentions.

The title sequence isn't a separate piece — it's the introduction to a specific work. It needs to know what it's introducing.

## Typography as the Primary Narrative Tool

Title sequences are fundamentally typographic — they convey cast, crew, and production information as their primary content. The typographic treatment is therefore both functional (communicating the credits) and expressive (establishing the visual language).

The typeface selection, the size and weight relationships, the timing and animation of text reveals, the relationship between type and background — these are the primary design decisions. Everything else supports or contextualizes the typography.

## The Relationship with Score

Title sequences are almost always experienced with a musical score. The sequence is designed to a specific piece of music (or the music is composed for the sequence). This musical relationship is foundational — the editing, the timing, the pace, and the emotional arc of the sequence are driven by the score.

I never design title sequence motion independently of the music. The music is the primary structural element; the visuals are its visual expression.

## Restraint and Conceptual Commitment

The most effective title sequences commit to a concept with full conviction. They don't hedge. A sequence built on geometric abstraction stays abstract throughout. A sequence using found footage maintains that documentary quality. A typographic sequence that uses only letterforms and color doesn't add imagery as a safety net.

This commitment requires confidence that the concept is strong enough to carry the full sequence — which in turn requires spending sufficient time developing and testing the concept before production begins.

The title sequence earns its audience before the main content begins. Make it count.
