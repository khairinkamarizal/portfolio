I started designing for screen printing out of necessity. A client wanted printed posters but had a small budget, and screen printing was the most economical option for the run size. I'd never designed specifically for the process before. What I discovered changed how I approach all poster work, not just screen-printed pieces.

## The Spot Color Discipline

Screen printing typically works in spot colors — one layer at a time, one ink at a time. When you design for two-color screen printing, you have exactly two colors, and every single element in the design has to be either color one, color two, or an overlap (which creates a third, depending on how the inks interact).

This forces a clarity that full-color work doesn't require. There's no hiding behind subtle gradients or photographic complexity. The design lives or dies on shape, value, and composition.

## Halftones and Registration

Simulating tonal range in screen printing means working with halftones — converting continuous tone images to dot patterns that create the impression of value variation. This is a skill that requires understanding both the printing process and the viewer's perceptual system.

Large halftone dots are a stylistic choice. Fine halftones are a technical one. Knowing which to use, and how to prepare the files for a specific mesh count, is knowledge that comes from doing, not just reading.

I always print test sheets at the intended mesh count before finalizing a design. Halftones that look right at 100% on screen can clog at 150 mesh or look too coarse at 230.

## Designing for Ink Variation

Unlike offset printing, screen printing has variation. Ink deposit varies across the print. Squeegee pressure shifts. Registration tolerances mean colors may be offset by a millimeter or more. I design with this variation in mind: I don't create designs that require perfect registration, because perfect registration isn't guaranteed.

Overprinting colors intentionally, using trapping on color boundaries, and avoiding thin hairlines near color transitions are all habits I developed from printing mistakes. Each one made me a better designer.

## The Physical Object Matters

Screen-printed posters are physical objects in a way that digital offset prints often aren't. The ink sits on top of the paper. You can feel it. The inks have a matte tactility that's specific to the process. I design for this tactile dimension — choosing paper weights and textures that complement the ink, thinking about how the piece will be handled and displayed.

A screen-printed poster is a craft object as much as a communication tool. Designing with that in mind changes the relationship between the work and the viewer.
