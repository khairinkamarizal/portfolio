Premium stationery is one of the most tactilely demanding applications in print design. The object will be held, written on, and sent. It needs to communicate quality before it's read. The design decisions that determine whether it succeeds happen before a single mark is made on the paper.

## Paper Is the Primary Material

In stationery design, paper selection is the most important material decision — more important than the print method, more important than the ink color. The paper is what the recipient touches. It communicates substance, quality, and attitude before the eye processes the design.

For premium correspondence stationery, I specify papers in the 90–120gsm range for letter sheets — heavy enough to feel substantial but light enough to fold cleanly in thirds. For business cards, 350–450gsm with an appropriate finish.

Cotton or cotton-blend papers have a specific quality — a slight texture and warmth — that standard wood-pulp papers don't. For high-end stationery, cotton paper is worth the premium. For functional business correspondence, a good quality uncoated wood-pulp sheet is entirely appropriate.

## Print Methods for Stationery

The three print methods most common in premium stationery:

**Letterpress:** Creates a tactile impression in the paper. The characteristic deboss of a letterpress business card or letterhead is unmistakable to anyone who's handled one. Requires bold, clean designs — fine hairlines don't letterpress well. The paper needs to be heavy enough to take the impression without tearing.

**Engraving:** Ink is applied under pressure, creating a slightly raised surface. Traditional for formal correspondence. Very fine line quality is possible. The raised ink is tactilely distinct from flat printing.

**Foil stamping:** Metallic or colored foil applied with heat and pressure. Combined with embossing for maximum premium effect. Bold, clean designs required.

**Digital offset (digital toner):** Flat but high quality. The most economical and flexible option. For anything less than premium tier, a well-printed digital offset letterhead on good paper is entirely appropriate and looks excellent.

## Proportions and Classic Formats

Letter sheet size (A4/US Letter), envelope format, and business card proportions are largely standardized, but within those standards there's room for distinctive proportion choices. A business card that's standard width but slightly thinner than standard height creates a distinctive format without requiring non-standard envelopes.

Envelope proportions and address placement follow conventions that have evolved from postal regulations and practical handling. Departing from them requires clear reason.

## Hierarchy and Restraint

Premium stationery design is restrained design. The aesthetic is typically one of quality through refinement rather than quality through abundance. A simple, elegantly spaced letterhead with a well-crafted logo and a single color — or just blind emboss — communicates more sophistication than a complex, heavily colored design.

I apply the principle that the most important element on a letterhead is the white space around the logo. Generous margins, quiet typography, a single point of visual interest. Less is more, and on premium stationery, more is usually worse.
