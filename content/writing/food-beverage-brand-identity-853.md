## The Shelf Is a Different Problem

Food and beverage brand identity lives in two very different contexts: the physical shelf in a retail environment, and digital channels including e-commerce, social media, and websites. These contexts have different requirements, and designing for one without the other produces brands that succeed in one place and fail in the other.

The shelf is a high-density, high-competition environment. The packaging needs to stand out from twenty adjacent products in less than a second of scanning. Digital channels require the brand to communicate quality and personality at thumbnail scale.

## Color and Appetite Appeal

Color in food branding has specific requirements that go beyond general brand color strategy. Certain colors are appetite-stimulating (warm reds, oranges, and yellows); others are appetite-suppressing (blues, greens in certain shades). These associations are powerful enough that they've shaped entire category aesthetics — fast food uses warm colors; premium and organic products often use muted naturals and greens.

Understanding where your brand sits in this matrix and whether to align with category conventions or deliberately break them is a strategic decision that needs to happen early in the identity work.

## Typography for Packaging

Type on packaging is read at close range, often by someone actively looking for specific information. The hierarchy is different from poster or brand identity work: product name and variant are primary, but certifications, ingredients, and nutritional information also need clear typographic treatment.

I treat packaging typography as a separate brief from brand typography — the same system, but applied to a specific set of informational requirements that differ from other brand touchpoints.

## The Premium vs. Accessible Signal

Visual identity signals price point. A premium food brand communicates differently from an accessible everyday product, and getting this signal wrong costs real sales. A premium positioning with a busy, colorful, accessible-feeling package confuses the target customer. An accessible everyday product with austere, minimal, luxury-feeling design may not reach its intended audience.

I map the pricing and positioning very carefully in the brief and treat the visual premium signal as a primary design constraint.

## When the Logo Lives on the Product

For food and beverage brands, the logo is often printed directly on packaging with real constraints — limited color printing, small sizes, complex backgrounds from the packaging artwork. I design for these constraints from the start, not as an afterthought.

Every logo mark for a food or beverage brand gets tested on a simulated packaging environment early in development. The beautiful mark that disappears on a craft paper bag or gets lost in packaging illustration isn't a finished mark.
