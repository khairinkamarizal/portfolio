Custom lettering is often dismissed as a luxury — something large budgets buy when they want to signal premium positioning. That's a narrow view of what it actually does. There are situations where custom lettering is the most pragmatic choice, not just the most prestigious one.

I've worked on both sides of this: projects where I recommended against custom lettering because a well-chosen typeface would do the job better, and projects where I made the case for it because nothing off the shelf could solve the specific problem. Here's how I think through it.

## What Custom Lettering Actually Provides

Custom letterforms provide three things that existing typefaces can't: exclusivity, specificity, and locked-down visual equity.

Exclusivity means no one else can accidentally use the same letterforms. When a brand's wordmark is set in a typeface anyone can license, a competitor or even an enthusiast can reproduce the typographic feel of the mark trivially. Custom letterforms are unique to the brand.

Specificity means the letterforms can be drawn to express something precise — a combination of formal qualities, historical references, or personality attributes that no single existing typeface carries. You're not approximating a feeling. You're defining it.

Visual equity means the letterforms themselves become a brand asset. Over time, viewers recognize the distinctive characters before they read the words. Think of Coca-Cola's script or Rolls-Royce's spencerian — the letterforms are as recognizable as the names they spell.

## When to Recommend It

I recommend custom lettering when: the brand has a long time horizon and the investment will amortize over decades; no existing typeface carries the right combination of formal qualities for the brand's positioning; or the category is so typographically saturated that a standard typeface selection can't differentiate.

I also recommend it when the budget and timeline allow proper development and testing. Custom lettering done quickly and cheaply often produces results worse than good typeface selection. The craft takes time.

## When Not to Recommend It

For brands with limited budgets, short time horizons, or extensive multilingual requirements, a well-chosen existing typeface is almost always the better recommendation. Custom lettering for a startup that might pivot or rebrand in two years is a poor investment. Custom lettering that only covers Latin characters for a global brand creates operational problems.

I'm also skeptical of custom lettering driven by client preference rather than strategic need. "I want something unique" is not sufficient justification if the uniqueness doesn't serve a brand objective.

## Working with Lettering Artists

When I do commission custom lettering, I provide an extremely detailed brief — reference material, formal qualities I'm after, the range of applications the letterforms need to perform in, scale requirements, and reproduction constraints.

Good lettering artists need to know whether their work will be primarily digital or print, whether it will be reproduced at large outdoor scale or small label size, and whether it needs to hold up to outline, emboss, or stamp.

I stay closely involved in the development process, testing letterforms in actual contexts rather than waiting for a finished specimen to react to.

## The Modification Middle Ground

There's a practical middle ground that often makes sense for mid-size brands: licensing a typeface and making targeted modifications to specific characters. A distinctive alternate character, a custom ligature, a modified terminal on a key letter. These changes can give a wordmark distinctiveness and some degree of exclusivity without the full cost of drawing letterforms from scratch.

This needs to be done with the type foundry's permission — most licenses don't permit modification. Many foundries will license modification rights or collaborate on custom versions for an appropriate fee.

Custom lettering is a tool, not a status symbol. Use it when it solves a real problem better than anything else available.
