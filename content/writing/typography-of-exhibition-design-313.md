Exhibition typography occupies a space between editorial print design and wayfinding systems. The text needs to inform and guide the way wayfinding does, but it also needs to support the intellectual and emotional experience of the exhibition the way editorial design does. Getting both right at once is genuinely hard.

## Hierarchy Across Physical Space

In print design, hierarchy is two-dimensional. In exhibition design, it's three-dimensional — hierarchy exists across the physical space as well as within each panel or label.

There's a hierarchy of reading distances: architectural text (exhibition title on the building exterior) read at twenty feet, zone titles read at ten feet, panel text read at three feet, object labels read at arm's length. Each level needs a different type size, weight, and treatment calibrated to its reading distance.

I map this hierarchy before I design anything. What text lives at what distance? What's the relationship between the levels? This spatial hierarchy is the structure the typography system serves.

## Type Size for Physical Contexts

The minimum readable type size at arm's length in a well-lit gallery context is about 12pt for typical viewing conditions. For object labels with supporting text, I typically set the primary text at 14–16pt and supporting text at 11–12pt.

Panel text read at two to three feet should be set at 18–24pt minimum for comfortable reading. Anything smaller requires the viewer to step closer than feels natural, which disrupts the flow of the exhibition experience.

## High Contrast for Variable Lighting

Exhibition spaces have highly variable lighting. Spotlights create pools of illumination and shadow. Natural light changes throughout the day. HVAC ventilation creates subtle color temperature shifts. Type needs high contrast against its background to survive these variations.

I specify black or very dark type on white or light backgrounds, or white type on dark or colored backgrounds, with contrast ratios that provide a significant margin above minimum accessibility standards. Colors that look fine in a well-lit studio can become unreadable in a spotlit gallery environment.

## Material Considerations

Exhibition text is often applied directly to walls, panels, or three-dimensional surfaces rather than printed on paper. Vinyl cut letters, direct printing to substrates, painted text — each material has implications for type legibility and minimum viable weight.

Vinyl cut letters can't hold very thin strokes or very tight letter spacing. Direct printing on textured surfaces loses fine detail. Painted text requires generous weight to survive the coating process. I specify type with the production method in mind.

## The Reader as Moving Body

Unlike a page, the viewer of an exhibition is moving. They approach, read, step back, approach again. The typography has to accommodate a reading experience that happens in fragments, from multiple angles, at different speeds.

I design for the skimmer first — what does someone get from glancing at this panel without stopping? Then I design for the engaged reader — what does someone get from reading every word? The skimmer experience should reward casual attention; the reader experience should reward investment.
