Logo redesign projects carry emotional weight that new brand creation projects don't. Clients have years of history with their existing mark. It's been on their business cards, their storefront, their website. In some cases, they or someone they care about designed it. Asking them to change it, or even significantly evolve it, involves loss as well as gain.

Ignoring this emotional dimension doesn't make it disappear. It just means the emotions surface unexpectedly and derail the process.

## Acknowledging the Equity

My first move in any logo redesign conversation is to explicitly acknowledge the equity in the existing mark. I say something direct: "Your current logo has been representing your brand for X years. That's real value — people recognize it, it's associated with everything you've built. Anything we do needs to take that seriously."

This isn't flattery. It's framing. It tells the client that I'm not approaching this as a blank slate problem, and it establishes that preserving relevant equity is part of the brief.

It also opens the door to a more honest assessment. Once the client knows I respect what exists, they're usually more willing to honestly discuss what isn't working.

## Separating Sentiment from Strategy

The most difficult part of a logo redesign conversation is separating the client's sentimental attachment to the existing mark from legitimate strategic reasons to preserve it.

"I've always loved the blue" might mean: the blue is a high-equity brand color that should absolutely be retained. Or it might mean: the client is personally attached to a color choice that isn't serving the brand strategically.

These look identical on the surface and require careful diagnosis. I ask questions: "When customers describe your brand, do they mention the blue? Have you heard the color mentioned in any customer research?" The answers distinguish between genuine equity and personal preference.

## The Evolution vs. Overhaul Spectrum

Very few logo redesigns need to be complete overhauls. Most are better served by systematic evolution — updating what needs updating while preserving what's working.

I present the evolution/overhaul question to clients as a genuine choice with explicit tradeoffs rather than assuming one direction. Evolution preserves equity but may not fully address strategic problems. Overhaul addresses fundamental problems but starts from zero on recognition and association.

This framing helps clients make an informed decision rather than simply reacting to whatever I show them.

## Showing the Before/After Bridge

In presentations for logo redesign work, I show the bridge between old and new explicitly. Here's the existing mark. Here's what we're preserving. Here's what we're changing and why. Here's the new mark.

This sequence helps clients understand that the new work is in dialogue with the existing work, not a repudiation of it. It also makes it easier to evaluate specific changes rather than comparing old and new as wholes.

## When the Client Can't Let Go

Sometimes clients understand intellectually that change is necessary but can't emotionally commit to it. Every proposed direction gets pulled back toward the existing mark. The redesign gradually becomes a minor refresh that doesn't address the actual problems.

When I recognize this pattern, I name it directly. "I notice that each round of feedback is moving us closer to the existing mark. I want to check whether we're actually aligned on doing a redesign, or whether a lighter refinement might be what's needed right now."

Sometimes this surfaces real ambivalence that changes the brief. Sometimes it helps the client commit to the direction they intellectually know is right. Either outcome is more productive than continuing a process that's fighting itself.
