Clients come to me asking for a logo. What they actually need, almost always, is a brand identity. These are not the same thing, and conflating them leads to expensive confusion down the line.

A logo is a mark. It's a symbol, a wordmark, or a combination of both that identifies an entity. That's it. It's one artifact in a much larger system.

A brand identity is the complete visual language of an organization — the logo, yes, but also the color palette, the typography hierarchy, the photographic style, the iconography, the spatial rules, the tone of voice in print. When all of those elements are working together, you have a brand identity.

## Why the Distinction Matters

I've taken over projects where a previous designer delivered a beautiful logo and nothing else. The client then went to a printer for business cards, to a web developer for a site, to an in-house team for event collateral — and every piece looked like it came from a different company. The logo was fine. The brand was a disaster.

When I start an identity project, I'm not designing a logo. I'm designing a system that scales. The logo is the anchor point, but the first questions I ask are: where will this brand live? What formats? What surfaces? Who is producing collateral day-to-day?

Those answers shape every decision I make.

## What a Minimal Brand Identity Needs

I won't take on an identity project and deliver just a logo. The minimum viable identity I'll hand over includes:

- Primary and secondary logo configurations (horizontal, stacked, icon-only)
- A defined color palette with exact specifications (CMYK, RGB, HEX, Pantone where relevant)
- A type system — primary and secondary typefaces with usage rules
- Clear space and sizing rules for the logo
- Do-not-do examples for the most common misuse cases

That's the floor. For most clients it goes much further — into pattern systems, photographic direction, print templates, and brand guidelines documentation.

## The Logo Is Not the Brand

Here's the hardest thing to communicate to clients who are early in building their businesses: no logo, however well designed, will carry a weak brand. A strong, consistent identity system with a modest logo will outperform an award-winning mark deployed inconsistently every single time.

I've seen brands with simple, almost plain logotypes build enormous recognition through rigorous, consistent application. Consistency is the multiplier. The logo is just the starting mark.

## What This Means in Practice

When someone asks me for a logo, I ask them: what happens after the logo? If they have an answer, we can scope a logo project appropriately. If they don't, we almost always end up talking about identity work, because a logo without a system to deploy it in is a solution to half a problem.

I'd rather scope the work correctly upfront than watch a good mark get mangled in application.
