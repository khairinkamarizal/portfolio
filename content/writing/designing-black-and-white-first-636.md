My standard practice for poster and identity work is to begin in black and white. No color, no gradients, no color-dependent hierarchy. Just value — dark, mid, light — and form.

This isn't a stylistic preference. It's a diagnostic tool. If a composition doesn't work in black and white, color won't save it. Color will obscure the structural problems while they continue to undermine the work.

## What Black-and-White Reveals

In grayscale, the only tools you have are:
- The shapes of elements
- The value relationships between them
- The spatial arrangement

You can't compensate for a weak hierarchy with a bold color contrast. You can't paper over a flat composition with a vibrant color palette. You can't disguise a poorly chosen typeface pairing behind an exciting color combination.

Everything is exposed. The bones of the composition are completely visible. If there's no clear primary focal point in grayscale, there's no clear primary focal point — and the design needs structural work before it gets color.

## Color as Enhancement, Not Foundation

When color is introduced after a sound grayscale foundation, it enhances rather than does the heavy lifting. The hierarchy is already working — the color makes it more expressive. The composition is already solid — the color makes it more vibrant.

When color is introduced at the start and the design is built around the color relationships, you often end up with work that falls apart when reproduced in single-color or when the color combinations aren't available in a particular medium. The design was dependent on color to function. That's a fragile foundation.

## The Value Check

Before I introduce color into any project, I run a value check: I desaturate the design completely and look at it. I ask:

- Is the primary element the darkest or lightest thing, clearly differentiated from everything else?
- Is the visual hierarchy clear? First, second, and third levels legible?
- Is the composition balanced in a way that creates the intended tension or equilibrium?
- Does every element have a reason to be where it is at the size it's at?

If the answer to all of these is yes, the design is ready for color. If not, I address the structural issues first.

## Practical Application for Poster Work

For poster design, my process is:

1. Sketch rough compositions in pencil — pure form and placement
2. Build the selected composition digitally in black and white — type, layout, all elements
3. Refine until the grayscale version is working
4. Introduce color as the final layer

The color decisions are made against a layout that's already functionally complete. This means I'm making color decisions about what feeling to add, not what problems to solve.

## For Identity Work

The same principle applies to logo design. A mark that works in black only is a mark that will work everywhere. A mark that depends on color to be recognizable is a mark with a fundamental design problem.

I always present logo concepts in black before showing color versions. If the client is excited about the black version, the color versions are a reward. If the black version is weak, no color execution will fix the underlying form.
