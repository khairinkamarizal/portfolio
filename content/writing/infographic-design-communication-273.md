Infographics have a bad reputation in serious design circles, and some of it is earned. The internet is full of infographics that are visually busy, statistically dishonest, and more concerned with being shareable than being informative. But the discipline of turning complex information into clear visual communication is genuinely valuable, and done with rigor, it produces some of the most useful design work there is.

## The Data First Principle

Before any visual decision, I understand the data. What is the actual story in the numbers? What comparison is meaningful? What trend should the viewer notice? What single insight should they take away?

Most infographic failures begin here — the designer creates a visual treatment before understanding what the data actually says. The visual becomes decoration for facts rather than a communicative structure for insight.

I spend as much time with the data as I do designing the visual. I make the charts and tables in a spreadsheet first, understand what they show, and only then ask how to visualize them effectively.

## Choosing the Right Chart Type

Chart type selection is a communication decision, not an aesthetic one. Each chart type communicates a specific type of relationship:

Bar charts compare discrete categories. Line charts show change over time. Scatter plots show correlations. Pie charts show part-to-whole relationships (though they're almost always replaceable with better alternatives). Maps show geographic distribution.

Using a chart type that doesn't match the data relationship produces misleading or confusing communication. A pie chart with fifteen segments communicates nothing. A scatter plot for data with no correlation reveals nothing useful. The chart type should serve the data's actual story.

## Design in Service of Comprehension

Every design decision in an infographic should serve comprehension. Color should highlight the most important data, not decorate evenly. Scale should be consistent and honest — truncated axes or inconsistent proportions create misleading impressions. Labels should be positioned close to what they label.

I evaluate every decorative element by asking: does this help the viewer understand the data better? If not, it's noise. Remove it.

Decorative infographic elements — illustrated icons, background textures, gradient fills on charts — are usually obstacles to comprehension, not aids to it. The content should be the visual.

## Hierarchy in Data Visualization

Good infographics have clear hierarchy: the most important data is the most visually prominent. Not every number is equally important, but default chart formatting treats them as equal.

I set headline findings in large, clear typography. Supporting data is smaller and visually subordinate. Context and methodology are the smallest, positioned where they're available to interested readers but not cluttering the primary communication.

## The Honest Scale Problem

One of the most common ways infographics mislead is through scale manipulation — axes that don't start at zero, comparative sizes that aren't proportional, timelines with inconsistent intervals. These manipulations can make small differences look dramatic or large differences look trivial.

I start axes at zero unless there's a specific, documented reason not to. I make proportional areas and lengths actually proportional. I label irregular scales clearly.

An infographic that makes your data look better than it is doesn't serve your audience or your credibility. Present the data honestly and let the actual story make the case.

## Simplicity as the Standard

The test I apply to every infographic: if someone looks at this for ten seconds, what do they take away? If the answer is "nothing clear," the design has failed. A viewer should be able to extract the key insight quickly, then choose to engage with supporting detail.

Complexity for its own sake impresses no one. Clarity that makes a real insight accessible — that's worth doing.
