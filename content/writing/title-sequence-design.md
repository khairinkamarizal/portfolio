A title sequence has an unusual job: it introduces the work that follows without competing with it. It sets tone, establishes visual language, and creates anticipation — all in typically 30 seconds to 2 minutes — and then it gets out of the way.

The best title sequences feel inevitable in retrospect. You can't imagine the film or show without them. Getting there requires understanding the work deeply before designing anything.

## Immersion Before Concept

I don't start designing title sequences until I've spent significant time with the source material. For a film, that means reading the script, watching cuts if available, understanding the thematic content, the visual style of the cinematography, and the emotional arc of the work.

The title sequence lives in the same world as everything that follows it. If it's designed in isolation — as a pure motion design exercise disconnected from the work — it will feel disconnected. The audience feels that discontinuity even if they can't articulate it.

## The Metaphor Is the Structure

Strong title sequences are built on a central metaphor — a visual idea that represents the work's themes in abstract or symbolic form. This metaphor then generates the visual elements, the motion vocabulary, and the compositional logic of the sequence.

The metaphor doesn't have to be literal. Often the best ones are oblique — they capture the feeling or the thematic territory rather than depicting the content directly. A title sequence built on the metaphor of decay might use organic forms slowly deteriorating. One built on the metaphor of surveillance might use geometric structures that feel watched.

## Pacing and the Score

Title sequences almost always live with music or sound design, and the relationship between motion and audio is central to how the sequence lands. I work with the score or sound design from the earliest stages of concept development — not as background while I design, but as a structural element that shapes the pacing, the timing, and the moments of emphasis.

If the score is percussive, the motion editing might be cut to beats. If it's ambient and tonal, the motion might be slow and continuous, building gradually.

## Typography in Context

The type in a title sequence carries the sequence's most critical communication — the title, the principal credits — and needs to be legible without dominating. The type is part of the sequence's visual system, not placed on top of it.

I think of title type as living in the same dimensional space as the motion — moving with it, emerging from it, or existing in clear contrast to it. Type that floats disconnected from the visual field reads as an afterthought.

## The Last Frame

The last frame of a title sequence is the first frame of the content that follows. The visual conditions of the final title frame — color, brightness, visual density — need to provide a clean handoff to the opening of the film or episode.

A title sequence that ends on a very dark frame creates a different transition than one ending on a bright or colorful frame. I design the ending deliberately, in consultation with the editor who will be cutting the sequence into the content.
