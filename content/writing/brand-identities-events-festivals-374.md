Event and festival branding is a specific sub-discipline with its own logic. Unlike organizational identities that need to last decades, event identities have a built-in lifespan — from announcement to aftermath. The identity needs to build anticipation, identify and unify all elements of the event during the event, and then either conclude or evolve for the next edition.

## Annual vs. One-Off Events

An annual festival is a different design problem from a one-off event. The annual festival needs a consistent master identity — the festival's brand — and a system for differentiating each year's edition. The one-off event gets a purpose-built identity that doesn't need to accommodate future editions.

For annual festivals, I design two systems: the master identity (permanent, stable, representing the festival itself) and the annual edition identity (the visual language for this specific year). The annual identity is a sub-brand of the master — clearly related but distinctly of its year.

## Time-Based Identity Thinking

Event identities work across a temporal arc: announcement, build-up, event itself, aftermath. The visual language can evolve across this arc. The announcement phase might use a teaser aesthetic — limited information, building mystery. The build-up phase introduces more content and energy. The event itself uses the full identity system at high density. The aftermath celebrates what happened.

Designing for this arc means the identity system needs to have multiple registers — from restrained and anticipatory to full-presence and celebratory.

## Environmental Scale

Festival identities operate at environmental scale: stage backdrops, field signage, wristbands, banners, wayfinding. The identity elements need to work at these extremes of scale, from a 30-foot stage banner to a 1-inch wristband button.

I design festival identities with scale testing as a core part of the process. Elements that read beautifully on a printed program can become meaningless at banner scale if they don't have enough graphic boldness.

## The Merch Consideration

Festival merchandise is both a revenue stream and walking advertisement. The identity needs to translate onto objects — t-shirts, tote bags, caps, prints — in ways that feel desirable to attendees who want to signal their participation.

Merchandise identity is slightly different from event identity. On merchandise, the design needs to be collectible and attractive independent of the event context. I design the core merch graphics as standalone items that happen to reference the festival, rather than as applied versions of the functional event identity.

## Year-Over-Year System Evolution

For recurring festivals, the challenge is evolving the visual language year-over-year while maintaining the identity's essential character. Too much change loses accumulated recognition. Too little change loses freshness.

I establish what changes and what stays fixed: the logo mark and wordmark stay constant, the color palette shifts with each edition, the typographic system has a fixed primary face with a changing secondary, the graphic pattern is redesigned annually within consistent structural rules.
