I've designed posters for music venues, film festivals, gallery exhibitions, political campaigns, and community organizations. The subjects and audiences vary enormously. The underlying compositional principles don't.

## Tension and Resolution

Every successful composition creates tension and then resolves it. Two elements competing for the same visual territory creates tension. The resolution is the hierarchy — one element yields, and the viewer knows where to look.

Without tension, a composition is boring. Without resolution, it's confusing. The skill is creating enough tension to be interesting while providing enough resolution to be clear.

## The Focal Point

Every poster has a focal point — the first place the eye lands. I design the focal point intentionally, usually placing it somewhere off-center (the rule of thirds is a useful heuristic, not a law) and making it clearly dominant.

Once the focal point is established, every other element supports it by creating a path from itself toward the focal point. The secondary element pulls the eye toward the primary. The tertiary elements pull the eye toward the secondary. The composition becomes a directed journey.

## Proportional Relationships

The relative sizes of elements in a composition should have clear proportional relationships. Two elements at nearly the same size fight. Two elements at dramatically different sizes create hierarchy. I work with clear size ratios — 2:1, 3:1, or more extreme — between adjacent hierarchy levels.

These ratios also apply to spatial relationships. The space between closely related elements should be smaller than the space between unrelated elements. The grouping created by spatial proximity is one of the most basic organizational tools available.

## Directional Flow

Diagonal lines create movement. Curves create rhythm. Verticals create stability. Horizontals create calm. Every element in a composition contributes to a directional flow — I work with these directional forces rather than against them.

In type-heavy posters, the natural reading direction (left to right, top to bottom in Western contexts) creates a dominant flow. I can reinforce this flow or counteract it with other directional elements. Reinforcing it creates a smooth, easy-to-navigate composition. Counteracting it creates energy and tension.

## The Empty Quarter

I try to preserve one section of every poster where nothing happens — a visual rest. This "empty quarter" is not wasted space; it's breathing room that makes the active areas feel more dynamic by contrast.

The temptation to fill every corner is real. Client feedback often pushes toward more content, bigger sizes, less space. The discipline is knowing when the empty space is doing more work than anything you could put in it.
