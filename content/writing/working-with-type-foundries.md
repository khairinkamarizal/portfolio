The relationship between a graphic designer and type foundries is one of the most important professional relationships in the field. Typefaces are the primary material of much of the work I do. Knowing how to navigate foundry relationships — licensing correctly, commissioning thoughtfully, and maintaining goodwill — matters both practically and ethically.

## Understanding Type Licensing

Type licensing is more complex than most designers initially realize, and the complexity has real consequences. The wrong license for a use case is a legal and ethical problem, not just a technical one.

The most common licensing structures:

**Desktop licenses:** For use in design applications on specific machines. Typically sold per computer or per user. Doesn't cover web use, app embedding, or large-scale print runs.

**Web licenses:** For serving typefaces as webfonts. Often priced per pageview tier or per domain. Separate from desktop licenses.

**App embedding:** For apps that include the typeface in the binary. Usually a separate, one-time or periodic licensing structure.

**Broadcast/video:** For embedding type in video content for broadcast or streaming. Many foundries license this separately.

**Large print run:** Some licenses cap the number of copies that can be produced. High-volume printing may require an extended or commercial license.

I check the license for every typeface I specify for a client, for every use case the client will have. If the license is unclear, I contact the foundry directly. Foundries are generally responsive to licensing questions, and it's far better to clarify upfront than to discover a licensing problem after a project launches.

## Building Foundry Relationships

I have ongoing relationships with several foundries whose work I admire and whose output I follow closely. These relationships have practical benefits: early access to new releases, responsive service when I have licensing questions or production issues, and sometimes discounts for regular clients.

Building these relationships requires being a genuine customer — actually purchasing licenses rather than pirating type — and being a professional communication partner: clear about what I need, prompt in response, and grateful for good service.

## Commissioning Custom Type

For projects that justify a custom typeface, the process involves engaging a type designer whose work style aligns with the project's needs. The briefing process is extensive: defining the character of the intended typeface, the character set required, the specific technical requirements, and the timeline.

Custom type is a specialized collaboration. The type designer needs the same quality of brief that I provide to my own clients — clear requirements, good references, defined criteria for success. Vague briefs produce vague typefaces.

## When to Use Free Type

High-quality free typefaces exist. Google Fonts includes several typefaces that I use in professional work. The quality ceiling of free type has risen significantly in the last decade.

The distinction between "free" and "lower quality" is no longer reliable. What remains true is that widely-used free typefaces appear everywhere, and using them requires awareness of this ubiquity. A brand that uses a generic widely-deployed free typeface as its primary brand font may find its type associated with dozens of other brands using the same face.
