One of the first decisions in any logo project is structural: does this brand need a graphic symbol, a wordmark, or a combination of both? It's a question most designers skip over because they assume clients always want "a logo with a little icon thing." But that assumption leads to a lot of marks that exist because they're expected, not because they're needed.

## When a Wordmark Alone Is the Right Answer

A wordmark without a graphic symbol is appropriate when the name itself is distinctive enough to carry the brand. This is especially true for brands with short, memorable names that can be rendered as a distinctive typographic form.

Many of the world's most recognized brands are pure wordmarks. The type is the mark. Adding a symbol would be redundant — it would compete with the name rather than reinforcing it.

If a client's name is clear, memorable, and distinctive, my first question is whether a symbol adds anything or just adds complexity.

## When a Symbol Earns Its Place

A graphic symbol justifies its existence when it does work the wordmark can't. Symbols are particularly valuable when:

- The brand name is long or difficult to abbreviate
- The brand operates in markets where the language barrier makes a visual mark more effective than text
- The brand identity needs to work on products or contexts where the full name can't fit
- The brand is building toward the ability to use the symbol alone (as recognizable as the name)

The symbol has to earn its place by adding meaning, recognition, or functional value. A symbol that's decorative only is a symbol that's in the way.

## Combination Marks and Their Structures

When both a symbol and a wordmark are needed, they can be arranged in several configurations: horizontal lockup (symbol left, wordmark right), vertical lockup (symbol above, wordmark below), or stacked with the wordmark inside or overlapping the symbol.

Each configuration has appropriate applications. I design at least two lockup configurations for any combination mark — a horizontal for wide applications, a stacked or vertical for square or constrained formats.

I also design the symbol to work independently and the wordmark to work independently. A combination mark that only functions as a unit is fragile — the brand system collapses in applications where the full lockup doesn't fit.

## The Recognition Trajectory

Young brands usually need to show the name alongside the symbol because the symbol doesn't yet carry recognition on its own. As the brand matures and the symbol becomes associated with the name through repeated exposure, it can begin to stand alone.

I design for this trajectory. The symbol variant — designed to work independently — is part of the initial deliverable, even if the brand isn't ready to use it alone yet. Building it now, in the system's design language, is much better than retrofitting it later.
