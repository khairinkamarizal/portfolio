Type-only poster design is one of the most demanding disciplines in graphic design. Remove the photograph, the illustration, the graphic mark. What's left is typography alone: letterforms, spacing, hierarchy, and the white space between them. There's nowhere to hide and no supporting elements to lean on.

The posters I find most compelling are often type-only. A great typographic poster communicates with clarity and authority that mixed-media posters often can't match. But it requires a different kind of design thinking.

## The Typeface Carries Everything

In a poster with images, the typeface is one of several communicative elements. In a type-only poster, the typeface carries everything. Its personality, its weight, its historical associations, its geometric character. Every quality of the letterform contributes to what the poster communicates emotionally.

This makes typeface selection the central creative decision, not one consideration among many. I spend more time on typeface selection for type-only work than for any other format. I'm looking for a face where the basic letterforms are interesting enough to carry a composition at large scale.

Some typefaces that are excellent for body text are visually boring at display scale. Others that are too quirky for extended reading are exactly right for a 200pt headline. Type-only poster work tends to reward display faces with distinct character — expressive cuts, unusual proportions, strong construction logic.

## Scale as Drama

When there's no image to provide drama, scale does the work. Type-only posters often work through extreme scale contrast: one element enormous, another element tiny, the relationship between them creating tension and narrative.

A single word at 400pt occupying most of the poster, with the event details in 10pt beneath it, is a classic type-only poster structure. The scale differential creates hierarchy, drama, and a reading sequence without any image to anchor it.

Extreme scale also allows type to become abstract. At a large enough size, letterforms stop reading as characters and start reading as shapes. The curve of an O becomes a graphic element. The diagonal of an A becomes a compositional force. This shift from linguistic to visual is unique to type-only work and worth exploring deliberately.

## Composition Built From Letters

Type-only composition is different from image-based composition because the raw material is already structured. Each letter has a fixed shape, weight, and proportion. You can scale, rotate, and position letters, but you can't change their fundamental form (without modifying the typeface itself, which is a different skill set).

This constraint is generative. Within the limitations of the letterforms, I'm looking for:

- Relationships between letter shapes in the composition
- Moments where letters from one word visually interact with letters from another
- Opportunities for negative space created by the gaps in and around letterforms
- Alignment structures that come from the geometric logic of the type

## The All-Caps Structure

All-caps type in a type-only poster creates a different visual quality than mixed case. All-caps is architecturally regular. The letterforms sit on a consistent baseline with consistent cap heights. The rhythm is steady.

Mixed case is more organic. Ascenders and descenders create vertical variation. The visual texture is richer and more complex.

Both approaches work. The choice depends on the poster's personality. All-caps tends to feel authoritative, graphic, confrontational. Mixed case tends to feel more literary, narrative, warm.

## White Space as a Typeface

The final element in a type-only poster is the space between and around the letters. In type-only work, I think of white space as a material as important as the ink. It has weight, direction, and shape. Designing the negative spaces created by the type is as important as designing the positive letterforms.

Some of my best type-only poster work has come from designing backward: starting with the shape of the white space I want and finding the typographic arrangement that produces it.
