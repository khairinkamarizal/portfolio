Design education is excellent at teaching you how to design and nearly useless at teaching you how to run a design practice. The gap between "skilled designer" and "successful independent designer" is mostly business knowledge — contracts, client management, project operations, financial management. I learned most of this through expensive mistakes over the first three years of my practice.

Here's what I wish someone had told me.

## Contracts Are Non-Negotiable

Before I do any paid work, there is a signed contract. No exceptions. Not for small projects, not for friends, not for clients who claim the paperwork will slow things down.

A contract specifies: what is being delivered, what the payment terms are, what happens if the client doesn't pay, who owns the intellectual property and when ownership transfers, and what happens if either party needs to cancel.

Most contract disputes are caused by ambiguity, not dishonesty. A clear contract eliminates the ambiguity before it can cause problems.

## Intellectual Property: Know What You're Selling

In most jurisdictions, copyright in commissioned work stays with the creator by default unless explicitly transferred in writing. This means that unless the contract says otherwise, you retain copyright in everything you create — the client gets a license to use it, not ownership.

I decide on an IP basis for each project: am I transferring copyright (typically for a higher fee), granting an exclusive license, or retaining ownership with a usage license? I document this explicitly in the contract.

Many clients assume they own everything you create for them. They don't, by default. Have the conversation and document the agreement.

## Cash Flow Is the Real Business Challenge

Being fully booked doesn't guarantee financial stability. The real challenge is cash flow — having money available when bills are due, regardless of when client payments arrive.

Practices that help: 50% deposit on every project (standard and widely accepted), net-15 or net-30 payment terms rather than net-60 or longer, late payment fees specified in the contract, and a retainer structure for repeat clients.

I also maintain three months of operating expenses in reserve. This buffer means that a slow month or a late-paying client doesn't create a financial crisis.

## The Discovery Phase Saves Projects

The most expensive thing in design practice is fixing a misalignment late in the project — when the client sees work that doesn't match what they imagined, or when fundamental strategic questions surface during design review that should have been answered in the brief.

A discovery phase — a paid initial engagement where I clarify the brief, align on strategy, and establish shared expectations — prevents most of these expensive late-project surprises. The discovery investment saves many times its cost.

I charge for discovery work. It has real value.

## Referrals Are the Best Marketing

The vast majority of my project work comes from referrals — former clients recommending me to their networks, or industry contacts passing on opportunities. This is true for most established design practices.

Referrals come from doing excellent work and being easy to work with. Easy to work with means: communicating proactively, meeting deadlines, being honest about problems before they become crises, and giving clients more than they expected.

No marketing strategy outperforms a reputation for excellent work delivered with professionalism. Build the reputation; the referrals follow.
