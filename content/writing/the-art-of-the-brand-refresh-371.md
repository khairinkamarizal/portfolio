A brand refresh is not a rebrand. A rebrand replaces the identity. A refresh renews it — updating what's dated, strengthening what's weak, and extending the system to cover new applications, while preserving the equity that's been built. The distinction matters because the process and the scope are completely different.

## Diagnosing What Needs Work

The first step in any refresh is honest diagnosis. What specifically isn't working? "The brand feels dated" is not a brief. "The primary typeface has become associated with a previous era of tech design and no longer reads as contemporary in our category" is a brief. "The color palette doesn't work on dark backgrounds, which we now use extensively in digital" is a brief.

I audit the existing system against current applications and identify specific gaps. Usually the problems are more targeted than clients initially think. The logo itself is often fine; the problem is the secondary typeface, or the photographic direction, or the color system's digital behavior.

## What to Preserve

Before changing anything, I establish what has genuine equity. Ask: what visual elements do the audience associate with this brand? What would they notice if it changed? What has been used consistently enough to have become proprietary to this brand?

These elements are protected. The refresh works around them. A thirty-year-old company that has consistently used a specific shade of green doesn't casually change that green. The color has become the brand as much as the logo has.

## Updating Typography

Typography is usually the most visible sign of dating in a brand system. Typefaces that were cutting-edge in 2005 or 2012 now read as of their moment. Updating the typeface is one of the highest-leverage moves in a refresh.

The replacement typeface should do everything the original did — maintain the same brand personality, work at all the required sizes and applications — while feeling contemporary. I look for typefaces that have the same formal DNA as the original but have been designed more recently or have digital-native qualities the original lacked.

## Extending the System

Many refreshes are triggered not by dating but by system gaps — the original identity was built for a narrower range of applications than the organization now needs. The refresh adds motion guidelines, social media templates, digital color specifications, or icon systems that didn't exist in the original.

This kind of refresh preserves the core identity entirely and builds infrastructure around it. The brand looks the same; it just works in more places.

## Communicating the Refresh

Unlike a full rebrand, a refresh usually doesn't need a public launch moment. It can be rolled out gradually as materials are updated. The internal communication is more important: the design team and communications staff need to understand what changed, why, and how the new elements work.
