## What Visual Metaphor Does

A logo that contains a visual metaphor communicates more than it shows. The mark depicts one thing but implies another — and the gap between what's shown and what's implied is where meaning lives.

The FedEx arrow hidden in the letterform negative space. The bear hidden in the Toblerone mountain. The Amazon smile that doubles as an A-to-Z arrow. These marks work because they hold a secondary reading that rewards attention, makes the mark memorable, and reinforces something true about the brand.

## Metaphor That Earns Its Place

Not every mark needs visual metaphor. A strong, distinctive, formally resolved mark can communicate effectively without double-reading. The standard isn't "does this mark contain a metaphor" — it's "does this mark communicate the right things effectively."

When metaphor is used, it should be there because it reinforces something genuine about the brand, not because it's clever for its own sake. Clever for its own sake produces marks that feel like parlor tricks rather than brand assets.

## Finding the Metaphor in the Brief

I look for metaphor opportunities in the brand's actual attributes — what the brand does, what it stands for, what it wants to be associated with. I ask: what visual forms are related to these qualities? What shapes encode these meanings? What geometric structures connect to these concepts?

The best metaphors feel inevitable in retrospect. They connect the form to the brand's truth in a way that seems obvious once you see it.

## Visible vs. Hidden Metaphor

Some metaphors are immediately visible — the shape is clearly doing double duty from first glance. Others are hidden and require a moment of perception to discover. Both are valid. Hidden metaphors reward return viewings and create moments of discovery that build emotional connection to the mark.

The risk of hidden metaphors is that they can be too hidden — viewers who never notice them receive no benefit. I test hidden metaphors with external viewers. If nobody finds it unprompted, it needs to be more legible or reconsidered.

## Metaphor and Cultural Context

Visual metaphors rely on shared cultural associations to communicate. A mark that encodes a concept through a shape assumes that the target audience associates that shape with that concept. Cultural differences in these associations are real and sometimes significant.

For brands operating across multiple cultural contexts, I research whether the intended metaphor carries the right associations in each context. A mark that works brilliantly for a North American audience may carry unintended connotations elsewhere.

## The Single Metaphor Rule

One metaphor per mark. Two metaphors compete with each other for attention and neither reads clearly. The discipline of committing to a single metaphorical reading — and making it work as well as possible — produces stronger marks than trying to say multiple things simultaneously.
