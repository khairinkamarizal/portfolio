Typeface selection for brand identity is different from typeface selection for a single project. A brand will use its typefaces for years — across hundreds of applications, produced by different designers, in different contexts. The typeface needs to serve the brand across all of those scenarios, not just look good in the initial presentation.

This long-term view changes the criteria.

## The Brand System Requirements

Before I evaluate any typeface, I map the system requirements: what roles does the type system need to fill? Most brand systems need:

- A display typeface for headlines, titles, and primary communication
- A text typeface for body copy, supporting content, and UI text
- Optionally, a functional or monospace face for specific applications

Some brands can use a single type family for all roles (a superfamily with enough range to span display through text weight and function). Others need two distinct families.

## Evaluating for Versatility

A brand typeface needs to work across print, screen, and motion — and across the full range of sizes from tiny (legal copy on packaging) to enormous (billboard and signage). Testing every typeface candidate at the extremes of the scale range reveals whether it has the versatility the brand needs.

Specifically:
- At small print sizes: does it remain legible? Are the counters open enough? Is the x-height adequate?
- At display scale: are the letterforms interesting enough? Does the spacing need extensive custom adjustment?
- On screen: does a web or screen version of the typeface exist? Is the hinting adequate for screen rendering?
- In motion: how does the typeface perform when animated? Are the letterforms clear at video frame rates?

## The Licensing Reality

Type licensing is one of the most frequently overlooked aspects of typeface selection for brand identity. A typeface that looks ideal for a brand may have a licensing structure that's prohibitive for the brand's actual needs.

I evaluate type licenses alongside visual qualities. For brands with digital products or high-traffic websites, webfont licensing costs can be significant and need to be budgeted as a brand investment. For brands using type in apps, app licensing is a separate consideration.

I've recommended against otherwise ideal typefaces because the licensing structure didn't fit the client's budget or use case. A typeface the brand can actually afford to license and use consistently is worth more than a perfect typeface they'll use improperly because the license is unclear.

## Custom Type as an Investment

For brands at a certain scale where maximum distinctiveness justifies the investment, custom type — designed specifically for the brand — is the strongest possible solution. A bespoke typeface carries no associations with other brands, can be designed to encode the brand's character in every letterform, and can be optimized specifically for the brand's primary use cases.

The cost is significant (a properly designed custom type family involves months of specialized work by a skilled type designer), but the value is proportionally significant for a brand that needs to stand out and can maintain the identity over decades.

## The System Over Time

The type system I specify today needs to be maintainable by the brand over years, potentially decades. Choosing typefaces from stable, well-supported foundries — rather than typefaces from foundries that may not exist in 10 years — reduces the risk of system disruption as the brand evolves.
