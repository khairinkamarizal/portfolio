Exhibition graphic design sits at the intersection of poster design, editorial design, and environmental design. The work appears in galleries, museums, trade shows, and cultural institutions — contexts where the graphic work is experienced spatially, in sequence, over time, and in relation to architecture.

These contexts require a different set of decisions than standalone poster work, and working in them has significantly expanded how I think about composition, hierarchy, and pacing even in my non-exhibition work.

## The Sequential Experience

A poster is encountered once, in a single moment. Exhibition graphics are encountered in sequence as a visitor moves through a space. The first panel creates expectations; subsequent panels fulfill, complicate, or subvert those expectations. The final panel resolves.

I design exhibition graphics with this narrative arc in mind. What does the visitor learn or experience at each point in the sequence? How does the graphic system develop and reveal itself as they move through the space?

This requires understanding the floor plan and traffic flow before designing a single piece. Where do visitors enter? What do they see first? What natural sightlines does the space create? The graphic system should work with these sightlines, not against them.

## Scale Hierarchy Across the Space

Exhibition spaces have a scale hierarchy that shapes every design decision. Large-scale environmental graphics — wall-spanning type, major photographic prints — establish the visual character of the space from a distance. Medium-scale panel graphics carry the primary content. Small-scale labels and captions provide detail for close engagement.

Each scale level requires different design decisions. The large-scale elements need to hold up to sustained scrutiny at close range and read clearly at distance. The small-scale elements need to be legible at arm's length without competing with the larger elements.

## Text Density for Standing Readers

Exhibition copy is read by people standing, often in groups, sometimes in poor light. This creates strict practical requirements: larger type sizes than editorial print work, generous line spacing, shorter paragraphs, and very clear hierarchy.

I specify body text for exhibition panels at a minimum of 14pt, often 16–18pt for standard reading distances. This feels extremely large in the design file and exactly right in the installed space.

## Color in Spatial Contexts

Color in exhibition spaces has architectural implications that color in print doesn't. A large wall painted or covered in a saturated color affects the quality of light in the entire space. It reflects color onto the objects and artwork in the space. It changes how visitors feel physically.

I think about exhibition color with these spatial consequences in mind. Saturated walls can be powerful design statements but require coordination with the curatorial and architectural teams to ensure they're serving rather than competing with the exhibited content.

## The Printing and Installation Reality

Exhibition graphics are produced at scales and through processes that require specialized knowledge: large-format printing on various substrates, fabric printing for hanging banners, vinyl application to walls, direct printing on rigid panels.

Each production method has specific design requirements: resolution specifications, color profiles, panel join positions, installation tolerances. I work with the exhibition production vendor early and thoroughly — the most brilliant design falls apart if it can't be executed correctly at the installation.

Walk the installed space during installation if at all possible. What you learn in thirty minutes there is worth more than hours of studio preparation.
