## The Confusion Is Common

I hear it constantly from clients: "We need a logo." What they usually mean — and what they need — is a brand identity. The logo is one piece of it. The most visible piece, maybe, but not the whole thing.

Getting clear on this distinction early in a project changes what you deliver and what clients expect.

## What a Logo Actually Is

A logo is a mark used to identify an organization, product, or service. It might be a wordmark — the company name set in a specific typeface. It might be a symbol or icon. It might be a combination of both. It might be an abstract mark or a letterform.

A good logo is distinctive, scalable, and appropriate for its context. It works in one color. It holds up at small sizes. It communicates something about the brand without needing words to explain it.

That's the logo. It's a single element.

## What a Brand Identity Is

A brand identity is the complete visual system that a brand uses to present itself. It includes the logo, yes — but also a color palette, a typographic system, a photographic or illustration style, iconography, layout principles, and the rules that govern how all of these elements work together.

A brand identity means that everything from a business card to a billboard to a packaging label feels like it comes from the same place. Consistency across all those touchpoints is what builds recognition over time.

## Why the Distinction Matters in Practice

When a client says they need a logo and you only deliver a mark without the supporting system, you've left them without the tools to actually use the brand consistently. The next person who touches their materials — a different designer, an in-house team member, a printer — will make their own decisions because there are no rules to follow.

That's how brands drift. That's how the carefully crafted mark ends up surrounded by inconsistent typography and arbitrary color choices.

## What I Deliver

On any brand engagement, I deliver both the logo and the identity system. The logo is the centerpiece. The identity system is the infrastructure that makes the logo mean something in the world.

Brand guidelines aren't bureaucracy. They're the manual that keeps the work alive after I'm done with it.

## The Practical Upshot

If you're a designer, push back (politely) when a client says they just need a logo. Have the conversation about what they're actually trying to build. If they're launching a business or a product that needs to show up consistently across multiple touchpoints, they need a system, not just a mark.

And if you're a client reading this: the logo is the beginning of the conversation, not the end of it.
