## The Equity Problem

Every brand refresh starts with the same tension: the existing visual identity has problems, but it also has years of accumulated recognition. People know this brand. They can identify it. That recognition is worth money, and a clumsy refresh throws it away.

My job in a refresh project is to solve the problems in the existing identity without discarding what's working. That requires being very precise about what the problems actually are — and resisting the temptation to redesign everything just because I have the brief to do so.

## Auditing the Existing System

Before I suggest changing anything, I do a thorough audit of the existing visual identity. I collect every branded touchpoint I can find — business cards, signage, website, social media, packaging, uniforms, vehicles, email templates. I want to understand how the brand actually looks in the world, not just how it looks in the brand guidelines PDF.

This audit almost always reveals a gap between the intended identity and the applied identity. The guidelines say one thing; the materials do another. Sometimes this gap is the real problem. The mark and palette might be fine, but the system hasn't been properly maintained or taught to the people applying it.

If the gap is the problem, the solution isn't a redesign — it's better documentation and training.

## Identifying What Has Equity

I assess each element of the existing identity for equity. How recognizable is the logo mark independent of context? How associated are the specific colors with this brand? Does the typeface family have any distinctiveness that people have come to associate with this brand?

Some elements have strong equity and should be evolved carefully. Others have been applied inconsistently and have no equity to preserve. The audit tells me which is which.

## Evolution, Not Revolution

Most successful brand refreshes evolve rather than replace. The mark gets refined — proportions cleaned up, details sharpened, reproduction issues resolved — but the core concept remains. The palette gets clarified — stronger primary/secondary relationships, better defined neutrals — but the primary color stays recognizable.

The test I apply: would someone who encountered the new brand after knowing the old brand feel like it's the same organization, only more confident and current? If yes, I've got the balance right.

## When Full Replacement Is Justified

Sometimes the existing identity is genuinely unredeemable. The mark is technically flawed, legally problematic, or so closely associated with a chapter of the brand's history that the organization is trying to move away from. In these cases, a full replacement is the right answer.

Even then, I usually preserve one continuity element. It might be a color, a general typographic approach, or a structural element of the mark. This gives audiences a perceptual bridge from the old to the new.

## Rollout Strategy Is Part of the Design

A brand refresh that goes live all at once on every touchpoint creates confusion. People encounter the new and old identities simultaneously during any transition period, and the contrast makes both look wrong.

I include rollout phasing recommendations in every refresh project. Digital properties first — they're free to update and reach the most people. Print and signage last — they're expensive and have long replacement cycles. Primary brand materials before secondary or tertiary applications.

The transition should feel like progress, not chaos.
