## The Problem with Most Brand Guidelines

I've seen beautifully designed brand guideline documents that no one uses. They're comprehensive, polished, and completely impractical. They document everything the brand should do and provide no actual tools to do it. The people who receive them look at them once, find them overwhelming, and go back to improvising.

That's a failure. Guidelines that don't get used are just expensive documents.

## What Makes Guidelines Practical

A usable brand guideline is built around the actual people who will use it. Not the designer who created the brand, but the in-house marketing team member who needs to make a social post, the external agency that needs to produce a campaign, the web developer who needs to implement the color system.

Before I build guidelines, I ask: who will use this, for what tasks, with what tools? The answers shape what I include and how I explain it.

## Show More, Explain Less

The most effective guidelines I've produced are heavy on examples and light on rules. Instead of "the logo must always have X pixels of clear space," I show the logo with X pixels of clear space around it in an actual application context. Visual examples communicate faster than written rules, especially for non-designers.

That doesn't mean rules have no place. But every rule should be illustrated.

## Organizing Around Use Cases

I organize guidelines around what people need to do, not around design theory. Instead of sections for "Logo," "Color," "Typography" in the abstract, I include sections like "Making a Social Post," "Setting Up an Email Signature," "Working with an External Vendor." These use-case sections pull in the relevant brand elements and explain how they work together in context.

This is more work to build. It's significantly more useful to use.

## Digital-First Documentation

Static PDF guidelines are increasingly obsolete for brands that live primarily on digital channels. Interactive guidelines — accessible at a URL, searchable, easy to navigate — are more practical for modern teams.

At minimum, guidelines should be delivered in a format that the people using them can actually open and reference without friction. A PDF that requires downloading and opening a specific application creates one more barrier.

## Maintenance and Versioning

Guidelines need to evolve as the brand evolves. I always include a versioning system and a clear owner — the person responsible for updating the guidelines when the brand changes. A guidelines document without a maintenance plan goes stale and eventually misleads rather than guides.

Living documentation beats dead documentation every time.
