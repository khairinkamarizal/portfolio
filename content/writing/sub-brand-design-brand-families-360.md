Sub-brand design is a balancing act. Too much connection to the parent and the sub-brand loses the distinctiveness it needs to do its job. Too little connection and the parent's equity doesn't transfer. The right calibration depends on what each brand needs to accomplish.

## Why Sub-Brands Exist

Organizations create sub-brands for several reasons: to address different customer segments without confusing them, to ring-fence a product category that carries different associations than the parent, to support an acquisition without immediately absorbing it, or to give distinct offerings a sense of independent identity while keeping them in the family.

Each of these reasons has different implications for how closely the sub-brand should connect to the parent. A sub-brand meant to serve a different audience segment needs more visual separation than a product line sub-brand meant to complement the parent's core offering.

## The Visual Connection Spectrum

I think about the visual connection between parent and sub-brand as a spectrum. At one end: the sub-brand uses the same primary typeface, same color family, and the parent's logo as a prefix. At the other end: the sub-brand has its own typeface, its own primary color, and its own logo mark — with only a small endorsement linking it to the parent.

The right position on this spectrum depends on the endorsement strategy: how much credibility does the parent brand need to lend to the sub-brand, and how much independent identity does the sub-brand need to do its job?

## Shared Graphic DNA

Even when a sub-brand needs significant visual independence, I build shared graphic DNA that connects the family without creating visual identity. This might be a shared spatial rhythm, a shared approach to photography, or a shared set of design principles that produce family resemblance without identical execution.

The way a brand uses white space, the proportional system it applies to layout, the way it crops photography — these are identity elements that can be shared across a brand family without making the family feel like a single monolithic brand.

## Color as a Differentiator

Color is the most efficient differentiator in a brand family. If the parent brand owns blue, the sub-brand can own a specific teal or navy or sky blue that's clearly related but distinct. Or it can step away from blue entirely and own a different hue — which creates more separation but also less connection.

I usually start with the parent's color palette and ask: where is there room for a distinct but related hue? The answer shapes the sub-brand's visual territory.

## Governance

Sub-brand systems need governance. Without clear rules about when to create a new sub-brand vs. extending an existing one, organizations develop sprawling brand families that nobody can manage. I build decision frameworks into the brand architecture documentation: what triggers a new sub-brand, what the approval process is, and how new sub-brands are introduced to the system.
