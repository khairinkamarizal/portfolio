## Color Ages Differently Than Marks

A logo mark can last twenty years without looking dated if it was designed on solid structural principles. Color ages faster. The cultural associations of specific hues shift over time, and palettes that read as contemporary in one decade can feel like relics in the next.

This doesn't mean color should be changed frequently — that destroys the recognition value built through consistent application. But it does mean color needs to be evaluated honestly as part of any brand health assessment.

## Signs a Palette Has Aged

The clearest signal: the palette looks more like a design trend than a brand decision. Palettes that were fashionable in a specific moment — the flat, desaturated pastels of the mid-2010s, the electric gradients of the late 2010s — carry a timestamp that becomes more visible as time passes.

A second signal: the palette no longer fits the brand's current positioning. A company that has moved upmarket may find that its original accessible, friendly color palette works against its premium aspirations.

A third signal: the palette doesn't function in digital contexts that didn't exist when it was created. A palette designed before mobile-first design may have colors that don't meet digital accessibility standards or don't read well on modern high-density displays.

## What a Responsible Color Evolution Looks Like

A color evolution — as distinct from a complete color rebrand — keeps the strategic logic of the existing palette while updating its specific values. The brand that owns a warm amber doesn't abandon warm amber; it refines the specific hue, saturation, and brightness to feel current while maintaining the recognition built over years.

The steps I follow:

**Audit what has equity.** Which colors are genuinely recognized and associated with the brand? These are worth preserving or evolving closely.

**Identify what isn't working.** Which colors have aged poorly, fail accessibility standards, or no longer fit the positioning?

**Evolve, don't replace.** Adjust hues and saturation values within the existing color family rather than switching to entirely different colors.

**Test across applications.** A color evolution needs to work across all existing brand touchpoints, not just the new ones being redesigned.

## The Communication Challenge

Color changes — even subtle ones — can create confusion among audiences who have built recognition around the existing palette. This means color evolution needs to be communicated and transitioned thoughtfully, not executed as a simultaneous global swap across all touchpoints.

A phased rollout, starting with new materials while existing materials remain in use, gives the audience time to adjust without creating the impression of a full rebrand.
