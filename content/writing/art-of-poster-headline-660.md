I spend more time on headlines than clients expect. The headline is not just the text that gets typeset large at the top — it's the primary communication, the thing the viewer takes away, the reason the poster works or doesn't. Getting the headline right is partly a design problem and partly a writing problem, and I engage with both.

## The Headline's Job

The headline of an event poster has a specific job: immediately communicate the most important thing about the event in the fewest possible words. Not everything — just the most important thing. The rest can be secondary, tertiary, supporting information.

For a concert poster, the most important thing is usually the performer's name. For an exhibition poster, it might be the artist's name or the exhibition title. For a film festival poster, it might be the festival name or an evocative tagline.

Once I'm clear on what the headline needs to communicate, every other decision flows from that clarity.

## Length: Fewer Words Are Almost Always Better

The temptation is to include more in the headline — to communicate context, to be precise, to avoid ambiguity. This usually produces weak headlines that require reading rather than immediate reception.

I push for shorter. One word is better than three. Three words are better than seven. Each word that's removed from a headline increases the weight and impact of the remaining words.

When a client gives me a headline that's too long, I ask: if you could only keep three words, which three? That answer is often the real headline.

## Line Breaks Are Design Decisions

Where a headline breaks across lines is a typographic and communicative decision. The line break controls the rhythm of reading, the emphasis of individual words, and the shape of the typographic block.

A headline set as a single long line reads differently than the same headline broken into three short lines. Breaking at meaningful phrase boundaries — after verbs, after natural pauses — produces more readable, more emphatic results than breaking at arbitrary width limits.

I set every multi-line headline with deliberate line breaks, never relying on the software's automatic wrapping to determine where lines end.

## Typography Amplifies or Undermines the Headline

The typeface, weight, size, and setting of the headline either amplify what the words communicate or work against it. A soft, rounded typeface set loosely undermines a headline that communicates urgency. A heavy compressed typeface set very tight undermines a headline about leisure and ease.

The typographic treatment should intensify the feeling of the headline, not contradict it. This means making the typographic decision in relationship to the words, not independently of them.

## The Test

I test headlines with the five-second rule: show the poster to someone for five seconds, then take it away. Ask them: what was the poster about? What do they remember? If they remember the headline message, it's working. If they remember a visual detail but not what the headline communicated, the headline isn't landing with enough force.

Five seconds is generous. In a real urban poster environment, viewers are moving, not stopping. The effective exposure might be two seconds or less. Design for the moving viewer, not the contemplative one.
