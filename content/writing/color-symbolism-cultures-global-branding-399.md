Color associations are not universal. A color that signals premium and sophistication in one cultural context can signal mourning or danger in another. For organizations operating globally, this is not an academic concern — it's a brand risk that can be anticipated and managed.

## The Research Process

Before finalizing any brand color for a global organization, I research the color's associations in every significant market. This research isn't just about avoiding negative associations (though that matters) — it's about understanding whether the intended associations are actually available in each context.

Primary research means talking to people from the relevant cultural backgrounds. Secondary research means reviewing available cultural color association studies — these exist for most major markets and are regularly updated. Neither source alone is sufficient.

## High-Risk Colors by Region

White in many East Asian contexts is associated with mourning and death — the contrast with its associations of purity and cleanliness in Western contexts is stark. A global brand with white as its primary color needs to understand how this reads in Japan, China, and Korea.

Red in China is a color of luck, celebration, and prosperity. In Western contexts, red signals warning, urgency, or prohibition. A brand entering the Chinese market with a red identity that's positioned around urgency will find that positioning working against its color.

Green in some Islamic contexts has sacred associations — it's the color of Islam. Using green casually or commercially in contexts where this association is strong requires care.

Purple in many Latin American countries has strong associations with mourning and Catholic penitence. A premium purple palette might read as luxury in North America and as funeral in Brazil.

## When to Adapt

The question of whether to adapt brand colors by market is genuinely difficult. A single global color maintains consistency and builds a unified global identity. Adapted colors can be more appropriate in specific markets but fragment the brand system and create confusion for audiences that see both versions.

My recommendation: if the association is negative (mourning, danger, prohibition), adapt. If the association is just different rather than negative, maintain consistency and invest in building the brand's own associations. Coca-Cola's red doesn't read as "warning" in China despite the Western association, because Coca-Cola has been consistent long enough to build its own color associations globally.

## Testing Before Committing

For global rebrand projects, I test color concepts in each key market before committing. This means showing the color system to people from each cultural context and asking open-ended questions about their reactions — not leading questions that ask whether they like it.

The test is about understanding, not approval. Uncovering an unexpected negative association before the brand launches is invaluable. Uncovering it after is expensive.
