## The More-Is-More Mistake

I regularly receive brand guidelines from clients who've had previous identity work done that includes a palette of twelve or fifteen colors. The logic seems sound: more colors give designers more flexibility. In practice, it produces incoherence.

When there are fifteen brand colors, every designer who touches the brand makes their own judgment about which colors to use in which contexts. The result is brand materials that look like they came from fifteen different brands.

## What a Tight Palette Does

A tight palette — three to five colors — forces coherence. When every designer working on brand materials has the same small set of options, the output looks unified. The constraint is the feature.

Tight palettes also tend to produce more distinctive brands. A brand that lives in two specific, unusual colors is more visually ownable than a brand that uses twelve colors that any brand could also use.

## The Minimum Viable Palette

For most small to midsize brands, the minimum viable palette is:

- One primary brand color
- One secondary color (complementary or contrasting)
- One or two neutrals (typically near-white and near-black, not necessarily pure white and black)

That's three to four colors. Enough for flexibility. Few enough for coherence.

I'll sometimes add one accent color — used sparingly, for emphasis — bringing the total to five. Beyond five, the complexity starts outweighing the benefits.

## When a Larger Palette Is Justified

A larger palette makes sense when the brand has a legitimate need for color-coded differentiation — multiple product lines or service categories that benefit from distinct color identities within a unified system.

But even in this case, the approach is a structured system, not an unconstrained set. The system might have a primary palette of three colors plus a secondary palette of six categorization colors, with clear rules about when each subset is used. This is different from "here are twelve colors, use your judgment."

## Specifying the Tints

A note about tints: some brand systems extend the palette through tints of the primary colors. A navy blue at 100%, 60%, and 30% gives three values from one base color. This can extend the visual vocabulary without adding color complexity.

Tints need to be specified explicitly in the guidelines with exact values, not left for designers to approximate. "A lighter version of the primary blue" is not a specification. "Primary blue at 30% opacity over white" is.

## The Enforcement Challenge

The most elegant palette in the world fails if it isn't used consistently. The guidelines need to specify not just which colors exist, but which colors are used in which contexts, which combinations are approved, and which are prohibited.

A palette document without usage rules is half a palette document. The rules are what make the palette into a system.
