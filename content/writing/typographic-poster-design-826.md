## The All-Type Poster

Some of the most powerful posters ever made use no imagery at all. Wolfgang Weingart's experimental typography, Armin Hofmann's stripped-back Swiss work, the long tradition of purely typographic concert posters and protest placards. Typography alone, doing everything.

The all-type poster is harder than it looks. When you remove imagery, you can't lean on a compelling photograph or illustration to do the visual heavy lifting. The type has to be the image. Every decision about size, weight, spacing, and position has to carry both content and visual energy.

## Starting Without Images

When I take on a typographic poster project, my process starts differently than image-based poster work. I'm not looking for a photograph or thinking about image-type relationships. I'm treating the text itself as raw material — letters and words as visual objects with weight, texture, and spatial behavior.

I'll spend time in exploratory sketching with the actual words of the piece, trying them at radically different scales, orientations, and weights. I'm asking: what is the most visually interesting and communicatively honest thing I can do with these specific letterforms?

## Scale Contrast as the Primary Tool

In the absence of imagery, scale contrast between typographic elements becomes the defining visual strategy. A single word or letter set massively large against small supporting text creates immediate hierarchy and visual drama.

This approach works because large type at high contrast is inherently compelling to look at. Human visual processing is attracted to high-contrast edges and large forms. A single letterform at 90% of the poster height is an image.

## Rotation and Placement

Rotating type breaks the expectation of horizontal reading and immediately creates visual interest. But rotation should serve a purpose — it should change how the text reads or feels, not just look dynamic for its own sake.

Diagonal text creates tension. Vertical text creates formality in some contexts and disruption in others. Flipped or mirrored text creates conceptual layering. Each of these has a communicative effect, and I use them when that effect is right for the content.

## Color in Typographic Posters

A two-color typographic poster — usually black plus one color — can achieve significant visual complexity through how those two values are distributed across the typographic elements.

I find that in typographic poster work, color restraint produces stronger results than rich palettes. When everything is two values, the composition has to do the work, and that usually produces cleaner, more memorable results than work that relies on chromatic complexity.

## The Gut Test for Type-Only Work

When the poster is purely typographic, there's nowhere to hide. A mediocre image can be covered by strong type. A great image can rescue weak type. But in all-type work, every decision is exposed.

My gut test: if I squint at the poster until the text becomes unreadable, does the composition still hold? Does it still have visual structure and energy as an abstract arrangement of masses? If yes, the design is sound. If it collapses without legibility, I have more work to do.
