## The Process Shapes the Aesthetic

Screen printing is a physical process where ink is pushed through a mesh stencil onto a substrate. Each color requires a separate screen, a separate pass, and additional cost. The process rewards simplicity and punishes complexity.

These constraints aren't obstacles. They're the source of the aesthetic. Screen-printed posters look the way they do because of the process — the slight texture of ink on paper, the possibility of intentional misregistration, the richness of dense opaque ink, the way layers interact.

Understanding the process makes you design better for it, not just design to a visual style.

## Color Planning for Screen Printing

With screen printing, every color is a significant decision. A four-color screen print is twice as expensive as a two-color one. This forces hierarchy thinking that's healthy for design: if you can only have three colors, which three are most important to the design?

The answer to that question usually produces a cleaner, more decisive composition than unlimited color would have.

I plan screen printed color choices in terms of the layers themselves — thinking about which color goes down first (the base), which goes on top, and how the layers will interact. When you print a translucent color over an opaque one, you get a third color where they overlap. This overlap color is free — it comes from the process rather than requiring a separate screen.

Designing to exploit these overlaps means three screens can produce five or six distinct colors in the final print.

## Halftone and Texture

Screen printing can simulate tonal variation through halftone patterns — dots of varying density that create the impression of shades between solid color and white.

For design purposes, this means a single screen can appear to produce multiple values of the same color through halftone variation. A 100% density area is full color; a 50% halftone area reads as a lighter tone of the same color.

I use halftones when a composition needs tonal depth that additional screens would be too expensive to provide. The resulting texture is characteristically screen-printed — you can see the dot pattern — and I lean into that texture as a design feature rather than trying to conceal it.

## Registration and Its Deliberate Imperfection

Screen printing registration — the alignment of successive color layers — is imprecise compared to offset printing. A small misalignment between layers is inherent to the process.

Most screen printed work is designed to minimize the visual impact of this misregistration: keeping different-color elements from sitting immediately adjacent, designing compositions that are forgiving of small alignment shifts.

Some of the most characterful screen printed work deliberately accepts and even emphasizes misregistration — treating it as texture and energy rather than a production flaw. When the misregistration is clearly intentional — controlled in its range and integrated into the design — it becomes an aesthetic quality.

## Ink on Paper

The relationship between ink and substrate in screen printing is different from offset. Screen printing ink sits on top of the paper rather than absorbing into it. This creates:

- **Opaque color** on any substrate, regardless of the paper's own color. White ink on black paper prints opaque white.
- **Tactile ink surfaces** that you can feel on the paper's surface
- **Rich color density** that offset printing on uncoated stock can't match

Designing specifically for screen printing means embracing these qualities. Opaque color on dark or colored stock. Dense solid areas. Bold, high-contrast compositions that the ink's richness amplifies.
