## Structure in Four Dimensions

Print design operates in two dimensions: x and y. Motion design operates in four: x, y, z (depth), and time. The grid — that fundamental organizing principle of print layout — applies to three of those four dimensions. The fourth requires a different kind of structural thinking: timing systems.

Understanding how to bring structural discipline to motion work produces more coherent, more professional results.

## The Motion Grid

A motion piece's spatial layout should follow the same grid logic as its print equivalent. Where text elements sit, how graphic elements relate to each other, the proportional system that governs the overall composition — these are layout decisions governed by a grid.

The difference from print is that the grid elements move, and their positions change over time. But at any given moment, the layout should be reading from the same grid.

## Safe Zones and Action Areas

Broadcast and social video have established safe zones — the areas of the frame where content is guaranteed to be visible and unaffected by overscan or platform cropping. These zones define the effective design space.

I design motion graphics within established safe zones by default, with decorative elements allowed to extend to the frame edge and beyond.

## Temporal Structure

The fourth dimension — time — requires its own structural system. Long-form motion pieces have a timing architecture: introductory beats, main content periods, transition moments, conclusion. Within those sections, individual elements have their own timing relationships.

A timing grid — a set of standard durations and rhythmic units that govern how long things take and when they happen — creates the temporal equivalent of a spatial grid. Elements that "land" on timing grid units feel metrically organized even when viewers can't articulate why.

## Typography in Motion Grids

Type elements in motion design should snap to grid positions as they enter, hold, and exit. Floating text that doesn't anchor to a consistent grid system looks unresolved. Type that finds its grid position and settles there confidently communicates structure.

This doesn't mean rigid uniformity. Off-grid moments can be used expressively, just as in print design. But the grid has to be established before it can be broken meaningfully.

## Transitions as Structural Elements

Transitions between sections — wipes, fades, graphic transitions — are structural elements in a motion piece. How a piece moves from one section to another communicates something about the relationship between those sections.

I design transitions as part of the overall structure, not as production decoration. The transition style should be consistent with the piece's visual language and should reflect the content relationship being bridged.
