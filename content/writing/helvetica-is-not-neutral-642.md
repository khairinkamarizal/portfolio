Helvetica is not neutral. It never was. The idea that Helvetica is a blank-slate typeface — a vessel that carries the designer's intent without contributing its own personality — is a myth that's persisted because it serves designers who want to avoid making typographic decisions.

## Where the Neutrality Myth Comes From

Helvetica was designed in 1957 as part of a broader project to create typefaces without historical associations — clean, modern, rational forms that communicated the present rather than carrying associations from previous eras. This was a reaction to the ornate, historically loaded typefaces that were common in European commercial typography at the time.

In that context, Helvetica was relatively neutral — it didn't carry the specific associations of Garamond (Renaissance scholarship) or Fraktur (Germanic tradition) or Bodoni (Enlightenment elegance). Compared to those, it was more formally restrained.

But "neutral relative to ornate historical typefaces" is not the same as "neutral in absolute terms." Helvetica now carries 65+ years of its own associations. Corporate communications. Government signage. International corporations. The New York subway. The ubiquity that made it seem like a non-choice has given it a very specific personality: the established institution, the corporation, the system.

## What Helvetica Actually Communicates

When I see Helvetica in a design today, I don't see a blank slate. I see: establishment, institutional authority, slightly dated corporate, the 1970s and 1980s, large organizations with large budgets, the generic rather than the distinctive.

That's not necessarily wrong. For some applications, those are exactly the right associations. But it's not neutral — it's a specific set of associations, just ones that have been internalized so thoroughly that they're not consciously registered.

## The Neutrality Myth as Excuse

Where the neutrality myth causes real damage is when designers use it to avoid making a real typographic decision. "I'll just use Helvetica — it's neutral" is a rationalization for not thinking about what the typeface is communicating.

Every typeface communicates something. The question is whether what it communicates serves the work. Helvetica communicates establishment and institutional authority. Is that what this project needs? If yes, use it with intention. If no, use something else.

## There Is No Neutral Typeface

The search for a neutral typeface is a misguided project. The moment a typeface exists and is used, it accumulates associations. Helvetica is the extreme case — used so widely for so long that its associations are deeply embedded — but the same principle applies to every face.

The designer's job is not to find the neutral vessel and fill it with meaning. It's to understand what the typeface is already communicating and decide whether that communication serves the project. That's a more demanding task than reaching for "neutral," but it's the actual task.

## What This Means in Practice

Before using any typeface — including Helvetica, including whatever is currently fashionable, including the one that "everyone's using" — ask: what does this typeface say before anyone reads the words? If the answer serves the project, proceed with intention. If not, keep looking.
