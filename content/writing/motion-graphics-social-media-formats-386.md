Social media platforms have completely fragmented the format landscape for motion design. Square, portrait, landscape, story, reel — each format has different dimensions, different viewing contexts, and different audience behaviors. Designing motion content without specifying the format first is designing for nowhere.

## The Platform Reality

Each platform has dominant format conventions: Instagram Stories and Reels are 9:16 portrait. Twitter/X is primarily landscape 16:9. LinkedIn is 1:1 square or landscape. TikTok is 9:16. YouTube is 16:9 with optional shorts in 9:16.

These aren't just dimension constraints — they're behavioral contexts. A 9:16 Story is viewed full-screen, thumb hovering, in a sequential feed. A 16:9 LinkedIn video plays inline in a desktop feed alongside text content. The design responses to these contexts are completely different.

## Native vs. Adapted

There are two approaches to multi-platform motion content: design natively for each format, or design one format and adapt it to others.

Adapting a 16:9 landscape video to 9:16 portrait almost always produces compromised results — content gets cropped, compositions fall apart, text ends up in dead zones. The efficiency gain is real but the quality cost is also real.

I default to native design for primary brand content, and adapted design for secondary or extended content. The primary brand presence on each platform gets a format-specific design. Adapted versions are acceptable for content where format-perfect execution matters less than coverage.

## Designing for Safe Zones

Every platform has UI overlays that obscure parts of the frame: progress bars, profile icons, caption text, action buttons. The safe zones — the areas guaranteed to be visible — vary by platform and change with platform updates.

I maintain a safe zone template for each primary platform. Every piece of motion content gets checked against the safe zone template before delivery. Content that violates safe zones is redesigned, not submitted and hoped for.

## The Three-Second Hook

Social media motion lives or dies in the first three seconds. The feed moves. If the first frame doesn't demand attention, the viewer has already scrolled past. I design the opening frame first — the still image that will show before any motion begins — and make sure it communicates enough to earn the watch.

The motion then has to deliver on the promise of that opening frame. A strong opening followed by weak content trains audiences to ignore you. A strong opening followed by a strong payoff builds the habit of watching.

## Audio-Off Design

A majority of social video is watched without audio. I design every motion piece to communicate completely without sound. Captions for spoken content, visual hierarchy for information delivery, motion that expresses the emotional tone without depending on a soundtrack.

When audio is present, it should enhance. When it's absent, the piece should still work.
