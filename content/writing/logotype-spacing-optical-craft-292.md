## Spacing is the invisible craft

In wordmark design, spacing is where the invisible craft lives. Two designers working from the same typeface and the same brief will produce different results based almost entirely on how they handle the spacing between letters. The one with better spacing intuition — better developed through study and practice — will produce work that feels more professional, more resolved, more permanent.

Developing this intuition takes deliberate practice. It's not mystical; it's learned. But it can't be learned from reading about it. It has to be developed through making decisions and studying results, repeatedly, over time.

## The problem with metric spacing at wordmark scale

Type designers build spacing metrics into their fonts — tables that specify the sidebearings of each character and the kerning adjustments for common pairs. These metrics are optimized for text sizes, where the eye is forgiving and reading speed is the primary requirement.

At wordmark scale — where the letters are large enough to be studied individually — the metric spacing almost always needs adjustment. Letter pairs that feel correctly spaced at 12pt feel either too tight or too open at 100pt. The eye becomes more demanding as the scale increases.

## The area-equalization principle

The governing principle for optical spacing is that the visual areas of white space between consecutive letters should feel equal — not measure equal, feel equal. This distinction is critical. Measured equal spacing between a round letter and a straight letter produces spacing that looks unequal because round forms create more visual openness than straight ones.

To equalize areas optically, I work pair-by-pair through the wordmark, adjusting each pair until the visual white area between them matches the visual white area of the adjacent pairs. This is done by eye, not by measurement.

## Tracking as a personality parameter

The overall tracking of a wordmark — the global letter-spacing adjustment — is a personality decision as much as a legibility decision. Tight tracking creates density and intimacy — the letters press together, the word becomes a single compact shape. Wide tracking creates airiness and formality — the letters breathe, the word becomes a procession of forms.

Neither is inherently better. The tracking choice should match the brand's personality. A bold, energetic mark benefits from tight tracking. A refined, premium mark benefits from generous tracking. I make the tracking decision based on the brief before I start the pair-by-pair optical adjustment.

## Testing and the settled feeling

After completing the spacing work, I leave the wordmark alone for at least a few hours before reviewing it with fresh eyes. Coming back to spacing work with distance reveals problems that intensive close work conceals — the pair that's still slightly tight, the overall tracking that's a bit looser than it should be.

The marker of finished spacing: the wordmark has a settled feeling. The eye moves through it without catching or jumping. It reads as a unified word, not as a collection of spaced letters. When I reach that feeling, the spacing is done.
