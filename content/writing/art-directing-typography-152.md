## The Limits of Font Selection

Most conversations about typography in design focus on font selection: which typefaces to use, how to pair them, what their personalities are. That's legitimate and important. But it's a small fraction of what typographic art direction actually involves.

The decisions that make typography exceptional — or merely adequate — are mostly about how type is used, not what type is selected.

## Setting Type With Intention

Every typographic decision is an art direction decision: the size and weight of each element, the tracking and leading, the measure (line length), the relationship between type and the space around it, the hierarchy established between different text elements.

These decisions are made dozens of times in every designed piece. Making them well requires not just knowledge of the rules but a cultivated sense of when to follow them and when to override them.

## The Measure Problem

Measure — the width of a column of text — is one of the most commonly mishandled aspects of typography in designed work. Too wide and the eye struggles to find the next line. Too narrow and reading rhythm is constantly interrupted.

The traditional guideline is 45-75 characters per line for comfortable reading. I use this as a starting range and adjust based on typeface, size, and context. What matters is the reading experience, not adherence to a rule.

## Leading and Atmosphere

The space between lines of type (leading) dramatically affects the atmosphere and readability of text. Tight leading feels compressed and intense. Loose leading feels airy and considered. The right amount depends on the typeface's x-height, the measure, and the intended reading experience.

I adjust leading by eye rather than by formula. The visual result is the standard.

## The Hierarchy Dance

Strong typographic hierarchy makes reading effortless. The viewer's eye always knows where to go next. Creating this hierarchy requires contrast — in size, weight, color, spacing, or a combination — between different levels of content.

The mistake I see most often is insufficient contrast. A headline 20% larger than body copy doesn't create hierarchy; it creates noise. The scale jumps need to be decisive.

## Restraint as Craft

The hardest lesson in typographic art direction is restraint. Fewer typefaces, less variation in size and weight, more space, simpler hierarchy — these constraints almost always produce more sophisticated results than trying to communicate everything through typographic complexity.

The sign of mature typographic skill is knowing what to leave out.
