## The Temptation of the New

Every year there's a new typeface that shows up on every poster, every book cover, every brand refresh. I've fallen for that trap. The work looked current for about eight months, then dated for the next five years.

What I do now is work backwards from the subject. A poster for a jazz festival in a 1940s venue has different typographic needs than one for a warehouse techno night. Neither is better — they just call for fundamentally different type voices.

## Matching Emotional Register

When I talk about emotional register in type, I mean the set of associations a typeface carries: its historical period, its cultural context, its optical texture on the page. A condensed grotesque feels urban and terse. A humanist serif feels considered and warm. A geometric sans feels rational and clean.

Before I open a type library I write down three adjectives that describe the poster's subject. Then I search for typefaces that embody those adjectives rather than typefaces I already like.

## Weight and Contrast on Large Formats

Posters get read at distance. This changes everything about type selection compared to screen or editorial work. High-contrast display faces that look elegant at 72pt on screen can turn into a flickering mess at 200pt on a printed A1. The thin strokes become hairlines that disappear.

For large-format print I strongly prefer faces with moderate contrast — nothing too spidery. When I do use a high-contrast display face I always print a rough proof at actual size before committing. Monitor previews lie.

## The One-Two Typeface Rule

Most of my poster work uses exactly two typefaces: one for the headline/title, one for supporting information. Sometimes I use just one, varying weight and scale. I almost never use three.

Three typefaces on a poster is usually a symptom of indecision — trying to solve a hierarchy problem with variety instead of with scale, weight, and spacing. When I feel tempted to introduce a third face, I ask myself what the real problem is. Nine times out of ten the answer is that I haven't pushed the contrast between large and small text far enough.

## Testing Before You Commit

My test for any typeface on a poster: set the most important line of text at the intended output size, print it, tape it to the wall, and look at it from the distance a viewer would actually stand. If it communicates instantly and clearly, it's earning its place. If it requires a step closer to read comfortably, the typeface or the size is wrong.

Type on posters exists to be read fast, sometimes from across a room. That constraint is a gift. It forces real decisions.
