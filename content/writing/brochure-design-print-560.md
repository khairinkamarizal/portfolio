## The Ignored Brochure Problem

Most brochures get picked up and put down in under ten seconds. The reader flips through, finds nothing that catches them, and sets it aside. The information inside might be excellent. The design failed to deliver it.

Designing a brochure that gets read requires thinking about the reader's experience at every stage: before they open it, as they move through it, and what they take away at the end.

## The Cover Is a Promise

The cover has one job: make the reader want to open it. Not explain what the brochure is about. Not list all the benefits. Make the reader curious enough to turn the page.

I treat brochure covers like posters — single-minded, visually arresting, hierarchy resolved in seconds. A strong image or typographic statement. A compelling fragment of information that requires the interior to complete.

The mistake is putting too much on the cover because the client wants "everything upfront." Everything upfront means nothing lands. One strong thing on the cover is more effective than five modest things.

## Thinking in Folds

A brochure is not a flat document — it's a physical object with a sequence of reveals governed by folds. Understanding the fold structure shapes how information should be organized.

In a trifold, the reader sees the cover, then opens to a partial interior panel, then opens fully to see all three interior panels simultaneously. Each of those stages is an opportunity to build expectation, deliver surprise, or escalate the narrative.

I map the information architecture against the fold sequence before I start designing. What does the reader see first? What does opening the first fold reveal? What is the full spread reveal? Each stage should feel satisfying and complete while pointing forward.

## Reading Flow Inside

Once open, the reader scans before they read. The hierarchy of the interior determines what they notice in their scan: headlines, large images, pull quotes, strong graphic elements. These are the hooks that pull them into the body text.

Body text in brochures should be shorter than clients want it to be. Long blocks of text don't get read. Short blocks with clear entry points — a bold lead sentence, a subheading, a strong image — do.

I fight hard for content reduction in every brochure project. The client has twelve things to say. I argue for six. The reader will receive six well-chosen things better than twelve cramped ones.

## Paper and Format as Experience

The physical experience of a brochure — its weight, its texture, how it opens, how it closes — is part of the design. A thin, flimsy brochure communicates cheap regardless of what's printed on it. A heavy, well-printed brochure communicates quality before the first word is read.

I always specify paper and print production as part of the brochure design, not as a separate downstream decision. The design should be created for the specific stock, not adapted to whatever paper is cheapest.

Format is also a design decision. The standard trifold is fine. But a gatefold has a different reveal quality. A saddle-stitched booklet communicates depth and investment. A single oversized sheet folded into a complex structure creates a memorable physical experience.

Match the format to what the brochure needs to communicate. Standard formats are not neutral — they carry associations too.
