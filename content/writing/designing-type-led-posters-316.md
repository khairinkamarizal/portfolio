Some of my favorite poster work involves no imagery at all — just type. Not because imagery isn't available or because the budget is limited, but because the typographic composition is itself the visual statement. Getting this right requires treating type as a form, not just as a carrier of information.

## When Type-Led Is the Right Approach

Type-led posters work best when the text itself is conceptually interesting or when the design benefits from a directness that imagery would dilute. Poetry events, literary organizations, certain music acts — these often call for a poster where the relationship between the words and the design is tighter than any image could make it.

Type-led work is also a discipline: it strips away the image as a safety net and forces compositional thinking based purely on letterforms and their spatial relationships.

## Scale Contrast as the Compositional Engine

Without imagery, scale contrast between typographic elements is the primary tool for creating visual interest. A single word set enormous against a body of text set small creates drama through the size relationship alone.

I think about these scale relationships the way a sculptor thinks about volume — what is the mass of each element, and how do those masses balance in the space? A single enormous character balanced against multiple smaller lines of text creates a conversation between elements rather than a hierarchy.

## Type as Texture

At small sizes, running text becomes texture rather than readable content. A block of body copy on a poster functions like a halftone screen — a tonal area. I use this deliberately, setting text that's too small to read at viewing distance as a textural background for display-sized elements.

This technique creates visual complexity from typographic material without introducing external imagery. The poster remains entirely typographic while having the visual richness of a layered composition.

## Breaking the Reading Direction

Western typography defaults to left-to-right, top-to-bottom reading. Breaking that direction — setting text at 90 degrees, 270 degrees, or at diagonal angles — creates visual energy and challenges the viewer's path through the composition.

I use vertical text sparingly. One rotated element creates dynamism. Multiple rotated elements create chaos. The break from reading convention is most powerful when it's the exception in an otherwise conventionally oriented composition.

## Color in Type-Led Work

With no imagery to provide color interest, the type itself carries the full color composition. I'm more deliberate about color in type-led work than in image-based posters. The color choices for each typographic element have to create the color harmony of the whole piece.

A technique I use often: a single accent color applied to one element against a black-and-white or monochromatic composition. The accent color draws the eye and creates an implicit hierarchy that pure black and white can't achieve as decisively.
