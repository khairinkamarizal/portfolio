## Typefaces Have Personalities

A brand chooses a typeface the way a person chooses a speaking voice. That's not an exaggeration — when people encounter your brand in writing, the typeface is doing the same work that tone of voice does in conversation. It signals sophistication or accessibility, tradition or modernity, warmth or authority.

This is worth taking seriously. A mismatched typeface can undermine everything else the brand is trying to communicate.

## The Category Reads

Within any product or service category, there are dominant typeface conventions. Finance tends toward conservative serifs or clean, precise sans-serifs. Tech startups cluster around geometric sans-serifs. Luxury brands often use high-contrast serifs or bespoke type. Craft food brands have their own distinct vocabulary.

I pay attention to these conventions because breaking them intentionally is a strategy, but breaking them accidentally is a problem. If you want your brand to stand out from the category, you need to know what the category looks like first.

## Serif vs. Sans: Beyond the Cliché

The serif-is-traditional, sans-serif-is-modern framing is oversimplified but contains a kernel of truth. More useful than that binary is thinking about specific typeface characteristics: humanist letterforms suggest people and process; geometric forms suggest systems and precision; slab serifs suggest solidity and authority; script types suggest personality and craft.

The richest type choices are often ones that hold productive tension — a humanist sans-serif that feels approachable but rigorous, for example, or a contemporary serif that carries historical weight without stuffiness.

## Pairing Logic

Most brands use at least two typefaces: one for headlines and one for body text. The pairing needs to create hierarchy without clashing. The most reliable approach is to choose typefaces with different formal structures — a more expressive display type paired with a more neutral text type — that share a compatible aesthetic sensibility.

Contrast creates hierarchy. Harmony creates coherence. The pairing has to do both.

## Custom Type as Brand Asset

For brands at a certain scale, custom typefaces are a genuine competitive advantage. A bespoke typeface that's designed specifically for the brand can't be appropriated by competitors. It becomes proprietary infrastructure.

Not every brand can justify the investment. For those that can, it's one of the strongest identity decisions available.

## Consistency Is the Point

The reason typeface selection matters so much in branding is that it's applied repeatedly across every piece of brand communication — from business cards to campaign posters to the company blog. That repetition is what builds recognition.

A typeface choice that seems arbitrary in isolation becomes meaningful through consistent use over time. Choose carefully, then commit.
