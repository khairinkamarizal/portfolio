Freelance brand design requires two distinct skill sets: design craft and business practice. Design schools teach the first. Almost nobody teaches the second. The result is that designers who are excellent at their craft frequently struggle with pricing, client relationships, contracts, and scope management.

After running an independent practice for several years, here is what I wish I had understood earlier.

## Pricing: The Value Conversation

The most common freelance pricing mistake is pricing by time. Hourly rates create a race to the bottom, reward inefficiency, and obscure the actual value being delivered. A logo designed by an experienced designer in three days produces more value than the same logo designed by an inexperienced designer in thirty days, and it should be priced accordingly.

I price brand identity projects by scope and value, not by hours. The conversation with the client isn't "how long will this take" but "what is a strong brand identity worth to your business." When a rebrand helps a company win more business, improve its hiring, and retain customers more effectively, the value can be significant even for a small company.

The practical floor: I estimate my time, multiply by my effective hourly rate, and use that as the floor below which I won't go. But the actual project price is often higher, based on the project's strategic value and complexity.

## Scope and Contracts

Scope creep is the primary cause of unprofitable design projects. Projects that seemed adequately priced at the proposal stage become unprofitable as rounds of revisions multiply and deliverables expand beyond what was agreed.

The solution is precise scope definition at the contract stage. Not "logo and brand identity" but a specific list of deliverables: primary logo (horizontal and stacked), color palette (5 colors with Pantone, CMYK, RGB, and hex specifications), typography system (2 typefaces, 4 weights, scale and hierarchy), one set of business card designs, one social media template set. Three rounds of revisions included. Additional revisions at [rate] per round.

Every item that's not on the list is out of scope and billable if it's requested. This isn't adversarial. It's clear. Clients generally prefer knowing what's included over discovering unexpectedly that something isn't.

## Client Management: Expectations Set Upfront

Most client relationship problems are expectation problems. A client who expects weekly updates and gets monthly ones is unhappy, not because the work is late, but because the expectation wasn't managed.

I now set expectations explicitly at the start of every project: communication cadence, review process, decision-making authority, revision scope, what happens if the project stalls on the client side. None of this is unusual to request. Most clients appreciate the clarity.

The most important expectation to set: the design process is a creative process with inherent uncertainty. I can commit to quality and diligence. I can't commit to specific creative outcomes before the work has begun.

## Saying No

Knowing which projects to decline is a business skill. Projects with poor budget-to-scope ratios, clients who are difficult to work with, briefs that are conceptually wrong, work that doesn't align with the practice I want to build. Declining them isn't lost business. It's kept capacity.

The freelance practice that takes every project it can get isn't a sustainable business. It's a job with bad hours. Selectivity about clients and projects is what makes the work better and the business healthier.

## The Portfolio as Business Development

In brand design, the portfolio is the primary business development tool. The work you publish determines the work you attract. If the portfolio is full of work you don't want to do more of, you'll get more of it. If it's full of work you'd love to do more of, you'll attract similar briefs.

This means publishing work strategically. The project that paid well but isn't representative of the practice you're building doesn't need to be in the portfolio. The project that stretched your skills and points toward where you want to go absolutely does.

Portfolio curation is business strategy.
