Technology sector brand identity has its own typographic conventions, visual history, and strategic logic. Working in this space without understanding those conventions produces either unwitting conformity to them or uninformed violations of them — neither of which is a deliberate creative choice.

Here's how I think about logotype work for technology brands specifically.

## The Geometric Sans Default

The technology sector has a strong gravitational pull toward geometric sans-serif typefaces for wordmarks. Futura, circular geometric sans variants, and the many geometric sans fonts developed in their lineage dominate the category. This prevalence has both a logic and a downside.

The logic: geometric sans-serifs communicate modernity, precision, and rationality — all values technology companies want to project. They also render cleanly on screens, work at small sizes, and have the neutrality to support a wide range of brand colors and contexts.

The downside: the prevalence of this choice means that a geometric sans wordmark in the technology sector provides essentially no visual differentiation. Every company looks like every other company because they've all made the same typographic choice for the same reasons.

When I work on technology brand identity, I start by asking whether the geometric sans is actually the right choice or whether it's the default. Sometimes it's genuinely right. Often, there are better options that the competitive context has left available.

## Humanist Alternatives

Humanist sans-serifs — typefaces with letterforms derived from calligraphic tradition, with more variation between strokes and more organic curve quality — offer an alternative to geometric sans that still reads as contemporary and professional but with more warmth and distinctiveness.

For technology companies that want to signal human-centered values, accessibility, or approachability — increasingly common positioning in the sector — a humanist sans can communicate those values directly through the typographic choice.

## When Serifs Make Sense

Serif wordmarks in technology are rare enough to be genuinely distinctive. For technology companies in the enterprise, financial, legal, or research sectors — where authority and institutional credibility are as important as innovation — a well-chosen serif wordmark can communicate values that the geometric sans mono-culture can't.

This isn't nostalgia. It's differentiation through a choice the category convention has made available by default.

## Custom Letterform Modifications

Whatever typeface direction I recommend for a technology wordmark, I always include custom modifications that make the mark proprietary. The modifications I favor for technology contexts: adjusted spacing optimized for screen rendering at common sizes, custom letterforms for one or two distinctive characters, weight modifications that improve performance at small sizes, and any character-specific refinements that add distinctiveness without reducing legibility.

The combination of a well-chosen typeface and careful customization produces a wordmark that feels both familiar (the typeface's category associations) and specific (the customizations that make it unique).

## The Small Size Performance Test

Technology wordmarks appear at extremely small sizes — favicons, app icons, small-format digital advertising, mobile interfaces. I test every technology wordmark at 16px, 24px, and 32px width as part of the standard development process.

Many letterform refinements that look elegant at display scale become liabilities at small sizes. The wordmark development needs to account for the full size range from the start, not retroactively solve problems discovered at small size after the main design work is complete.

Get the small size right. Technology users encounter the brand at small size constantly.
