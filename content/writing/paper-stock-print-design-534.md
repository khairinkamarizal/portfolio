## Paper Is Not a Neutral Surface

When I hand a business card to someone and they feel it before they read it, the weight, the texture, and the finish are communicating before the eyes process anything. That tactile first impression is part of the design whether you planned it or not.

Most print design education focuses on what goes on the paper. I spend a significant amount of time thinking about the paper itself — its weight, its texture, its coating, its color — as an active design element.

## Coated vs. Uncoated: The Fundamental Choice

The most significant paper decision in almost any print project is whether to use coated or uncoated stock.

**Coated paper** has a smooth, sealed surface that makes colors reproduce with high saturation and fine detail. Photography looks crisp. Solid colors look dense. It's the right choice when the design relies on photographic imagery, very fine detail, or bold saturated color that needs maximum fidelity.

The feel of coated paper is slick and precise. It communicates modernity, efficiency, and polish. It can also feel cold and generic if that's not the right personality signal.

**Uncoated paper** has an open, absorbent surface that softens edges and slightly desaturates colors. This is not a weakness — it's a character. Printing on uncoated stock produces ink that feels like it belongs to the paper rather than sitting on top of it. The result feels warmer, more tactile, more artisanal.

For brands where warmth, craft, and humanity are part of the personality — many food brands, cultural institutions, independent businesses, creative services — uncoated paper often communicates the brand better than coated paper does, even if the print quality is technically inferior.

## Weight and What It Signals

Paper weight is a proxy for quality in the reader's mind. Heavier paper feels more substantial, more considered, more expensive. A business card on heavy stock (400gsm and up) communicates investment and care. The same design on thin stock feels disposable.

I specify paper weight based on the context and the brand's positioning. Premium brands get heavy stock. Everyday communications can use lighter weights. The weight should be consistent with the overall brand positioning — inconsistency is jarring.

## Special Finishes

Beyond stock selection, print finishing techniques create tactile and visual effects that screens can't replicate.

**Embossing and debossing** — pressing forms into the paper to create raised or recessed areas. Pure texture with no ink. Excellent for logos on premium materials.

**Foil stamping** — metallic or colored foil bonded to specific areas. Creates reflective surfaces that change with viewing angle.

**Soft-touch laminate** — a matte coating that feels velvety and warm. Creates a distinctive tactile experience that feels premium and considered.

**Spot UV** — a glossy coating applied to specific areas over a matte background. Creates contrast between matte and gloss surfaces on the same piece.

I use these techniques when they serve the brand, not just when they're technically possible. Over-finishing a simple piece feels try-hard. A well-chosen single finish on the right stock can be more powerful than multiple techniques layered together.

## Designing for the Stock

Some designs don't translate well across different stocks. A very fine logotype that depends on precise hairlines may need uncoated stock to be specified with slightly heavier strokes to compensate for ink spread.

I always get paper samples before finalizing a print design, and I print test sheets when the budget allows. What looks correct on screen looks different on paper — always. The relationship between the design and the substrate has to be evaluated together.

Paper is the last physical thing your audience touches. Make it deliberate.
