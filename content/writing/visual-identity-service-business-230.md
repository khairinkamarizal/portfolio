## Service brands have no object to photograph

Product brands have an enormous creative resource that service brands don't: the product itself. A shoe brand can photograph its shoes. A food brand can photograph its food. The product is a visual anchor that carries enormous communication weight.

Service brands have no such anchor. A consulting firm, a law practice, an accounting service, a design studio — these are businesses where the product is intangible expertise, and the visual identity has to do more of the work of communicating what the business is and why it matters.

## The trust problem

Service businesses are fundamentally trust propositions. Before a client engages a service provider, they need to trust that the provider will deliver. The visual identity is often the first trust signal — it communicates competence, stability, and professionalism before any human interaction occurs.

This shapes my approach to service business identities. I'm more conservative with formal risk-taking. I'm more attentive to the signals that communicate competence and stability — refined typography, restrained color, materials quality in physical touchpoints. A service business identity that looks playful and experimental might attract some clients while actively repelling the larger group who read experimentation as instability.

## What the identity needs to communicate

For a service business, I ask the identity to communicate three things: the category (what kind of business is this?), the positioning (where in the quality/price spectrum?), and the personality (what kind of people will I be working with?).

The category communication needs to work quickly — if someone sees the business card and can't tell whether it's a law firm or a restaurant, the identity has failed at its most basic function. This sounds obvious but it requires real discipline. Distinctive design that sacrifices category legibility is a self-defeating exercise in service businesses.

## Differentiation within category norms

Service businesses need to be different enough to be memorable while respecting enough category conventions to be trustworthy. This is a more constrained creative problem than product branding, and requires more strategic precision.

For a mid-market law firm, "different enough" might mean a custom wordmark with distinctive letterforms, a color palette that moves slightly beyond the expected navy and gray, and photography direction that shows real people in real situations rather than stock-photo handshakes. The framework is conventional; the execution is distinctive.

## The stationery system as identity showcase

For professional service businesses, the physical stationery system — business cards, letterhead, envelopes — is often the highest-impact brand touchpoint. These are the objects that clients handle, that get pinned to bulletin boards, that live in wallets.

I invest disproportionate care in the stationery system for service businesses because the physical quality of these objects communicates something that no digital touchpoint can: the business is substantial enough to invest in quality materials. That signal is worth more than any tagline.
