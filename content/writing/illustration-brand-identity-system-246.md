## Illustration is not decoration

When I recommend illustration as part of a brand identity system, I'm not recommending decoration. Illustration in brand identity is a strategic choice — one that says this brand has a distinct worldview, a specific personality, a visual voice that couldn't be assembled from stock.

A custom illustration style is one of the hardest brand assets to copy. A competitor can use the same typeface (after identifying it) and approximate the same color palette (after measuring it). They can't use a proprietary illustration style without it being immediately obvious.

## When illustration is the right choice

Illustration makes strategic sense when the brand has a strong personality that photography or abstract design can't fully express. Consumer brands with warmth and character — food and beverage, children's products, creative services — benefit most from illustration.

It also makes sense when the brand needs to represent content that doesn't photograph well: abstract concepts, process diagrams, impossible or imaginary scenarios, historical references, diverse human representations that feel genuinely inclusive rather than stock-photo diverse.

## Commissioning vs. developing in-house

Most brand illustration systems are commissioned from illustrators rather than developed by the brand designer. This is appropriate — illustration is a specialized craft that takes years to develop, and a talented illustrator brings a fully developed style that would take years to evolve internally.

When I commission illustration for a brand, I brief the illustrator on the brand's personality, the audience, the emotional register (warm vs. cool, serious vs. playful, realistic vs. abstract), and the range of subjects the illustration needs to cover. I also specify what the illustration style should not be — this negative space often communicates as much as the positive description.

## Building a scalable illustration system

A single illustrator doing a handful of brand assets creates a cohesive system. But brands grow, illustrators move on, and the illustration system needs to remain consistent when new assets are commissioned from new artists.

I document illustration systems with detailed style guides: the line weight range, the color palette the illustrations draw from, the level of detail, the way shadows and highlights are handled, specific design decisions (do faces have eyes? do characters have fingers? are backgrounds detailed or suggestive?). With a good style guide, a new illustrator can produce on-brand work without having worked with the brand before.

## The maintenance commitment

Custom illustration requires ongoing investment. Unlike a logo that can go unchanged for a decade, an illustration system needs to grow — new scenes, new characters, new situations — as the brand's communication needs grow.

Brands that commission a beautiful illustration system and then can't afford to add to it end up with a limited set of assets that get reused until they feel stale. I always discuss the long-term maintenance commitment with clients before recommending a custom illustration approach.
