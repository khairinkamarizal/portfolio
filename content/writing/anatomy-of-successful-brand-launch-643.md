Most brand identity projects end at file delivery. The client has the logo files, the brand guidelines, the color specifications, the typeface licenses. In theory, they have everything they need to launch. In practice, they're standing at the beginning of a second project: deploying the brand across every touchpoint, retiring the old materials, and introducing the new identity to their audiences.

This second project — the brand launch — is often underplanned and underfunded, and when it goes badly, it undermines the identity work that preceded it.

## The Rollout Plan

A brand launch needs a rollout plan. What changes on day one? What changes in the first month? What's a longer-term migration?

Day one items are the highest-visibility touchpoints: the website, social profiles, email signatures, the primary digital presence. These need to go live simultaneously with the announcement, because they're the first places anyone will look after hearing about the rebrand.

First month items are the collateral that gets produced and consumed regularly: business cards, email templates, letterhead, presentation templates. These should be updated immediately but don't need to go live at the exact moment of announcement.

Long-term migration covers the items that are expensive to replace and don't need immediate attention: signage on owned buildings, existing printed inventory, vehicle graphics. These migrate as they need replacement.

## The Announcement Strategy

How you communicate a rebrand matters as much as the rebrand itself. For consumer-facing brands, an unexplained change can confuse or alienate loyal customers. For B2B brands, existing clients may be concerned about continuity.

The communication should explain: what changed (and a compelling reason why), what stayed the same, what customers can expect. It should be delivered through the channels where those audiences are most attentive.

## The Internal Launch Is the External Launch

I've seen rebrands fail because the internal team wasn't aligned before the external launch. Employees using old materials, sales team presenting old decks, customer service working from old templates — these undermine the brand credibility the launch was meant to build.

The internal launch should precede the external one. The team needs to understand the new brand, have access to the new assets, and have the old materials replaced before the public announcement goes out.

## Governance: Who Has the Files?

One of the most practical post-launch questions is: who controls the brand assets and how does the rest of the organization access them? Brands that don't have a clear answer to this end up with assets scattered across email threads and personal desktops, used inconsistently and often in outdated versions.

I recommend establishing a brand asset library as part of the launch project — a single source of truth for brand files, accessible to everyone who needs them, controlled by someone responsible for keeping it current.

## Measuring the Launch

Six months after a brand launch, the relevant question is: is the brand being applied consistently across touchpoints? This is a more achievable short-term goal than "is the brand building recognition" (which takes longer) and a more directly actionable metric.

Consistency is the thing the brand launch was designed to establish. Checking it early allows problems to be corrected before they become entrenched habits.
