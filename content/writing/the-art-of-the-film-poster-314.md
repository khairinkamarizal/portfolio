Modern Hollywood poster design is largely a disaster. The formula is known: floating heads, gradient backgrounds, title in Impact-derived condensed type, billing block at the bottom. The resulting posters are indistinguishable from each other and communicate nothing about the specific film.

The great film posters — Saul Bass's Vertigo, the Alien teaser, the original Star Wars — did something different. They communicated the emotional experience of the film before anyone had seen it. They were arguments about what the film was, not lists of its components.

## The Concept Question

Every film poster design should start from a concept: what is this film about at its core, and what visual metaphor expresses that? Not "here are the main characters and the title." What is the film trying to do to its audience emotionally, and what single image carries that?

Saul Bass was a master of this. Vertigo's spiraling eye isn't about the film's plot — it's about the psychological experience of watching it. The concept preceded every formal decision.

## Typography as Mood

The title treatment of a film poster is the loudest typographic statement in the design. The typeface, the scale, the color, the position — all of these communicate genre and emotional register before the viewer reads the title.

Horror films traditionally use deteriorated, rough, or heavy type that signals threat. Romantic comedies use light, open, sometimes script-influenced lettering that signals warmth and lightness. Thrillers use clean, geometric type that signals precision and tension.

These aren't rules — they're starting points that can be subverted to interesting effect. A horror film set in a clean, clinical typeface says something specific about the nature of its horror.

## Compositional Approaches

The best film posters usually make a single strong compositional decision rather than distributing interest across the frame. The face occupying the full frame. A single symbolic object in stark isolation. An environmental establishing shot that creates mystery and context.

The distributed-interest approach — multiple characters at various scales, all competing for attention — is the default studio approach because it reassures executives that all the elements are present. It almost never produces a good poster.

## The Billing Block

Billing block design is a technical discipline. The contractual requirements are strict: specific actors' names must appear in specific sizes relative to the title, specific credits must be present in specific formats. These requirements don't leave much design latitude, but the choices within the constraints — type size, weight, line spacing, position — all contribute to the overall design.

I always design the billing block from the requirements first, then integrate it into the composition, rather than designing the poster and squeezing the billing block in at the end. The billing block is part of the design, not an afterthought.
