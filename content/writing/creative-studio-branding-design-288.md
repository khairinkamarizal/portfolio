## The cobbler's children problem

Branding for a design studio is famously difficult — so difficult that there's a cliché for it: the cobbler's children have no shoes. Design studios are usually terrible at their own branding. They understand the problem deeply, have opinions about everything, lack the external perspective that comes from being a client, and tend to over-intellectualize decisions that should be made quickly.

I've been the cobbler more than once. I've also helped other studios with their branding. The difficulty is real, and understanding why helps navigate it.

## The audience is both client and peer

A design studio's brand has to work for two distinct audiences simultaneously: potential clients, who need to trust the studio's capabilities and fit their quality expectations; and the design community, who will evaluate the work with professional rigor.

These audiences have different and sometimes contradictory needs. Clients need clarity, professionalism, and trustworthiness — qualities that can sometimes push toward conservative, accessible design. The design community values conceptual rigor, formal sophistication, and originality — qualities that can push toward complexity that clients find off-putting.

The best studio brands navigate this tension by being conceptually sophisticated in their foundations and approachable in their expression.

## The portfolio vs. the brand problem

A design studio's most powerful brand communication is its work, not its identity. The portfolio — the quality and range of client work — does more to build the studio's reputation than any logo or website design.

This creates a risk: the studio's identity becomes secondary, designed as a placeholder that won't embarrass the work rather than as a genuine brand statement. I've seen many studio brands that are technically competent and completely unmemorable precisely because they were designed to not compete with the portfolio.

The challenge is designing a studio identity that has genuine character — that communicates something specific about the studio's approach and personality — while being capable of receding gracefully when the work is the focus.

## Process as brand differentiator

For most creative studios, the most meaningful brand differentiator is not their visual style — it's their process. How they discover what the client actually needs. How they generate and evaluate ideas. How they present and refine. How they deliver and support.

A studio brand that communicates process distinctiveness — "we start from strategy, not aesthetics" or "we work in intensive two-week sprints rather than elongated timelines" — says something genuinely differentiating that visual style alone can't.

## Self-branding as practice

I treat my own studio's brand as a living practice rather than a fixed identity. It evolves as my work and thinking evolve. It's a commitment to articulate what I actually believe about design and how I actually work — not the ideal version, the real one.

That honesty, when it reads through the design, is more distinctive and more trustworthy than any amount of formal sophistication.
