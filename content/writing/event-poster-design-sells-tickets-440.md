I've designed event posters for music venues, festivals, arts organizations, and cultural institutions. The posters live on telephone poles, in bar bathrooms, on community boards, in social media feeds. Their job isn't to be beautiful. Their job is to sell tickets.

Those two goals can align. A beautiful poster can also be an effective poster. But when they conflict, effectiveness wins every time. A poster that wins design awards but fails to communicate the event details is a failed poster.

## Information First, Then Aesthetics

Every event poster has a set of required information. At minimum: what, who, when, where, how to get tickets. The first question I ask before I design anything: can someone extract all five of those elements in under ten seconds?

If no, the poster isn't done, regardless of how good it looks.

I often do this test with non-designers. I show them the poster for five seconds, take it away, and ask them to tell me the five things. What they remember is the hierarchy the poster actually has. What they can't remember is what the design failed to communicate.

## The Competition Context

An event poster competes for attention against every other piece of visual noise in its environment. On a telephone pole, it's next to twenty other posters. In an Instagram feed, it's next to an infinite stream of content. On a community board, it's overlapping with flyers from months ago.

Designing for context means asking: what does this poster need to do differently from everything around it to stop someone in their path? Usually the answer involves one high-contrast, high-impact element that works at the scale the poster will be seen. For a telephone pole, that's a passing pedestrian at arm's length. For Instagram, it's a screen-sized image glimpsed for 0.3 seconds.

## The Date Is Critical

Event poster failure mode I see constantly: the date is beautiful and small. The date is the most time-sensitive piece of information on the poster. If someone can't find it in two seconds, they have to work to know if they're even available.

I give the date visual prominence disproportionate to its typographic size. Sometimes it's large. Sometimes it's in a contrasting color. Sometimes it's isolated from other information so it reads as a distinct unit. Whatever it takes for the date to be the third or fourth thing the eye finds after the event name and the headliner.

## Genre Signals

Every event poster communicates genre before the viewer reads a word. The typeface, the color palette, the image treatment, the density of the layout. A classical concert poster should look like a classical concert poster. A techno night at an underground club should look nothing like a classical concert poster.

These genre signals help the right audience self-select. The person who goes to underground techno nights should recognize this poster as for them within the first visual impression. If the poster tries to appeal broadly across genres, it usually appeals to nobody.

I lean into genre signals deliberately. I don't use them as a limitation but as a communication tool. The visual language of the genre is part of the message.

## What Happens to the Poster After Design

Something I think about that most designers don't: the lifecycle of the poster. For a social media post, it might be seen once by a large audience and then disappear. For a physical poster, it might hang for three weeks before the event. For a festival, the poster might be collected and live in someone's apartment for years.

Each lifecycle creates different requirements. A long-hanging poster needs to work in faded, weathered versions of itself. A digital-only poster needs to work at compressed JPEG quality on a bright screen. A collectible poster can invest in production quality that wouldn't be justified for a one-week flyer.

Design for the full lifecycle of the object, not just the ideal presentation.
