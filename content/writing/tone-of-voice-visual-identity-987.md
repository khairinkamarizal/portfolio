## Visual and Verbal Are the Same Problem

A brand identity that only covers visual elements is half an identity. The words a brand uses — its vocabulary, its sentence structure, its level of formality, its humor or lack thereof — communicate as powerfully as its colors and typography. When the visual identity says one thing and the verbal identity says another, the brand feels incoherent.

I raise this with every brand identity client. Some are ready to address verbal identity alongside visual identity. Others want to handle it separately. The outcome is always better when they're developed together.

## What Tone of Voice Actually Covers

Tone of voice isn't just word choice. It covers: vocabulary level (simple vs. sophisticated), sentence length and structure (short and punchy vs. complex and layered), formality level (first-name, casual vs. institutional, formal), how the brand talks about its own capabilities (confident and direct vs. humble and self-deprecating), how it addresses its audience (peer-to-peer vs. expert-to-student), and what it's willing to say that category norms wouldn't allow.

Each of these dimensions has a range, and a brand's position on each dimension should reflect its character and its audience's expectations.

## The Character Map Exercise

I develop tone of voice by creating a character map for the brand. If this brand were a person, describe them. Not their demographics — their personality. How do they walk into a room? How do they tell a story? What do they laugh at? What do they never joke about?

This exercise makes the abstract specific. "Confident but not arrogant" becomes more actionable when it's embodied in a character description. "Warm but professional" becomes easier to write in practice when you have a clear picture of the specific person who exhibits exactly that combination.

## The Vocabulary List

From the character description, I develop two vocabulary lists. The first is the brand's natural vocabulary — words and phrases that feel like this brand, that appear in the character description, that fit the brand's category and audience. The second is the excluded vocabulary — words and phrases that feel wrong for this brand, that belong to a competitor or to a category default the brand is trying to avoid.

These lists become reference tools in the tone of voice guidelines. Writers checking uncertain copy can test it against both lists.

## Connecting Voice to Visual

The verbal identity should feel like a natural extension of the visual identity's character. A restrained, minimal visual identity needs a writing style that's similarly precise and economical. An expressive, bold visual identity needs a voice with more energy and personality.

I evaluate the alignment explicitly. If the visual identity is characterized by large scale, bold contrast, and minimal elements, the verbal identity should have short sentences, decisive statements, and minimal qualifying language. The two should feel like they come from the same person.

## Making It Practical

Tone of voice guidelines that describe character without demonstrating it are difficult to apply. I include worked examples for every major brand communication type: email subject lines, body copy, social posts, headlines, call-to-action text, error messages.

For each example, I show the correct tone, an example that's too formal, and an example that's too casual. The range examples are often more useful than the correct example, because they establish the edges of acceptable territory.

A writer who has read the guidelines and internalized the character, vocabulary, and range examples can produce on-brand copy for situations the guidelines didn't anticipate. That's the goal.
