I didn't take grids seriously early in my career. I used them loosely — the default margins InDesign gave me, a rough sense of column structure — but I treated them as suggestions rather than infrastructure. My layouts took longer, felt inconsistent, and rarely had the decisiveness that I admired in work I wanted to emulate.

The shift came when I studied Swiss typographic design seriously. What struck me wasn't the aesthetic — the precision and restraint — but the underlying logic. A well-designed grid doesn't constrain a layout. It accelerates it.

## What a Grid Actually Does

A grid is a decision made once that answers the same question thousands of times. Where does this element sit? How wide should this column be? How much space separates these sections? Without a grid, you answer each of these questions individually, inconsistently, and slowly.

With a strong grid, those questions have predetermined answers. The cognitive load shifts from placement decisions to content decisions. That's where the real design work is anyway.

## My Default Structure

For most print projects I work with a modular grid — columns and rows — rather than a simple column grid. The modular grid gives me more flexibility in how I combine units for different content types.

My standard setup for A4 and letter-format documents:

- 12 columns, 4mm gutters
- Baseline grid at 12pt (or matching my body text leading)
- Generous outer margins — I usually give the outside margin more space than the inside, especially for single-page documents

For poster work, I adapt this depending on format. A landscape A1 might use a 16-column grid. A square format often benefits from a simpler 6×6 modular grid.

## The Baseline Grid Is Non-Negotiable

If I'm setting body text in a print piece, I lock it to a baseline grid. This is the practice that most clearly separates print-literate designers from those still thinking in screen terms. When text on adjacent columns shares a baseline, the page feels resolved. When it doesn't, there's a visual restlessness that the reader feels even if they can't name it.

I set my baseline grid to match my body text leading. If body text is 10/14pt, my baseline grid is 14pt. Headlines and subheads I size in multiples of the baseline unit whenever possible, so they either align or land on a consistent half-step.

## Breaking the Grid on Purpose

A grid is only useful if it creates meaningful contrast when you break it. An element that extends across multiple columns, bleeds to the edge, or sits at an unexpected angle reads as intentional only when the surrounding layout is disciplined.

I break my grid regularly — for images, pull quotes, or headline treatments that need to feel dramatic. But I'm aware of when I'm breaking it and why. The break is a decision, not a default.

## Setting Up the Grid Before Content

My process: I determine the format and constraints, then design the grid before I touch any content. This means thinking about column ratios, margins, and baseline increments as a first creative act, not an afterthought.

When I skip this step, I always pay for it later. Mid-project grid retrofitting is painful and usually incomplete.

Spend thirty minutes on your grid. Save hours on your layout. The math is straightforward.
