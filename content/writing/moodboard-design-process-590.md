## The Problem With Most Moodboards

I've seen thousands of moodboards. Most of them are collections of beautiful things. Gorgeous photography, compelling typography, rich color combinations. They're visually impressive. They're also nearly useless as design direction because they tell you what looks good, not what's right for this brand.

A moodboard that's built from "things that look great" produces identity work that looks great in a vacuum and communicates nothing specific about the brand. The client likes the moodboard because it's beautiful. The designer uses it as permission to make beautiful work. And the result is a well-executed identity that could belong to any sophisticated brand.

## The Evidence Standard

I use a strict filter for moodboard images: every image must be there because it's evidence of a specific brand truth, not because it looks good.

Before adding anything to a moodboard, I ask: what specific aspect of this brand's strategy does this image illustrate? If I can't answer precisely — if the answer is "it has the right mood" or "it looks sophisticated" — the image doesn't go in.

This is harder than it sounds. The instinct is to collect images that evoke a general emotional territory. The discipline is to collect images that illustrate specific strategic claims.

## Building the Moodboard in Sections

I organize moodboards by the strategic attributes they're meant to illustrate. If the brand strategy has three core personality attributes, the moodboard has three sections — one for each attribute.

Each section contains four to eight images that specifically demonstrate that attribute. Not the brand as a whole — that one attribute.

This structure does two things: it forces me to find evidence for each specific claim, and it makes the moodboard reviewable. When I present it, the client can evaluate whether each section is communicating the intended attribute, not just whether they like the overall collection.

## What Goes In

The range of material I include:

**Photography** — not stock photography of "people doing business" but photography that captures a specific quality: texture, light, composition, atmosphere.

**Typeface samples** — not the full font, but the brand name and a sample phrase set in candidate typefaces. This makes the type evaluation concrete.

**Color swatches** — broad color territories, not specific brand colors yet. What color world does this brand inhabit?

**Competitor and non-competitor reference** — both what the brand wants to emulate and what it wants to avoid.

**Material and texture references** — for brands with significant physical presence, the materials associated with the brand's world.

## What Stays Out

Things I consciously exclude from moodboards:

- Other brand logos (to prevent mistaken direction: "make it look like that brand")
- Extremely trendy design work (to avoid styling the brand to a current moment)
- Work that's aspirational for the designer personally but not strategically relevant

## The Presentation

I present the moodboard before the client sees any logo concepts, and I present each section with its strategic rationale. Section one: what it is, why these images, what they're meant to communicate. Section two, same. Section three, same.

I ask for approval of the moodboard before proceeding to design. If the client disagrees with any section, we resolve it at the moodboard stage rather than after three rounds of logo concepts.

The moodboard is a strategic checkpoint. Treat it like one.
