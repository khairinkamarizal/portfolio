A color palette is not a list of swatches. It's a system with defined roles, relationships, and usage logic. The difference between a functional palette and a pretty collection of colors is whether the system makes clear decisions for the people who use it.

## The Structural Layers

I build brand palettes in structural layers, each serving a distinct purpose:

**Primary colors:** The one or two colors that define the brand. These should be usable at 60–70% of brand surface area and should be immediately, distinctively associated with the brand. A strong primary palette often has just one true primary color.

**Secondary colors:** Supporting colors that complement the primary. Used for variety, hierarchy, and contrast in multi-element layouts. Typically two to four colors that work harmoniously with the primary.

**Neutral colors:** Off-whites, grays, and dark tones for backgrounds, body text, and structural elements. Neutrals carry a color temperature (warm or cool) that should be consistent with the overall palette direction.

**Functional colors:** Colors assigned to specific system functions — success (green), warning (amber), error (red), information (blue). These are often used in digital contexts and are derived from the brand palette rather than generic defaults.

## How I Derive the Palette

The primary color usually comes from the brand strategy work — the character, the competitive landscape, and the communication goals define a color direction. Within that direction, I evaluate specific hues against each other, considering saturation, value, and their behavior in print and digital contexts.

Secondary colors are derived from the primary using color relationships that create harmony: analogous (adjacent on the color wheel), split-complementary (flanking the complement), or triadic (evenly spaced). I don't derive them mechanically — I use these relationships as starting points, then adjust by eye and by testing.

Neutrals are chosen to complement the overall palette temperature. A warm-dominant primary palette gets warm neutrals (creamy off-whites, warm grays). A cool palette gets cool neutrals.

## Specifying for All Contexts

Every color in the palette needs to be specified in four formats:

- **Pantone** (for print and physical production)
- **CMYK** (for 4-color process printing)
- **RGB** (for digital and screen contexts)
- **Hex** (for web and digital product)

The Pantone specification is the authoritative reference. All other formats are approximations of the Pantone color, and they may not be exact matches — particularly for vivid or saturated colors that push the CMYK gamut.

## Testing the Palette

Before finalizing a palette, I test it in context:

- Logo on primary color background
- Primary on neutral background
- Text in all specified colors on all specified background colors, evaluated for contrast
- Full-color comps in the brand's typical application contexts

The palette that looks beautiful as a swatch collection may have contrast problems, competing visual weights, or unexpected interactions that only appear in context.

## Documenting Usage

Knowing the colors isn't enough — understanding when to use which color is what makes the palette functional. I document usage guidance: which is the dominant color for most surfaces, which is used for emphasis, which is used only for functional indicators.

Without usage guidance, designers make inconsistent decisions, and the palette fragments into individual swatches rather than functioning as a system.
