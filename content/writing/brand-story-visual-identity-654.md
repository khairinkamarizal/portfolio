Every brand has a story. The job of a visual identity is to tell that story visually — not illustrate it literally, but communicate its essential character through color, form, type, and the relationships between them. When an identity is working, a viewer who encounters the brand for the first time has an intuitive sense of what kind of company this is, what values it holds, who it's for.

That immediate communication is the story being told.

## The Story Is in the Details

The most powerful brand stories are told through the accumulation of specific, deliberate details rather than through a single dominant gesture. The weight of the typeface, the saturation of the brand color, the amount of white space in the layout, the type of photography, the tone of illustration — each of these communicates something. When they all communicate the same story, the result is a brand that feels coherent and specific.

When they each communicate something different — because they were chosen separately, without a unifying narrative — the result is a brand that feels assembled rather than conceived.

## Finding the True Story

Before I can design an identity that tells a story, I need to know what story is true. This is more demanding than it sounds. Most brands have an aspirational story — the story they want to tell — and an actual story — the story that's true about who they are and what they do.

The most effective identities are built on the intersection: aspiration that's grounded in something real. Pure aspiration produces identity that feels empty, like a claim without evidence. The unique truth about a brand, when it's communicated visually, produces work that no competitor can replicate because it belongs to this specific brand and no other.

## Specificity Is the Differentiator

Generic brands tell generic stories: professional, innovative, trustworthy. Every brand claims these qualities. They differentiate nothing.

The brands I find most interesting to work on are those with a specific story — a founder with a genuine point of view, a product with a distinctive origin, a community with a particular character. The more specific the story, the more specific and differentiating the identity can be.

Specificity requires a willingness to exclude. A brand that tells a very specific story will resonate strongly with the audience it's for and be less legible to audiences it's not for. This is correct. A brand that tries to appeal to everyone typically appeals strongly to no one.

## The Identity as Narrative System

Once I've found the true, specific story of a brand, I build the identity as a narrative system — a set of visual decisions that collectively and consistently tell that story. The color palette, the typographic personality, the graphic language, the photographic approach — each is a chapter in the same story.

When a client adds an element that tells a different story — a trendy typeface that doesn't match the brand's personality, a photography direction borrowed from a competitor, a color that was chosen because it tested well in a survey — the narrative coherence breaks down. The identity stops feeling like a coherent story and starts feeling like a collection of parts.

My job is to build the narrative and then protect it. Both parts matter equally.
