Annual reports, company publications, exhibition catalogues, books — long-form print design requires a different typographic discipline than poster or brand identity work. The challenges are different: not impact and immediate communication, but sustained readability, navigational clarity, and typographic consistency across dozens or hundreds of pages.

## Setting Up the Type System

Before I set a single word of long-form print, I establish the complete type system: every level of the hierarchy, every context, every style. I define:

- **Body text:** Face, size, leading, tracking, paragraph spacing, hyphenation settings, justification parameters
- **Headings:** H1 through H4 or however many levels the content requires, with complete specifications
- **Captions:** Size, leading, tracking, color
- **Pull quotes/callouts:** Treatment that distinguishes them from body text
- **Running heads/feet:** Size, tracking, color, positioning
- **Index/reference matter:** If applicable — usually needs special attention to density and legibility

This system gets built in the document's paragraph styles before the layout begins. Every style gets tested against real content before I'm satisfied with it.

## Body Text Is the Foundation

The body text decision is the most important typographic decision in long-form print, and it gets less attention than headline typography because it's less visually exciting. This is backwards.

The body text is what the reader spends most of their time with. A face that's slightly wrong for the reading context — wrong x-height, wrong stroke contrast, wrong spacing — accumulates reader fatigue over a 200-page document in ways that don't show in a paragraph test.

My body text requirements: high x-height for legibility in the typical size range (9-11pt), good ink trap behavior for the specific print process, generous and open counters, and a stroke contrast that works on the specified stock. I test at actual print size, on the actual stock, before finalizing.

## Column Width and Legibility

Line length is one of the most important body text settings, and one of the most commonly compromised by layout decisions. The classic guideline — 45-75 characters per line for optimal readability — exists for good reason. Very long lines cause the eye to lose its place returning to the next line. Very short lines interrupt the reading flow too frequently.

When a grid system creates column widths that push toward the edges of this range, I adjust either the grid or the type size to get the column within a comfortable line length.

## Consistency as a System Property

In long-form print, a typographic inconsistency that would be minor in a one-page piece becomes glaring when it appears on 30 pages. One incorrectly applied heading style, recurring across a chapter, reads as careless.

I use InDesign's paragraph and character styles rigorously — nothing is set by direct formatting if it can be handled by a style. I also use the Find/Change function to audit style application before finalizing. Every body paragraph gets the body style. Every H2 gets the H2 style. No exceptions.

## The White Space Economy

Long-form print is a white space economy. Every element that takes up space — chapter openers, section dividers, pull quotes, images — either earns its space by adding to the reader's experience or steals it from content that could have been there instead.

I'm conservative about decorative white space and aggressive about functional white space — leading that aids legibility, margins that allow comfortable handling, paragraph spacing that groups related content clearly.
