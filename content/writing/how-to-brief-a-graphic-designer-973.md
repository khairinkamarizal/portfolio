## The Brief Is the Foundation

The quality of the brief determines the quality of the outcome more than any other single factor. I've worked on projects with exceptional briefs that produced exceptional work with minimal friction. I've worked on projects with poor briefs that produced mediocre work despite everyone's best efforts, because the design was solving the wrong problem.

When I evaluate a potential project, the brief quality tells me as much about the likely success of the engagement as anything else about the client.

## What a Good Brief Contains

A good brief answers six questions clearly.

What is the organization, product, or service? Not the marketing description — the actual reality. What does it do, who does it serve, how does it make money or fulfill its mission?

Who is the audience? Not "everyone" — that's not an audience. The specific type of person who uses this product, attends this event, or donates to this cause. What do they value? What are they already responding to visually?

What is the goal of this design project? What should be different after this work exists? More ticket sales, better brand recognition, stronger staff pride, improved donor conversion? Specific, measurable goals make design decisions evaluable.

What are the constraints? Budget, timeline, technical requirements, mandatory elements (required logos, regulatory copy), format requirements. Constraints are not obstacles — they're parameters that help me design the right thing rather than a beautiful thing that doesn't work.

What is the competitive context? Who else is operating in this space? What visual territory do they occupy? What do they do well and poorly? The design should make this brand distinctive within its context.

What does success feel like? This is the hardest question to answer but the most useful. If you could show this design to your ideal customer and they responded exactly as you hoped, what would their response be? What words would they use to describe it?

## What a Bad Brief Does

A bad brief describes the deliverable without the purpose. "We need a new logo." That's a deliverable request, not a brief. Why? What problem does the current logo create? What should the new logo do that the old one doesn't?

A bad brief describes the aesthetic rather than the character. "We want it to look modern and professional." Every client wants this. It tells the designer nothing. What does modern mean in the context of your specific brand and audience? What would professional look like that's different from your five main competitors?

A bad brief includes design direction as the brief. "We want it to look like [reference brand]." This is a design solution, not a brief. The brief should describe the problem; the designer should develop the solution.

## The Interview I Always Conduct

Before every project, I conduct a briefing interview with the key decision-maker. Even when a written brief has been provided, the conversation surfaces information the written document doesn't contain. The tone the client uses to describe their audience. The competitive example they mention with unmistakable admiration or contempt. The moment when they say "well, what we really need is..."

I record these interviews (with permission) and review them during the design phase. The brief isn't just the document. It's everything I learned about the brand during the intake process.

## Brief Alignment Is the First Deliverable

I synthesize the briefing into a written brief recap — my interpretation of the project goals, audience, constraints, and success criteria — and present it to the client for alignment before any design begins.

This recap is the first deliverable. If we're not aligned on the brief, no amount of excellent design execution will produce a successful outcome.
