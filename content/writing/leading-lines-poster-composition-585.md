## The Eye Follows Lines

The human visual system is wired to follow lines. It's a survival trait: lines in the environment indicate edges, boundaries, paths, and directions. When there's a line in a visual field, the eye follows it.

Designers can exploit this directly. By placing directional elements in a composition — lines, edges, gestural marks, sequences of points — I can lead the viewer's eye to wherever I want it to go.

## What Counts as a Leading Line

A leading line doesn't have to be an actual line. It can be:

- The edge of a geometric shape that points in a direction
- A sequence of elements arranged along an implied path
- The direction of gaze in a portrait — the viewer's eye follows where the subject is looking
- A perspective convergence creating a vanishing point
- A diagonal created by a text block's alignment
- An architectural element like a road, a corridor, a horizon

Any element that has directionality can function as a leading line.

## Designing With Intent

The question I ask about every directional element in a composition: where is this line pointing? And is there something important at the destination?

A leading line that points to dead space is a missed opportunity. A leading line that points to the most important element in the composition is doing real compositional work.

I map the leading lines in a composition explicitly during development — drawing arrows on a rough sketch to show where each directional element points. If the arrows converge on the key element, the composition is working. If they diverge or point to nothing, I find the problem.

## Diagonal as Energy

Horizontal and vertical elements create stable, static compositions. Diagonals create energy and movement. A composition dominated by diagonals feels dynamic; one dominated by horizontals and verticals feels ordered and calm.

For poster work where energy and urgency are appropriate — event posters, concert posters, activist work — diagonal compositions can amplify that energy directly.

For poster work where authority and stability are appropriate — institutional work, formal events, premium brands — horizontal and vertical dominance with restrained diagonals creates the right feeling.

Neither is better. Both are choices.

## The Gaze Direction Technique

Portrait photography in poster design creates a specific leading line: the subject's gaze direction. Viewers automatically follow where a depicted person is looking.

I position portrait subjects to look toward the most important text element in the composition. A face turned slightly toward the headline pulls the viewer's eye there. A face turned away from the headline creates tension — the viewer is pulled away from the information.

This technique is so reliable that I consciously check gaze direction in any composition that uses a portrait. If the gaze is pointing away from key content, I either reposition the text or find a different image.

## Circular Leading Lines

Not all leading lines are straight. A curved line leads the eye along its arc. A circle or oval creates a continuous loop that keeps the eye within the composition — useful for compositions where dwell time matters.

Creating a subtle oval path through key compositional elements — with major elements placed along the curve — keeps the viewer cycling through the content rather than moving through it once and exiting. For exhibition posters and event materials where extended attention is valuable, this technique is worth considering.

Lead the eye. Know where you're taking it.
