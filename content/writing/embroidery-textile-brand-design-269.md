Embroidery and textile applications are often the last thing considered in brand identity development and the first thing that reveals a mark's weaknesses. A logo that works perfectly in print and on screen can be completely impossible to embroider without significant simplification — and if no embroidery-optimized version exists, someone will improvise one, usually badly.

I include textile applications in my brand system planning from the start.

## How Embroidery Works (and Why It Matters for Design)

Embroidery produces designs using thread stitched into fabric. Unlike printing, which deposits ink in any shape, embroidery builds shapes from stitches — and stitches have minimum sizes and can only approximate curves, not achieve the precision of vector printing.

Very fine details — thin lines, small text, intricate curves — can't be embroidered accurately. Thin lines turn into thread masses. Small text becomes unreadable blobs. Intricate curves become jagged approximations.

A logo with hairline details, very small supporting text, or complex interlocking geometric forms will require substantial simplification for embroidery. The simplified version needs to be designed deliberately, not improvised by the embroidery shop.

## Minimum Stitch Sizes

Standard commercial embroidery has practical minimums: text below about 4mm cap height is usually unreadable; fine lines below about 1mm stroke width are difficult to produce consistently; very small negative spaces (enclosed counters in letters, small gaps between elements) tend to fill in.

I test logo marks against these minimums at the embroidery scale. A logo at 70mm wide (common for left-chest shirt embroidery) needs to be checked character by character and line by line for reproducibility.

## Creating the Embroidery Version

For any brand that will use embroidery, I create a dedicated embroidery-optimized version of the primary mark. This version:

Removes or enlarges any elements below minimum stitch size. A supporting tagline that works at 6pt in print may need to be removed entirely or set at a much larger size for embroidery.

Simplifies fine details. A mark with hairline geometric details needs those details replaced with heavier strokes or removed.

Increases negative space in enclosed forms. Letter counters, small enclosed spaces in the mark — these need to be opened up to prevent fill-in.

This version is a legitimate, approved brand asset with the same standing as other registered versions of the mark. It's not a compromise — it's the correct form for this specific application.

## Color Limitations

Embroidery thread comes in a limited color range, and color matching to Pantone or brand specifications is approximate rather than exact. Most embroidery vendors can match commonly used thread colors to standard Pantone values within a reasonable tolerance, but custom thread colors are expensive and often unavailable.

I check that the brand's primary color is achievable in thread with acceptable accuracy. When it isn't, I work with available thread colors that are closest to the brand palette and document the approved thread color choices in the brand guidelines.

## Screen Printing and Heat Transfer Alternatives

For very complex marks or very fine details, screen printing or heat transfer on fabric may be better alternatives to embroidery. Screen printing achieves much finer detail than embroidery and can match brand colors precisely. Heat transfer can reproduce virtually any design.

I recommend the appropriate production method for each application rather than defaulting to embroidery for all textile applications. The brand asset that a method can reproduce accurately is always better than an approximation.
