## Outdoor reading is different reading

Indoor and outdoor reading contexts are completely different design environments. A poster on a gallery wall will be viewed from three feet by a stationary person in controlled light. A poster on a bus shelter will be viewed from ten feet by a person in motion, in variable light conditions from bright sun to rain-diffused gray.

Most poster design is done in a controlled, static environment — on a monitor, in a studio. The gap between design context and viewing context is a design problem that requires deliberate bridging.

## The distance calculation

Before I finalize any outdoor poster, I calculate the minimum type size for legibility at the intended viewing distance. A rough rule for high-contrast type on a clear background: 1 point of type size for every foot of viewing distance. At 20 feet, type smaller than 20pt will be difficult to read for most viewers.

This rule is a floor, not a ceiling. Context matters. A dimly lit bus shelter at night, a poster surface with ambient glare, a moving viewer who has two seconds before the bus doors close — all of these push the minimum size up.

## Contrast as the primary legibility tool

In variable light conditions, contrast — the difference in value between type and background — is the most important legibility tool. High contrast type (black on white, or white on black) maintains legibility across a wide range of light conditions. Medium contrast type (gray on white, or blue on navy) loses legibility rapidly as light conditions change.

For outdoor work, I use maximum contrast as the default and deviate from it only when there's a strong design reason. A slightly gray subhead that reads beautifully in my studio at 72dpi becomes invisible on an overcast day at 20 feet.

## Typeface selection for outdoor scale

Typefaces that work at text sizes for print don't always work at outdoor display sizes. At 6 inches tall on an outdoor poster, the optical corrections built into most text typefaces become visible and sometimes look wrong — the ink traps open up, the subtle weight variations in stroke that create visual stability at small sizes create visual noise at large sizes.

I prefer display-specific or optical-size-optimized typefaces for outdoor work. These faces are designed to be read at large sizes, with more even stroke weights and reduced optical corrections that would be distracting at scale.

## Color and light interaction

Colors behave differently in outdoor light than in artificial or controlled light. Warm colors (reds, oranges, yellows) tend to advance and stay vivid in sunlight. Cool colors (blues, greens, purples) can appear to recede and lose saturation in bright direct sunlight.

For outdoor posters that will be illuminated by sun, I shift colors slightly warmer in my specifications than I would for indoor work. A blue that's perfectly vivid on the studio wall will look faded on a south-facing bus shelter in summer sun.

## The backlit context

Bus shelter posters, transit advertising, and outdoor light boxes are backlit — the light source is behind the print rather than in front of it. This inverts the normal relationship between ink and light, causing colors to behave very differently than in reflective print.

I always proof backlit work on a light box before finalizing it. Colors that look neutral in reflective mode can look garish backlit. Dark backgrounds that feel elegant in reflective print can look heavy and oppressive when backlit.
