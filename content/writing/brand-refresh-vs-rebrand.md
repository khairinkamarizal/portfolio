The difference between a brand refresh and a rebrand isn't cosmetic — it's strategic. Getting this wrong is expensive: a full rebrand when a refresh was sufficient wastes money and risks losing brand equity. A refresh when a rebrand was needed produces an identity that still doesn't solve the underlying problem.

## What a Refresh Actually Is

A brand refresh updates the visual execution of a brand while preserving its core identity. The brand equity, the recognition, the established associations — these are retained. What changes is how those things are expressed.

A refresh might involve: modernizing the logo's proportions and technical execution, updating the color palette to current printing and screen standards, refining the typography to a more appropriate or distinctive typeface, or cleaning up inconsistencies in how the brand has been applied.

What stays the same: the brand's fundamental character, its core color direction, its basic mark structure if it's well-established.

## When a Refresh Is the Right Answer

A brand that is fundamentally healthy but visually dated needs a refresh. The brand equity is real — customers recognize it, have positive associations with it, trust it. Throwing that away with a complete rebrand is wasteful.

Signs that a refresh is sufficient:
- The brand is recognized and associated with the right qualities, but looks dated
- The technical quality of the existing files is poor (fuzzy logos, wrong specifications, no vector sources)
- The brand system is incomplete (good logo, no type system, no color specifications)
- Individual elements need modernization without a change in direction

## When a Rebrand Is Necessary

A full rebrand is justified when the current brand is actively working against the organization's goals — when the existing identity communicates the wrong things, attracts the wrong audience, or carries associations that need to be left behind.

Signs that a rebrand is necessary:
- The brand's existing associations are negative or irrelevant to its current direction
- The organization has fundamentally changed (merger, acquisition, major strategic pivot)
- The brand is in a category where it's failing to differentiate at all
- The name itself needs to change (which necessitates a new identity)

## The Conversation With the Client

I always ask clients to articulate what isn't working about the current brand before recommending a course of action. Sometimes the answer reveals that the problem isn't the identity — it's the way it's been applied, or the strategy behind it, or the product itself.

A new logo doesn't fix a bad product. A refreshed identity doesn't solve a strategic positioning problem. My job is to be honest about what visual design can and can't solve.

## Preserving Equity in a Rebrand

Even in a full rebrand, there's often equity worth preserving — a color that's strongly associated with the brand, a typeface that's distinctive, a structural element of the mark. I identify these equity elements early and make deliberate decisions about which to carry forward and which to leave behind.

Carrying forward strong equity elements isn't a failure of imagination. It's understanding what the brand has built and deciding to build on it rather than abandon it.
