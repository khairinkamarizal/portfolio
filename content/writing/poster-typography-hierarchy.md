A poster has one job: communicate a message at a distance. The moment someone has to work to parse your type, you've already lost them. Typographic hierarchy isn't about making things look nice — it's about controlling the sequence in which information is read.

I've designed hundreds of posters. The ones that work best are the ones where the viewer never consciously registers how they moved through the information. They just did.

## The Three-Level Rule

I operate with three levels of type in most poster work. Primary: the single most important piece of information, usually the event name, product name, or campaign headline. Secondary: supporting context — dates, location, tagline. Tertiary: the fine print — pricing, legal, credits.

Most designers understand this in theory. Where they fail is execution: the size jumps between levels aren't dramatic enough. On a poster, you need to exaggerate the scale contrast. Something that looks extreme in a layout file reads as subtle in print. I typically make primary type 4–6x the size of secondary, not 2x.

## Weight Contrast Versus Size Contrast

You have two main levers for establishing hierarchy: size and weight. Beginners reach for size first, always. But weight contrast is often more elegant, especially when you're working with a tight format.

A heavy black headline next to light-weight supporting text creates clear hierarchy even when the size difference is modest. The eye reads weight first, then size. Use both together and you get a layered read that feels rich without being cluttered.

## White Space Is an Active Element

The hierarchy you build with type only works if the surrounding white space supports it. Every centimeter of space around your headline is doing work — it's telling the eye "this matters, there's nothing competing with it."

I've seen designers shrink everything to fit more text on a poster and wonder why it feels flat. The answer is that white space isn't wasted space. It's the silence that makes the important notes loud.

## One Font Family, Multiple Personalities

My default rule: one typeface family per poster, used at extreme weights and scales. A geometric sans at ultra-light body text and black display headline can carry an entire poster system without ever feeling monotonous.

Bringing in a second typeface is a choice that needs justification. A serif body with a geometric sans headline works when there's a conceptual reason — formal versus modern, classic versus current. When designers add a second face just for visual interest, it usually shows.

## Test at Print Size

Every hierarchy decision needs to be tested at actual print size, not on screen. Print a half-scale version at minimum before finalizing. Things that look balanced at 100% zoom often collapse in physical reality. The headline you thought was dominant might read the same weight as the supporting text once you're holding the sheet in your hands.

Build the hierarchy on screen. Verify it on paper. Finalize it only after you've stood across the room from a print.
