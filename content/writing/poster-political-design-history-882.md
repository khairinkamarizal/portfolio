## The Oldest Broadcast Medium

Before radio, television, and the internet, the poster was mass communication. Political movements, labor unions, governments in wartime, revolutionary organizations — all used the poster as their primary communication tool. The visual and design decisions in that tradition were not aesthetic choices; they were strategic ones, made under conditions of real urgency.

Studying political poster history is studying visual persuasion at its most concentrated and purposeful.

## Simplicity as Strategy

The political poster tradition converged — across very different movements and ideological traditions — on a common formal language: bold color, simple typography, strong contrast, single-image focus. This wasn't aesthetic consensus. It was practical convergence on what works.

Posters were read at distance, by people who hadn't chosen to be in their audience, who had other things to attend to. The message had to land in a glance. Visual complexity that required close reading was a strategic failure. Simplicity was weaponized in service of persuasion.

## The Role of Typography

In political posters, type is often not supplementary to an image — it is the message, set at scale with authority. The Workers' Inquiry, the date of the strike, the name of the candidate. These words were the content, and the design work was about making them impossible to ignore.

The lesson for contemporary poster design: when the words are the message, treat them as the image. Give them the visual weight and scale that the image would get in an image-driven composition.

## Iconography and Recognizability

Political poster iconography developed a visual vocabulary of shared symbols — the raised fist, the dove, the ballot. These symbols worked because they could be recognized instantly and associated with their cause without reading. They communicated across language barriers and literacy levels.

Building recognizable visual symbols — marks that carry meaning through form rather than language — is as relevant to brand design as to political communication. The same cognitive economy that makes a raised fist legible makes a well-designed logo mark memorable.

## The Ethics of Persuasion

Political poster design raises a question worth sitting with: design is always persuasion to some degree. Every communication choice creates an impression, shapes a feeling, advances an argument. The political poster tradition makes this explicit. Contemporary brand work, which often has significant real-world effects, benefits from the same explicit awareness.

This isn't a reason to be paralyzed by design ethics. It's a reason to be clear about what work you're doing and in whose interest.
