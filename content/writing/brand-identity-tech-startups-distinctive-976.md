## The Tech Brand Default

Walk through a startup ecosystem and look at the brand identities. Many of them converge on the same visual language: a geometric sans-serif wordmark, a gradient or flat blue-to-purple palette, abstract geometric mark, clean and sparse layout. This aesthetic emerged as a reaction to the clunky, corporate look of legacy technology companies, and it communicates the right things — modernity, clarity, intelligence.

It also communicates them for every other company using the same visual language. Differentiation has been sacrificed for category legibility.

## Why Differentiation Matters More Than Category Legibility

Category legibility — looking like a tech company — is table stakes. Potential customers already know what category you're in from context: the platform, the pitch, the referral. The identity's job isn't to prove you're a tech company. It's to differentiate you within a category of tech companies.

When a startup's identity looks like every other startup's identity, the brand becomes invisible. Every subsequent touchpoint — the pitch deck, the website, the email signature, the conference booth — blends into the undifferentiated mass of other startups doing the same thing.

## Finding the Actual Differentiator

Meaningful differentiation starts with the actual brand strategy, not the visual system. What is genuinely different about this company? Not "we're faster" or "we have better technology" — those are claims every competitor makes. What is the specific perspective, approach, or value system that makes this company distinct?

That differentiation should drive the visual identity. A company built around the principle that software should feel human and warm needs a very different identity than one built around the principle that software should be invisible and frictionless. Both are tech companies. Both need tech-appropriate communication. Neither needs the same visual identity.

## Typography as the Primary Differentiator

Color palette in tech branding has converged so dramatically that it's difficult to claim meaningful territory. Most of the obvious positions — blue, teal, purple — are occupied by many players.

Typography is where I find more distinctive ground for tech brands. A tech company that uses a refined serif has an immediate visual point of difference from the geometric sans landscape. A company that builds its identity around a distinctive display face rather than a generic san has immediate differentiation at the brand name level.

The risk with differentiated typography in tech is that it can read as off-category — too traditional, too luxurious, too edgy for the audience's expectations. The calibration is about finding the typography that's distinct from the category default while still communicating the right things to the target audience.

## The Color Opportunity at the Edges

The heavily occupied middle of the tech color spectrum — medium blues, purple-blues, teals — leaves the edges underexplored. Deep, saturated colors with more personality (a forest green, a warm burgundy, an unexpected mustard) can create strong category differentiation when the brand strategy justifies them.

I look for the color that serves the brand's specific positioning. A developer tools company targeting individual engineers might use a bolder, more expressive palette than an enterprise security company needs. Both are tech. Both need different colors.

## Avoiding Trend Dependency

The geometric-sans-gradient aesthetic will eventually read as dated, just as the skeuomorphic aesthetic before it became a marker of a specific moment. Brands built entirely on trend-dependent aesthetics face expensive refreshes when the trend passes.

I build tech brand identities around principles with longer durability: distinctive typography, a coherent and well-documented color system, and a graphic language that's derived from the brand's specific character rather than from the ambient visual culture of the startup ecosystem at this particular moment.

The goal is a brand that looks considered in 2030, not one that looks current in 2025.
