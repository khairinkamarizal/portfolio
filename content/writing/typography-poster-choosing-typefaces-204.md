## The typeface is not neutral

Every typeface carries a personality. It has historical associations, cultural baggage, and formal qualities that communicate before a single word is read. When I'm designing a poster, choosing the typeface is not a stylistic decision that comes after the concept — it's part of the concept.

The typeface selection for a poster about a 1920s jazz revival is doing completely different work than one for a contemporary tech conference. Getting this wrong undermines everything else in the design.

## The first question: period or timeless?

I start by asking whether the poster calls for a period-specific reference or a timeless quality. Period-specific work — historical events, revival concerts, themed exhibitions — often benefits from typefaces that carry the visual language of that era. Geometric sans-serifs for the Bauhaus era. Art Nouveau display faces for early 20th century references. Transitional serifs for Enlightenment-adjacent themes.

Timeless work — where the goal is longevity and universal readability — calls for faces that have proven themselves across decades. A well-set Garamond or Helvetica doesn't date itself the way a trendy display font does.

## The second question: display or text?

Posters typically need at least two typefaces working together: a display face for headlines and a text face for supporting information. These can be from the same family (a variable font or an extended type family) or from different designers — but they need to share enough formal qualities to coexist without fighting.

My general rule: pair faces with similar x-heights and similar stroke contrast. A high-contrast didone headline with a low-contrast grotesque text face creates visual dissonance that readers feel even if they can't name it.

## The third question: how will it print?

For posters that will be silkscreened, letterpress printed, or reproduced at large scale on uncoated stock, I consider the production method when choosing typefaces. Very thin serifs and hairlines disappear in silkscreen. Heavy ink trapping in letterpress can fill in tight counters on condensed faces.

I've had a beautiful headline completely fall apart at the printer because I chose a typeface with hairline details that the screen couldn't hold. Now I test typefaces in simulated print conditions before finalizing any poster going to a non-digital production process.

## Building the system

Once I have my primary and secondary faces selected, I build the full typographic system: scale, weight, spacing, and hierarchy. All of these decisions compound. A slightly too-tight tracking on the headline, combined with a too-small point size on the secondary text, and suddenly the poster feels cramped even with generous white space.

Typography in poster design is a system problem, not a single-choice problem. The individual typefaces matter less than how the full system works together at the scale the poster will actually be seen.
