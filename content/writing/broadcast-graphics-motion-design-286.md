Broadcast motion design — the graphics that appear on television channels, news programs, sports broadcasts, and streaming platforms — operates under technical and aesthetic conventions that distinguish it sharply from social media or web motion work. Working in broadcast for the first time without understanding these conventions produces work that looks wrong to broadcast-literate viewers even when nothing is technically broken.

## Technical Specifications Matter Enormously

Broadcast has rigid technical specifications that must be followed precisely. The specifications depend on the broadcaster and the market: US broadcast, European broadcast (PAL vs NTSC), online streaming, and different platform types all have different requirements.

Key specifications I verify before any broadcast project: frame rate (23.976, 25, 29.97, or 59.94fps), color space (Rec. 709 for standard HD, Rec. 2020 for HDR), audio levels if applicable, safe zones (title safe and action safe are still relevant for some contexts), and delivery format (ProRes HQ, XDCAM, specific codec requirements).

Delivering broadcast graphics in the wrong specification can mean an entire package is unusable. I confirm specifications in writing with the technical contact at the broadcaster before producing anything.

## Safe Zones

Television broadcasting historically cut off the outer edges of the image on many display devices (overscan). Safe zones — a title-safe inner rectangle and an action-safe outer rectangle — indicated where content would definitely be visible on all displays.

Modern displays have eliminated overscan, and most streaming contexts don't use it. But some broadcasters still specify safe zones for backward compatibility, and working broadcast designers still use them as compositional guidelines even when they're not technically required.

I check whether the specific broadcaster requires safe zone compliance and design accordingly.

## Color and Broadcast Legality

Broadcast video has legal color limits — saturation and luminance values that exceed the broadcast-legal range can cause problems with transmission equipment and may distort or blow out on some displays.

Colors that look fine on a calibrated monitor may be broadcast-illegal. I use a vectorscope and waveform monitor (either hardware or software equivalents) to check that all colors in broadcast motion graphics are within legal limits. After Effects has a broadcast colors effect that can limit illegal values, but I prefer to correct them at the source rather than apply a blanket limiter.

## The Lower Third Language

Lower thirds — the graphic package that appears at the bottom third of the frame to identify speakers, locations, and other information — are one of the most common broadcast design deliverables. They have their own design language and conventions.

Effective lower thirds are: legible in the two to four seconds they're typically on screen, identifiably part of the broadcast's visual package, not so visually complex that they compete with the on-screen content, and animated in a way that doesn't distract from what the viewer is watching.

I design lower thirds as part of a complete broadcast package rather than as standalone elements — they need to share typography, color, and animation language with other graphics in the package.

## Long-Shelf-Life Design

Broadcast graphics packages are expensive to produce and typically run for years. Unlike social media graphics that might change weekly, a broadcast news package might be used for three to five years.

I design broadcast graphics for longevity: avoiding trend-dependent aesthetics, using typefaces that won't date quickly, and building packages with enough flexibility to accommodate content changes (new presenter names, changing topics) without requiring redesign.

Broadcast is built to last. Design accordingly.
