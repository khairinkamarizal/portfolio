## Signage is design at human scale

Environmental signage — the signs that guide people through buildings, campuses, transit systems, and public spaces — is design that interacts with people at the scale of their bodies and their movement. It's not a document they hold or a screen they look at; it's part of the physical environment they move through.

This spatial quality changes everything about how it should be designed. The viewing distance varies as people move toward it. The viewing angle changes depending on where they're standing. The lighting changes throughout the day. The viewer is often moving, not standing still.

## The hierarchy of information needs

Wayfinding signage serves a navigation need. Before I design any signage system, I map the information hierarchy: what does someone need to know, in what order, to successfully navigate from any point in the space to any other point?

This map — sometimes called a decision point analysis — identifies where decisions happen (corridor intersections, elevator banks, building entrances) and what information is needed at each decision point. Signage that appears at the wrong locations, or provides information in the wrong sequence, fails regardless of its design quality.

## Type selection for environmental use

Signage typeface selection is governed by legibility at a range of distances and under variable light conditions. The typefaces that work best in environmental signage share specific qualities: large x-heights for legibility at distance, moderate stroke contrast (high contrast can fail in certain light conditions), open apertures that don't close up at small sizes or at distance, and generous spacing defaults.

Typefaces designed specifically for signage use — Johnston, Frutiger, Wayfinding Sans — have been optimized for these conditions. They may not be the most visually exciting choices for other contexts, but their legibility performance under real-world signage conditions is excellent.

## Color and contrast in wayfinding

Wayfinding systems often use color coding to indicate zones, floors, or building sectors. The color coding needs to be: distinguishable by people with color vision deficiencies (approximately 8% of the male population); visible under the full range of lighting conditions in the space; and clearly differentiated from the background colors of the space itself.

I specify signage colors with reference to the actual space's color palette, and I test all color combinations against WCAG contrast requirements as a minimum threshold. A wayfinding system that's inaccessible to a portion of users has failed its core function.

## The coordination problem

Signage systems are manufactured and installed by sign fabricators, not by designers. The design intent needs to survive translation to specification documents that fabricators can follow precisely. This requires detailed technical drawings, material specifications, mounting specifications, and tolerances — documentation that goes well beyond what's needed for print or digital design delivery.

I work closely with the sign fabricator during specification development on every environmental signage project. Their knowledge of what can and can't be fabricated to spec is essential design information, and incorporating it early prevents expensive revisions.
