## Why Proofs Exist

A print proof is a physical sample produced before the full press run, used to verify that the printed result matches the design intent. It's the final checkpoint before ink goes on paper in quantity.

Proofs exist because the gap between a digital preview and physical print can be significant — in color, in contrast, in type rendering, in special finishes. Discovering problems in a proof costs time. Discovering them after a press run costs money and time.

## Types of Proofs

Different proof types offer different levels of accuracy and cost. A soft proof is a calibrated screen preview — fast and cheap, but still a screen. A digital proof (laser, inkjet) uses the correct file but a different printing process — better than a screen, but not the same as press output. A press proof uses the actual press, paper, and inks — the most accurate but also the most expensive.

For routine work, a digital proof is sufficient. For work where color accuracy is critical — brand materials where color match is non-negotiable, premium packaging, special finishes — a press proof is worth the cost.

## What to Check in a Proof

I check proofs systematically, not impressionistically. Color accuracy (does the brand color match the spec?), type rendering (does small text hold up?), special finishes (does the spot UV or foil cover the intended areas without bleed or mis-registration?), paper feel (is the stock right?), and image quality (are images sharp at the print resolution?).

I also check for production artifacts that don't appear in digital previews: moiré patterns in halftones, visible rosette patterns in process printing, color shifts at the edges of large flat color areas.

## The Sign-Off Process

After reviewing a proof, I provide formal written sign-off (or a documented list of required changes) before authorizing the press run. A verbal sign-off is not a sign-off.

I retain copies of proof sign-offs as project records. If a press run comes back incorrect despite a signed-off proof, that documentation is essential for resolving disputes.

## Getting Clients to Sign Off on Proofs

Clients need to understand that proof sign-off transfers responsibility for the printed result to them. Before signing off, they should check everything — content, spelling, colors, layouts — because changes after press-run authorization are expensive.

I walk clients through proofs rather than just sending them. Walking through what to look for produces more thorough reviews and fewer post-print surprises.

## Remote Proofing

When physical proofs can't be reviewed in person, photographed proofs can be sent digitally — but these have limitations. A photograph of a proof is not a proof. It's a reference for gross issues. For color-critical work, in-person or courier-delivered physical proofs are the standard.
