Social media motion graphics operate under constraints that broadcast and film motion design don't: tiny screens, often silent viewing, extremely short attention windows, compressed codecs, and autoplay behavior where the first frame has to work as a static image.

Ignoring these constraints produces technically competent motion work that fails on platform. Working within them — really working within them, not just grudgingly acknowledging them — produces motion that's built for how people actually consume content.

## The Silent Viewing Imperative

A significant portion of social media video is watched without sound. Autoplay on most platforms defaults to muted. Viewers in public spaces often don't use audio. If your motion graphics require audio to understand or feel complete, you've limited your audience and your impact.

I design all social motion work to function fully silently. Sound is an enhancement, not a requirement. Text on screen where audio captions would otherwise convey information. Visual cues that communicate what sound effects would communicate.

When I review social motion work, I always watch it muted first. If it works muted, adding sound will only improve it. If it only works with sound, I have a problem.

## The First Three Seconds

Autoplay gives you attention you didn't earn. Viewers didn't choose to watch your video — it started playing while they were scrolling. You have roughly three seconds to give them a reason to stop and continue watching.

The first three seconds need to: establish what this content is about, signal visual quality (so the viewer trusts that the rest is worth their time), and create a question or hook that's resolved later in the piece.

I design first-frame-first: what does the static first frame look like? Does it communicate enough to stop a scroll? Only then do I think about the motion that follows.

## Square and Vertical Formats

Desktop-designed motion for 16:9 widescreen exported to square or vertical format loses most of its composition. Elements designed to fill a wide canvas look small and under-scaled in a square crop.

I design social motion natively in the delivery format: 1:1 for feed, 9:16 for Stories and Reels. This isn't just cropping the widescreen version — it's designing for a different compositional reality.

Vertical formats favor centrally-placed content, larger type, and less horizontal motion. The compositional logic is different from widescreen and needs to be treated that way.

## Compression-Aware Design

Social platforms recompress uploaded video aggressively. Very fine details — thin lines, subtle gradients, small text — often degrade significantly after platform compression. What looks sharp in the original file can look muddy in the Instagram or Twitter-compressed version.

I test against platform compression by uploading test exports and checking them on mobile devices before finalizing production work. I increase text sizes beyond what seems necessary and avoid very thin strokes in elements that need to remain crisp.

## Duration and Pacing

Social media motion should almost always be shorter than your first instinct. I routinely cut fifteen-second animations down to eight. The discipline of cutting forces prioritization: what is the single most important thing this animation communicates? Lead with that.

Pacing is faster for social than for broadcast. Cuts are quicker, holds are shorter, the rhythm is more urgent. Calibrate to the platform's native content pace — watch the platform content your target audience watches, and match the energy.

Constraints are creative tools when you stop resisting them.
