## The Structure Is the Argument

A brand identity presentation is not a show-and-tell. It's a structured argument: here is the problem, here is the thinking, here is the solution. The work is presented as a conclusion to a logical sequence, not as a proposal awaiting judgment.

When presentations fail — when clients start redesigning the work, when personal taste dominates the discussion, when feedback goes off-topic — it's usually because the structure was wrong. The work appeared before the argument was made.

## Act One: The Brief Recap

I open every brand identity presentation by recapping the brief. Not because clients have forgotten it, but because shared context creates shared evaluation criteria.

I describe: the brand, its audience, its competitive context, the core challenge the identity needs to solve. I take five minutes doing this. By the end of act one, the client and I are looking at the same problem together.

This sets up everything that follows. The creative work is a response to a problem. By establishing the problem first, I've given the client a lens through which to evaluate the response.

## Act Two: The Strategic Foundation

Before showing any visual work, I present the strategic foundation: the personality descriptors I developed, the visual territory I identified, the competitive white space I'm working into.

This is brief — five to eight slides, ten minutes at most. I'm not over-explaining. I'm creating a logical bridge between the brief and the design.

By the end of act two, the client understands the criteria I used to make creative decisions. When I show work in act three, the question has shifted from "do I like this?" to "does this satisfy these criteria?"

## Act Three: The Work

Now I show the logos. In context first — applied to materials the brand will actually use. Business cards, signage, a social post, a tote bag. Real things the client can see themselves using.

Then I pull back to the mark itself and discuss its construction: what the form is, why it was built this way, what it communicates.

I present two or three directions, explain each one's strategic rationale, and state clearly which one I recommend and why. My recommendation is specific and defended, not hedged.

## What I Never Do

I never say "these are just options" — that phrase transfers the creative decision to the client, which is not what they hired me for. I present options as strategic alternatives with specific trade-offs, not as a menu.

I never show speculative concepts I'm not confident in. If I wouldn't defend it under pressure, it doesn't go in the presentation.

I never let the presentation become open to "what if we tried..." during the first meeting. Feedback is welcome. Redesign in the room is a process failure.

## Act Four: The Next Step

Every presentation ends with a clear next step. Approval to proceed to refinement. A round of feedback with a defined scope. A follow-up meeting with a decision-maker who wasn't present.

The next step is agreed in the room, not left open. Open-ended endings produce stalled projects.

The presentation is the design of the client experience. Give it the same care as the work itself.
