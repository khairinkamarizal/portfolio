Rebranding is one of the highest-stakes projects in design. You're not building something new — you're replacing something that exists, that people recognize, that has equity. Do it right and you energize a company's trajectory. Do it wrong and you spend years recovering brand recognition you didn't realize you had.

I've been part of successful rebrands and I've watched rebrands fail from close range. The failure modes are consistent.

## Failure Mode 1: Rebranding Without a Strategic Reason

The most common reason rebrands fail is that they happen for the wrong reason. A new marketing director wants to put their stamp on things. Leadership is bored with the existing brand. The competitors updated their look. None of these are strategic reasons to rebrand.

The question that should precede every rebrand conversation is: what business problem is the rebrand solving? Is the brand failing to attract a new target audience? Does it communicate something that's no longer true about the company? Is it so dated that it's undermining credibility? These are real problems a rebrand can address.

"It feels stale" is not a problem a rebrand solves. It's a feeling that will attach itself to the new brand in three years too.

## Failure Mode 2: Abandoning Equity Without Acknowledgment

Brand equity is the sum of all the times someone saw that mark, that color, that typeface and associated it with the company. Every business that's been operating for more than a few years has accumulated brand equity, even if the brand is imperfect.

The Gap's famous 2010 logo failure is the canonical example: they replaced a logo with decades of equity with a new design that had none. The equity wasn't in the design quality of the old logo. It was in familiarity built over time.

Before I recommend a full rebrand, I ask: what does the existing brand have that's worth preserving? Sometimes the answer is nothing — the brand is genuinely broken and a clean break is correct. More often, there are elements worth carrying forward: a color that's become associated with the brand, a typographic personality that's recognizable, a mark form that has recognizable equity.

## Failure Mode 3: Design by Committee

Rebrands that go through excessive internal approvals tend to converge toward mediocrity. Each stakeholder removes the things they're uncertain about, and what's left is the lowest-common-denominator of inoffensiveness.

Strong rebrands require a clear decision-maker with vision and trust in the design process. This doesn't mean the client isn't involved — it means that internal consensus isn't the goal. The goal is a brand that works, and sometimes working means being distinctive enough to make some people uncomfortable.

## What Successful Rebrands Have in Common

The rebrands I've been involved with that worked shared a few traits: a clearly articulated strategic rationale, a point of view about where the brand needs to go (not just what it needs to stop being), preserved equity elements where they existed, and a client who was willing to make decisions.

## The Rollout Is Part of the Rebrand

A rebrand that launches without a transition plan for existing materials, without communications to key audiences about what's changing, and without a timeline for retiring the old brand is a rebrand that will exist in a messy limbo for years. The design work is half the project. The implementation is the other half.
