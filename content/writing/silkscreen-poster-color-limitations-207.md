## Constraints are creative tools

When I started designing posters for silkscreen printing, I treated the color limitations as a problem to work around. Two or three spot colors instead of full CMYK process. No gradients. No photographic halftones unless carefully planned. I resented the restrictions.

I don't anymore. The constraints of silkscreen printing are what give screen-printed posters their distinctive visual identity. Working within them forces decisions that full-color process printing never demands, and those decisions produce design solutions that digital-only work doesn't arrive at naturally.

## Planning around ink layers

Silkscreen printing is additive — each color is a separate ink layer applied in sequence. The order matters. Light inks on dark stock, dark inks over light base layers, colors mixing where they overlap to create a third color you can use intentionally.

Before I design anything, I think about my ink sequence. If I'm working with two colors on a cream stock, I have five potential colors available: the stock color, color one, color two, and the two overlap mixes. Planning those overlaps deliberately doubles the visual richness of a two-color job.

## The halftone conversation

Photographic images in silkscreen require converting continuous tone to halftone dots — a regular grid of dots that simulate gradations from a single ink. The dot frequency (lines per inch) determines how fine the simulation can be, and it's constrained by the mesh count of the screen being used.

I always have a conversation with the printer before finalizing any halftone work. The mesh count they're using for the run, the squeegee pressure, the stock absorbency — all of these affect how fine a halftone I can use without it filling in or breaking up on press.

## Embracing the aesthetic

The most successful screen-printed posters I've designed are ones where the printing method is visible and celebrated rather than disguised. Slight registration shifts between color layers. The texture of ink sitting on uncoated stock. Bold flat color that would look generic in process printing but has physical presence when it's actual pigment on paper.

I lean into those qualities now. If I'm designing for silkscreen, the design should look like it belongs on a screen-printed poster, not like a digital design that got screen-printed by accident.

## File prep is the design

The design is only half the job on a silkscreen project. File preparation — separating the artwork into individual color layers, setting up registration marks, confirming trap values with the printer — is the other half. Get the file prep wrong and the best design in the world prints badly.

I keep a checklist for every screen-print file prep: all colors on separate layers, all overlapping areas checked for intended vs. accidental color mixing, trap values confirmed with the shop, artwork at 100% final size with proper bleed. No shortcuts.
