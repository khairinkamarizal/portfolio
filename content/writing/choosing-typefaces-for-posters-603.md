Picking a typeface for a poster is nothing like picking one for a book or a screen interface. The scale is different, the distance is different, and the stakes are different. A poster typeface has to work at a glance from across a room and hold up under close inspection. Most typefaces can't do both.

Here's how I actually approach it.

## Start With the Feeling, Not the Category

My first question is never "serif or sans?" It's "what does this event, brand, or message feel like?" A metal concert poster and a jazz festival poster might both use heavy display type, but the emotional register is completely different. One needs aggression and edge, the other needs swing and warmth.

I start by writing three to five adjectives that describe the intended feeling. Then I look for typefaces that carry those qualities in their letterforms — in the terminals, the stroke contrast, the overall rhythm. A high-contrast didone feels elegant and sharp. A compressed grotesque feels urgent and industrial. A humanist serif feels considered and warm.

## The Display Trap

There's a trap a lot of designers fall into: using a display typeface for everything because it looks exciting in the header. Then the subhead is the same face at a smaller size, and the supporting info is the same face even smaller, and suddenly everything reads at the same visual weight and nothing is hierarchical.

I almost always pair a display face with something much quieter for secondary information. The contrast between an expressive headline typeface and a clean, neutral secondary face is what creates hierarchy. The supporting information should recede. It's content, not decoration.

## Legibility at Distance

Posters get read from distance. I test every poster at reduced size — I zoom the screen down to thumbnail, or I print small and step back. If the hierarchy and the key information don't land at that scale, something is wrong.

Thin type is a frequent offender. It looks refined at large sizes on screen but disappears when viewed at distance or printed on uncoated stock. I'm skeptical of anything below a regular weight for headline use unless the size is enormous.

## My Current Working Palette

I keep a rotating short list of workhorses I return to for different contexts:

- Heavy humanist sans for contemporary event work — Aktiv Grotesk Extended, Founders Grotesk Condensed
- Sharp didones for luxury and fashion contexts — Canela, Freight Display
- Geometric for modernist or institutional work — Neue Haas Grotesk, Graphik
- Expressive display for editorial and cultural work — Canela Deck, GT Sectra Display

These aren't rules. They're starting points I push against.

## The Typeface Is Already Communicating

Before anyone reads the words, the typeface is already communicating. It's telling the viewer what category this thing belongs to, what era, what attitude. That's a design decision, not a neutral technical choice. Own it.
