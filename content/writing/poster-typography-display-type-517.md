## Scale Changes Everything

Type at 8pt and type at 200pt are not the same experience. The same typeface at opposite ends of the size spectrum communicates differently, requires different treatment, and has different strengths and weaknesses.

Most type education focuses on body text — legibility, readability, line spacing for sustained reading. Poster typography inverts all of that. The goal isn't sustained reading. The goal is immediate impact and clear hierarchy at a glance, often from across a room.

## Choosing the Right Typeface for Large Scale

Not all typefaces survive at display scale. Subtleties that give a typeface character at small sizes can become problems at large ones.

Typefaces with high stroke contrast — thick strokes and thin strokes with large difference in weight — can look elegant at display size but create awkward optical rhythms when you're setting a word at 300pt. The thin strokes become delicate to the point of fragility.

Typefaces designed specifically for display use tend to have adjusted spacing and slightly different proportions than their text counterparts. Some type foundries offer optical size variants — I use these when available.

For bold headline work, I favor typefaces with moderate stroke contrast and generous, consistent letter spacing. They hold up better at large sizes and across different print conditions.

## Tracking at Display Sizes

Tracking — the overall letter spacing across a word or block of text — needs to be reconsidered at every size change.

At large display sizes, the optical spacing between letters often feels too tight at the default setting. I add tracking until the word breathes. How much? It depends on the typeface and the size, but it's always more than I initially expect.

There's also a compositional consideration: a tightly tracked headline at 200pt can feel aggressive and compressed. A loosely tracked headline at the same size feels spacious and confident. The tracking is part of the tone.

## Alignment as a Compositional Force

Left-aligned type creates a strong vertical axis on the left side of the text block. This axis becomes a design element — I align other elements to it.

Centered type creates symmetry, which can feel formal or ceremonial. Good for certain categories of poster (concert posters for classical music, formal events, institutional work) and wrong for others.

Right-aligned display type is underused and underrated. It creates tension — the text is anchored on the right, which is unconventional enough to read as deliberate. The strong right axis also creates interesting spatial relationships with imagery placed on the left.

## The Role of a Single Big Word

Some of my favorite poster work uses a single word at enormous scale as the primary visual element. The word is simultaneously typographic and illustrative — it fills space, creates texture, and communicates meaning.

When I do this, I think about the word as a shape. The specific letterforms, their proportions, the word's overall silhouette. I adjust letter spacing and sometimes modify individual letterforms to make the whole word read as a coherent shape, not just a sequence of individual letters.

## Mixing Type Sizes

The contrast between large display type and small supporting text is one of the most expressive tools in poster design. A 200pt headline paired with 9pt body copy creates dramatic tension that a 200pt headline paired with 48pt subhead cannot.

I push the contrast further than feels comfortable. The gap between the largest and smallest text on a poster should be significant — that gap is visual energy.

## Practical Rules I Follow

- Set display headlines at the actual print size before finalizing — what reads well on screen at 50% can look different when printed
- Always evaluate tracking at final size, not at a reduced zoom level
- Test the poster at viewing distance — step back to five or ten feet and evaluate hierarchy
- Avoid more than two typefaces on a single poster; the type system should feel unified

Poster typography rewards discipline. The fewer decisions you make, the more intentional each one feels.
