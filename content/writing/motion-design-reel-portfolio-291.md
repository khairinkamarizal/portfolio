## The reel is a design problem

Most motion designers treat their reel as a collection of work samples. The best motion designers treat it as a design problem. A reel has a beginning, a middle, and an end. It has pacing, rhythm, and structure. It makes an argument about the designer's capabilities and sensibility. It's a piece of motion design in its own right.

The difference between a collection and a designed reel is visible and significant. A collection of work samples says: here are things I've made. A designed reel says: here is who I am as a designer.

## The ruthless curation principle

The reel is only as strong as its weakest piece. One mediocre project included for volume or client name recognition brings the entire reel down to its level. The viewer's perception of your work is shaped by the worst thing they see, not the average.

I would rather have a 60-second reel of genuinely strong work than a 3-minute reel padded with mediocre client work. Shorter, better is always the right call. If you only have 45 seconds of work you're truly proud of, make a 45-second reel.

## The opening and closing positions

The first and last pieces in a reel receive the most attention and carry the most weight in the viewer's impression. The first piece establishes expectations; the last piece is what's freshest in memory.

Put your single strongest piece first. Put your second strongest piece last. Everything in the middle is the middle — it matters less than the bookends.

## Pacing and variety

Within the reel, pacing variety keeps the viewer's attention. A sequence of three similarly-paced, similarly-toned pieces reads as monotonous regardless of their individual quality. Alternating between fast and slow pieces, between typographic and figurative work, between bold and subtle treatments, maintains energy throughout.

I cut reels in the editing software rather than just concatenating clips in sequence, treating the transitions and pacing between pieces with the same care I'd give to any motion design project.

## The music decision

Music in a motion reel is a double-edged tool. Right music that's well-synced to the cut makes the reel significantly more engaging. Wrong music, or music that fights the work, actively hurts the impression.

I choose reel music based on the tonal register I want to establish — the emotional experience I want the viewer to have while watching. The music should support the work's visual register, not contrast with it. And it should be licensed — using unlicensed music in a professional reel demonstrates poor judgment about intellectual property.

## Self-initiated work

The strongest motion design reels include self-initiated work alongside client work. Self-initiated projects demonstrate what you'd do with complete creative freedom — they show your actual aesthetic sensibility, not your ability to execute a client brief.

If you don't have self-initiated motion design work, make some. Create a typographic animation based on a text you love. Animate a logo you designed. Build a short motion piece about a subject that interests you. These pieces often become the most distinctive parts of a reel.
