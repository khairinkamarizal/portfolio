## The Translation Problem

A brand with a distinctive illustration style has invested in a specific visual language. When that illustration style needs to move — in video content, social media, animated explainers — the animation has to feel like it comes from the same world as the static illustrations.

This sounds obvious. It's surprisingly easy to get wrong.

## The Style Informs the Motion

Before I animate any branded illustration, I spend time understanding the qualities of the illustration style itself. What's its energy? Are the forms loose and gestural, or precise and geometric? Does it feel handmade or mechanical? Does it have a sense of humor?

The answers to these questions should directly inform the animation decisions. Loose, gestural illustration calls for organic, fluid motion. Precise geometric illustration might call for snappier, more mechanical motion. The movement should feel native to the visual world, not imported from somewhere else.

## Character Animation Principles

When a brand's illustration style includes characters, animating them requires understanding character animation principles beyond standard motion design. How does this specific character move? What's their physical weight? What's their personality in motion?

I treat character animation as a design challenge distinct from general motion design — it requires different skills and different references. The character's motion becomes part of the brand expression just as the drawn character is.

## Rigging vs. Frame-by-Frame

For illustration-based motion work, there are two fundamental approaches: rigging (building a puppet from the illustration elements and animating the rig) and frame-by-frame animation (drawing each frame or keyframe individually).

Rigging is more efficient for repeating movements and character cycles. Frame-by-frame is more expressive and preserves the handmade quality of drawn illustrations. The right approach depends on the budget, the illustration style, and the intended effect.

## Maintaining Illustration Quality Through Compression

Illustrations animated in video form are subject to compression artifacts that can degrade the visual quality of fine detail. High-contrast edges, fine lines, and complex textures are particularly vulnerable.

I design animated illustration content with compression in mind — slightly bolder lines, slightly simplified fine details — so that the compressed output preserves the integrity of the illustration style.

## Extending the Style for Motion-Only Elements

Sometimes motion content needs visual elements that don't exist in the static illustration system — transition effects, background animations, graphic transitions. These elements need to be designed in the same visual language as the existing illustrations.

Extending a brand's illustration style for motion requires understanding the rules of the style deeply enough to generate new work that feels genuinely part of it.
