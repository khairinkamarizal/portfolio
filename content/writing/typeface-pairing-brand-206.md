Typeface pairing is one of those topics where everyone has opinions and most of the conventional wisdom is vague. "Choose contrasting typefaces." "Make sure they harmonize." "Don't use similar styles." These sound like principles but they don't tell you anything concrete about how to actually make a decision.

Here's how I actually do it.

## Start with the Brand's Typographic Personality

Before I look at a single typeface, I write down three or four words that describe the brand's typographic character. Not the brand's personality in general — the specific typographic register I want. Rigorous. Playful. Authoritative. Warm. Minimal. Expressive.

These words guide every pairing decision. A typeface either reinforces the desired character or it doesn't. This is how I avoid falling into the trap of pairing typefaces I personally like that have nothing to do with the brand.

## The Primary/Secondary Function Distinction

In a brand identity system, paired typefaces have distinct jobs. The primary typeface — usually a display or headline face — expresses the brand's personality most directly. It's what people remember. It might be unusual, distinctive, or expressive.

The secondary typeface handles utility: body copy, captions, functional text in UI and print. It needs to be readable, versatile, and subordinate enough not to compete with the primary.

This functional distinction makes pairing decisions easier. When I evaluate a secondary typeface, I'm asking whether it's readable at small sizes, whether it has the weights I need, and whether it stays out of the way of the primary. I'm not asking whether it's interesting.

## Contrast vs. Harmony

The most debated pairing question: should the two typefaces contrast or harmonize?

My answer: always contrast, but harmonize structurally. What I mean: the two faces should feel clearly different — usually this means a serif/sans pairing, or a display/text pairing — but they should share underlying structural proportions. X-height, stem weight, overall density. When two faces share these structural qualities despite looking different on the surface, the pairing feels right without looking like they're trying too hard.

Faces from the same designer or foundry often have this structural compatibility even when they're stylistically different. Klim Type, Commercial Type, and Optimo all have families where pairing across styles is built into the extended family structure.

## The Monoculture Problem

I've worked at studios where every brand got the same pairing formula — geometric sans plus humanist serif, or condensed grotesque plus transitional serif — and the work started to look like it came from the same designer regardless of client.

I resist this by deliberately expanding my typeface vocabulary each year, picking a few families I don't know well and using them in real projects. The discomfort of working with unfamiliar type is worth it for the range it builds.

## Testing the Pairing in Context

Pairing decisions made by looking at type specimens are theoretical. They need to be tested in actual layouts at actual sizes with actual content.

I'll set the same paragraph of brand content in five to eight pairing candidates and then evaluate them together. The one that feels right at that scale, with that content, for that brand — that's the answer. Sometimes the "correct" pairing by every principle looks flat in practice, and a more unexpected combination comes alive.

Trust the test over the theory.
