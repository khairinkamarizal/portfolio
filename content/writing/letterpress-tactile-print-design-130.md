## Why Letterpress Is Back

Letterpress printing — the process of pressing inked type or plates into paper to create a debossed, tactile impression — is a 500-year-old technology that effectively died commercially in the mid-20th century. And yet there are now more letterpress studios operating in North America than at any point in recent memory.

The revival isn't nostalgia. It's a response to something real: in a world of pixels and ephemeral digital content, things that have physical presence, texture, and craft evidence feel more meaningful.

## What Letterpress Does That Offset Can't

The defining characteristic of letterpress is the impression — the slight indentation of type or plate into the paper that you can feel with your fingertips. This tactile quality is impossible to replicate in offset or digital printing. You can simulate the look of letterpress with texture overlays and shadows, but the print medium itself creates the physical reality.

There's also a quality of ink coverage unique to letterpress. Because the process presses type into the paper under pressure, the ink saturation at the edges of letters is different from offset. Letters have a certain weight and presence in good letterpress printing that offset and digital can approach but not match.

## Designing for Letterpress Constraints

Letterpress has technical constraints that shape design decisions. It works best with limited colors (each color requires a separate pass), with relatively simple forms that translate well to plate or movable type, and with papers that are substantial enough to accept the impression without damage.

Fine details that work in offset can disappear or fill in with letterpress. I simplify form when designing for letterpress, knowing that the process will add richness through the impression itself.

## The Right Papers

Paper selection is as important as any design decision in letterpress work. Thick, soft cotton papers accept the impression beautifully and look extraordinarily luxurious. Hard-surfaced papers resist impression. The feel of the paper in the viewer's hands is part of the piece's communication.

I treat paper selection as a design decision, not a production detail.

## Where Letterpress Fits in a Modern Practice

Letterpress is appropriate for work that needs to signal craft, care, and significance: wedding invitations, special edition prints, business cards for brands where physical presence matters, event programs for premium occasions. It's a commitment — higher cost, longer lead times, more production complexity — that's worth making when the object itself is part of the message.

When the artifact is the communication, letterpress is unbeatable.
