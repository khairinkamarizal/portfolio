Lettermarks are deceptively simple. It's just a letter or two, right? How hard can it be? In practice, a lettermark is one of the most demanding logo formats because there's so little material to work with. Every curve, every weight, every spacing decision is visible.

## Why Lettermarks Exist

Organizations choose lettermarks for a few reasons: the full name is too long for practical logo use, the initials are already well-known (IBM, CBS, BBC), or the name doesn't lend itself to an icon and the letterform is more distinctive than any symbol could be.

When the rationale is weak — when the lettermark is chosen because it seems "professional" rather than because it's the right format — the result usually shows. Lettermarks need to justify their reductiveness.

## Starting Material: Custom vs. Modified

I rarely use an existing typeface unmodified for a lettermark. At the scale and isolation of a logo, a standard typeface looks like a typeface, not a logo. The mark needs to feel designed — custom, specific to this organization.

My typical approach: start from a typeface with strong character, then customize aggressively. Extend strokes, modify proportions, create custom joints, add unique details. The result should be clearly descended from a legible typographic tradition but unmistakably itself.

Fully custom drawn lettermarks are another option — more expensive, but they allow total control over every form. For organizations with strong visual identities and the budget to support it, custom lettering creates marks that are impossible to confuse with anyone else.

## The Monogram Problem

Two or three-letter monograms have an additional challenge: the letters have to work as a unit, not just as individual characters. The visual interaction between the letters — where they share strokes, overlap, interlock, or sit in relationship — is the design.

I test monogram arrangements exhaustively. Horizontal, vertical, diagonal, overlapping, nested. The right arrangement usually becomes obvious after exploring enough variations, but you have to explore. The first arrangement you try is rarely the right one.

## Negative Space in Lettermarks

Enclosed letterforms — O, D, B, P, Q — have counters that are active negative space. I treat these as designed shapes. A round counter in a heavy lettermark creates a circular punch of white that has visual weight. I can use that weight to balance the composition.

Modified lettermarks that open a counter deliberately — cutting through the enclosure of a B or C — create visual dynamism without adding elements. Less is more, but less must be intentional.

## Color and Lettermarks

A lettermark with a strong form doesn't need color to work. But color can amplify a lettermark's expressiveness. A two-letter mark with the letters in different colors from the brand palette reads as more dynamic than a monochromatic mark. I test both and let the form guide the decision.
