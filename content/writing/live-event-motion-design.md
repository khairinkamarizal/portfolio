Live event motion design is as different from post-produced motion as live television is from film. The environment is hostile — large screens at extreme angles, ambient lighting competing with the projection or display, audiences who are there for the event, not the graphics. And there's no second take.

I've designed motion for live events ranging from corporate conferences to music festivals. The discipline is specific.

## The Screen Environment Is Not Your Monitor

The most dangerous assumption in live event motion design is that the work will look like it does on your screen. It won't. Conference screens are often rear-projection or large LED panels with different color gamuts, different brightness levels, and different gamma characteristics than any monitor. Outdoor LED screens have completely different color and brightness characteristics than indoor LED.

I ask for technical specifications from the production company before designing: screen type, resolution, native file format, expected brightness in the ambient conditions. I design to those specifications and test in conditions as close to the actual environment as possible.

## Color for High Brightness Environments

In bright ambient conditions — outdoor events, large conference halls with overhead lighting — colors need to be particularly bold and high-contrast. Subtle color relationships that look refined on a monitor become invisible on a screen that's competing with sunlight or overhead fluorescents.

I use bolder, higher-contrast palettes for live event work than for broadcast or digital work. Pure white on pure black. Saturated colors at full intensity. No subtle gradients that compress into flat fields in live conditions.

## Loop-First Design

Most live event motion content needs to loop seamlessly — it plays continuously during the pre-show, during breaks, between segments. Loop-first design means building every piece as a seamless loop before thinking about other use cases.

A loop that glitches or resets visibly is immediately obvious to a live audience. Seamless loops require careful attention to the transition between the end and beginning of each animation cycle.

## File Format and Playback Reliability

Live event playback happens on specific hardware — media servers like Resolume, Disguise, or QLab. Each platform has its own preferred file formats and codec requirements. Providing content in the wrong format creates playback problems that are unacceptable in a live environment.

I always confirm the playback platform and its specific technical requirements before starting production. HAP codec, ProRes, specific frame rates, specific resolutions — whatever the platform requires.

## Designing for Operator Control

Live event motion is operated by a human — a video operator who triggers cues, adjusts timing, and responds to unexpected changes in the live environment. I design with operator control in mind: content that can be held on a specific frame, looped extended if needed, cut at any point without looking wrong.

Designing content that's only correct when played from beginning to end at exactly the right timing is fragile in live contexts. Building in flexibility for human operation makes the design more resilient.
