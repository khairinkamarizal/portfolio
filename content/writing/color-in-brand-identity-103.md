## Color Does the Heavy Lifting

When I think about the brands that stick with people, color is almost always part of the reason. Not the only reason, but a significant one. Color bypasses language. It triggers associations before the conscious mind has processed anything else.

That's not a small thing. It means that getting color right in a brand identity is one of the highest-leverage decisions you can make.

## The Mistake of Picking Colors Too Early

Most designers — myself included, early in my career — pick colors too early in the process. The visual is underdeveloped and the strategy is unclear, but there's already a palette on the moodboard. That's backwards.

Color should come after you understand what the brand needs to communicate. It should feel inevitable, not arbitrary. When I look at a finished brand identity and the colors feel exactly right, it's because they were chosen to do a specific job, not because they looked nice together.

## Hue, Saturation, and Value All Matter

A lot of brand color work focuses too much on hue — the basic red, blue, green choice — and not enough on saturation and value. But those dimensions carry as much meaning as the hue itself.

A desaturated teal feels completely different from a highly saturated one. A mid-value navy reads differently from a near-black navy. These are not subtle distinctions. They shift the entire personality of a brand.

I spend as much time calibrating saturation and value as I do choosing the hue itself.

## Primary, Secondary, and Accent

A functional brand color system needs at least three tiers: a primary color that carries the brand's core identity, secondary colors that support and expand the palette, and accent colors used sparingly for emphasis. The mistake is treating all colors equally. If everything is accent, nothing is.

I also think carefully about how colors work in combination. A palette that looks harmonious as swatches can feel muddy in actual application. I always test colors in context — on mockups, on the actual materials where the brand will live.

## Color Across Applications

Brand color has to work across a range of surfaces: digital screens, printed materials, signage, merchandise, packaging. Each of those contexts has different technical constraints. RGB and CMYK don't map perfectly. Spot colors behave differently from process printing.

I always deliver brand colors with values for each relevant color space. Not just a hex code. A brand that looks great on screen and falls apart in print is a half-finished brand.

## The Emotional Layer

Finally — and this is the part that can't be fully systematized — color carries emotion. Warm yellows feel different from cool blues. There's psychology here, but also culture and context. What reads as trustworthy in one market might read as cold in another.

I treat color research as part of the brand strategy work, not as a post-process decoration step. That shift in mindset changes everything.
