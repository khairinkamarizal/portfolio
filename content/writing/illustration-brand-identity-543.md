## Why Illustration Is Having a Moment

Illustration in brand identity isn't new, but it's more prevalent now than it's been in decades. The pendulum swung hard toward photography in the 2000s and 2010s — stock photography became cheap and ubiquitous, and illustration felt old-fashioned in comparison.

Now the pendulum is swinging back. Partly because stock photography became so cheap and ubiquitous that it stopped differentiating. Brands using the same stock library look like each other. Custom illustration, by definition, can't be used by anyone else.

## What Custom Illustration Actually Adds

The most important thing custom illustration adds is a visual world that's proprietary. A style of mark-making, a color treatment, a way of depicting figures or objects that belongs to the brand and only to the brand.

This is different from a logo or a typeface, both of which can eventually be licensed or adopted by others. An illustration style, if distinctive enough, is genuinely owned. When a viewer sees it in any context, it reads as that brand.

The second thing illustration adds is humanity. Photographs can feel human, but they capture a real moment in the real world. Illustrations are entirely constructed — every mark was made by someone's hand, with someone's sensibility. That handmade quality communicates craft and care in a way that photography can't replicate.

## Finding the Right Illustration Style

Not every illustration style is right for every brand. The illustration style needs to match the brand's personality as precisely as the typography and color do.

A brand that's precise and technical needs illustration that's clean, geometric, diagrammatic. A brand that's warm and personal needs illustration that's loose, expressive, gestural. A brand that's bold and contemporary needs illustration that takes compositional risks.

I evaluate illustration style against the same personality descriptors I use for the rest of the identity. If the illustration style says something the brand doesn't believe, it doesn't belong in the system.

## The Consistency Problem

The biggest challenge with illustration in brand systems is maintaining consistency over time when multiple illustrators work on the same brand. Style consistency across different artists is hard.

I address this with a detailed illustration style guide: specific notes on line weight, color palette, level of detail, perspective approach, how figures are depicted, how objects are depicted, what is never depicted. The more specific the documentation, the more consistent the output.

I also recommend brands work with a small, consistent pool of illustrators rather than hiring different people for every project. The relationship between a brand and its core illustrators deepens over time in ways that style guides can't fully capture.

## When to Commission vs. When to Use a Style

There's a practical middle ground between custom illustration and stock: a defined illustration style applied consistently using available resources.

Some brands can't afford continuous custom illustration but can afford a style guide that specifies how illustrations should be created — even by non-specialists. A clear enough style guide, with examples and rules, can produce reasonably consistent illustration even without a dedicated illustrator.

The key is specificity. A vague style guide produces vague results. A specific one produces specific ones.

## The Integration Test

Illustration works in a brand system when you can place a piece of illustration next to the logo, the type, and the color palette and have it feel like part of the same world.

When the illustration feels like it came from a different creative planet — too polished when the brand is raw, too casual when the brand is refined — the system is fragmented. The integration test catches this before it ships.
