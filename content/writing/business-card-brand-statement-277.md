## The business card is not obsolete

Every few years there's a think piece declaring the death of the business card. Digital contact sharing, LinkedIn profiles, NFC chips — the functional need for a physical card is arguably declining. And yet the business card persists, and it persists with good reason.

A beautifully designed business card handed to someone in a meeting communicates something that a phone tap or QR code cannot: I invested in this. I care about how I present myself. This organization has aesthetic standards. These are brand messages that digital contact sharing simply doesn't carry.

## The physical object as brand expression

The business card is a tiny physical object that someone takes away from an encounter and holds in their hand. It lives in a wallet or on a desk. It gets picked up again when the person needs to remember the contact. Every time they handle it, they receive a brand impression.

This repeated physical encounter is an opportunity that most designers underuse. A card printed on 400gsm stock with letterpress type has physical presence — weight, texture, depth — that communicates quality before the person has read a word. A card printed on 14pt stock with digital printing has none of that presence.

## The information hierarchy problem

Business cards have a severe space constraint. In 3.5 by 2 inches, a card needs to carry: the brand mark, the person's name, their title, contact information (phone, email, website), and sometimes a physical address.

That's a significant amount of information for a tiny surface. The design challenge is creating a clear hierarchy — name and brand mark dominant, contact details legible but subordinate — without the card feeling cluttered.

My approach: work with the constraint rather than against it. Be ruthless about what information is truly necessary. In 2022, a physical address on most business cards is low priority. The phone number might be lower priority than email for many professional contexts. Edit the content before designing the layout.

## Production specifications that make the difference

The difference between a mediocre business card and an excellent one is almost entirely in the production specifications. Paper stock weight and texture. Printing method (digital offset vs. letterpress vs. foil stamping). Finish (uncoated, soft touch matte, spot UV). Edge treatment (gilded edges, painted edges, edge printing).

I work with the client's budget to identify the one or two production upgrades that will have the most impact. For most budgets, moving from standard offset printing on 14pt stock to letterpress on 600gsm cotton stock is the single most impactful upgrade. It costs more per card but produces something that genuinely stands out in a stack of business cards.

## The two-sided opportunity

Both sides of a business card are design opportunities. The conventional approach uses the front for primary contact information and the back for secondary information or leaves it blank. An alternative approach treats the back as a visual brand moment — a full-bleed illustration, a pattern, a bold color. This approach produces a card that people turn over, examine, and comment on.
