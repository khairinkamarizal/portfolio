## Why study design history at all

I hear designers sometimes dismiss historical study as academic — something for schools, not studios. I disagree. Understanding where graphic design's best ideas came from gives you access to those ideas in their pure form, before decades of dilution and misapplication turned them into clichés.

The International Typographic Style — Swiss Style — is the single most influential design movement of the 20th century. Its influence is so pervasive that most contemporary designers are already using its principles without knowing where they came from. Studying the source makes you better at using the ideas deliberately.

## The primacy of the grid

The grid in Swiss Style is not a convenience — it's a philosophical position. It says that visual organization should be systematic, rational, and reproducible. Every element has a place determined by a rational system rather than intuitive feeling.

I don't apply this dogmatically. Pure grid adherence produces rigidity that serves structure at the cost of expression. But the underlying idea — that visual decisions should have a rational justification, that organization should be systematic rather than arbitrary — is one I've internalized completely. Every layout I make has an underlying organizational logic, even when elements break from it deliberately.

## Objective photography over illustration

One of the central Swiss Style positions is the preference for photography over illustration for informational content, on the grounds that photography is more objective and universally legible across cultural contexts.

I've modified this position. Photography is not inherently objective — it's as authored and culturally specific as illustration. But the underlying principle — that visual choices should be motivated by communication goals rather than personal style expression — is sound and useful.

## Sans-serif for clarity

The preference in Swiss Style for sans-serif typefaces was partly ideological (sans-serifs were associated with modernist rationalism) and partly practical (sans-serifs are more legible in poor printing conditions, in small sizes, in environmental signage).

The practical argument is less compelling now than it was in 1960 — printing quality has improved enormously, and many highly legible typefaces are serifed. But the emphasis on legibility as the primary criterion for typeface selection over aesthetic preference or historical association is a principle I hold onto. The typeface serves communication first.

## Asymmetric layouts over centered composition

Swiss Style's preference for asymmetric, grid-based layouts over centered, symmetrical compositions was a deliberate break from the decorative composition styles that preceded it. Asymmetry creates visual movement and hierarchy; centered composition creates stability and ceremony.

Both have their place. I use centered composition for work that benefits from its ceremonial quality — invitations, diplomas, certain formal typographic posters. I default to asymmetric layouts for everything else, because asymmetry is inherently more dynamic and creates more opportunities for visual hierarchy.
