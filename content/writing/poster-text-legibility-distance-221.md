Legibility at distance is a problem that screen-based design workflows actively hide from you. Everything looks fine at 100% zoom on a 27-inch monitor. The poster goes to print, gets installed, and the headline that looked perfectly readable in InDesign is illegible from where the actual viewer will be standing.

I've made this mistake. I've also learned exactly what to do to prevent it.

## Understanding Viewing Distance

The first question for any poster project: where will it be displayed and what's the typical viewing distance? These two variables should drive every typographic sizing decision.

A music venue poster behind the bar: viewers are 3–5 meters away. A conference room information poster: viewers read it from 60–90cm. A transit station poster: viewers might scan it from 6–8 meters. A billboard: 30+ meters.

I write the viewing distance into the project brief and refer to it constantly. It's not a background fact — it's a design specification.

## The Legibility Math

There are rough formulas for minimum legible type size at distance, based on the assumption that a 1mm character height is readable at about 3 meters under normal conditions. So at 5 meters, characters should be at minimum 1.7mm tall; at 10 meters, 3.3mm.

These are starting points, not absolutes. They assume good contrast, clean letterforms, and unobstructed sighting lines. In real conditions — lower light, complex backgrounds, motion — you need larger type than the formula suggests.

## The Reduction Test

My practical testing method: reduce the layout to a small size where the viewing distance-to-poster-size ratio approximately matches reality.

For a poster that will be viewed at 4 meters at an A1 (84cm) format: the format will appear roughly 21cm tall from that distance (84cm / 4). I print or display the layout at 21cm height and check legibility from normal viewing distance at my desk.

If I can't read the primary hierarchy clearly, the type is too small or the contrast is insufficient. This test catches problems that zoomed screen review completely misses.

## Contrast Is as Important as Size

A large but low-contrast type treatment is harder to read at distance than a smaller but high-contrast one. The eye needs crisp edge definition to resolve letterforms at distance, and low contrast blurs those edges.

I default to maximum practical contrast for primary typographic elements on posters meant for distance viewing — white on dark, black on white, or near-equivalents. Sophisticated low-contrast typography is for close-range work: editorial, packaging, exhibition display with typical viewing distances under a meter.

## Typeface Selection for Distance

Not all typefaces perform equally at distance. Typefaces with large apertures (open counters), clear differentiation between letters (no ambiguous character pairs), and moderate to high stroke contrast legible at scale perform better.

Ultra-thin weights disappear at distance. Extremely tight or decorative letterforms that require close reading to decode fail. I evaluate typeface candidates specifically for the viewing distance of each project, not just for how they look at display scale on screen.

## The Walk-Away Test

For projects where I have physical space available, I do a walk-away test with printed or projected mock-ups at scale. I pin the poster to a wall and walk to the expected viewing distance without looking at it. Then I turn around and look fresh.

What do I register first? Is it what I want to be primary? Can I read the essential information (event, date, location) before I've moved closer? If yes, the hierarchy and legibility are working.

If not, I go back to the type sizes and contrast before anything else. Everything is legible from six inches. Design for where people actually stand.
