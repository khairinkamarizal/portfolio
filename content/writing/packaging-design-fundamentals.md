Packaging design is graphic design in three dimensions, experienced in a retail environment, handled physically, and competing with dozens of adjacent products on the same shelf. Each of these conditions creates requirements that flat graphic design doesn't have to address.

## The 360-Degree Problem

A flat poster has one side. A package has multiple faces, each with its own relationship to the viewer and its own communication requirements. The primary panel — the "billboard" face the retailer displays forward — has to do the most work. The secondary panels carry product information, ingredients, legal copy. The bottom panel has barcodes and production codes.

I design primary panel first, but I design all panels simultaneously — the brand needs to feel consistent whether someone is looking at the front of the box on the shelf or reading the back at home.

## Shelf Impact Before Product Communication

A package needs to stand out before it can communicate anything about the product. In a retail environment, a package that blends into its category is invisible. The first job of a package design is to be noticed.

I study the shelf environment before designing. Photographing or rendering the competitive shelf set — all the adjacent products in the category — shows me what the current visual conventions are and where there's differentiation opportunity. A category dominated by white backgrounds is disrupted by a color or dark background. A category dominated by photography is disrupted by illustration or pure typography.

## Hierarchy for Multiple Reading Distances

Retail packaging is read at three distances. Far: from across the aisle, brand and category recognition. Mid: from one meter, the product name and primary sell. Close: at arm's length while holding the package, product details, ingredients, instructions.

Type and design decisions need to work at all three distances. The brand name and product name need shelf impact. The secondary information needs to be legible when read closely.

## Physical Handling and Material Selection

A package is a physical object. The material communicates as much as the graphics. Heavy board stock communicates premium quality. Lightweight flexible packaging communicates convenience or economy. Matte surfaces feel different from gloss in the hand and communicate different qualities.

I specify materials and finishes as part of the design, not as a production afterthought. The paper stock, the laminate finish, the structural format — all of these are design decisions.

## Structural and Mechanical Constraints

Package graphics have to work within structural constraints that don't exist in flat design. Folds, die cuts, flaps, perforations, windows — each of these creates constraints on where elements can be placed and how graphics cross structural boundaries.

Working with a structural designer (or understanding the structural template before designing) is non-negotiable. Graphics that look great in a flat layout can be bisected by fold lines or hidden under glue tabs in the real three-dimensional package.
