Festival identity design combines brand identity work with campaign design in a specific way. A festival has a permanent identity — the name, the brand, the mark — that persists year over year. And it has an annual campaign identity — the visual language for a specific year's edition — that's temporary and distinctive.

Designing both, and making them work together, is the specific challenge.

## The Permanent vs. The Annual

The permanent identity is the festival's brand: its logo, its core typeface, its foundational visual language. This is what people recognize year over year. It's what makes it possible to say "oh, that's [Festival Name]'s style" without seeing the name.

The annual campaign is the distinctive visual expression for one year's edition. It's built on the permanent identity but has its own character — a color treatment, a graphic language, an illustrative style, a theme. It's what makes each year feel fresh and worth attending again.

The tension between these two layers — consistency (permanent identity) and freshness (annual campaign) — is the central creative challenge of festival design.

## Building the System for Durability

The permanent identity has to be built with longevity in mind. A festival identity might be used for 20 or 30 years. The mark, the typeface, and the core visual language should feel as appropriate in 15 years as they do at launch.

This means avoiding trend-forward design choices in the permanent layer. The logo and core type should be relatively timeless — character and quality over style. The annual campaign is where trend and contemporary visual language can live, precisely because it's replaced every year.

## The Annual Campaign Structure

The most successful festival design systems I've worked with establish a template for the annual campaign — a structure for how the annual layer is built — rather than designing each year from scratch.

The template might define: the format and scale of key-art posters, the placement and treatment of the permanent identity elements, the zones available for the annual graphic language, and the type hierarchy for event-specific information.

Within this template, each year's campaign fills in the annual expression. The structure is consistent; the content is new.

## Lineup and Scheduling Graphics

Festival communication includes specific graphic challenges: lineup announcements (presenting performer names in hierarchy of billing), scheduling graphics (communicating a complex grid of simultaneous events across multiple stages and days), and wayfinding for the physical event.

These are information design problems that require clear hierarchy, legible typography at varying scales, and the ability to update as details change. I design lineup graphics with a modular system — a hierarchy of name sizes and weights that can be applied to any lineup composition — rather than custom-setting each announcement.

## Merchandise as Brand Expression

Festival merchandise is a major brand expression vehicle — worn and used long after the event, functioning as brand ambassadors. T-shirts, tote bags, programs, and posters need to work as objects people want to own, not just event ephemera.

The best festival merchandise is designed to be desirable independently of the event — visually interesting enough that someone would want to wear it regardless of whether they attended. This means treating merchandise design with the same care as key poster design, not as a production afterthought.
