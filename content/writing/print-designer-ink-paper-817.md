## The Material Is Part of the Design

I've watched designers treat print production as an afterthought — design the piece, send the file, collect the result. The material decisions get delegated to the printer, which means they get made for cost and convenience rather than for the design.

When I'm working on print, the paper stock and ink choices are design decisions I make, not production details I outsource. The material the design lives on shapes how it reads, feels, and is perceived. Getting this right is the difference between a printed piece that feels considered and one that feels generic.

## Stock Weight and Surface

Paper weight affects perception before a reader processes a single word. A 300gsm business card signals investment and quality. A 120gsm card signals budget constraint. Neither is inherently better — they're communicating different things, and the choice should be intentional.

Surface matters equally. Uncoated stock feels warm, human, tactile. It absorbs ink in a way that creates softer edges and a matte finish with real texture. Coated gloss stock feels slick and vivid — colors pop, images have punch, but it can feel cold or corporate depending on context. Silk coated is a middle ground many people underuse: good color reproduction, readable sheen, more tactile than gloss.

## CMYK Ink Behavior

The transition from screen to CMYK print is where most color problems happen. RGB colors that look vibrant on a calibrated monitor can look flat or muddy in four-color offset print.

The main culprits: saturated blues and purples lose vibrancy in CMYK. Bright oranges can shift orange. Very light tints can look patchy if they're below about 10% coverage.

I always check final colors in CMYK preview before sending to print, and I specify Pantone matches for brand colors that need to be exact. Trying to hit a specific orange or teal in process CMYK is unreliable. Specifying a Pantone value and running a spot color is reliable.

## Specialty Finishes

Foil, emboss, deboss, spot UV, soft-touch laminate — these finishes are tools, not decoration. I use them when they serve the communication:

Foil on a small area draws the eye precisely because it behaves differently from the rest of the surface. Deboss creates tactile hierarchy. Soft-touch laminate on a cover creates an irresistible physical quality. Spot UV on a matte surface plays light and shadow games that shift as the piece is moved.

Each finish adds cost and complexity. Each should earn its place.

## The Proof

I never approve a print job without a physical proof. PDF proofs on screen are not substitutes for a printed proof on the actual stock with the actual ink and finishes. Color rendering, finish behavior, stock weight — none of these are predictable from a PDF on a backlit monitor.

The proof step costs time and sometimes money. It costs less than a bad print run. Get the proof, review it in good light, and approve only what you actually want to receive in quantity.
