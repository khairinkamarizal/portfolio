When I started working in brand identity, motion was an afterthought. You designed the logo, the color system, the typography, and then someone in a different department figured out how to animate it for broadcast. That separation made a certain kind of sense when motion only meant TV spots and title sequences.

It doesn't make sense anymore. Motion is now everywhere in brand experience — social media, digital advertising, loading screens, product interfaces, environmental installations. Brands that treat motion as a post-production bolt-on end up with animation that contradicts everything the static identity is communicating.

## Motion Has to Come from the Brand

The most common failure I see in brand animation is technically proficient motion that feels completely disconnected from the brand's character. Smooth, beautiful easing curves. Elegant transitions. Well-crafted timing. And none of it feels like the brand.

A financial services brand that communicates stability and precision shouldn't have its logo revealed through a bouncy, elastic animation — even if that animation is technically well-executed. A youth streetwear brand shouldn't get a stately, slow reveal with cinematic black letterboxing.

The personality of the motion should match the personality of the brand. That means defining motion principles at the identity level, not at the execution level.

## The Motion Principles Framework

When I'm developing a brand identity that will have significant motion touchpoints, I establish motion principles alongside color and type guidelines. These are usually three to five descriptors that define how the brand moves — not specific easing values, but character.

"Precise, direct, no excess" might mean: cuts over transitions, tight timing, minimal decorative animation. "Organic, alive, breathing" might mean: subtle scale animations, easing that mimics natural physics, elements that feel like they exist in physical space.

These principles then inform the technical choices: easing curves, timing, the types of transitions used, whether elements move independently or in coordinated groups.

## Designing for Frame Zero and the In-Between

Static brand design is about the resolved state — the lockup as it appears when everything is settled. Motion design requires thinking about every state the brand passes through.

The logo reveal isn't just the final frame. It's every frame from blank to fully formed. Does each intermediate state look intentional? Does the way the mark assembles reinforce the concept behind it? I've seen beautifully designed static logos that look terrible in the moment of assembly because no one thought about the in-between.

I now design with the motion in my head from the beginning. If I'm designing a mark that I know will animate, I think about how it comes apart. Does it break along logical structural lines? Does the sequence of assembly tell a story about the mark's construction?

## Timing and Brand Tempo

Every brand has a tempo — a sense of pace that fits its personality. Luxury brands tend toward slower, more deliberate timing. High-energy consumer brands move faster. B2B software brands often land in a middle register: efficient, not frantic.

That tempo needs to be consistent across motion touchpoints. A loading animation, a slide transition in a presentation, a social media cutdown, a broadcast ident — they should all feel like they're moving at the same speed, even if the specific timing varies. Inconsistent tempo is as disorienting as inconsistent color usage.

## Working Between Disciplines

The practical challenge is that brand design and motion design are often practiced by different people with different tools and training. Brand designers work in Figma and Illustrator. Motion designers work in After Effects and Cinema 4D.

I've spent time getting comfortable in After Effects specifically because I don't want to hand off brand animation work and lose control of how the brand moves. Not every brand designer needs to be a motion designer, but understanding the medium well enough to specify intent — and to evaluate whether the motion execution is on-brand — is increasingly essential.

Motion is now part of the brand. Treat it that way from day one.
