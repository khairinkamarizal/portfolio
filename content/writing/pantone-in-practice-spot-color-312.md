Most designers treat Pantone as a premium option — something clients with big budgets get. That's not wrong, but it misses the strategic value of spot color specification. Knowing when to use it, and why, separates designers who understand print from designers who just send CMYK files.

## What Spot Color Actually Is

In offset printing, standard process color uses four inks: cyan, magenta, yellow, and black (CMYK). Any color you want is built by overprinting these four inks in varying percentages. The range of achievable colors — the gamut — is large but not unlimited.

Spot color replaces one or more of the process plates with a pre-mixed ink of a specific, known formula. Pantone is the dominant spot color system, though others exist. A Pantone 485 C is mixed to that specification at the ink manufacturer — not simulated by combining CMYK values.

## When Spot Color Is Worth It

Brand-critical colors. If your client's brand color is a specific orange that falls outside the CMYK gamut, the only way to hit it accurately on press is with a spot color. Brand consistency across years and print runs depends on this accuracy.

Metallic and fluorescent effects. These are impossible in CMYK. Metallic gold, silver, copper — these require a metallic ink. Fluorescent pink or green — CMYK can't achieve the luminosity. If the design calls for these effects, spot color is the only path.

Two-color print runs. For projects with a limited budget where full-color isn't necessary, two-color printing (typically black plus one Pantone) is often more cost-effective than four-color. The result can also be visually stronger — more graphic and direct.

## The CMYK Simulation Problem

Every Pantone swatch book shows both the spot color and its CMYK simulation. Compare them directly and the gap is often shocking — especially for vivid blues, deep purples, and bright oranges. The CMYK simulation is usually duller and slightly different in hue.

For anything brand-critical, I specify Pantone. For general print work where accuracy is less critical, CMYK is fine. Knowing which category your project falls into is part of the job.

## Coated vs. Uncoated

The same Pantone number looks different on coated vs. uncoated stock. Coated paper gives brighter, more saturated results. Uncoated paper absorbs ink and produces a softer, slightly muted version. Pantone designates this with the suffixes C (coated) and U (uncoated).

Always specify the correct suffix. Pantone 200 C and Pantone 200 U are different formulas, mixed to produce the correct result on their respective substrates. Specifying C when printing on uncoated stock will produce a color that doesn't match.
