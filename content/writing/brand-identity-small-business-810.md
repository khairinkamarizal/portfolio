## The Small Business Brief

Small business clients typically come to me with one of two problems. Either they've been operating with no visual identity and they're ready to look serious, or they have a logo someone made them years ago that no longer fits who they are.

Both situations are actually the same underlying question: who are you, who are you for, and what do you want people to feel when they encounter your brand?

I spend a disproportionate amount of time on this question before I touch any design tools.

## Constraints Are Design Tools

Small business branding has real constraints that enterprise projects don't: limited budgets, no dedicated marketing team, no in-house designer to maintain brand consistency. These aren't just inconveniences — they shape what kind of identity will actually work.

An identity that requires a sophisticated in-house design team to execute correctly will fail a small business. What I design instead is a system simple enough that a non-designer can implement it consistently: one or two typefaces available on Google Fonts, a color palette with clear application rules, a logo that works as a PNG without losing integrity.

This isn't "dumbing down" the work. It's designing for the actual conditions of use.

## Discovery Is Non-Negotiable

Even for a small budget project, I insist on a real discovery process. I ask:

- What three words would you want customers to use to describe your business?
- Who are your most important competitors, and what do they look and feel like?
- Who is your ideal customer, and what do they care about?
- Where will this identity appear most — physical space, digital, packaging, uniform?

The answers to these questions shape every decision that follows. Without them, I'm just making something that looks nice, and that's not enough.

## The One-Logo Problem

Most small businesses only need one mark, and it has to work everywhere. This means I design with flexibility in mind from the start. The primary lockup includes the mark and the business name. I also design a stacked version, a horizontal version, and a standalone mark if applicable. All in full color and single-color versions.

This gives the client enough flexibility to handle almost any application without needing to call me every time a new use case comes up.

## Longevity Over Trend

Small businesses can't afford to rebrand every three years. I design identities that are meant to last a decade without feeling dated. This means avoiding whatever is fashionable in the design community right now and focusing on visual decisions that are grounded in craft rather than trend.

I've watched logo trends come and go — the gradients, the geometric sans phase, the brutalist revival. The identities that hold up through all of it are the ones based on clear thinking rather than current fashion.
