Food and beverage is one of the most visually competitive brand categories. Every product on a supermarket shelf is a brand design screaming for attention. The person shopping has a fraction of a second to notice, identify, and make a purchase decision. The brand identity does most of that work before any copy is read.

I've worked on brands across this category — from artisan food startups to established regional beverage brands — and the design requirements are specific enough that I think of food and beverage branding as a distinct discipline.

## Appetite Appeal Is a Design Requirement

Every food brand design decision needs to be evaluated against a question most design projects don't ask: does this make the product more or less appetizing?

Color psychology matters here in a way it doesn't in, say, B2B software branding. Certain colors are well-established appetite suppressants in Western culture: blue-purples, grays, and certain greens can reduce the perceived appeal of food products. Warm reds, oranges, and yellows enhance perceived appetite.

This isn't absolute. Blue works for alcoholic beverages, water, and certain fish products. The rules shift by category. But before I finalize any color palette for a food brand, I test it against the specific product and the cultural associations of the target audience.

## The Shelf Standout Problem

On a supermarket shelf, a product is surrounded by competitors. The packaging design isn't evaluated in isolation — it's evaluated in context. The question isn't "is this beautiful" but "does this stand out from what's around it while still communicating the right category?"

This requires the same competitive visual analysis I do for any brand, but intensified by the physical reality of shelf placement. I look at actual shelf sets — photographing or visiting retail environments — to understand what the visual landscape looks like at the point of purchase.

Sometimes the opportunity is color: a category where everyone is using warm orange-red tones has room for a brand that uses deep teal. Sometimes it's structure: a category of busy, pattern-heavy labels has room for a dramatically minimal approach.

## Regulatory Constraints

Food and beverage packaging is subject to regulatory requirements that vary by market. Nutrition information panels, ingredient lists, allergen declarations, country of origin labeling, alcohol content statements — these are mandatory and non-negotiable.

I design for regulatory compliance from the start, not as an afterthought. This means understanding the minimum type sizes required for regulatory copy (typically 1.6mm x-height for EU food labeling), the placement requirements for specific panels, and the content requirements.

Regulatory copy that's been squeezed into leftover space at the end of the design process looks exactly like what it is. Designing with regulatory copy as a genuine element from the beginning produces layouts where required information is integrated into the design rather than appended to it.

## Front-of-Pack vs. Full Wrap

For packaged goods, the front of pack is the primary brand communication surface. The sides and back are functional: ingredient lists, nutrition panels, storage instructions, barcode.

I design the front of pack first and let it establish the visual language, then extend that language to the secondary surfaces in a way that allows the functional information to be clear without competing with the brand communication on the front.

## Heritage and Provenance

Many food and beverage brands compete on authenticity. "Small batch." "Family recipe." "Locally sourced." The visual design has to support these claims or contradict them.

A brand making claims of artisan provenance with a design that looks mass-produced is sending mixed signals. The design should reinforce the authenticity claim through its visual logic: slightly irregular geometry that suggests hand production, textures that reference natural or craft materials, typography that echoes historical print traditions.

The opposite failure: a genuinely artisan brand with an amateurish design that looks handmade in the wrong way. "Looks homemade" is not the same as "communicates craft." The design has to be executed with enough professionalism that the authenticity reads as quality rather than limitation.
