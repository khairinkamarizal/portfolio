## Concept Approval Is Not the Finish Line

When a client approves a logo concept, the concept phase is done. The refinement phase is just beginning. The gap between an approved concept and a fully resolved, deliverable mark is where a significant portion of the actual craft work happens — and where designers who rush to delivery shortchange both the client and themselves.

Refinement is the work of taking a mark that communicates the right idea and making it communicate that idea as precisely and completely as possible.

## What Refinement Actually Involves

**Geometric precision**: a concept sketch or initial vector might have curves and angles that are approximately right. Refinement means making them exactly right — tangent curves, precise angles, mathematically correct proportions. This work is often invisible in the final mark but is immediately visible in poorly refined marks when they're used at large scale.

**Optical correction**: after geometric precision, the optical corrections that make the mark look right even when it's geometrically precise. Overshoot on curved forms, adjusted stroke weights on horizontal vs. vertical elements, spacing that looks even rather than measures even.

**Weight and proportion calibration**: the relative weights of different elements — mark to wordmark, thick strokes to thin strokes, mark height to overall lockup height — all need to be evaluated at multiple scales and adjusted until they feel right across the range.

**Spacing and clear space**: the internal spacing of the mark and the defined clear space around it. Clear space rules are set in this phase, based on what the mark actually needs to breathe rather than what seems abstractly appropriate.

**Color refinement**: the brand colors as specified may need slight adjustment based on how they interact with the mark's form. A color that seemed right as an isolated swatch may need calibration when applied to the specific forms of the mark.

## Testing During Refinement

Refinement testing is more intensive than concept testing. I test the refining mark at:

- Actual output sizes (not just comfortable screen sizes)
- Single color in black and white
- Reversed out of dark backgrounds
- On the surfaces where it will most frequently appear
- In the lockup variations that will be delivered

Each test can reveal issues that aren't visible in the standard presentation view.

## When Refinement Is Done

Refinement is done when I can't find anything to improve. Not when I've addressed the feedback. Not when it looks good in the presentation file. When I've examined the mark at every scale and in every application context and everything is right.

This standard is harder to meet than it sounds. It's also what makes the difference between work that holds up over years of use and work that starts to look slightly off the moment it's seen in a new context.
