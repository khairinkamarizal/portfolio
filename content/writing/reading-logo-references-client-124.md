## References Are Evidence, Not Instructions

When a client sends reference logos during a project, I don't treat them as instructions — as if the client is saying "make something that looks like this." I treat them as evidence: data about what qualities the client responds to, what values they associate with certain visual forms, and sometimes what category conventions they're consciously or unconsciously absorbing.

Reading references well is one of the most useful skills in client work.

## What to Look For Across Multiple References

If a client sends six reference logos, I look for what they share. Not always in terms of style — though that's revealing — but in terms of underlying qualities. Are all six marks simple? Do they all use dark, muted colors? Do they all feel authoritative? Do they all have a certain precision to the geometry?

Those shared qualities are what the client is actually communicating. The specific style of any individual reference is less important than the qualities that appear consistently across all of them.

## When References Conflict

Sometimes clients send references that pull in different directions: a refined, high-end mark alongside something loud and energetic. This is useful information. It tells you they haven't resolved what they want, or that they're trying to hold two values simultaneously.

My job in that situation is to surface the tension explicitly. "I notice these references suggest different qualities — here's what I'm hearing. Can we talk about which direction is more aligned with how you want to position the brand?"

## What References Reveal About Category Conventions

Client references are often drawn from the same category as their business. A tech startup's references are often other tech startup logos. A law firm's references are other law firm logos. This tells you what visual language the client has absorbed as "looking like a [category] brand."

Whether you design within that language or deliberately break from it is a strategic decision. But you need to know what the category looks like before you can make that call.

## The Mood Beneath the Style

Some of the most useful information in a reference set is emotional rather than stylistic. References communicate the feeling the client wants to evoke — confident, approachable, playful, serious, innovative, established. These emotional qualities often transcend style.

I try to articulate the emotional quality of each reference and then look for patterns. Those patterns usually point directly to what the mark needs to feel like, even if the form that achieves that feeling is very different from any specific reference.

## References I Collect Myself

I also build my own reference set at the start of every project — drawn from competitive research and from deeper design history. These references are for me, not the client. They show me the range of what's been done in the space and open up formal possibilities the client may not have considered.

Good reference work is the foundation of good logo work.
