## The Pattern as Background Character

In a well-developed brand identity, a custom pattern serves as a secondary graphic element that adds visual richness without competing with primary brand elements. It lives on backgrounds, packaging, envelopes, retail environments — places where a flat color would be bland but a mark or type would be overcrowded.

Patterns extend the identity into surfaces and scales that other brand elements don't naturally occupy.

## Deriving the Pattern from the Brand

The strongest brand patterns have a clear conceptual relationship to the primary identity. The pattern might be derived from:

- The geometric construction of the logo mark
- A form or motif connected to the brand's subject matter
- A structural principle from the brand's conceptual foundation
- The negative space of a typographic or graphic element

Patterns that are conceptually grounded feel like parts of the identity system rather than decorative additions. Patterns that are chosen for aesthetic reasons alone can look like they belong to a different brand.

## Technical Construction

I construct all brand patterns in vector, with repeat tile documented precisely. The repeat unit needs to be specified clearly — its dimensions, its alignment, its construction logic — so anyone who receives the pattern file can work with it correctly.

For brand patterns that will be used across digital and print contexts, I test the tile at multiple scales: close-up where individual elements read clearly, medium distance where the tile repeat is visible, and far distance where the overall texture is the perception. The pattern needs to work at all three.

## Common Structural Approaches

A few structural approaches I return to regularly:

**Half-drop repeat**: the tile drops half its height on each horizontal repeat. Creates diagonal visual movement and a less grid-like feel than a straight repeat.

**Brick repeat**: similar to half-drop but aligned to horizontal rows. Feels more structured and architectural.

**Rotation and reflection**: a single motif reflected and rotated to create a symmetrical pattern with visual complexity from a simple starting element.

**Irregular scatter**: elements placed in an apparently random distribution that actually follows controlled rules about density and proximity. Harder to construct but feels more organic.

## Density and Color in Patterns

Pattern density significantly affects how a surface feels. Dense patterns feel rich and material-like; sparse patterns feel airy and modern. The right density depends on how the pattern will be used and what surrounding elements it will be adjacent to.

Color in patterns is usually constrained to two to three brand colors. More colors create visual chaos at pattern scale; single-color patterns often work better than expected, especially on colored backgrounds where the contrast creates a tonal relationship.
