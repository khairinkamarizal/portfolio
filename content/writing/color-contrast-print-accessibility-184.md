## Contrast Is a Print Issue Too

Accessibility contrast requirements are most commonly discussed in the context of digital design, where tools like contrast checkers and WCAG guidelines are readily available. Print designers sometimes assume accessibility is someone else's concern.

It isn't. Print materials are read by people with low vision, color vision deficiencies, and other visual impairments. Designing for legibility and contrast in print is an accessibility obligation, not just a best practice.

## Color Blindness in Print

Approximately 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of color vision deficiency. The most common form — red-green color blindness — makes it difficult or impossible to distinguish red and green hues from each other.

A print design that uses only red and green to communicate different categories of information is inaccessible to a significant portion of its audience. The fix isn't necessarily removing the colors — it's adding a second differentiator (shape, pattern, text label) so that color isn't the only way to extract the information.

## Luminosity Contrast and Text

The legibility of text on colored or dark backgrounds depends on luminosity contrast — the difference in perceived brightness between the text and its background. Dark text on a light background or light text on a dark background creates sufficient contrast. Medium-toned text on a medium-toned background doesn't.

I evaluate contrast ratios on colored backgrounds before finalizing any text-heavy print design. The WCAG 4.5:1 ratio for normal text and 3:1 for large text are reasonable targets for print as well as digital.

## Paper Stock and Contrast

The paper stock affects the effective contrast of any printed design. Uncoated paper absorbs ink more, reducing the effective contrast of dark-on-light type. Toned paper — cream, off-white, natural — reduces the apparent brightness of what reads as white space, affecting contrast relationships throughout the design.

I always account for the actual paper stock when evaluating contrast. A design that has sufficient contrast on coated white paper may not on uncoated natural.

## Type Size as Accessibility

Beyond contrast, type size is the other primary accessibility variable in print. The minimum comfortable reading size for body text in most print contexts is 9-10pt for adults with good vision. For materials intended for older audiences or anyone with visual impairment, 12pt or larger is a much safer baseline.

I push back on layouts that demand undersized type to fit more content. Prioritizing content density over legibility is a false economy — content that can't be read doesn't serve anyone.
