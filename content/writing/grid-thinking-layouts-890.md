## Grids Are Not Always Visible

When most designers think of a grid, they think of visible column structure — the kind of layout where the grid is apparent to any trained eye. But grid thinking is more fundamental than visible structure. It's a way of relating elements to each other through shared spatial logic, even when that logic produces layouts that look organic or free.

The best expressive layouts aren't free of spatial logic — they're built on spatial logic that's been intelligently violated.

## The Hidden Grid

Every well-composed layout, regardless of how organic it appears, has underlying spatial relationships that give it coherence. Elements share alignment axes that aren't explicitly drawn. Spacing intervals repeat in ratios that feel natural. Margins relate proportionally to the content area.

When I look at a layout that seems effortless and free, I try to identify its hidden grid — the spatial relationships that make it cohere. This practice reveals that very few truly excellent compositions are spatially arbitrary.

## Alignment Without Columns

One of the most useful grid-thinking tools for free-form layouts is deliberate alignment axis creation. Rather than pre-defining columns, I let the most important element establish alignment axes and then relate all other elements to those axes.

A headline placed at a specific left edge creates an alignment axis. Everything I want to associate with that headline should share that edge or relate to it in a deliberate way. Elements that don't share the axis read as belonging to a different group.

## Interval Repetition

Spatial interval — the distance between elements — creates rhythm when it repeats. In a free-form layout, I identify the primary spacing interval (often derived from the leading of the body text or the size of the primary element) and use multiples and fractions of that interval throughout the layout.

This doesn't produce a visible grid, but it produces a layout where the spacing feels internally consistent rather than arbitrary. The viewer can't identify why the spacing feels right — it just does.

## The Organizational Test

The test for whether a layout has sufficient spatial logic: can I explain every significant positioning decision in terms of its relationship to another element or a shared alignment principle?

If I can, the layout has structural integrity even if it looks free and expressive. If some decisions are genuinely arbitrary — positioned by feel without reference to the other elements — those are the decisions most likely to create visual noise rather than visual interest.

Grid thinking is not about making grid-looking work. It's about making work where spatial decisions are intentional.
