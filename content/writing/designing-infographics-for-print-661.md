Infographic design has a reputation problem. A decade of digital content marketing produced an enormous amount of infographic work that prioritized visual complexity over communicative clarity — overdesigned, cluttered with icons and gradients and decorative elements that added visual noise without adding information.

The infographics I produce for print aim for the opposite: maximum clarity through structural discipline, with visual design that supports rather than competes with the information.

## Structure Is the Design

The first task in infographic design is understanding the information completely. Not the visual treatment — the information. What is the data or narrative trying to communicate? What is the core argument or insight? What are the relationships between data points?

Only after I understand the information can I design the structure that makes it clear. The structure comes from the information's own logic, not from an aesthetic preference for a certain infographic format.

A timeline communicates sequence and duration. A comparison chart communicates relative magnitude. A flow diagram communicates process and causation. A map communicates geographic distribution. Using the right structure for the information is the first design decision and the most important one.

## The Hierarchy of Information

Not all information in an infographic is equally important. The headline insight — the thing the viewer should take away if they see nothing else — should be visually dominant. Supporting data that contextualizes the headline insight is secondary. Methodology and source notes are tertiary.

I design infographics in layers, starting with the headline insight and building supporting structure around it. The composition should be readable at three levels: the quick read (headline and main visual), the engaged read (supporting data and relationships), and the deep read (detail and methodology).

## Limiting Complexity

The most common infographic mistake is trying to communicate too much. When every data point is given equal visual weight, the viewer can't distinguish what's important, and the piece requires too much effort to parse.

I try to limit each infographic to one or two core insights. If there are more insights worth communicating, they belong in separate infographics rather than crammed into one.

This constraint is often resisted by clients who feel that their data is all important. My argument: a piece that communicates one thing clearly is more valuable than a piece that attempts five things and communicates none of them clearly.

## Typography in Infographics

Infographic typography has specific requirements that differ from other print contexts. Labels and annotations need to be legible at small sizes while remaining clearly secondary to the data they describe. Data values need to be easily scannable — usually set in tabular-figure numerals that align on decimal points.

I use a consistent type hierarchy throughout the infographic: display text for the headline insight, subhead treatment for section labels, small caps or a secondary face for axis labels and annotations, and a distinct treatment for source notes and methodology.

## Color as Information

Color in infographics should carry meaning, not just aesthetics. I assign colors deliberately: one color per data category in comparison charts, consistent use of a specific color to highlight the key data point, neutral colors for supporting data that provides context rather than carrying the main argument.

Using brand colors in infographics requires care. If the brand has three colors, using all three in a comparison chart implies the chart has three categories. Using brand colors decoratively rather than informationally confuses the color vocabulary.
