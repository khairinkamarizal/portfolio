## Posters Are Read in Layers

The first thing to understand about poster design is that posters are not read linearly, the way a page of text is. They're scanned. A viewer's eye moves to the most visually dominant element first, then follows a path determined by size, contrast, and position.

Designing a poster means designing that path deliberately. What does the eye hit first? What comes second? What's the last thing they read before they look away?

If you can answer those three questions confidently about your poster, you probably have a working hierarchy. If you can't, the poster isn't finished.

## The Three Zones

I think about poster layouts in terms of three informational zones:

**The hook**: the element that captures attention at distance. Usually the largest typographic element or the most visually striking image. It doesn't need to communicate everything — it needs to earn a closer look.

**The substance**: the key information delivered once someone has engaged. Event name, date, headline concept. This is where most of the communication happens.

**The fine print**: supporting details, credits, sponsors, URL, QR code. This zone exists for people who've committed to reading the whole thing.

Each zone gets a different visual weight, and the contrast between zones creates the hierarchy. When everything in a poster has the same visual weight, nothing gets read.

## Image and Type Relationships

The relationship between image and type is where most poster design happens. The options:

**Type over image**: works when the image has tonal areas that allow legible type. I always check type legibility against the actual image, not in a vacuum.

**Type and image as equals**: both occupy defined areas of the composition. The challenge is making the boundary between them feel intentional rather than arbitrary.

**Type as image**: the type itself carries the full visual weight. No photography. This demands both excellent type choices and confident layout.

**Type and image unified**: the most ambitious approach — image and type interact, overlap, and support each other in ways that make separating them feel wrong. When this works, it's usually the most memorable poster in the room.

## Negative Space Is Not Wasted Space

I keep having to defend generous negative space to clients who feel the poster has too much white. The argument I make: space is what makes the elements that are there feel important. Crowded posters flatten hierarchy. Space creates emphasis.

The best poster designers use negative space aggressively — not because they ran out of content, but because they understand that what's absent is doing work.

## Printing Isn't the End

A poster isn't finished when the file is sent to print. It's finished when it's on the wall. I try to see my printed poster work hung in actual environments — taped to a lamp post, pinned in a gallery, stapled to a bulletin board. The difference between a poster on screen and a poster in the world is real, and closing that gap requires actually going out and looking.
