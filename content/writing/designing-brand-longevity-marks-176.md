## The Durability Problem

A logo is expected to last. The investment in building brand recognition through consistent use of a mark is only worthwhile if the mark can be used consistently over time. A mark that needs to be replaced every five years because it feels dated undermines that investment.

Designing for longevity is a specific design discipline, distinct from designing something that looks good today.

## What Makes Marks Date

Marks date for identifiable reasons. They reflect a specific aesthetic era — the visual style of a particular decade is embedded in their form. They use graphic effects that are tied to a specific moment in technology (early drop shadows, bevels and glows, the specific gradient style of the early 2000s). They follow a trend that's visible in dozens of contemporaneous marks.

Avoiding these pitfalls requires being able to see the era-specific qualities of work made today — which is genuinely hard, because the present is invisible to people inside it.

## Formal Simplicity as Durability

The most durable marks tend to be formally simple. Not boring — simple. The Nike swoosh. The Apple apple. The Mercedes star. These are not elaborate marks. Their simplicity means there's nothing in them that's tied to a particular era's graphic sensibility.

Elaborate marks with many elements have many opportunities to look dated. Simple marks have few. This is a practical argument for simplicity beyond the aesthetic one.

## Concept Over Style

Marks built on a strong concept — an idea that's genuinely connected to the brand's identity — tend to outlast marks built on a strong style. The concept ages better because it's not contingent on aesthetic fashion.

A mark that encodes something true about the brand will still be encoding that truth in twenty years. A mark that just looks contemporary in its year of design will look dated in ten.

## Testing for Durability

I try to test marks for durability by asking whether they look like they could have been designed at any point in the last two decades. If a mark looks emphatically new — if it's clearly of-the-moment — it's also susceptible to looking clearly dated when that moment passes.

This doesn't mean avoiding all contemporary influence. It means keeping the core form independent of fashion while the surrounding identity system absorbs the contemporary expression.

## The Client Conversation

Durability is a selling point that clients respond to when it's framed correctly. "This mark is designed to last twenty years, not to follow this year's trend" is a positioning that a brand steward can understand and value.

Frame durability as a strategic benefit. It is one.
