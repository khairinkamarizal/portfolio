## Two Kinds of Black

In CMYK printing, you can print black two ways. K-only black is exactly what it sounds like: only the black (K) plate is used. Rich black adds percentages of cyan, magenta, and yellow to the black plate, creating a deeper, more saturated-looking black.

Both are valid. They're appropriate in different situations. Confusing them creates production problems.

## What K-Only Black Looks Like

K-only black at 100% is a solid, flat, medium-dark black. It lacks the depth and richness of a rich black but it has significant advantages: it prints cleanly on a single plate with no registration issues, it can be used for small text without color fringe artifacts, and it's consistent across different printing conditions.

For body text, small type, fine lines, and thin rules, K-only black is the correct choice. The registration requirements of multi-plate printing make rich black unusable at small sizes — even tiny misregistration creates visible color fringing.

## What Rich Black Looks Like

Rich black — a common formula is 60C/40M/40Y/100K, though formulas vary — has significantly more depth and density than K-only. On coated paper, it produces a luxurious, deep black that looks dramatically better than K-only at large scales.

Rich black is appropriate for large areas of solid black: backgrounds, oversized display type, large graphic shapes. At these scales, the multi-plate printing is manageable and the depth difference is clearly visible.

## The Registration Problem

Rich black requires precise registration of four color plates. If any plate shifts even slightly — which happens in the course of a normal print run — the edges of rich black elements will show color fringing. On body text at 10pt, this is catastrophic to readability. On a large background field, it's invisible.

This is why the rule is: K-only for small, rich black for large. Not aesthetic preference — technical requirement.

## Maximum Ink Coverage

Rich black formulas that add up to more than 240-280% total ink coverage can cause print problems: slow drying, set-off, surface texture issues. I use rich black formulas that keep total ink coverage within the printer's specified maximum.

I always check with the print shop what their maximum total ink density is and formulate rich black accordingly.

## Setting Up Files Correctly

In design software, I set separate swatches for K-only black and rich black, both named clearly. All body text and small graphic elements use K-only. Large background elements use rich black. Applying the wrong black is a common production error that costs money to fix at proof stage — or worse, after a press run.

Naming your black swatches clearly saves everyone from mistakes.
