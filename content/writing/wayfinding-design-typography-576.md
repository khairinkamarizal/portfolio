## Design You Navigate Through

Wayfinding design is unusual in graphic design because the viewer is moving. They're walking, often quickly, sometimes stressed, carrying things. They glance at signs for fractions of a second. The design has to work under those conditions.

This changes almost everything: the scale of type, the simplicity of iconography, the contrast of color, the distance at which elements must be legible. Wayfinding design is not poster design or branding translated to physical space — it's a distinct discipline with its own requirements.

## Hierarchy in Physical Space

In a wayfinding system, hierarchy isn't just visual — it's spatial and temporal. The viewer encounters different levels of information at different distances and at different stages of their journey.

**Primary wayfinding** — the big signs that orient travelers from a distance. Are you in the right building? Which direction to the main areas? These need to work from 15 meters or more.

**Secondary wayfinding** — intermediate decision points. Turn left or right at this junction? Which floor? These work at 5-10 meters.

**Tertiary wayfinding** — final identification. Room numbers, specific names, door labels. These work at close range.

Each level has different scale requirements, different typographic needs, different contrast standards. Designing a wayfinding system means designing all three levels as a coordinated whole.

## Type at Wayfinding Scale

Type for large-format wayfinding needs different properties than print or screen typography. The most important: strong differentiation between similar letterforms at reading distance.

Typefaces that work well in wayfinding tend to be: open apertures (the openings in letters like a, e, c), generous x-heights, clear differentiation between characters that can be confused (I, l, 1; O, 0; c, e). Humanist sans-serifs are generally the best category for wayfinding because these properties are built into their design.

Letter spacing (tracking) in wayfinding text is typically set wider than print defaults — this improves legibility at distance and in motion.

## Color Coding Systems

Color coding in wayfinding systems needs to be robust enough to work under variable lighting conditions and for viewers with color vision deficiency.

I design color coding with pattern or shape reinforcement: never color alone. A colored band plus a distinctive symbol ensures the coding works for colorblind viewers.

Contrast between text and background must be very high in wayfinding — at least 7:1, well above WCAG AA standards for digital screens, because the physical conditions (glare, variable light, distance) reduce effective contrast.

## The Motion Test

I test wayfinding designs by simulating the user's motion. I walk toward the design from the relevant decision point distance, at a walking pace. Can I identify the primary message before I would need to act on it? If not, the scale or contrast needs adjustment.

This test reveals problems that static evaluation never catches. A sign that looks clear when you're standing next to it during design review can be completely unreadable at the distance where a visitor actually needs to read it.

Design for the motion. Test at scale. Get out of your chair.
