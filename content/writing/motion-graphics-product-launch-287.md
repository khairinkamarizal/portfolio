Product launch motion graphics are among the highest-stakes deliverables in the discipline. The launch is a single event — often live, often webcast, sometimes in front of thousands of people — and the motion work needs to perform perfectly the first time with no opportunity for revision based on actual performance.

I've worked on motion packages for several significant product launches. The process and discipline for this work is distinct from regular motion design projects.

## The Live Event Constraint

In a product launch context, everything runs live or to a fixed timeline. The video plays at a specific moment and the audience experiences it in real time. There are no second chances. This fundamentally changes how I approach quality assurance and delivery.

Every transition must be seamless. Every loop must be invisible. Every file must play back without glitch on the specific hardware it will run on. Accepting any assumption about playback quality without verifying it on the actual system is professional negligence.

I insist on a technical rehearsal — running the complete motion package on the actual event hardware in the actual venue — before the event. Problems found in rehearsal can be fixed. Problems found during the live event cannot.

## The Emotional Arc

A product launch has a narrative arc: anticipation, revelation, specification, call to action. The motion graphics reinforce this arc emotionally.

Pre-reveal graphics build tension and anticipation. The reveal moment is the climax — it needs to earn the buildup. Post-reveal graphics settle into explanation and detail. Closing graphics drive action.

I map the motion graphics to this emotional arc in the brief stage, ensuring that the motion treatment at each stage of the launch serves the narrative purpose of that moment.

## The Hero Animation

Every product launch needs a hero animation — the primary piece of motion work that defines the visual character of the entire launch. This is typically the product reveal itself: the moment where the product appears for the first time.

The hero animation gets the most development time and the most iteration. It needs to be genuinely excellent — not just technically competent, but emotionally compelling. The product reveal is the moment the audience has been waiting for; the animation should honor that moment.

## Consistency Across the Full Package

A launch motion package includes many elements beyond the hero: title cards, speaker lower thirds, section transitions, background loops, social media cutdowns. These all need to share a visual and motion language while serving their different specific functions.

I develop the system from the hero animation outward. The motion language, color palette, and typographic treatment established in the hero become the system that all other elements draw from.

## File Redundancy

For live events, file redundancy is professional practice. Every motion element is delivered as multiple file types (H.264, ProRes, potentially PNG sequences for critical elements), on multiple storage media, with backup hardware available.

The cost of a technical failure during a product launch — in brand damage, in opportunity cost, in reputation — is vastly greater than the cost of prepared redundancy. Prepare for failure so you can prevent it.

## Post-Launch Social Cutdowns

Product launch motion work doesn't end at the event. Social media cutdowns — optimized versions for Instagram, YouTube, Twitter, and other platforms — extend the launch's reach and life.

I plan these cutdowns at the outset of a launch motion project, ensuring that the motion work is produced at quality levels and in configurations that will allow efficient cutdown production. Planning for cutdowns in advance produces better cutdowns and avoids retrofitting work that wasn't designed for it.
