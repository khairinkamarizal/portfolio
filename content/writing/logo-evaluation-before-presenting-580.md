## The Problem of Proximity

After hours of working on a logo concept, I've lost my objective eye. The decisions I've made look right because I made them — not because they are right. Familiarity has replaced judgment.

The evaluation process I run before any client presentation is designed to break that familiarity and see the work as a stranger would.

## The Fresh Eyes Test

I step away from the work for at least an hour — longer if the deadline allows. Then I come back and look at the mark without opening the file: I view it as a screenshot, printed out, or on a different device. The physical distance from the production environment helps.

I ask myself: if I saw this for the first time, what would I think? What does the mark make me feel? What does it communicate before I read any supporting context?

If the answers don't match the brief, something needs to change.

## The Seven Evaluation Criteria

I evaluate every logo concept against seven criteria before presenting:

**1. Distinctiveness** — Is this mark unique? Could I mistake it for a mark in a different category or, worse, in the same category? I do a quick competitive scan to make sure there are no obvious similarities.

**2. Scalability** — Does it work at 32px and at 32 inches? I check both extremes.

**3. One-color performance** — Does it work as a flat black mark? As reversed white on black?

**4. Hierarchy** — If it's a combination mark, do the elements have clear visual hierarchy? Does the eye know where to enter?

**5. Optical balance** — Does the mark feel balanced, or does one area feel heavier than intended? I check both at full size and small.

**6. Geometric integrity** — Are the underlying proportions intentional? Are curves smooth? Are angles consistent?

**7. Brand fit** — Does this mark feel like it belongs to the brand I was briefed on? Does it communicate the right personality to the right audience?

Any criterion that produces doubt gets addressed before the presentation. Not after.

## The Stranger Test

I show the mark to someone who has no context for the project. I don't tell them what it's for or what it means. I ask: what do you think this brand does? What does it feel like?

Their answer reveals what the mark is actually communicating versus what I intend it to communicate. If the gap is large, I have work to do.

## The Time-Away Test

For any logo I feel uncertain about, I leave it overnight. Fresh eyes in the morning reveal things that extended working sessions conceal. What feels like a finished mark at 11pm sometimes looks like an unresolved sketch at 8am.

This isn't indecisiveness. It's an evaluation technique that respects the reality of how judgment works over time.

## What I Do With the Results

If the evaluation surfaces problems, I fix them before presenting. Not during the presentation, not after. The client presentation is not a critique session — it's a presentation of resolved work.

If the evaluation produces doubt that I can't resolve before the deadline, I present the concept with honesty about what's still developing. That's better than false confidence in work that isn't ready.

Present what you can stand behind.
