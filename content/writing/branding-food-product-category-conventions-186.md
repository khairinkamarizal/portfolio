## The Food Category Problem

Food branding is one of the most codified sectors in visual identity work. Every sub-category — craft beer, artisanal snacks, premium condiments, children's food, health supplements — has its own visual conventions so established that buyers use them to navigate grocery shelves.

The conventions exist because they've worked. The challenge for a new brand is deciding how to position within that system: align with conventions and claim category membership, or break from them and claim differentiation.

## Reading the Competitive Shelf

Before designing any food brand identity, I spend time on the actual category shelf — in stores, not just in research documents. I look at how the shelf reads as a whole, what visual patterns dominate, where there are gaps, and what a new brand needs to do to stand out while being appropriately categorized.

The shelf is the competitive context. Any design decision needs to be evaluated in that context.

## Craft Signals and Their Erosion

The craft food movement established a recognizable vocabulary: aged illustration, kraft paper textures, hand-drawn type, muted earthy palettes, vintage-style label formats. These signals worked brilliantly when they were differentiated. Now they're everywhere.

A brand entering a category saturated with pseudo-craft aesthetics faces a choice: authentically use those conventions (which requires genuine craft credentials), modernize them (keeping some signals while updating the execution), or abandon them entirely for something different.

## Premium vs. Accessible

The visual language of premium food brands and accessible food brands are distinct but often confused by brands trying to do both. Premium signals include: generous white space, high-contrast type, photography with significant craft investment, restrained color, material quality. Accessible signals include: warmth, approachability, saturated color, energetic layout, visible personality.

Trying to communicate both simultaneously usually results in communicating neither clearly.

## Typography on Packaging

Food packaging typography needs to do more work than typography in most other design contexts. It's viewed at various distances, under variable lighting, while the viewer is in motion. The typographic hierarchy needs to be extremely clear and the primary type elements need to hold up at reading distance from 60cm.

I also pay particular attention to the relationship between the brand name type and the product descriptor type. These two elements need to be visually distinct enough to read separately but harmonious enough to feel designed together.

## Regulatory Text as Design

Every food product carries required regulatory information: ingredients, nutrition facts, certifications, allergen information. This text is often treated as a production afterthought — crammed in wherever there's space.

Good food packaging design treats regulatory text as a designed element. It gets an appropriate type size, clear organization, and a considered location in the layout hierarchy. The regulatory text is part of the design, not an intrusion on it.
