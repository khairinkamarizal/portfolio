## Campaigns vs. Core Identity

A brand's core identity is built for consistency and longevity. A campaign has different requirements: it needs to feel fresh, timely, and distinct from the everyday brand expression. The tension between consistency and freshness is the central challenge of campaign typography.

The answer is not to abandon the type system. It's to push it.

## What Pushing the System Looks Like

Pushing a type system in campaign work might mean: using typefaces from the brand system at scales or weights that aren't part of the everyday application, introducing tighter or looser tracking than the standard, using display faces in ways that are more expressive than normal use, or applying typographic treatments (reversed out on color, overlapping, overscaled) that are more dramatic than the core identity typically employs.

The key is that the campaign work is clearly operating within the same type system — using the same typefaces, the same fundamental character — but with more intensity.

## When to Bring In a Campaign Typeface

Some campaigns benefit from a typeface not in the core brand system: a display face used only for the campaign, adding a distinctive voice to the campaign period without permanently altering the brand.

This works when: the campaign typeface is clearly subordinate to the brand's primary type (it doesn't compete with the brand's voice, it amplifies it), it has a specific campaign life after which it's retired, and it's used in ways that don't conflict with the core identity's typographic conventions.

## Maintaining Brand DNA in Campaign Type

Even at its most expressive, campaign typography should be recognizable as coming from the same brand. This means maintaining the proportional logic of the type system, the color relationships, and the overall aesthetic character — even when individual elements are pushed to their limits.

If a viewer can't tell which brand a campaign belongs to from the typography alone, the campaign has gone too far from the brand's identity.

## The Post-Campaign Return

One practical consideration often overlooked: the brand will exist after the campaign ends. Campaign typography that's too distinctive can make the return to the core identity feel like a step backward.

I think about the transition in both directions — from core identity into campaign, and from campaign back to core — when making campaign type decisions. The campaign should feel like an excursion, not a departure.
