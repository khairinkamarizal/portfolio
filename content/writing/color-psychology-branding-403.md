There's a genre of design content I've grown tired of: the color psychology infographic. You know the one. Red means passion and urgency. Blue means trust. Green means nature or money. Yellow means optimism. These claims get passed around as if they're laws of perception, and every year a new crop of designers builds brand color systems on top of them.

The reality is messier and more interesting than that.

## What Color Actually Does

Color communicates, but not in a fixed dictionary. What a color means depends heavily on context: the industry, the competitive landscape, the cultural background of the audience, and — critically — what every other color in the composition is doing.

Tiffany blue means luxury and precision because Tiffany has spent 180 years building that association. Take that exact same color and put it on a budget fast food chain and it means something entirely different. The color didn't change. The context did.

This is the first thing I tell clients who come to me with "we need blue because blue means trust." Blue is the single most used color in corporate branding precisely because so many brands think it means trust. In a crowded category, defaulting to blue might mean your brand disappears into the noise. Sometimes the more trustworthy move is to avoid blue entirely.

## Where Color Psychology Holds Up

That said, color perception isn't purely arbitrary. There are some tendencies that hold across many contexts:

**Warm colors (reds, oranges, warm yellows)** tend to feel activating. They pull attention. In food branding, they're common because they're associated with appetite and warmth. This is real.

**Cool colors (blues, blue-greens)** tend to feel calming, stable, receding. There's a reason hospital environments and financial services lean into them. This is real.

**High saturation** reads as energetic, sometimes aggressive or cheap depending on context. **Desaturated or muted tones** tend to read as sophisticated, restrained, or melancholy.

But "tends to feel" is doing a lot of work in those sentences. These are tendencies, not rules. They bend based on cultural context, combination, and application.

## How I Actually Build Brand Color Systems

When I'm developing a color palette for a brand identity, I start with competitive mapping. I want to know what every major competitor is using. I'm looking for the dominant color logic in the category — and I'm looking for where there's space to differentiate.

From there I think about brand personality. Not "what does this color mean" but "what does this color feel like in this context, for this audience, in this category." Those are different questions.

I build palettes with hierarchy in mind: one primary brand color, one or two secondary colors that can carry different content registers, and a set of neutrals that do the heavy lifting in everyday application. The primary color doesn't have to do everything — it just has to be ownable and appropriate.

## The Ownable Color Conversation

One of the most valuable things a brand color can be is ownable in its category. Hermès orange. Caterpillar yellow. Those brands aren't using those colors because of what they symbolize generically — they're using them because no one else in their space is, and over time the color has become an asset.

When I'm advising on color direction, I push for specificity. Not just "green" but which green, at what saturation and lightness, in what combination. The difference between a forest green and a neon lime is enormous, even though both are technically green. Precise color selection matters.

## The Bottom Line

Color is a real tool with real effects. But treat it as a language with rules, not as a cheat code. And always ask: what does this color say in this specific context, for this specific audience, next to everything else this brand does? That's the question that leads to good decisions.
