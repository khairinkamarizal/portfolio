I've audited brand systems for companies with 14 approved brand colors. Fourteen. Every single piece of collateral looked different because when people have 14 options, they make 14 different decisions. A palette is only as useful as it is constraining.

## The Problem With "More Options"

Clients often ask for a richer palette because they want flexibility. More colors means more moods, more contexts, more possibilities. This sounds reasonable until you watch it in practice.

A marketing coordinator building an event flyer doesn't choose from 14 colors systematically. They choose the colors they like best that day, or the ones they used last time, or whatever feels festive. The result is brand erosion through a thousand small autonomous decisions, none of which seemed wrong at the time.

The brands I admire most have ruthless palette discipline. One or two primary brand colors. Maybe a neutral. A small set of functional secondary colors for specific use cases. That's it.

## How I Build a Brand Palette

My starting point is almost always a single hero color — the color that is the brand. Everything else is in service of that hero.

I ask: what does this color need to work against? White backgrounds for print, likely. Dark backgrounds for digital. Photographic imagery. Other brand colors in the real world (competitor shelf presence, physical environment). The hero color has to hold its own in all of those contexts.

Secondary colors come from relationships to the hero. A complementary accent for moments that need energy. A neutral — warm or cool depending on the hero — for body copy, backgrounds, space. The secondary colors support the hero; they don't compete with it.

## Specifying Color Correctly

Bad color specifications cause enormous problems in production. I always specify:

- Pantone (PMS) for single-color or spot-color print work
- CMYK for full-color print — built from the Pantone if possible, not converted naively
- RGB for digital display contexts
- HEX for web
- A note on what to do when the brand color can't be matched exactly — is it better to go darker or lighter?

That last point sounds minor. It's not. When a printer comes back and says they can't hit the Pantone exactly, your client needs to know the answer before it's a crisis.

## The Neutral Is Not an Afterthought

I've seen countless brand systems where the neutral was clearly added as an afterthought — some shade of gray that doesn't actually relate to the primary palette. A well-chosen neutral is one of the hardest and most important decisions in palette building.

Warm brands need warm neutrals. Cool, minimal brands need cool neutrals. The neutral carries a huge portion of the visual weight of any brand because it's everywhere — backgrounds, text, structural elements. Get it wrong and every piece of collateral will feel slightly off, and no one will be able to tell you why.

## Restraint Is a Feature

When I hand a client a palette of four or five colors with clear usage rules, they sometimes feel shortchanged. They wanted more. But six months later, when every piece of collateral looks like it came from the same place, the restraint is the thing that's working.
