A rebrand that launches beautifully and then falls apart in execution has failed. I've seen this happen when studios focus entirely on the design deliverables and treat rollout as someone else's problem. It isn't. The transition from old brand to new is a design problem.

## Defining the Transition Strategy

There are three approaches to brand transition: hard cutover (everything changes at once), phased rollout (categories of materials transition on a schedule), and gradual drift (materials transition as they're reprinted or refreshed). Each has contexts where it's appropriate.

Hard cutover works for high-profile rebrand moments where the announcement itself is a story — a name change, a major strategic pivot, a merger launch. It requires significant upfront investment and logistical coordination but creates a clean break.

Phased rollout works when the organization has a complex materials landscape and can't change everything simultaneously. I help clients create a priority tiering: what must change immediately (digital presence, core collateral), what can wait (interior signage, branded merchandise), what changes on natural replacement cycles (printed materials, vehicle fleets).

Gradual drift is almost always the wrong choice. It creates a prolonged period of brand inconsistency that confuses audiences and signals organizational disorganization.

## Co-Existence Standards

During a phased rollout, old and new brand materials will coexist. I build co-existence standards into every phased rebrand — rules that govern how the old and new identities are each used to minimize confusion during the transition period.

Co-existence standards typically specify: the new brand applies to all new production immediately, the old brand is retired from customer-facing digital contexts first, internal materials can transition on the phased schedule.

## Stakeholder Communication

Employees are brand ambassadors. A rebrand that confuses internal stakeholders produces confused external communications. I advise clients to brief employees before the external launch — explaining the reasoning for the change, the new brand values, and what the new identity represents.

This internal launch is often a missed opportunity. It's a moment to get the organization aligned around the new positioning. Done well, employees become advocates. Done poorly, or skipped, employees become a source of off-brand behavior.

## The Day-One Asset List

Every rebrand needs a day-one asset list: the minimum set of materials that must be ready at launch for the brand to present coherently. This typically includes the website, social media profiles, email signatures, business cards, and the primary piece of sales collateral.

Everything else can follow on the phased schedule. But the day-one list must be complete before launch. Launching with a new brand identity and an old website undermines the whole effort.
