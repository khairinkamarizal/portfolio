## Why geometry matters in logo marks

A logo mark is a shape that needs to work at the size of a favicon and the size of a building. It needs to reproduce in one color, embossed on leather, cut from vinyl, and animated on screen. The marks that do all of this reliably share a common quality: they're built on geometric logic that gives them structural integrity at any scale and in any medium.

Geometric construction isn't about making logo marks that look mathematical or cold. It's about building marks whose proportions are internally consistent — where the relationship between one curve and another, one stroke width and another, is governed by a underlying system rather than visual judgment alone.

## Starting with a grid

Every mark I build starts with a construction grid. The grid size depends on the complexity of the mark — simpler marks need less resolution, more complex ones need finer grids. My standard starting grid is 32×32 units, which gives enough granularity for detail work while forcing decisions about what actually needs to be detailed.

The discipline of working on-grid is valuable precisely because it's constraining. When I can only place anchor points at grid intersections, I'm forced to make structural decisions rather than fussing with curves. The mark becomes cleaner, simpler, and more reproducible.

## The golden ratio is often wrong

There's a tendency in logo design circles to reach for the golden ratio as a universal proportion system. I've used it, and it's sometimes useful. But it's not magic, and applying it mechanically produces marks that feel labored rather than elegant.

The proportional system that matters is internal consistency within the mark itself. If the stroke width of one element is 4 units, and a related element has a stroke width of 6 units, that 3:2 ratio should mean something — it should relate to the proportion of another element pair elsewhere in the mark. The mark should have its own logic that a viewer could in theory reverse-engineer, even if they never would.

## Circles and their power

Circles are the most common geometric primitive in logo mark design, and for good reason. They're universally readable, they reproduce reliably, and they carry associations of completeness and continuity that are useful for many brands.

When I use circles in a mark, I use them as construction scaffolding even when the final shape isn't circular. Three circles of different sizes, positioned in relation to each other, can generate a shape that feels hand-drawn in its complexity but is geometrically precise. This is how the marks that seem effortless are actually built.

## Knowing when to break the grid

The grid is a tool, not a law. After I have a solid geometric foundation, I make optical corrections — adjustments that break the strict geometry to produce something that looks correct to the eye rather than to a ruler. Optical corrections are almost always tiny: 1-2 unit adjustments to vertex positions, slight modifications to curve tension.

The goal is a mark that feels right, not one that plots correctly. The geometry gets me most of the way there. The optical corrections get me the rest.
