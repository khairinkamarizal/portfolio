Brand architecture — the structural relationship between a parent brand and its sub-brands, product lines, and offerings — is one of the least visible and most consequential aspects of brand work. When the architecture is wrong, no amount of visual refinement fixes the underlying problem.

## The Three Classic Architectures

The Branded House uses one master brand across all offerings. Virgin, Apple, and FedEx are examples. Every product or service leads with the master brand. The advantage is concentrated equity — every experience builds the same brand. The disadvantage is that a failure in one offering can contaminate the whole brand.

The House of Brands maintains distinct brands for each offering with the parent brand mostly invisible to consumers. P&G is the canonical example — most consumers know Tide, Gillette, and Pampers without knowing or caring that P&G makes all of them. The advantage is isolation between brands. The disadvantage is the cost of building each brand from scratch.

The Endorsed Brand architecture falls between: sub-brands have their own identities but are visibly connected to the parent. "A Google Company" or "A Marriott Property" are endorsements. The parent provides credibility; the sub-brand provides specificity.

## Signals That the Architecture Needs Work

I look for a few patterns that indicate architecture problems: customers are confused about the relationship between offerings, a strong sub-brand is cannibalizing the parent, marketing budgets are spread impossibly thin across too many distinct brands, or an acquisition has created a brand collision that nobody has resolved.

These aren't always identity problems — sometimes they're business strategy problems that manifest as identity problems. The identity work can't fix a strategy problem, but it can reveal one.

## The Audit Process

Before recommending any architecture change, I map the existing portfolio: every brand, sub-brand, product line, and offering, with their relationships to each other and to shared resources. This map usually reveals patterns that aren't obvious from inside the organization.

I also audit customer perception: how are the different brands understood? Are the distinctions meaningful to customers? Would consolidation lose meaning that customers actually use? Would separation create meaning that's currently muddied?

## The Cost of Change

Architecture changes are expensive. They're not just rebranding exercises — they involve restructuring how the organization talks about itself, how sales and marketing resources are allocated, and sometimes how products are positioned and priced.

I'm honest with clients about this cost. Architecture changes are worth it when the current structure is creating real problems. They're not worth it as aesthetic exercises.
