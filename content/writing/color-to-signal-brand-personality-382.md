I've seen brands torpedo their positioning with the wrong color choice. A premium brand that should own deep navy and gold choosing a bright, approachable orange because someone on the leadership team liked it. A youthful consumer brand picking a muted, corporate palette because they wanted to be "taken seriously." Color and positioning have to be aligned. When they're not, the brand argues with itself.

## Color Has Cultural Associations

Color associations aren't universal — they vary by culture — but within a specific market context, they're consistent enough to be strategic. In Western commercial contexts: blue signals trustworthiness and professionalism. Green signals health, nature, or finance. Red signals urgency, energy, or appetite. Black signals premium, authority, or sophistication. Yellow signals optimism and accessibility.

These are tendencies, not rules. And they're heavily mediated by specific hue, saturation, and value. A pale, warm blue is not the same signal as a deep navy. A neon yellow is not the same signal as a muted gold. The specific color matters enormously.

## Category Codes and When to Break Them

Every industry has a dominant color code — the color palette that most brands in that category use. Finance is blue. Health food is green and earth tones. Technology is blue and gray. Luxury is black, white, and metallics.

These codes exist because they've been built up over decades of use. Following them signals "we belong here." Breaking them signals "we're different."

Both are valid strategies. A new entrant in a blue-dominated finance space might choose red or yellow as a deliberate differentiator. A new premium brand in a casualwear category that normally uses bright colors might choose muted, desaturated tones to signal elevation.

The strategy depends on whether the brand wants to belong or to differentiate — and whether it has the resources and positioning to sustain the break from category convention.

## Warm vs. Cool: More Than Temperature

The warm/cool distinction does more strategic work than people realize. Warm colors advance — they feel closer, more energetic, more immediate. Cool colors recede — they feel more considered, more controlled, more distant.

For a brand that wants to feel urgent, accessible, and energetic, warmth serves it. For a brand that wants to feel calm, expert, and premium, cool serves it. This isn't about aesthetics. It's about the emotional relationship the brand wants with its audience.

## Testing Color in Context

I never evaluate a brand color on white. I evaluate it on every background it'll actually encounter: on the brand's primary packaging material, on the primary digital surface, on co-branded materials, in black-and-white reproduction. A color that looks perfect on white can look muddy on kraft paper or washed out on a screen with different white balance.

Real-world testing is the only test that matters.
