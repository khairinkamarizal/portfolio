Brand color specifications in guidelines are almost always given in CMYK, RGB, and Pantone values. These specifications cover printing and screen applications. But when a brand's colors need to appear in physical materials — architectural paint, upholstery fabric, powder-coated metal, anodized aluminum — the specification system breaks down entirely.

## Why Print Specs Don't Transfer

Pantone's color matching system was designed for printing inks on paper. The same Pantone number applied to an architectural paint mix or a fabric dye will not produce the same color. Different materials absorb, reflect, and transmit light differently. The surface texture of matte paint is completely different from the surface texture of coated paper. The dye chemistry of fabric produces color through a different physical process than printing ink.

This isn't a problem that better specifications can solve — it's a fundamental property of how color works in different materials. Managing brand color across materials requires material-specific specification, not a single universal value.

## The Material Specification Process

For each material category where the brand color needs to appear, I identify the appropriate color system. Architectural paint uses NCS (Natural Color System), RAL, or manufacturer-specific matching systems. Powder coating uses RAL or Pantone Industrial. Fabric uses thread color systems like Pantone Fashion Home + Interiors. Plastic uses manufacturer-specific color libraries.

For each material, I work with suppliers to find the closest available match to the brand color, then evaluate that match against the brand color in the actual material. The specification I deliver is the material-and-supplier-specific color reference, not a translation from Pantone to another system.

## The Light Environment Variable

A brand color match that looks accurate in the supplier's showroom can look wrong in the actual installation environment because the light is different. Fluorescent office lighting, incandescent retail lighting, LED task lighting, and natural daylight all render colors differently.

I always evaluate material color samples under the intended lighting conditions before approving them. This often means visiting the site or obtaining lighting samples to test in my studio. The specification that matters is what looks right in context, not what matches on paper.

## Managing Metamerism

Metamerism is the phenomenon where two colors that match under one light source look different under another. A paint and a fabric that appear to match in natural light can look completely different under fluorescent light. This is a real risk when brand colors need to match across different materials.

I test for metamerism by evaluating material samples under multiple light sources. If metamerism is present, I note it in the brand guidelines and specify which material takes priority — the walls match the brand standard; the upholstery is the closest achievable match.

## Documentation for Facilities Teams

The people who repaint offices, reorder upholstery, and specify new environmental materials are often not designers. The brand guidelines need to include material specifications in terms that are actionable for facilities management: the specific paint manufacturer, the color number, the finish, and the supplier contact.
