## The Word That Means Everything

"Clean" is the most common single word in design feedback. Clients say they want something clean. They say a design doesn't feel clean enough. They ask for it to be cleaned up.

In my experience, "clean" is being used as a proxy for at least five different things, and understanding which one the client means is the difference between productive iteration and a cycle of confused revisions.

## Clean as Simple

Sometimes "clean" means the client wants fewer elements, less complexity, more restraint. They're looking at a design that feels busy and using "clean" as the available vocabulary for "too much is happening."

The diagnostic question: is the feedback coming from a composition with many elements? If yes, this is probably the interpretation.

## Clean as Spacious

"Clean" often means "more white space." The client feels the design is cramped or crowded, even if the individual elements are well-chosen. The fix isn't removing elements — it's giving them more room.

## Clean as Legible

Sometimes "clean" means "easier to read." The typography is competing with the image, or the hierarchy isn't working, and the client is describing the reading experience rather than the aesthetic.

## Clean as Corporate/Conventional

This is the most problematic interpretation. Some clients use "clean" to mean "safe" — less personality, less visual distinctiveness, more like what they've seen in their industry. This is often in tension with what the brief asked for.

When I suspect this is the interpretation, I ask directly: "When you say clean, do you mean you'd like the design to feel more like others in your category, or are you referring to a specific visual quality?"

## Clean as Polished

Finally, "clean" sometimes just means "more refined." The execution isn't tight enough — inconsistent spacing, slightly misaligned elements, rough transitions. This is entirely addressable and usually not about the concept at all.

## The Right Response

When I receive "clean" as feedback, I ask one question before revising: "Can you point to a specific element or area that doesn't feel clean to you, and describe what you'd like it to feel like instead?"

Specific, directed feedback is actionable. Uninterpreted aesthetic vocabulary isn't. The question isn't challenging the client — it's genuinely asking for the information needed to revise well. Most clients are relieved to be asked, because they weren't sure how to say what they meant.
