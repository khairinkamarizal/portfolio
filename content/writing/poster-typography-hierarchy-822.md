## The Hierarchy Problem

Walk past a row of posters on a city street and notice which ones make you stop. The ones that stop you have one thing in common: you immediately know what they're about. Not after reading every line — immediately, from a glance.

That instant legibility is hierarchy working correctly. One element dominates, everything else supports. The visual weight is organized from most important to least, and your eye follows that path without effort.

Most poster typography fails at this. Everything competes. The eye has no clear path and moves randomly, or gives up.

## Scale Is the Primary Lever

The most powerful typographic hierarchy tool is scale. The relationship between the largest and smallest type on a poster directly controls how strong the hierarchy reads.

In my poster work, the scale ratio between primary and secondary type is almost always at least 3:1, and often much higher. Primary headline at 120pt, supporting information at 24pt — that's a 5:1 ratio, and the hierarchy is unmistakable.

Many designers are afraid to go this large with their primary element. The fear is that it takes up too much space and crowds everything else. The reality is the opposite: going very large with the primary element forces you to be selective about everything else, which usually makes the poster better.

## Weight as Complement to Scale

Weight contrast reinforces scale contrast. If the largest type is also the heaviest weight, the hierarchy is doubled. But weight can also work in counterpoint to scale — light display type at large size can be more elegant than heavy type, as long as the scale contrast still carries the hierarchy.

The combination I avoid: large type in a light weight alongside smaller type in a heavy weight. This reverses the hierarchy visually — the heavy small type pulls the eye before the large light type does.

## Spacing Defines Zones

Spacing between groups of information creates visual zones that the eye reads as separate layers. Information grouped closely together reads as related. Space between groups reads as separation and priority shift.

I use generous space between the primary type zone and the secondary information zone — much more than feels comfortable initially. This space is not empty — it's doing active work, separating the two registers of information so the hierarchy is physically visible.

## The One-Glance Test

My final test for poster typography: I pin the poster on the wall, walk to the other side of the room, and look at it for two seconds. What did I read? What did I not read?

If I absorbed the one most important piece of information — usually the event name or primary message — the hierarchy is working. If I absorbed more than that in two seconds, the hierarchy is excellent. If I'm not sure what I read, something is wrong.

The poster exists in a competitive visual environment. It earns attention for a moment and then loses it. Every typographic decision should serve those few seconds.
