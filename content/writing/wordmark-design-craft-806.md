## Wordmarks Are Not Just Fonts

The most common mistake I see in wordmark design is using an off-the-shelf typeface, changing the color, and calling it a logo. That's a font treatment, not a wordmark. A real wordmark has been customized — letterforms modified, spacing tuned, proportions adjusted — so that it reads as a unified, intentional object rather than set text.

This distinction matters because off-the-shelf typefaces can be reproduced by anyone. A customized wordmark is unique to the brand.

## The Customization Toolkit

When I develop a wordmark, I typically start with a typeface as a foundation and then modify it. My customization process usually involves:

**Letter modifications** — adjusting terminals, simplifying or adding detail to specific characters, modifying cap heights or x-heights selectively to create rhythm.

**Spacing decisions** — tracking in a wordmark is not the same as body text tracking. I'll often go tighter than default to feel more branded and ownable. Sometimes specific letter pairs get adjusted individually.

**Proportion tweaks** — making certain letters fractionally narrower or wider to improve the visual balance of the whole word as a horizontal object.

**Connection and overlap** — sometimes creating subtle connections between letters that make the wordmark feel like a single drawn object rather than a sequence of characters.

## The Weight Question

Wordmark weight is one of the most consequential decisions in the whole process. Heavy wordmarks feel authoritative and stable — they also compete with everything around them. Light wordmarks feel refined and modern — they also disappear at small sizes and in less-than-ideal print conditions.

I almost always pick something in the medium-to-semibold range as a starting point, then adjust based on the specific letterforms and the brand's positioning.

## Testing Across Contexts

A wordmark needs to work in isolation and in context. Testing I do before calling a wordmark finished:

- On a white background and a black background
- Reversed out of the brand's primary color
- At business card size
- On a long horizontal surface (banner, website header)
- Alongside body text and supporting graphics
- In grayscale

If any of these contexts breaks the wordmark, I go back and figure out why. Usually it's a spacing or weight issue that wasn't visible at presentation scale.

## When Bespoke Lettering Makes Sense

For some clients, I'll go further and draw the wordmark from scratch rather than modifying existing letterforms. This makes sense when the brand has a very specific personality that no existing typeface captures, when owning every curve and stroke matters for licensing reasons, or when the budget and timeline allow for it.

Bespoke lettering is slower and more expensive, but the result is genuinely unique and the process often surfaces brand ideas that modifications of existing type wouldn't reveal.

## The Longevity Standard

I ask one question about every wordmark before I deliver it: will this still feel right in fifteen years? Wordmarks shouldn't be redesigned every three years like a campaign visual. They're infrastructure. That means avoiding whatever is fashionable right now in favor of what is structurally sound and typographically considered. The brands with the most recognizable wordmarks have been running essentially the same mark for decades.
