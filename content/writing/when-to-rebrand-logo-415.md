Rebranding is the most expensive, highest-risk creative project most organizations undertake. It takes months, costs significant money, risks alienating existing customers, and disrupts every piece of communications collateral you have. It should not be undertaken lightly.

And yet I see brands pursuing full visual rebrands when what they actually need is something cheaper, faster, and less disruptive. New brand guidelines. Consistent application of existing assets. A better website. A different messaging strategy. Occasionally just a new color for their social media templates.

This isn't me advocating against rebrand work — obviously, as someone who does it, I'm not. But it's important to be honest about when a rebrand is the right tool and when it isn't.

## Signs You Actually Need a Rebrand

**The identity no longer reflects the business.** If the company has fundamentally changed — different market position, different customer, different product category — and the identity was built for who you used to be, a rebrand is often necessary. Trying to stretch an old identity to cover a new reality creates confusion and friction.

**The identity has insurmountable technical problems.** Logos built for a pre-digital era often fail at the scale and versatility requirements of modern brand use. If the logo can't be made to work at 16px, can't be printed in one color, and requires special handling in every application, it may be more efficient to redesign than to maintain an ever-growing collection of workarounds.

**The identity is creating competitive disadvantage.** If the brand consistently looks and feels less credible than direct competitors — and research or anecdote supports that it's costing business — a rebrand may be warranted.

**The brand carries actively negative associations.** Sometimes a brand has become associated with a scandal, a failure, or a value set the company no longer holds. When the existing identity is actively working against the organization's interests, change is necessary.

## Signs You Probably Don't Need a Full Rebrand

**You're bored with it.** Internal fatigue is not a business reason to rebrand. Designers and executives see their own brand materials constantly and tire of them long before the market does. What feels stale internally often still reads as recognizable and trusted externally.

**A competitor just rebranded.** Competitive pressure to rebrand because a competitor did is almost always the wrong reason. Unless their rebrand fundamentally changed the competitive landscape in a way that disadvantages you, resist the urge to react.

**The logo "just feels dated."** This one requires more investigation. Is it dated in a way that signals untrustworthiness? Or is it dated in a way that signals heritage and longevity? Those are very different problems.

**Inconsistent application is making the brand look bad.** This is a brand management problem, not an identity problem. New guidelines and enforcement will solve this without the cost and risk of a rebrand.

## The Evolution Option

Between "no change" and "full rebrand" there's a third path: evolution. A systematic refinement of the existing identity — updated color values, refined typography, improved logo construction — that preserves brand equity while improving the system's functionality.

Evolution is almost always less disruptive than a rebrand and often achieves similar goals. The brand's recognizable DNA stays intact. The awkward technical problems get resolved. The dated elements get modernized. The customer still recognizes the brand.

When a client comes to me with a rebrand brief, the first question I ask is: does this need to be a rebrand, or can we get there through evolution? Getting that answer right is worth more than any subsequent design work.
