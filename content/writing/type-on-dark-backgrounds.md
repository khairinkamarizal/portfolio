Most designers learn typography on white backgrounds. The conventions of typesetting — leading, tracking, weight — are developed for dark type on light paper. When you flip that to light type on dark backgrounds, the same settings often look wrong. The type is too tight, too heavy, or too small.

Dark background typography requires adjustment, not just color inversion.

## Why Type Looks Different on Dark

Type set in white on black looks visually heavier than the same type set in black on white at the same point size. This is an optical phenomenon: the white letterforms appear to expand slightly against the dark background, making strokes look wider.

This means that a typeface that looks refined and elegant in positive color can look crude and heavy when reversed. The same optical expansion effect makes tight tracking look even tighter and thin strokes look thicker.

## Adjustments for Dark Backgrounds

**Reduce weight:** A regular weight typeface often reads better reversed than a medium weight. A medium that's ideal in positive context often needs to drop to regular when reversed.

**Open tracking:** Slightly more tracking (letter spacing) compensates for the optical crowding that dark backgrounds create. Not dramatically more — I typically add 2–4% tracking where I'd use 0% in positive text.

**Increase leading:** Dark backgrounds make text blocks feel denser. Slightly increased line height makes the text breathe.

**Test at small sizes:** Thin strokes in serif typefaces disappear at small sizes on dark backgrounds before they disappear on light ones. If you need small text on a dark background, use a typeface with low stroke contrast and robust thin strokes.

## White vs. Tinted Type

Pure white on pure black is very high contrast — almost uncomfortably so at large areas of text. I often use a slightly warm or cool off-white for body text on dark backgrounds to reduce the harsh contrast while maintaining legibility. 90% warm white on a neutral dark background is gentler than 100% white.

This small adjustment has a disproportionate effect on how readable and comfortable extended reading is on a dark surface.

## Dark Palette Variation

Dark background design doesn't have to mean black background design. Dark navy, deep charcoal, dark forest green — rich dark hues as backgrounds change the character of reversed type significantly compared to pure black. The type color doesn't have to be pure white either — an off-white, a light tint of the brand color, or a light accent hue can all work as reversed type colors.

The constraint is contrast: the contrast between the type and its background has to be sufficient for legibility. 4.5:1 contrast ratio is the minimum accessibility threshold, and I typically exceed this for any primary communication.

## Poster Applications

Reversed-out posters have a specific quality — dramatic, bold, night-time, premium. The dark background creates a sense of depth that light backgrounds don't have. When a poster's concept calls for these associations, designing in reversed-out type is a natural choice.

The adjustments I make in print are more extreme than in digital. Printed dark fields absorb light differently than screens emit it, and the resulting optical experience on paper is different. I always proof dark posters before finalizing.
