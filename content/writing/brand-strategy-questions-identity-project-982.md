## Design Without Strategy Is Decoration

A beautifully crafted logo for a brand with no clear strategy is a beautiful object with nowhere to live. The logo needs to represent something true about the organization — its positioning, its values, its relationship to its audience. Without that clarity, the designer is making aesthetic choices in a vacuum.

The strategic work happens before the design work. When it doesn't, I do it anyway — pulling it into the process through the intake questions I ask every new client.

## Who Are You, Actually?

The first question sounds easy and almost never is: who is this organization, actually?

Not who they say they are in their marketing copy. Not who they wish they were. Who are they in practice, day to day, in the way their customers experience them?

This distinction matters because a lot of briefs describe an aspirational identity — the brand the organization wants to become — while the actual organization has quite different qualities. Designing for the aspiration without acknowledging the current reality produces a brand that feels false, because it doesn't match the actual customer experience.

I want to understand both: who they are now and who they're working toward being. The identity design can bridge the gap, but it can't create a gap so large that it becomes dishonest.

## Who Is Your Audience, in Detail?

The audiences clients describe are almost always too broad. "Adults 25–54" isn't an audience. "Small business owners who've tried three other solutions and are willing to pay for something that actually works" is an audience.

The more specific the audience description, the better the design decisions I can make. What does this specific person value? What visual language signals quality and credibility to them? What visual language feels generic or corporate in a way they'd distrust?

This level of audience specificity isn't always available from the client. In those cases, I supplement with my own research — looking at the visual landscape the audience actually inhabits, what brands they respond to, what category conventions they've seen enough times to be skeptical of.

## What Do You Want People to Feel?

After understanding what the brand is and who the audience is, I want to understand the intended emotional response. Not "professional" — everyone wants professional. Not "modern" — everyone says modern.

What do you want the ideal customer to feel thirty seconds after their first encounter with this brand? Reassured? Excited? Curious? Impressed? Welcomed?

These emotional states are design inputs. Reassurance demands different visual choices than excitement. Curiosity demands different choices than being impressed. Getting the intended emotional response specific and honest is one of the most important things the strategy conversation can produce.

## What Makes This Brand Genuinely Different?

Every brand claims differentiation. Most don't actually have it, or can't articulate what it is. This question pushes until I find something real.

Sometimes the differentiation is genuinely in the product or service. Sometimes it's in the approach — how the company behaves with customers. Sometimes it's in the people — the specific expertise or perspective that can't be easily replicated. Sometimes it's in the positioning — a combination of qualities that nobody else occupies, even if individual qualities are shared.

Whatever the differentiation is, the visual identity should express it. A brand that's genuinely different in a specific way should have an identity that communicates that specific difference, not a generic "professional and modern" identity that could belong to any competitor.

## Who Are Your Competitors and What Do They Look Like?

Competitive analysis is always part of the intake. I want to see what the visual landscape looks like in this specific market — what colors are overrepresented, what aesthetic approaches are standard, what territory is unclaimed.

This isn't about being different for the sake of being different. It's about understanding the context the new identity will live in. A brand that looks like its competitors doesn't stand out. A brand that looks completely unlike its category can feel like it doesn't belong.

The strategy is to find the position that's both genuine to the brand and distinctive in the competitive context.
