A wordmark is deceptively simple. No icon, no mark, no illustration — just the name, set in type, refined until it holds together as a single visual unit. Because there's nowhere to hide, every decision shows. The typeface selection, the tracking, the weight, the custom letterform adjustments — all of it contributes to whether the mark feels authoritative or arbitrary.

## Starting with the Right Typeface

For most wordmark projects, I'll sketch and explore with hand-drawn letterforms first, but I also run a broad typeface exploration in parallel. I want to understand what genre of type the brand occupies before I start customizing.

The genre question matters: is this a geometric sans, a humanist serif, a transitional roman, an industrial display face? Each carries associations that precede any customization. A brand that needs to feel precise and rational probably shouldn't start from a calligraphic base. A brand that wants warmth and approachability probably shouldn't start from a cold geometric grotesque.

I'm usually looking for a typeface that's 70–80% right. Close enough that I'm not fighting the base, but with enough room for meaningful customization that the result isn't just "type set in a font."

## The Customization Layer

The real work in wordmark design happens after you've selected your base. This is where you move from "type" to "mark."

Common modifications I make:

**Tracking and spacing.** Optical kerning from most fonts isn't good enough for display-weight wordmarks. I adjust every pair manually. I'm looking for even color across the word — not even mathematical spacing, but even visual density.

**Weight adjustment.** Sometimes I'll stroke individual strokes to add weight to specific letters, or thin out connections that feel heavy. This is easier when you're working with geometric or neo-grotesque types that have consistent stroke widths.

**Letterform customization.** Modifying terminals, simplifying details that read as noise at small scale, adding unique character touches that make the mark feel custom rather than typeset. The goal isn't to be clever — it's to make the word feel like it was designed as a unit, not assembled from off-the-shelf parts.

**Fit and optical corrections.** Round letters (O, C, G) need to extend slightly above the cap height and below the baseline to optically appear the same height as flat-topped letters. Most fonts do this, but check it at your intended display weight.

## Testing at Real Sizes

Wordmarks get used at a huge range of sizes — from a 10mm business card to a 3-meter building sign. I test every wordmark candidate at both extremes before finalizing anything.

At small sizes, details collapse. Tight letter spacing becomes muddy. Thin strokes disappear. Anything too clever in the letterforms becomes unreadable noise. A wordmark that only works at large scale isn't a functional identity — it's a poster element.

At large scale, coarseness shows. Imprecise curves, inconsistent stroke widths, poorly resolved corners — these are invisible at 30pt but glaring at 300pt on a banner. I work in large artboards and zoom out to check, rather than designing small and assuming it will scale up.

## Single-Weight vs. Multi-Weight

Some wordmarks live at a single weight. Others need a companion — a heavier version for applications where impact matters, a lighter version for refined contexts. If I think a brand will need range, I design both from the start and make sure they feel like siblings: same character, different register.

## The Final Check

Before I present a wordmark, I set it on every background color in the palette, at three sizes, in lockup with any tagline or descriptor. I want to see it working in context, not just floating in white space. A mark that looks elegant in isolation can feel wrong in the context of actual brand use.

The wordmark is the brand's name given a specific visual personality. That responsibility is worth the time it takes to get it right.
