## What Brand Equity Actually Is

Brand equity is the value a brand adds to a product or service beyond the functional value of the product itself. A customer pays more for a brand they trust and recognize than for an identical product without that brand. The difference in price they're willing to pay is a rough measure of brand equity.

Visual identity — particularly the logo — is one of the primary vehicles through which brand equity is stored and communicated. When someone sees the mark and it triggers associations of quality, reliability, or aspiration, those associations are equity. The mark is the trigger.

## How Equity Accumulates in a Mark

Equity doesn't accumulate because a mark is well-designed. It accumulates through consistent, positive experience over time. Every positive interaction with the brand, paired with a consistent identity, deposits a small amount of equity into the mark.

This is why consistency matters so much in brand identity work. Inconsistent application dilutes the equity accumulation. Every time the mark appears in a different form, color, or context than the established standard, the deposit is smaller or negative.

It's also why sudden redesigns can destroy value. If the mark changes significantly, the accumulated equity is partly or entirely disconnected from the new mark. The recognition is gone. The associations don't automatically transfer.

## What Designers Often Underestimate

Designers — myself included, when I was earlier in my career — often underestimate how much equity is sitting in visual details that look like they could be improved.

A specific shade of blue. An unusual letterform choice. An idiosyncratic proportion in the logo mark. These things might look arbitrary or even suboptimal from a pure design perspective. But if they're what customers have been recognizing for twenty years, they carry equity.

Improving them has a cost. Sometimes the improvement is worth the cost. But the cost has to be acknowledged rather than dismissed.

## When Changing the Mark Is the Right Decision

There are legitimate reasons to change a mark even at the cost of some equity:

**The mark is genuinely dysfunctional** — it doesn't reproduce across required contexts, it has technical problems, it was wrong from the start.

**The brand has fundamentally changed** — new ownership, new positioning, a pivot to a new market. The old mark carries associations that are now inaccurate or damaging.

**The equity has gone negative** — the brand has been associated with something negative and the mark carries that association. A fresh start can be strategically justified.

Outside these cases, I recommend evolution over revolution. Preserve what has equity. Fix what's broken.

## The Designer's Responsibility

When I'm redesigning a mark with equity, I carry a specific responsibility: not to let aesthetic preference override strategic sense. The previous mark might not be what I would have designed. That's irrelevant. What matters is what it's worth to the brand's audience and what will be lost if it changes.

I document the equity assets explicitly in my project brief: what specific elements of the existing mark have recognition and should be preserved. That document keeps me honest when the temptation to redesign everything is strong.

Restraint in a redesign is not a creative failure. It's a strategic success.
