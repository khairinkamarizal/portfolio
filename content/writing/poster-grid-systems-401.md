When I started designing posters, I thought grids were for timid designers who couldn't trust their eye. I was wrong. After producing hundreds of posters for music venues, cultural institutions, and festivals, I now set up my grid before I do anything else. Not because I lack confidence, but because a grid is how I move fast without losing control.

## What a Grid Actually Does

A grid isn't decoration. It's a set of invisible decisions you make once, up front, so you don't have to remake them for every element. It tells you where type can live, where images breathe, where white space becomes intentional rather than accidental.

The most common poster mistake I see from newer designers: they place elements by feel, nudging things pixel by pixel until it "looks right." The result is usually a composition that's almost balanced — which reads worse than something confidently asymmetric or rigorously centered.

## The Systems I Actually Use

For event posters, I almost always work with a 12-column grid at whatever size I'm designing. Twelve columns give me flexibility: I can do halves, thirds, quarters, all from the same base. For a concert poster that needs to hit hard, I'll collapse everything into a 3-column structure. For something more editorial — a festival program cover — I'll use all 12 and let hierarchy guide the eye.

Baseline grids matter just as much as column grids. Locking body copy and caption text to a consistent baseline makes a poster feel coherent even when there's a lot of information competing for space. The viewer doesn't consciously notice the baseline grid, but they notice when it's absent.

## Breaking the Grid on Purpose

Here's the thing: once you have a working grid, you earn the right to break it. A headline that bleeds past the column margin, a photograph that ignores the safe zone, a single word set at a diagonal — these only work when everything else is structured. The contrast is what gives the violation its power.

I broke the grid on purpose for a theatre poster last spring. The title treatment ran 15 degrees off axis, cutting across the grid lines. Because every other element was locked to the baseline and column structure, the diagonal read as intentional drama, not sloppiness.

## Starting Points for Your Grid

When I start a new poster, here's my typical setup:

- **Format first.** Know your print dimensions before you draw a single guide.
- **Set your margins.** For A2 posters, I usually use 12–15mm margins. For tabloid, around 18mm.
- **Choose column count.** 3, 4, or 6 columns for most work. 12 for complex layouts.
- **Establish baseline.** Match it to your primary body text leading. For poster work that's usually 16–24pt.
- **Test with text.** Drop a block of your smallest copy onto the grid before you touch images. If it reads cleanly, the grid is working.

## The Discipline Pays Off

I can set up a poster grid in under three minutes. The payoff is that I spend the rest of my time making actual design decisions — what to say, how loud to say it, which element earns the viewer's eye first. The grid handles the rest. That's not a constraint. That's leverage.
