## Scale Changes Everything

A design that works at A3 poster size may fail catastrophically at large format. The type that read clearly at arm's length becomes impossibly small from thirty meters. The photographic detail that added richness looks blurry and pixelated at billboard scale. The fine rule that provided elegant structure becomes invisible.

Large format print is its own design discipline. I've produced work for event banners, trade show displays, building wraps, transit advertising, and billboards. The rules are consistent: simplify everything, increase everything, and think about viewing distance before touching any design element.

## The Viewing Distance Rule

The first question I ask on any large format brief is the minimum viewing distance. This determines everything: how large the type needs to be, how detailed the imagery can be, how much information can realistically be communicated.

The standard rule: for every meter of minimum viewing distance, allow approximately one inch of type height for primary headline text. Viewing from ten meters means a minimum of roughly 250mm headline height. From a hundred meters — a highway billboard — the headline needs to be close to 600mm tall.

These numbers make most designers uncomfortable because the type is so much larger than anything they work with in standard print contexts. But these are the numbers that work. Smaller type is invisible at distance.

## Simplicity as a Production Requirement

Large format simplicity isn't just aesthetic — it's functional. A complex layout with many elements reads as undifferentiated noise from viewing distance. The eye cannot parse fine detail at scale. Everything that isn't essential to the primary message becomes visual interference.

I reduce every large format design to its absolute minimum. One primary headline. One visual element. One supporting piece of information if essential. One call to action or brand identification. That's the complete inventory for most effective large format work.

## Resolution Requirements for Different Outputs

File resolution for large format printing is counterintuitive. The standard 300 DPI requirement for standard print does not apply. Large format printers typically output at 72–150 DPI — but at the intended print size.

A billboard that's 4 meters wide needs images that are large in absolute pixel dimensions, but the DPI can be much lower than standard print. A 4-meter-wide image at 72 DPI is 11,340 pixels wide. Most photographers can provide images that large from a contemporary high-resolution camera file.

The check I always run: open the image in Photoshop, set the document size to the final print dimensions, and confirm the DPI at that size. If it's above 60 DPI for viewing distances of 10 meters or more, the image will look acceptable in print.

## Typography for Distance

All of the principles of typography at distance apply in their most extreme form to large format work. Sans-serif faces with open apertures and generous x-heights are almost always the right choice for primary text. Serif typefaces are too detailed for most large format contexts.

Tracking should be generous. At distance, tight letter spacing reads as a smear of text. Wide tracking helps each letter maintain its identity.

Contrast between type and background needs to be high. The contrast that looks strong in a design file is reduced by sun glare, atmospheric haze, and distance. Add contrast beyond what looks right on screen.

## Color in Outdoor Environments

Outdoor printing is affected by ambient light in ways that indoor printing isn't. Direct sunlight bleaches colors toward the lighter end of the spectrum. Overcast light makes colors look flat and muted. Shade shifts colors toward the cool end.

I design outdoor materials with slightly higher saturation than my final intent, knowing that real-world conditions will reduce the perceived saturation. I also test the design under simulated outdoor lighting if possible before final production approval.
