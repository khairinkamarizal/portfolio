## The Stationery System as Brand Statement

A stationery system — business cards, letterhead, envelopes, notecards, compliment slips — is the most tactile brand touchpoint most organizations produce. It's held, handed to people, handled repeatedly. The physical quality of the materials and the coherence of the design across pieces communicate more about the brand's character than almost any other designed object.

When a stationery system is done well, picking up any piece of it immediately communicates the brand and feels like handling something from a considered, complete world. When it's done poorly, the pieces feel like they were designed separately by different people — same logo, different spatial sensibility, different paper stock, different typographic approach.

## The Grid That Links All Pieces

The first design decision in a stationery system is the spatial system that will link all the pieces. I establish consistent margins and layout zones that apply across different format sizes.

The key proportional elements: the position of the logo on each piece, the consistent margin from the logo to the nearest edge, the position of the contact information block. These relationships should feel geometrically consistent even though the actual dimensions change between a business card and an A4 letterhead.

I derive the proportional system by designing all pieces at the same time rather than designing the business card first and then scaling the system to other formats. Concurrent design reveals conflicts and opportunities that sequential design misses.

## Paper as a Design Material

Paper stock selection is not a production decision — it's a design decision. The physical weight of a business card communicates instantly. A 400gsm cotton business card communicates very differently from an 85gsm standard business card, even if the printed design is identical.

I specify paper stock in my stationery briefs with the same precision I specify colors. The front-of-card paper, back-of-card paper, letterhead stock, and envelope stock should all feel like they belong to the same material family. Mixing a heavy card stock with a thin, flimsy letterhead paper undermines the overall quality impression.

## Print Method as Identity Signal

Standard offset printing is a given. The premium brands I design for often add a print technique that elevates the system: letterpress or thermography for the business card, foil on the envelope closure, blind emboss on the notecards.

These techniques are chosen for what they communicate, not just for their visual quality. Letterpress has a craft, hand-produced quality. Foil signals luxury. Blind emboss is understated and tactile. I match the technique to the brand's character rather than applying premium techniques uniformly.

## Typography Across Small Formats

Business cards are typographically demanding because so much needs to communicate in a tiny space. I approach business card typography as a miniature layout challenge, not as a scaling problem. The type sizes that work for letterhead don't simply scale down to card size — they need to be reconsidered for the specific format.

I typically design business card type at 6.5–8pt for contact information, and I test every size on an actual printed proof before final approval. Screen rendering at business card size is unreliable — the proof is the only truth.

## The Reverse Side

The back of a business card is a design surface that many designers leave blank or treat as an afterthought. I design both sides intentionally. The back might carry a strong brand color field with the logo reversed out, or a graphic pattern derived from the brand's visual grammar, or simply white space with carefully positioned secondary contact information.

Turning a card over should feel like a continuation of the front, not like discovering the back of a printed label.
