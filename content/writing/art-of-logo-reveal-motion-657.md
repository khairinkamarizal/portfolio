Every brand that has a digital presence eventually needs an animated logo. A logo reveal — the animation that introduces the brand mark in video contexts — is one of the most frequently produced pieces of brand motion work. It's also one of the most commonly overworked, underconsidered, and brand-inconsistent pieces of motion design.

Here's how I approach logo reveal design.

## Start With the Brand Personality, Not the Technical Capabilities

The most common mistake in logo reveal design is leading with technique. Someone saw a liquid metal reveal on a competitor's reel and wants one. Someone discovered the shattering particles effect in After Effects and wants to use it. The technique comes first and the brand rationale is constructed afterward.

The right order: understand the brand's motion personality first. What does this brand's motion language feel like? Is it precise and snappy? Organic and fluid? Bold and declarative? Quiet and refined? The logo reveal should feel continuous with that motion language — not like a separate production piece that happens to contain the logo.

## The Mark Determines the Animation Approach

The structure of the logo mark itself suggests animation approaches. A mark built from geometric forms — circles, lines, angles — naturally animates through construction: the forms build, assemble, arrive. A mark with organic flowing forms suggests reveal through draw-on or fluid-fill approaches. A typographic mark might animate through character reveal, tracking, or typesetting.

The best logo animations feel as if the mark is being created before your eyes in the way it was drawn. They respect the geometry and logic of the mark rather than applying a generic animation on top of it.

## The Hold: The Most Important Moment

Every logo reveal has three phases: the animation in, the hold, and often an animation out. The hold — the moment when the logo is fully formed and simply present — is the most important moment. It's when the brand is at rest, confident, fully itself.

A hold that's too short cheats the moment of recognition. The viewer barely registers the logo before it's gone. A hold that's too long can feel static in a motion context. The right hold duration depends on the overall length of the piece but should be at minimum one second — enough for the viewer to absorb the mark.

## Timing and Easing for Logo Work

Brand logo reveals tend to have more gravity and less bounce than other motion elements. The mark shouldn't feel like a toy — it should feel like an arrival. Easing that decelerates to a clean stop (ease out) feels more authoritative than easing that overshoots and settles (spring/bounce).

Duration: I target 1.5-2.5 seconds for a full logo reveal animation, excluding the hold. Shorter feels rushed and undercuts the brand presence. Longer can feel indulgent.

## Sound Design

A logo reveal without sound design is incomplete. The sound should reinforce the visual personality — clean, tonal, and brief for minimal brands; more complex or layered for brands with richer personalities.

The sound should arrive at the moment of logo lock-up — the moment the mark becomes fully formed — not before or after. Sound-to-vision synchronization at the key moment is what makes the reveal feel intentional.
