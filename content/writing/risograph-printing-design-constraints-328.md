I printed my first Risograph run three years ago at a local arts collective. I had never worked with the process before, and the first prints looked nothing like what I designed on screen. The registration was imprecise. The ink colors were more muted than expected. Some of the fine detail I'd spent hours on completely disappeared.

I went back and redesigned the poster for the process. The second version was better than anything I'd have designed without those constraints.

## What Riso Is

Risograph printing is a stencil-based duplicating process originally developed for office printing. Each color is a separate drum and a separate pass through the machine. The inks are soy-based and sit on the paper rather than absorbing into it, creating a characteristic flat, matte quality with a slight texture.

Like screen printing, Riso works in flat layers of single colors. No CMYK mixing — each color is its own discrete layer. Where two colors overlap, they print transparently, creating a third color from the combination.

## The Transparency Overlap as Design Element

The most visually distinctive quality of Riso is the way overlapping colors interact. A yellow and a blue overlapping produce a green — but the specific green depends on the ink coverage, the paper, and the exact registration of the two passes. This unpredictability is a design material.

I design Riso work with the overlaps as the primary compositional element. Where two colors cross is often the most visually interesting area of the print. I plan these overlaps deliberately and accept that the exact result will vary between prints.

## Registration as Character

Riso registration is loose by nature. The paper feeds through the machine twice (once per color), and alignment is never perfect. This imprecision creates a slight double-image quality at the edges of overlapping elements that reads as handmade and warm.

Fighting the registration leads to frustration. Designing with it leads to a quality that you can't get from any process where perfect registration is possible.

## What the Constraint Teaches

Designing for Riso teaches economy. Every line has to justify its existence — fine detail disappears, so only confident, significant marks survive. Color relationships have to work with a limited, fixed palette. Compositions have to read when registration is imperfect.

These lessons transfer back to work in any medium. The question "would this survive Riso printing?" is a useful diagnostic for any design. Would the essential composition hold up if the fine detail disappeared? If yes, the design has structural integrity. If no, the design is relying on detail to compensate for a weaker underlying structure.
