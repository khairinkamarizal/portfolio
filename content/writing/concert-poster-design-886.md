## The Format with Real Freedom

Concert posters are the format where I feel most creatively free. The brief is typically short: here's the artist, here's the date and venue, make something that captures the feel of the music. No brand guidelines to navigate, no committee approvals, sometimes not even an art director — just the music and the format.

That freedom is its own discipline. Without constraints, the decisions are all yours, and making them well requires having clear opinions about what the work should do.

## The Gig Poster Tradition

The concert poster has one of the most interesting visual histories in graphic design. From the San Francisco psychedelic posters of the late 1960s to the Austin screen-printing scene of the 1990s and 2000s, concert poster design has produced remarkable work that influenced graphic design far beyond music.

What I find compelling about this tradition: it developed largely outside the mainstream of commercial design, with practitioners who were often deeply immersed in the music they were designing for. The design and the music were in genuine conversation, not in the client-vendor relationship of most commercial work.

## Translating Sound to Form

The most interesting challenge in concert poster design is translating sound to visual form — capturing something about the music's character through color, texture, image, and type choices. This isn't illustration of lyrics or visualization of waveforms. It's asking: what visual language has the same emotional register as this music?

For a drone metal band, I might reach for heavy black forms, claustrophobic negative space, type that feels geological in weight and age. For a jazz pianist, something different: more space, more conversation between elements, type with swing and breath.

## Screen Print as Design Constraint

Many of the best concert posters are screen printed — a process that limits the number of colors and forces design decisions that digital print doesn't. Designing for two or three colors of screen print is designing for a system where every color needs to earn its place.

I design for screen print even when the final output is digital, because the constraint improves the work. Limiting myself to three colors forces me to work with what those colors can do together rather than reaching for photographic complexity.

## The Collectible Object

Concert posters have a life after the event as collectible objects. A poster for a notable show by a significant artist can be worth real money in the right collector community. This gives the work a longevity that most commercial graphic design doesn't have — people frame it, preserve it, build collections around it.

Designing something that aspires to that kind of longevity requires thinking beyond the immediate event. Not just "does this communicate the gig?" but "does this work as an art object someone would want to keep?"
