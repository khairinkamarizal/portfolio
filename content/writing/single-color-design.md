One of the most productive constraints I've ever imposed on my own work is the one-color poster. One ink color. No gradients. Everything achieved through positive form, negative space, and composition.

I've given this constraint to students and watched what happens. The first reaction is usually panic. The second, after they've worked with it for a while, is usually revelation.

## What the Constraint Removes

One color removes every shortcut that color provides. You can't create hierarchy with color variation. You can't distinguish categories with different hues. You can't create depth with value modulation across a color scale.

Everything that would normally be solved by reaching for a different color has to be solved by something else: scale, position, density of form, presence or absence of elements.

When those tools have to do the work alone, they get stronger.

## What Survives the Constraint

The formal qualities that survive a one-color constraint are exactly the ones that define whether a design has structural integrity. Strong compositions work in single color because they're built on solid spatial logic. Strong type hierarchies work in single color because they're built on real size and weight contrast, not just color contrast.

Weak designs collapse in single color because they were relying on color to carry them. The constraint is a diagnostic tool as much as a creative one.

## Halftone and Its Possibilities

Within a one-color constraint, halftone expands the design vocabulary. Screened percentages of the single color create value variation — the 100% solid area is full weight; 20% screen is a light tint; 50% screen is a mid-value. This allows for tonal range within the one-color rule.

Large halftone dots are a design element in themselves — not just a way to create gray, but a texture with its own graphic character. Coarse halftone screens create a bold, retro quality. Fine screens create a subtler value range.

## Overprint in Two-Color Work

The logical extension of single-color discipline is overprint thinking in two-color work. When two spot colors overprint, they create a third color — but only where they overlap. Designing specifically for overprinted areas creates a color palette of three from two inks.

This is the thinking behind a lot of great screen print and risograph work. Two inks, strategically overlapped, create the impression of a richer palette. The constraint produces creative problem-solving that unrestricted color doesn't require.

## When I Apply the Constraint to Real Projects

I don't impose single-color constraints on client work without reason. But I do apply single-color review to every design I make — evaluating the composition, hierarchy, and spatial logic without the assistance of color, before finalizing. If the design holds up in single color, it's structurally sound. If it collapses, I know what to fix before adding color back.
